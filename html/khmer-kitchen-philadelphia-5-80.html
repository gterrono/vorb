    I've been here about 4 or 5 times, I can't remember. The reason I continuously come back is because of the food. This family owned business cooks authentic Cambodian cuisine with bursting, rich and deep flavors. Everything made in this kitchen comes out incredibly fresh, from chicken, beef, fish to simple ingredients like vegetables and herbs. Their food tends to be spicy and their taste profile is something in the middle of Thai and Vietnamese. I give their food a 5/5.

    Some of the dishes that I always order at Khmer Kitchen are Bok La Hong (papaya salad with a spicy lime fish sauce) very refreshing, tasty and spicy! I also order the Cha-Mussels which comes in this curry sauce with bell peppers and onions sprinkled with fresh basil on top. These mussels are deep in flavor, juicy and completely savory with every slurp. The Prah-Hok Kahteeh is a dish that has an assortment of fresh cut vegetables with a special pork sauce dip. It makes you feel guilt free since you're eating your vegetables with the most delicious meat sauce ever. Cha Lok-Lak are sauté beef cubes sitting over a bed of lettuce and caramelized onions and is accompanied by a salt, pepper and lime dipping sauce. It is very similar to Bo Luc Lac, a Vietnamese dish which basically means shaking beef. Their whole fried Tilapia is probably the best fish you'll ever pick bones off. Its deep fried and the skin of the fish is crispy but once you puncture the skin into the meat you'll see steam coming off the flaky rich delicious flesh. It usually comes with lettuce, herbs and cucumber. It also comes with a mango relish and sometimes tamarind sauce. Also try their grilled beef skewers, again deep in flavor and gives characteristics of hard to find street foods. Their dishes are served family style and cooked homestyle.

    Now for some improvements... The actual restaurant is really small so it fills up quick on weekends and holidays and they do not take rsvp. They often run out of dishes and ingredients which is understandable because everything is made from scratch. However this happens too often and they need to manage their supplies. They don't open on time sometimes, once I tried to get lunch and a gentleman told me that the chef was out buying groceries! It was around 12pm and their regular hours are 10am-9pm. I found that odd and unprofessional. The wait in between dishes could be consecutive or it could take forever. They have a tv in the restaurant. The interior of this place is very colorful. It's not a fancy place so do not expect fine dining. It is what you see. They don't really take you through the menu but if you ask them, they will gladly give you recommendations. They need to work on clearing the table since food is served family style and there isn't much room on the tables to fit incoming dishes. Otherwise I love that they are a family owned business which makes their food that much more special because it's made with love and care. They come to check on you often to refill beverages. They speak English well so its easy to communicate. They are also a BYOB. They are truly a gem in South Philly. The price for the quality and quantity of the food will leave you speechless and coming back for more. I continue to come back because of the food and genuine service, it's that plain and simple.

    Next time I go back I'm definitely going to try to be more open minded to try new dishes even though it's going to be hard to not order some of my favorites.

    Keep in mind, their food is really spicy but they will adjust it for you, they do not take RSVP and they are CASH ONLY. ENJOY!

    Imagine making a smelly cheese using minced fish instead of milk, by leaving said fish in a sealed jar in the sun for 2 weeks.

    Call it "prahok" (pronounced pruh-HOK).

    Then imagine that the smell is considered a delicacy by everyone you know over the age of 50. It ranks (pun intended) up there with durian, animal carcasses (not that far off when you think about it), and turpentine mixed with wine that's turned into moldy vinegar.

    You and your cousins used to make jokes about keeping the boogeyman away by putting some prahok under your beds.  You used prahok in every dare you could think of, growing up. You chickened out every time because you remembered the one time your dad had you try it on a slice of cucumber and it tasted like an old sweaty gym sock that had been wrapped around an infection of some sort as a desperate measure, and then burned. THAT smell.

    Then, imagine your surprise when people who know what they're doing take some of this stuff, cook it up with ground pork, and transform it into something that's like a Cambodian chili meat dip that smells less like a chemical weapon and more like a bowl of meaty heaven.

    The above experience also applies to fish sauce, but most of you probably have already had fish sauce. Prahok, I feel, is still in "white people hipster territory."

    Anyway, take a moment to really appreciate that Khmer Kitchen has helped to remove my 28 year old, deeply ingrained aversion tied to multiple bad experiences. I will still never eat raw prahok like my parents do. But, I might ask my mom for a jar that I can use the next time I want to make food taste like home.

    Anyway, you should try Khmer Kitchen's prahok appetizer, and wonder what the hell I was talking about up there.

    Other notable dishes:

    Loc Lac - cubed, marinated steak with lime, salt, and pepper dipping sauce over a bed of vegetables. A classic in both Cambodia and Vietnam, you can't really go wrong with this one, and it's great for less adventurous eaters.

    Srelau kari (they may spell it differently) - Khmer red curry. They make theirs pretty simple with a curry base, dark meat chicken, and potato chunks, served with rice. I prefer mine with green beans, pork tenderloin, sweet potatoes, eggplant, and a french baguette instead, so this dish wasn't my favorite when I had it there.

    Kreung samot - this is an assortment of stir fried sea creatures spiced with kreung, which next to prahok could be considered one of the national flavors. Think lemongrass, kaffir lime leaf (if you've ever had it), chilis, garlic, and some other stuff I can't recall, all ground into a paste. Delicious, authentic, and surprisingly light.

    Pleah sach ko - Cambodian beef carpaccio. Tender beef that's "cooked" in citrus juices. If you've ever had the Vietnamese analogue, this one has a lot more herbs to go with it, not all of which I can name in English. It's also traditionally made as a spicy dish, though the meat itself is room temperature, and also incorporates mint leaves. It's a mix of a lot of different flavors that's both hearty and refreshing. One of my favorites while I was in Cambodia, and Khmer Kitchen does it justice.

    So those are my favorites. They also have standards like "sach ko ang," which is grilled beef kabobs, though they were out of them when I went. I don't know how many varieties of kabobs they have, but one of them is probably spiced in kreung. Try them all.

    The super awesome cherry on top is that this place is a BYO. Now I can bring shame to my family in front of other Cambodians. Woo!

A lot of our food starts with a krueng paste that is made by pounding a mix of fresh herbs and spices in a mortar. Let me tell you..if you ever want a good workout for your arm, I suggest you invest in a mortar and pestle and pound you some krueng paste! If you were to ask me what Khmer cuisine is similar to, I wouldn't be able to answer. History

Established in 2012.

We wanted and notice that there were no actual true Cambodian restaurant. A Cambodian restaurant that has no Chinese or Vietnamese influence. Not only that, when we think about Asian foods, automatic ally Pho, or Lo-mien come to mind, with shrimp fried rice. So the family agreed lets do something to get true Cambodian cuisine out there and show that our Cambodian culture is very unique, and intriguing. What better way to show case that but through a universal language of food. A language that that can be spoken in with out words but understood by every one

Meet the Business Owner

Sopheak N. Business Owner

First generation American Cambodian. Loves food, loves to cook, and is blessed with wife, a sister, and a mother that can cook unbelievably great. Main intention is to revive a slowly forgotten tradition, culture, and cuisine of Cambodia. Re-introducing the Khmer heritage to the younger (Americanized) Cambodian generation.
Ultimate goal is to distinguish Cambodian Cuisine from the style of plating, to the time of preparation, ingredient, and layering of flavor from your main stream Asian food.
