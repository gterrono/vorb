    This was probably the second best if not best meal that I have had yet in Philly.  Nick's French training and skill are on display with a seafood heavy tasting menu that continues to deliver again and again.  

    Booked our reservations the night he won Top Chef after much deliberation and were able to get in on the last seating at 10pm on a Friday night.  

    It's BYO - to make sure to bring something great, and if there's any left over, leave it with the front of the house staff who does a killer job.  

    Tasting menu style, my favorite for a phenomenal chef to just showcase their abilities and let you sit back and enjoy the ride.

    If you take a look at their site, you'll see a sample tasting menu.  What we had was pretty close, started with an uni amuse on a chicken skin cracker, followed by the tuna with horseradish, pear and yogurt that was crazy good.

    Follow that up with the torn sea scallop with a really interesting apple-flavored sauce.  And, oh yes, foie gras terrine? Holy crap this was a huge piece of foie gras, very heavy and served with toast, but so good.  We definitely couldn't eat it all!

    Probably my favorite thing all night - the charred Spanish Mackerel - with the mustard seed and mustard oil.  Wow this will clean out your nostrils but man does this thing pack flavor!

    The ricotta gnocchi was also pretty tasty, but it came out at the same time as the mackerel and was a bit overshadowed!

    Hellooooo beautiful butterfish, or walu.  This thing was cooked to perfection and incredibly flavorful, but then was followed by the wagyu beef and quickly forgotten!!

    The wagyu beef was served with some fresh beets and beet sauce, which I have to say, as a man who doesn't like beets (and Nick doesn't either!), these were incredibly tasty!

    Top it all off with the desserts? Yup, the panna cotta with Buddha's hand.  Look it up, you've probably never heard of it.  Dehyrdated meringue? Oh yes! Oh, and the white chocolate pudding? To die for.  If this were always dessert, trust me, I would always clear every plate before it to get to this one!

    Front of house service was awesome. But the final piece that made it awesome? Nick comes to talk to every table to see how their meal was and get people's opinions.  It was late night, and he's a regular guy, so he sat down at the empty table next to us to enjoy a beer and chit chat before realizing "oh crap it's late I need to clean up."

    Yup, we'll be back. Probably like 12 more times, this meal was awesome.

    Before dessert, the Chef came out to see how we enjoyed the meal. I'll just say that Chef Elmi is just as humble as he seems on TV. For more insight, feel free to check out the blog!

    Chef's Daily Selection of Cheese [$9]
    Tarentaise, Madurat, Forest Honey

    We couldn't help but get the cheese plate. A selection of two cheese were offered including Tarentaise, which is a farmstead cheese, meaning that it's produced from the milk from herds on the farm where the cheese is produced, primarily by hand. Though a semi-soft, the nutty flavors were reminiscent of gruyere and complemented nicely by the sweet forest honey. The GF and I are suckers for goat cheese and the Madurat had all the flavors in spades. Even the "ash" rind was enjoyable and the sweet, creaminess of this mild goat cheese had subtle hints of the innate acidity of the goat's milk. It was perfect to spread on the crusty, buttery cinnamon raisin bread.

    Citrus "Curd" [$8]
    Chicory, meringue, Buddha's hand

    We also ordered the citrus curd, which was definitely more my style than the GF's. I prefer desserts that aren't overly saccharine and while the buddha's hand added citrus notes in both flavor and scent, the curd wasn't overly sweet. The meringue offered a lightness which was a great bookend to the meal.

    Chef Elmi's Laurel recently received three bells from Craig Laban, but I wouldn't be surprised if a 4th was in order. The entire meal was well-paced and our server Grace was extremely knowledgeable and attentive. Dishes were well-executed, well-seasoned, and most importantly, delicious. If the restaurant wasn't Top-Chef certified, I would still return for a great meal. The fact that it's a BYO is simply a bonus. Pro tip: if you're having trouble getting a reservation, there is a wait list and the occasional cancellation. So don't hesitate to call. Also, do order dessert. There's a high probability that if the Chef is in the kitchen, he'll come out with the dessert to see how the meal was. Because really, it's not the celebrity, but how his food is received. As it should - that's what appears to matter.

    If you're a fan of Bravo's Top Chef, you know about Nicholas Elmi. If you're into food and live in… If you're a fan of Bravo's Top Chef, you know about Nicholas Elmi. If you're into food and live in Philly, you've heard of him. And if you have any modicum of Philly pride, the name should ring a bell. And as a NYC transplant, I couldn't help but root for Chef Elmi - humble, intense, and clearly work and family-driven - what's not to like?

    Following in the steps of Chef Kevin Sbraga's season 7 Top Chef win, Chef Elmi brought the title back to Philly, along with a cool $125,000. With that prize money, he opened up Laurel, a phenomenal entry in the South Philly BYO scene. And despite all the well-edited "drama" that was highlighted throughout Top Chef, after a recent meal, it's clear that Chef Elmi's food speaks for itself.

    Tuesdays through Thursdays are a la carte. And on the weekends, you can indulge in a set tasting for $85 pp. Due to the dearth of available reservations, I took what I could get. So on a Thursday evening at 8:45PM, the GF and I stepped into the Fond's former space and indulged. Being that Laurel is a BYO, the GF and I brought a bottle of Doc Lettere, a red sparkler that we were introduced to Vetri. Easy drinking, to be sure.

    Bread Service

    The meal began with two slices of house-made sourdough and a pat of browned butter topped with salt. A nice start for what was to come.

    Foie Gras and Cocoa Terrine [$20]
    Blood orange, candied celery root, brioche

    Our first courses came out shortly thereafter. I had to go with the foie gras, which came with a quartet of brioche toast points. The heft of the creamy foie was tempered by the bitter cocoa and the sweet candied celery root and tangy drops of blood orange. The salt heightened the innate flavors of the foie. This was a deft preparation that provided a bright note for the rest of the meal.

    Torn New Jersey Scallop [$14]
    Hot house rhubarb, sea lettuce, grapefruit

    The GF's first course proper brought forth refreshing notes. The scallop is truly hand torn and then cured with kombu to draw out the water and heighten the salinity. The bits of grapefruit imparted additional tartness provided by the hot house rhubarb, poured tableside.

    Fresh Ricotta Gnocchi [$14]
    Pancetta, sourdough, garlic

    The lightness of the scallop dish was the perfect entry into a heftier second course. The oft-acclaimed gnocchi provided all the flavors that are associated with comfort and guilty pleasures. The pancetta was salty, fatty, and crispy, provided a textural contrast to the creamy, pillowy ricotta gnocchi. The tangy crunch of the sourdough bread crumbs offered an additional layer of flavor. It's unsurprising that this dish was praised by the Top Chef judges and Padma, in particular, was moved when Chef Elmi spoke about his daughter's love for this particular dish. A must-order!

    Roasted Walu [$29]
    Sunchoke, hazelnut, apple blossom

    For my second course, I decided to opt for the roasted walu, which is actually listed under the mains. In fact, our server Grace noted that this was the first time she had ever seen anyone do this, but that it was genius. Personally, I didn't think any further than the fact that I love butterfish (aka walu aka escolar aka "white tuna) and knew I had to try the pork belly dish for my main. They don't call me the Belly of the Pig for nothing! But back to the roasted walu.

    The fish was firm on the outside and moist within. The apple blossoms added some tang, while the hazelnuts offered some heft and texture. The sunchokes provided sweet nuttiness. The walu was cooked perfectly, a touch more would have been over. Having enjoyed walu much rarer in the form of escolar sushi and knowing that despite the delicious butteriness, there are indigestible esters involved (I'll let you figure out what that consequence is), it's not surprising that the walu is cooked fully. A definite favorite and highly recommended.

    Niman Ranch Beef [$31]
    Salsify, onion, Woodland Jewel mushrooms

    For her main, the GF went with the Niman Ranch beef. Cooked perfectly tender, there was certainly no lack of seasoning here. The heft of the beef and the earthiness of the mushrooms were heightened by the accompanying bordelaise. The salsify puree was a necessary complement to the dish, providing a counterbalance to any saltiness.

    Berkshire Pork [$28]
    Crushed chestnuts, huckleberry-kale vinaigrette, pumpernickel

    I couldn't help but get excited for this next dish. Essentially pork three ways, fall-apart tender pork belly, perfectly cooked pork loin, and a technically exquisite crepinette of shoulder and cheek were highlighted. The crepinette was perfect - a succulent melange of pork shoulder and tender beef cheek were wrapped in caul fat and crisped on the outside to provide great flavor. This all came atop a crushed chestnut puree, which had an earthy nuttiness that underscored the heft of the pork. The tangy huckleberry and kale vinaigrette helped to temper the dish.

    I should start by saying I'm not a Top Chef devotee so perhaps that took away from part of my experience.  We are spoiled in Philadelphia with the number of fantastic dining options, especially affordable BYOB's, so I was excited when the buzz about Laurel started because I am ready to add a new one to my rotation. Unfortunately,  I don't think this restaurant has quite hit its stride.

    In addition to having to constantly request to have our water glasses filled,  the servers tending to us were fairly uninformed about the dishes.  For that, I blame the kitchen too. One of our diners has celiac (not an "I'm pretending to avoid a food group to get skinny" person) and asked twice about dishes and each time was misinformed of the ingredients and actually had to educate the staff. When the end of the meal rolled around, we were already less than enthralled about our meals so we skipped dessert and opted for espressos. Our waiter gave us a snarky response when one diner asked for a lemon rind by saying "that's what Italians do," and then proceeded to give us a history on sanitizing glasses. The list goes on and on about our server,  but I'll spare the details because it's not fair to let one inexperienced person kill the night.

    We ordered one of everything on the menu. Highlights:

    1st: Tuna (safe choice) or Scallop (adventurous)
    2nd: Gnocchi
    3rd: Duck

    The 1st courses were just about the only enjoyable part of the experience. The bibb lettuce was the least favorite,  but not because it wasn't tasty,  because it was the least interesting of the four.  The Foie was decadent and balanced, literally melting in your mouth.  The tuna was hands down our favorite and we all agreed we could have ordered 3 and made an entree of it.  The scallops were the most creative and memorable, I'd say this could be considered their signature dish.

    For the 2nd course the gnocchi was the favorite.  Soft and balanced with a slight texture from the pancetta. The rest of the options were sub par and the Arctic char was barely edible. To call it salty would actually be doing it a favor. Though the kitchen had offered to alter it to be gluten free, so the sauce was overwhelming,  but the fish itself was like salty jerky.

    The 3rd course we ordered one of everything by default. 2 diners ordered the Walu, but the preparations were inconsistent.  One was really "fishy" tasting  and the other was mild and meaty. We exchanged the fishy one for duck.  The seabass was recommended,  but lackluster.  If skin is served on the fish,  it should be crispy. That was not the case here. The pork was substantial and "safe",  but the duck ended up being the winner for the table. Cooked perfectly medium rare.

    By the time the chef made his rounds,  we were the last table left and probably the only table in a while to send something back so he was less than ebullient when he stopped to say hello.  But in a day and age when young chefs rarely leave the kitchen,  that was a pleasant surprise.

    Overall, this is a restaurant that isn't quite ready to remove its training wheels.  It has the right parts but needs a front of house manager to whip it into shape in order to justify the price tag.  Assuming their goal is to be a top dining destination and not just another reality TV tour stop.

Specialties

Laurel Restaurant is an intimate French/American influenced BYO by Chef/Owner Nicholas Elmi. Meet the Business Owner

Nicholas E. Business Owner

A 2013 Philadelphia Rising Star Chef and current Bravo Top Chef Contestant, Chef Nicholas Elmi has worked at some of the top rated French restaurants on the east coast, including Guy Savoy (Paris), Le Bec Fin, Union Pacific, Oceana and Lutece.
