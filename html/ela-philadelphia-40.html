    Ela has a lot going for it: a beautiful, sexy space with a nice, long bar; attractive and engaging servers who know the menu; a young, enterprising chef with fresh ideas. Ela has great potential, but on my first visit I wasn't really wowed.

    Our starters of kale salad, scallop noodles and gnocchi were all nice but not anything I'll crave again, with the exception of the kale salad, which had a very tasty, mustard-y vinaigrette. The scallop dish seemed a bit gimicky - I far prefer the nice sear and texture of a regular scallop to this manipulated version. The gnocchi was a teeny tiny portion - maybe I didn't get the full array of flavors after sharing the dish with two friends.

    The cocktail list, meanwhile, had some gems. I started with Last Chance to Lose Your Keys, a tequila mixture served straight up. It was a bit on the sweet side but still enjoyable for someone like myself, who prefers a basic drink over something special. The beer list was decent and had something for everyone.

    While all of the entree options were unique, I had a hard time narrowing in on one that appealed to me. Eventually, I ordered the veal skirt steak, which was fine but again, not overly memorable. I was also able to try a piece of the skate wing, and I was not a fan of the preparation. It was very similar to a version I had at Blackfish last summer, with the outer parts folded under, resulting in a taste and texture that seemed undercooked.

    Desserts were probably the best part of the meal, although the hot chocolate chip cookie dough did nothing for me. It was a bowl of warm, caramelized bananas with kind of a dulce de leche-type sauce. I was quite content to pass that up in favor of the red wine poached stawberries with white chocolate whipped cream and shortbread crumbles. That, friends, was the star of the night for me. The manjari chocolate was pretty awesome as well - definitely worth ordering if you're really in the mood for chocolate.

    I'll be honest. Parting with more than eighty dollars for my share of the bill (three shared apps, three entrees, three desserts and two drinks per girl) wasn't easy. But the bright side of all of this is that restaurants like Ela have the freedom to change dishes with the seasons and available ingredients. Maybe there will be a future menu that appeals to me more. In the meantime, I'll happily settle for the strawberry dessert at the bar.

As a new restaurant, they're still clearly working out some kinks, but based on the high quality of the food, I'm looking forward to seeing this place come into its own.

    Overall, this is a beautiful space with some very interesting food.  On the whole, our meal was well-executed and delicious.  The service was attentive, and the timing of the meal was absolutely perfect, three courses all exactly when we wanted them.

    Highlights:

    Truffle Banh Cam with white beans, prunes, jalapeño romesco.  Absolutely delicious.  Two big deep fried balls of sweet truffle-y... something!  The smell when they're cut open is heaven.  And those sides... Ela really tends to nail it on the side items.  The dish doesn't come all the way together until you get some prune and some of that jalapeno sauce on the bahn cam.  To quote my girlfriend: "Weird!  And delicious!"

    I loved the duck magret.  Thick, perfectly-cooked slabs of duck breast, with a delicious carrot and "burnt onion" pancake... but it was the tart rhubarb dip that tied it all together.  I would definitely order this again.

    We had foie gras two ways: on the menu we had the whipped foie gras with gooseberry and lovage on celery raisin bread, and it was quite good.  A generous mound of foie gras on both pieces of bread, with delicious accompaniments.  Not my favorite foie gras in town, but more interesting than most, and I award points for originality.  The other foie dish was the Foie Friday special, with pressed watermelon and almonds, and this was less successful for me.

    The famous Diver Scallop Noodles did not disappoint me.  Thicker-than-udon noodles made of scallop and an I-could-drink-the-leftovers broth of "Thai-style" peanut sauce.  I dipped every bite into that broth and wish I could have poured the rest into a little jar for later.

    Finally... the bread!  It was amazing!  Rye focaccia with oregano and a smoked oil to drizzle on top... incredible.  I told the guy who took the empty plate not to bring any more because I wouldn't be able to stop myself and had to save some space for dinner!

    Ela is innovative.  Fascinating combinations of flavors.  They might not always work, but that kind of experimentation is something I look for as a diner.  That's also true of Modo Mio, and that's one of my favorite places in town.

    So before I review the food itself, for those of you reading this review that think I just don't "get" molecular gastronomy or deconstructivism I've been to Alinea...TWICE.  I love haute cuisine and have the experience to prove it.

    WHAT WAS NOT GOOD:

History

Established in 2011.

In November of 2011, chefs Jason Cichonski and Chip Roman partnered to open Ela, an inventive new American bar and restaurant in Philadelphia's Queen Village neighborhood. Ela  showcases 27-year-old Cichonski, who Philadelphia Inquirer's Craig LaBan called "one of our most exciting young chefs," is combining an approachable menu of innovative dishes with an extensive wine and craft beer program and inspired cocktail list in a rustic-modern setting.
"Creatively, it's wonderfully freeing to prepare food that doesn't fit into any one genre or style; this is seasonal food, using exceptional ingredients, prepared perfectly," says Chef Cichonski. Ela is a serious-yet-comfortable restaurant with a menu that that changes frequently throughout the year, to reflect the latest offerings.

Meet the Business Owner

Jason C. Business Owner

Chef Jason Cichonski is one of Philadelphia's brightest rising star chefs. The Philadelphia Inquirer's Craig LaBan wrote in his glowing "Three Bells: Excellent" review that "few kitchens in town can match Ela's food value-wise for pure excitement...from the first course to the last, the chef at Ela is hottest where it matters most," while Philadelphia Magazine's Trey Popp called the restaurant "deliciously off-the-wall...completely worth it...a transporting peek inside this rising young chef's head."Jason Cichonski served as executive chef of Lacroix at the Rittenhouse Hotel when he was just 24 years old.  Lacroix was one of the city's only "Four Bells: Superior" fine dining restaurants at the time.  Before his success at Lacroix, Chef Cichonski worked in other highly praised kitchens, including the Pluckemin Inn, a fine dining Italian restaurant in Bedminster, NJ and Moonlight, a progressive American restaurant in New Hope, PA.
