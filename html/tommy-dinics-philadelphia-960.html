    Listed in Yelp 100 Challenge - 2014, Lived up to the hype!, Ph-ph-illy

    Tommy DiNic's was on my to do list. As soon as I stepped into the reading terminal market, I was on the lookout. I guess all I really had to do was spot the longest line. I fell in line, which wrapped around the counter and was maybe 20 people deep. Considering it was lunch time, this wait was expected and the line moved at a decent rate.

    Their menu is really simple: Roast pork, roast beef, Italian sausage, pulled pork, or brisket. But they're known for their roast pork, so it wasn't much of a decision to make here. I opted for the sharp provolone and broccoli rabe as my toppings. Again, as suggested by the other Yelp reviews. The components of the sandwich came together to make a really good flavor combination. The bread was a good balance of firm yet still soft to the bite. The bread has to be able to soak up some of the au jus without becoming soggy/crumbly. The bread held up well. The roast pork was thinly sliced and I did not find it dry. The broccoli rabe is what sold me. I'm not too familiar with rabe, so I wasn't sure what to expect for the taste. Apparently, it is bitter. For me, the bitterness of the rabe was a great complement to the subtle flavor of the roast pork.

    I paid $11.75 for the sandwich and a drink, which is a little pricey for my regular lunch break, but I suppose more acceptable for vacay lunch.

    I was supposed to save the other half for my bf who was meeting me. I accidentally ate half of his half.

    It may be a mistake that I didn't eat a cheesesteak during my visit to Philly, but I think I can say Tommy DiNic's just about makes up for it.  An American sandwich shop located in the Reading Terminal Market, Tommy DiNic's claim to fame is their roast pork sandwich, traditionally topped with broccoli rabe and sharp provolone.  I say American, because this place is listed as "traditional American," but they're really an Italian sandwich shop well into their fourth generation serving up meatball, Italian sausage, and roasted meat sandwiches to the lucky denizens of Philadelphia.

    Unfortunately for me, I was traveling alone and already had a meal at the Dutch Eating House, so I wasn't too hungry when I sat down to order that classic sandwich of theirs.  Nevertheless, the roast pork sandwich with the ultra-garlicky broccoli rabe and mound of provolone was the most glorious sandwich I've ever had.  The meat was juicy and flavorful, but not too salty, and that broccoli rabe was perfection!  That is, if you're a fan of your breath smelling garlic fresh.  Fortunately for me - I was traveling alone.  Hah.  I'm someone who doesn't usually go for sandwiches for a meal, so it's quite a statement when I say this was possibly one of the best decisions that I made on my trip to Philly. Yes, I'm talking big.  So next time you're in the city, have a taste yourself!

    As an unabashed Foodie, I always want to go to the places I see on TeeVee and read about in the rags. It seems EVERYONE, to a man, has earmarked DiNic's as a "don't-miss" place to have a Sandwich. And in further research, I've learned that Cheesesteaks are tourist food, and I cognoscenti Mange something else.
    I've been a Chicago guy my whole life, and grew up on "Italian Beef" sarnies with sweet peppers and hot giardinera. When I heard about the Philly "Italian Pork with Broccoli Rabe and Provolone", which apparently reaches its apotheosis here at DiNics.
    So when a trip to Philly went on the books, I subjugated the itinerary and made it my business to get into town early. Downtown was between my suburban port of call and the Airport.
    The Reading Terminal Market was easy enough to find, as was Parking. I just about fainted upon entering the place. Foodie heaven!! I LOVE Chicago, but we ain't got NUTTIN' that's like this, and it makes me heartsick. You would think a transit hub like Chicago, being in the middle of the nation would be a perfect place for a big market like this, but NOOO! Folks of Philly: You've got it good!
    I purposely didn't previously ascertain exactly where DiNic's was on the floor, so I would have the fun of snooping around. It was hard not to stop at any of the stalls I encountered, as they were all appetizing as hell; but I had to save myself for DiNics. I finally saw the signs, AND the line encircling the small stall. There was a counter, and it was about noontime. PRIMETIME! As I got into line, as if pre-determined, two guys got up from the counter RIGHT in front of me. I asked the Gal behind the counter if the line was "seated" or "First come, first serve"-she replied the latter, and I plopped my fat ass down PRONTO! I had scored a Noontime seat at DiNics!!
    Of course I ordered the house specialty, which arrived in short order. It was worth the wait. The Pork was sweet and juicy, the Broccoli Rabe just bitter enough and Garlicky, and the spikes of Provolone were sharp. All together, one of the best Sarnies ever. The hype wasn't hype. It IS that good.
    Why the hell don't they make this sandwich in Chicago? It would be a perfect compliment to the Italian Beef. It's such a no-Brainer, I can't believe Portillos, Buona's or Mr. Beef haven't jumped all over this. Especially now the foodies know about this sandwich...

    Of note: You are NOT, I repeat, NOT going to find your traditional Philly Cheesesteak here. But who gives a damn? What you'll eat will still reign supreme.

    You'll deal with a long line wrapping around the middle of Reading Terminal Market, but one, it moves fast, two, they take and dole out orders like clockwork, and three, if you're lucky, you can grab a few seats at the counter and watch the insanity for yourself.

    The beau and I each got the same: A roast beef sandwich with sharp provolone and sweet peppers. Topped off with an icy cold root beer, this is a behemoth of a lunch, and no matter how full you are, you will happily finish every single morsel. There is not a single bad thing about that particular sandwich. The bread is crusty yet doughy. The meat is a perfect thickness that melts in your mouth. The cheese will give you a swift kick to the taste buds. The peppers will round the rest out nicely, with the char from them giving it an even more robust flavor.

    As I sit back in NYC now, I'm thinking about it, craving it, perhaps even pondering planning my next trip to Philly around it. (Too much? I beg to differ.)

First they take a crisp roll cut partially through, and they expertly slice sharp provolone cheese between the roll and the meat.  The thinly sliced meat comes next, one employee hand slices the meat old school with a knife and folk.  A very nice portion of wet meat is then applied to the creation.  Finally, they add the broccoli rabe and sweet peppers, cut the creation in half and serve it in a red basket lined with serving paper.
