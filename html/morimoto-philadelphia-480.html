    I started with the Morimoto Sashimi.  The request to replace any shellfish with an alternative to accommodate an allergy was declined at it was against the chef's vision (forget about being against anaphylaxis, but hey), but the crab could at least be placed on another plate.  For the price, you don't get a lot of fish (2 pieces of 4 fish and 1 crab), but they are incredibly fresh, which makes it worth the splurge.

    I picked Duck Duck Duck for my entree.  The duck breast is amazing (the skin was fantastically crispy and tasty); the confit fried rice was delicious (I usually hate fried rice, but this is a different dish all together); and the runny duck egg was rich and soaked nicely into the rice.

    We finished with the Yuzu Meringue Tart, which was like a lemon meringue.  The fresh mango provided a sweet balance, and overall the dessert was light enough that you should opt for it, even if you're stuffed full of other good food.

    Listed in My Happy Tummy

    After living across the river for 3 yrs, I never made it here. Shame on me! Luckily I was able to rectify that mistake when in Philly on business.

    When at Morimoto's get the Omakase, be brave! Your taste buds will not be sorry!

    First course: Toro tartare with fresh wasabi, osetra caviar and paired with Veuve Cliquot champagne. The wasabi wasn't like that paste you sometimes get. I could have eaten this straight! (okay wait a minute, i did). The toro was succulent and exactly what you expected it to be.

    Second course: Kumamoto oysters served three ways; one with Japanese orange salsa, one as a citrus cilantro ceviche and the last with a Thai fish sauce with a bit of jalapeno. My favorite - the Japanese orange salsa. The sauce didn't mask the taste of the oyster and had a nice little punch to it. This was served with a nice white from Spain called Ostatu, Riura, Rioja Cosecha.

    Third course: Seared whitefish carpaccio: This consisted of hotate, scallop, and uni. It was seasoned with yuzu citrus-soy, garlic, ginger, mitsuba and chives. It was paired with the Chateau de Sancerre Savignon Blanc. This was absolutely my favorite dish of the night. The searing came from heated olive and sesame oil that was on the plate. Delicious!

    Fourth course: Chef selection of sashimi: My favorite - the salmon. The best I have ever had, though the snapper came a close second. This was paired with Wakatake Onikoroshi "demon slayer sake. Yummy!

    Fifth course: Lobster epice with eight spice lobster and citrus creme fraiche and chives. This was a half a lobster covered in this wonderfully flavorful spice. Finger licking good I might add. The creme fraiche was the perfect "cooling" bite, though the spice was really not that spicy. Paired with a Joseph Drouhin, Pouilly Fuisse, Maconnais.

    Sixth course: Seared foie gras, kimchi-miso vegetables, scallion pancake with a ginger, carrot and scallion "salad". The foie gras was very nicely seared and tasted just as it should. (my co-worker was in heaven!). This was paired with a nice slightly dry riesling - Ratzenberger, Kabinett Riesling, Steeger St Jost

    Seventh course: Seared Australian tajima wagyu sirloin with morel and ramp chowan mushi. The wagyu was oh so good. The morels gave it a wonderful "earthy" taste. Perhaps my second favorite dish. This was served with a nice Bergstrom Cumberland Reserve Pinot Noir.

    Eighth course: Chefs selection of sushi. No favorites among the sushi - tit was all delicious. This was paired with Narutotai Gingo Namagenshu Tokushima sake.

    Dessert: Chocolate sour cherry bavarian creme fraiche mousse with morello cherry swirl and morello miroir. The best part: Taylor Fladgate 20 yr tawny port.

    Last recommendation: Uni ice cream if they happen to be serving it. You will be pleasantly surprised.

    Came here twice and both times, we all had a great time.

    The decor is amazing...the seating area changes different colors from pinkish-red to green to blue (along with the sign for his restaurant). The silverware has a very delicate feel and you can feel that the atmosphere is very relaxing and modern. The quality of the food is amazing.

    I would def. recommend the ishi yaki buri bop, which is your Japanese version of a Korean kimbap (like a salad). You can either get the king yellow tail or the kobe beef. I opted for the yellow tail. This entree is prepared at the table and served in a hot river bowl. If you're not careful, you will burn yourself so be careful. They mix the ingredients right in front of you and you can really smell the sauces and ingredients. Yum. They sear the fish/beef on the hot river bowl, so it's quite an experience. In it, you will get daikon and carrots, an egg, and it's decorated with hijiki. As you're eating this dish, the bowl is still heating up the food. I got quite warm and had to take off my blazer jacket.

    The BF got his angry chicken and he enjoyed his chicken (in case you don't eat seafood like himself--haha and we're at a Japanese restaurant!). Last time he got the duck duck duck and he enjoyed that too.

    Last time, I got the black cod miso but I didn't like it that much.

    We had other people who ordered the wild striped bass and the seafood toban yaki. They seemed to enjoy their food. I was actually split on getting the bass or the ishi yaki, but decided for the ishi yaki since the waitress recommended the ishi yaki--which I do agree was a good choice. Nevertheless, the bass was also very good.

    The BF really enjoys the shiitake mushroom roll, which I agree is quite amazing. Also, if you do get a chance, try the soft shell crab roll.

    Oh yes, how could I forget--we had the cha-soba noodles. It's chilled green tea noodles. In the sauce, it is with chopped scallion, fresh wasabi, sesame seed. Quite good on a hot day.

    The other appetizer I would recommend is the pork belly buns--which we got last time and enjoyed it.

    Oh yes, last time we had the complimentary palette-cleansing drink. This time we got a certain type of fish (I don't remember what exactly) with avocado and basil.

    Almost forgot-- if you inform them that you're celebrating someone's birthday, they'll give you a free dessert with a little candle in a jello thing. It's pretty cute. The first time the birthday girl got a piece of cake and this time the bday boy got a type of mint ice cream with a slice orange.

    Enjoy~

    I first encountered Chef Masaharu Morimoto back in the late 1990s while watching the TV show, 'Iron Chef'. Then one birthday my wife surprised me with a trip to Nobu in Tribeca where we met Morimoto-san in person. (He was head chef there at the time). His cuisine tasted every bit as good as what I imagined it would be after seeing it on TV.  In 2001, I attended Morimoto's opening in Philadelphia. Since that first week I have been back multiple times with different groups of friends & family, and the experience has always been exceptional.

    I'll get to the food in a minute, but first, a few words about the restaurant itself. For starters, it's a Stephen Starr creation. Mr. Starr has a flair for creating hip & trendy restaurants in Philly. I imagine Starr consulted Morimoto-san on the overarching Nature/Elements theme of the restaurant, but it was certainly Starr's idea to bring in industrial designer Karim Rashid to create the restaurant's unique look. The main dining room is segmented by clear plexi booths that continually change color.  The smooth-curving bamboo walls seem to undulate in the soft lighting. There's a sushi bar (hidden?) at the back of the main dining room, and a cramped lounge/bar area upstairs that overlooks the main room. Bathrooms are on the lower level, along with a private dining area. And yes, there are immovable phallic-shaped lights at the center of each table that you'll be compelled to comment on before realizing what a nuisance they can be when the meal arrives. (Invariably someone tries to move the candle out of the way to make room for an incoming dish, only to find that the thing is bolted down.) The restaurant is intended to feel alive and flowing, and you are meant to be a part of the spectacle. The upside? You've never seen a Japanese restaurant like this before. The downside? Privacy is at a bare minimum, and you're about to spend a boatload of money to sit in a place that might not be what you were expecting. Again, if you're hoping for some traditional Zen-like ambience, prepare to be utterly disappointed. I give Starr high marks for going in the direction that he did, and I'm guessing that he knew taking the restaurant's design to such a degree would leave him open to criticism.

    Thankfully, Starr stayed far away from the kitchen. Chef Morimoto's creations reign supreme, and his kitchen staff consistently reproduces his signature dishes. Hats off to an amazing crew! Here are two 'must have' dishes that I order every visit:

    1. Rock Shrimp Tempura: What can I say about this dish? A little bit sticky-sweet, a little bit spicy, and all tempura'ed up to a golden deliciousness that is unrivaled in the shrimp world. It comes with strips of endive that you can use to load up the RST and munch away. My condolences to anyone with shrimp allergies, but I'll eat your share of this app!

    2. Black Cod Miso: I'm from New England, so to impress me with a cod dish is nigh-impossible. And yet every time I get this entrée it blows me away. First off, the fish just melts in your mouth. You put a piece on your tongue and press it to the roof of your mouth and - where did it go? It's just gone, disintegrated, leaving behind the richness of the sweet miso-based sauce. I would love to know how many days they marinade the cod. Two? Three? Seriously, do not leave without trying this dish.

    Of course Morimoto does omakase. Spend $80, $100, $120 (or more) per person, and you'll get a multi-course meal that will knock your socks off...but be careful. Why? Well, if you go with a group of people, and they all order an app and an entrée, or just an entrée, and you get the tasting menu, guess what happens? They all sit there and watch you eat seven dishes over the course of two hours. Meanwhile, their one dish has come and gone in that time, and the drink tab gets out of control. A better bet is to have everyone in your party do the tasting menu. There is also a beverage omakase and a sake omakase, but those are in addition to the tasting menu, so don't bother with them unless you're getting the tasting menu itself.

    The sushi at Morimoto is, well, at this level of dining, anything less than perfection would be downright unacceptable. Having eaten my fair share of sushi over the past twenty years, I'll just say that good sushi is good sushi. You know it when you taste it, and as long as the restaurant's knowledge of sushi preparation and proper fish selection is above average, you're good to go. At Morimoto, that part of the formula is in place, so enjoy. Morimoto does a Chef's Combination for their sushi ($30, $50, $75 and up), so if you're going with a group, this might be your best bet.

    Morimoto is a fun time and exceptional food.  But don't take my word for it.  There are hundreds of reviews here on Yelp about this restaurant.  So read a few more, and then book your own reservation!  You will not be disappointed.
