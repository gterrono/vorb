    Only been once, came from out-of-town.  Have tried Pat's and Geno's, and ordered a steak with whiz and fried onions.

    It may have been just my particular steak, but the cheese amount was far less than the other standards I tried.  Like, not even close; enough that I originally thought they forgot the cheese when I opened the paper.  However, it completely worked.  The meat was fantastic, and there was a ton of it.  The bread was soft with the right amount of chew, and they put on the perfect amount of onions to get the flavor without the dragonbreath feeling.  Out of Tony's, Pat's, and Geno's, Tony's was my favorite so far.

    Notes on the place: the parking is easy.  They have their own lot with probably 12-20 spaces and there are places to park nearby if it's full.  Also, do not expect to be in and out of this place lightning fast like at Pat's and Geno's; the menu is too extensive.  I was just ordering the single cheesesteak at 8PM on a Saturday and it took a solid 45 minutes to get through the line and pick up my order.  But, of course, it's worth it.

    Luckily we got there before the after work rush. We were able to go right to the window and order and claim a bench to eat on. I ordered the "beef w/ greens", getting spinach, while my uncle ordered the chicken steak hoagie. We decided to share the signature "pork w/ greens"- choosing the broccoli rabe.     In general, I am not a big fan of the heaviness of philly cheese steaks, but when in philly, do as the philadelphians do, right? After speaking with a few locals, I was easily convinced that there is excellent philly cheesesteak at every corner in town. So, we decided to just choose one and felt assured any place is fail proof. So, we picked Tony Luke's mainly because of the high reviews and parking situation. There is a free private parking lot (though sketch and underneath a highway).

    I came on a Sunday evening, so there wasn't a line and it wasn't packed, though there were a good amount of people. It was a order at the counter type of place and sit as you please (this place really reminded me of Pink's Hot Dog in LA).

    Must Gets:

    Rib-eye Beef Steak Hoagie cheesesteak - American cheese, mayonnaise, fried onions, shredded lettuce, tomato, and pickets $9.25.

    Rib-eye Beef Steak Italian cheesesteak - Sharp provolone cheese, broccoli rabe, sauteed onions, bell peppers $9.75

    Sweet Potato Fries - caramel and powdered sugar (I decided to opt out on the caramel) $4.50

    My favorite was definitely the Steak Hoagie. The added vegetables and mayo added a lightness to the normally greasy meaty sandwhich. The meat was not that salty at all, which was a pleasant surprise. And the bread. My goodness, it was fresh, soft, and delicious philly bread. It tastes like fresh baguette, but it definitely is not crunchy, its some sort of italian bread and grilled pressed, but not exaclty sure what is the name and type of the bread. The portions (about 12 inch long and heavily loaded with steak) for the two sandwhiches is definitely more than enough for 3 people! So, don't be greedy and share with your friends. In addition to the cheesesteaks, we ordered sweet potato fries. I was skeptical about ordering fries at a place like this, but after we ordered our sandwhich, we heard this lady complement the cooks on how delicious the sweet potato fries were, so obviously, we we had to test it out - and we were glad we did! The sweet potato fries were cut criss-cut style (like at Carl's Jr Burger). The texture was soooo delicious and it was not oily at all with a perfect balance of sweetness. The sweet potato fries are a MUST order!!  

    Most people say this place is a tourist trap, but I don't mind, I thoroughly enjoyed their Rib-Eye Steak Hoagie and sweet potato fries... the freezing snowing weather may have played a factor into the heightened goodness of the cheesesteaks. Nonetheless, the service was fantastic here! Everyone was so friendly and courteous. A thank you can go a long way. Tony Lukes is definitely a must try when in Philly!

    The moment I bit into my first sandwich from Tony Luke's, I heard a distant strain of "Philadelphia Freedom" fill my ears, and I felt the foundations of my mid-Atlantic culinary knowledge begin to shake. A couple of bites later, they were lying crumbled at my feet, victims of popular knowledge and misinformation. Oh, how wrong I was! Wrong, wrong, wrong!

    For instance: the meat on a cheesesteak does NOT need to include bits of gristle and fat. It can be lean, meltingly tender and packed with flavor. It can also include roast pork in addition to beef: I don't really even like pork, but this was so delicious I think I'd have to do some soul-searching before placing my next steak order.

    Or: the idea that cheesesteaks must include plastic cheese to be authentic. In my former ignorance I held to this heresy, but at Tony Luke's I was peer-pressured into ordering sharp provolone instead, and the sandwich came full of melty, creamy goodness, but without any of the petroleum-byproduct tinniness from the can -- just biting, pungent cheese flavor in blistered bubbles of perfection.

    And that other pesky ideal, that cheesesteaks are best enjoyed plain? Only true if the add-ons are as mediocre as they usually are. Here, you can get the requisite fried onions, which lend a smoky flavor to the sandwich, but you can also order broccoli rabe, which has been stewed to remove any bitterness and adds a juicy, fresh depth. Oh, my, it was delicious.

    The one thing I remain convinced of is that the bread makes the sandwich. In this case, it's delivered fresh each day; my Philly source says the other Tony Luke's locations receive par-baked loaves and finish them onsite, but the result is less than stellar. This bread was a classic French baguette: chewy outside, fluffy inside, a meal in itself or a spectacular case for the tender meat and melting cheese of a perfect lunch.
