    Due to my proximity to Chhaya, I've been here daily since my review Tuesday past; it's quite literally straight down the street from me.
    I stand by my previous assessments of the masala chai and the decoction: for me, they're not as strong-tasting as I'm accustomed, but other people seem to rave, so what do I know?
    Nonetheless, now that I've tried just about every possible thing on the menu, I've got quite a lot to say, and ALL of it is positive.
    Firstly, the coffee. My gosh, I rather enjoy the press. The El Salvador coffee from the press pot is my go-to drink when I'm at Chhaya. I was warned that it's strong, but I didn't find this to be the case; at least as far as the taste is concerned. "It's bitter" I was told, but I couldn't disagree more; I found it pretty smooth, in fact.
    The strength came later. Chhaya should really market this as their signature "wake-up" beverage. The caffeine content is through the roof and likely enough to wake the dead themselves, the lazy bums.
    V, the proprietor, prepares every food item (save the bagels) that Chhaya serves to its guests. The muffins and waffles (ESPECIALLY the waffles) are superb. I've had the opportunity to sample a variety of the baked goods Chhaya offers and have not yet been let down. Of the muffins I've tried, fig, strawberry and cream cheese and blue-or-blackberry (I'm now not quite sure which of these two the muffin was), the strawberry and cream cheese was my muffin-of-choice. Sweet, somewhat tartish, moist and delicious, it was the most well-balanced of the three. The fig muffin was cinnamon-spiced somewhat and good as well as very sweet. The blue-or-blackberry was standard, moist and not skimpy on the berries so I wouldn't be surprised if it becomes their most popular.
    Now... the waffles.
    Dear me, the waffles are superb. Not burned (Bonté could learn something), firm yet moist and not too sweet, they seem to go well with everything, which is all for the better since Chhaya intends to serve them with, well, everything.
    I've had now maple and blueberry topped waffles, a South Asian rendition of chicken and waffles with the titular bird served in a curry sauce rather than deepfried and waffled eggs benedict. The waffle served as the perfect catch-all for each of these toppings. The curry-soaked waffle being my favourite of the three, the eggs benedict comes as a VERY close second. The hollandaise sauce is buttery and not too lemony and just perfect. I never thought the standard English muffin could be usurped, but waffle is now the lord-regent of the land of Benedict.
    I now await a marriage of the curry and benedict where curry replaces hollandaise but leaving eggs and ham as they are. I'd be convulsing in foodgasm.
    I've had, also, the espresso. At first turned off by the idea of an American roaster producing it, I was surprised with exceptional glee at how smooth it was. A cappuccino made from this coffee was sweet without the need for added sugar.
    The staff (just how many people work here? Six? Seven?) is courteous, helpful, friendly and quick and V is knowledgeable and proud of Chhaya's ever-changing menu (daily, in fact).
    Dinner has yet to be had, but I'm sure I'll be supping on feta-stuffed Hungarian peppers and other night-time offerings soon enough.

    Chhaya is a cafe that specialises in various roast-styles of various coffees.
    I went to Chhaya this…     Chhaya is a cafe that specialises in various roast-styles of various coffees.
    I went to Chhaya this afternoon with a group. The four of us got various things each. I ordered the masala chai (I fiend for good masala chai). I wasn't disappointed with it, too much... to be completely honest, the taste was great, but subtle. Too subtle. I don't much care for a masala chai that whispers sweet nothings in my ear, to be frank. I want one that'll punch me in the face and yell "CHHHHHHHHHHHHAAAAAAAAAAA­AAAAAIIIIIIIIIIIIIII," but even if it wanted to, this one in particular was too weak to do it.
    Don't let that deter you from trying it: it's still very good, but nonetheless, more subtle in taste than to which I am accustomed.
    Another order amongst our group was the so-called "South Indian Decoction." The name threw me off; I had no idea what this item might be, but when I saw the familiar metal container used in brewing it, I understood immediately what it was: coffee.
    For those familiar with Turkish/Arabic/Greek-­style coffee, this is very similar. It's brewed in a similar fashion, too. Ultrafine coffee grounds are put into cool water, heated until it foams, then removed from the heat. The process similarities, however, end there and the grounds used in this brewing method often have chicory added and are made not just from flat-shaoed coffee beans with which most of us are familiar, but also with the rounder "peaberry" coffee, which is reputed to be of better quality somehow, but I don't really know how or why.
    Anyway, after foaming, the coffee is then allowed to cool and the grounds allowed to settle. Once they've settled to the bottom, the decoction is carefully poured through muslin, cheesecloth or similar filter. From that point, the coffee decoction is poured into a cup or tumbler with some sugar (I've also had it with honey) and some milk is heated (alone, not with the decoction.) Often, the milk is watered down a little bit, but I'm not sure if Chhaya does that or not. When the milk is near boiling, it is poured into another tumbler of equal volume, but half the height and much wider than the one for the coffee liquid. It is then poured from the squat tumbler into the taller one, then back into the squat one, back into the taller one and so on and so on and so on, usually from increasing heights each time. This aerates and froths the coffee/milk mixture somewhat and thoroughly dissolves what sugar was there.
    I had a sip. Again, as with what I'd ordered, it was less strong than other Madurai-style coffees I've had, but nonetheless good.
    A third in the group ordered an iced masala chai. Good news: they don't charge you for ice cubes! A novel idea (I'm glaring at you, every other cafe place, ever)! It wasn't as good as the hot one I'd had, and we all agreed on that, but decent nonetheless.
    A brownie was also ordered. It was fairly standard and nothing to write home about, but the iced coffee and espresso were both above standard for the area.
    I asked one of the workers about the various methods of brewing and various types of coffees Chhaya carried. A problem was met: he didn't know the origins of the beans, and a place that's supposed to pride itself in it's coffees really should. He'd said that the roasts were generally indicative of the region, which may well be true, but he couldn't speak with certainty about where each may have been roasted.
    Chhaya boasts an impressive array of methods for brewing coffee. In addition to the one detailed above, there is also siphoned coffee (previously, only Ray's in Chinatown had this method in Philly, to my knowledge), Chemex and I suspect automatic drip, though I didn't see the machine and didn't scan the menu enough to see if they did, indeed, carry it, but it would be an anomaly if they didn't.  Chhaya also offers the full gamut of espresso-based drinks from cappuccino to latte and beyond.
    I've yet to try their food menu as they currently do not yet carry the food menu in its entirety, but from what I can see, this is a place to watch out for. Regardless of how harsh my review may seem, I'm going to be retrying and updating it as it comes.     Read more

    ***I am editing just to insert a note. I got a message from the owner of Chhaya, and he said I can order French press coffee if I am in a hurry. I will do that next time! ***

    When this place first opened over the summer, I thought I had found the best dang coffee place in the whole world: the iced coffee was fresh, flavorful, and blast-you-to-the-moon strong.

    Then it got a little chilly one day and my boyfriend and I went in for hot coffee. We literally sat there for 20 minutes (I timed it) waiting for them to finish concocting two cups of coffee for us.

    They brew using one of those chemistry-set-looking chemex pots, which as far as I can tell, will get you from coffee beans to a hot cup of coffee in about 100 steps.

    And to be honest, I wasn't that impressed with the result. I feel like if you wait 20 minutes for a coffee, it should make you see Jesus.

    I will still go there, because it's a block from my house and I like their food, but be forewarned: if you need coffee to go,and you are in a hurry, not such a great idea.

    Some positive things: The staff is friendly and the food is good.

    They have the best bacon I have ever tasted (apparently, it is local) and even the simplest things--two fried eggs, for example-- are prepared with care and are delicious.

    If you go for dinner, expect very small sharing plates. It's an ideal place to go when you are not ravenous but you would like to sit with some friends and share a bottle of wine and relax.

    Oh, and their warm figs and prosciutto are TO DIE FOR!

    Chhaya is the best brunch place in South Philly.

    You know the saying, "Dont judge a book by its cover," right? Well, that advice applies to Chhaya. From the outside, it looks like a little coffee spot, not unlike dozens of others in South Philly alone.

    But go inside, see the menu and wow, will you have a surprise on your hands. Yes, the French pressed coffee is fabulous (among the best in the city, and I've had them all) but what really pleases is the breakfast selection.

    A menu based mostly around waffles isnt exactly ground-breaking. Yes Chhaya's delicious fluffy waffles are the perfect base for a number of delicious, creative plates.

    Are you interested in sweet waffles? Well, you're in luck. Choose from a number of delicious options, including Pumpkin and fruit adorned waffles. And there is a yogurt parfait, which I have not had but looks delicious.

    If you are more inclined to choose a savory breakfast item, Chhaya's got ya covered as well. I had the sausage gravy on a cornbread waffle. My wife got the eggs benedict waffle, which looked amazing. I can tell you the Hollandaise sauce was magnificent. There is also a waffles which comes with vegetarian chili, cheese and a fried egg. Outrageous!

    As for the service and environment, there is simply a good feeling in the place. Maybe it is the synergy of a place on the verge of busting out, maybe it is people who really care. I dont know, I just love this spot.

    This is exactly the sort of place my wonderful home of East Passyunk needs more of.

    Tried Chhaya this past Saturday for brunch.  As described by their blackboard "Chhaya" means "shadow" in Sanskrit, "life" in Hebrew and "teahouse" in Japanese.  (How this is three languages I am not certain.)  A small space, it felt cozy but also a bit cramped with closely spaced tables.  There was some pleasant pseudo-electronica playing in the background (I asked the waiter.  Zola Jesus anyone?)

    On the blackboard there were lots of options for coffee and how you want it brewed.  Also some tea options and I ended up getting a pretty delicious chai latte ($3).  The brunch menu is pretty excellent.  They have options for quick meals (like yogurt, bagel, etc.) as well as sandwiches and their various waffle options as described by other Yelpers here.

    We opted to try the waffles and both went with a special huevos rancheros waffle.  The waffle initially looked small and came on a plate that barely fit the waffle.  It was a tasty cornmeal waffle (fabulous idea) and included black beans, chorizo, hollandaise, avocado with a fried egg on top.  Admittedly it was pretty delicious and more filling than I originally thought it would be.

    Did it taste good and was the meal well prepared?  Yes.  But, unfortunately, as far as my normal brunch parameters are concerned, I just don't think it was enough food to be worth the cost of $12.  After a night of even minimal drinking I would have liked a few side dishes for $12.  Also, the 2 servers were busting their humps, but were a bit "inefficient" as my ex-server girlfriend would put it.  Some elements of the meal including the check took awhile.  We weren't in a rush, but considering we were an arms length from the counter you'd think we might have gotten the check a little sooner.

    Overall, a good experience, just a tad pricey in my eyes for me to give it a raving review.
