    Barbuzzo opened its doors in the fall of 2010, the sixth food-related endeavor from chef Marcie Turney and partner Valerie Safran, all situated on a single block of 13th Street. Though it was still on the early side for a Friday evening, the place was already at capacity, outside seating included, and buzzing with conversation. A smoky aioli aroma greeted us at the door and attached itself to our clothes in friendly welcome. We hadn't made reservations and I was trying to convince myself that a dinner of frozen yogurt wouldn't be such a bad plan B when the host informed us there were two available seats at the kitchen bar.

    The atmosphere inside was polished rustica, the bar and open kitchen dominating the left side of the long, narrow dining area, with tolerably cramped seating on the right. The lighting was reminiscent of an old-time warehouse; single bulbs suspended from wires and overlaid with metal latticework bathed the bar in a warm yellow glow.

    Our view from the counter into the goings on of the kitchen afforded us a show with our meal. I found out later that Marcie Turney was the woman standing next to us the entire night, tracking orders as they made their way from kitchen to table.

    I had a roasted squash and parsnip salad to start, which was tossed with arugula, spiced pumpkin seeds, matchstick green apples, and shaved grana padano. The vin cotto dressing was a bit on the salty side but the veggies were seasoned and roasted to perfection. The apple's sweet tart nicely complemented the creamy, slightly grainy texture of the grana padano.

    Brenna ordered chestnut ravioli as her entrée. Each bite of stuffed goodness released a blend of sheep's milk ricotta and nutty chestnut flavor that practically melted in your mouth. It was served with roasted squash, pancetta, and grated parmesan, all simmered in a sage butter aioli sauce.

    I went with the grilled bronzini, a flaky white Mediterranean fish. The bronzini had a mild sweetness to it and the crux of the dish was pairing it with a lemon parsley-pinenut pesto, which gave it the kick it needed. The dish was accompanied with barley (a tad crunchy for my taste), grilled artichoke pieces, and wild mushrooms.

    For dessert we shared the heavenly salted-caramel bundino, a must have when you come here. Served in a glass container resembling a candle-holder, it consists of a dark chocolate cookie-crumb bottom, a generous layer of vanilla bean pudding drizzled with sea salted caramel, and a dollop of whipped crème fraîche to top things off. Dessert lovers, I'm happy to report Barbuzzo sells these lip licking creations in to-go packs of six.

    I have saved my virginal review for a restaurant worthy of such a cause. Barbuzzo is that restaurant. While I have not dined at the uppermost echelon of Philadelphia restaurants, (Vetri) I believe that Barbuzzo may be the finest our city has to offer.

    Ambiance - *****

    Very trendy, very comfortable, and intimate. Even on a hot Philly night, the room (doors wide open) was comfy and cool. The room is dark and candlelit, with a humming and catchy playlist. Great date spot.

    Service - *****

    We had a great server who was very knowledgeable and friendly. Not overbearing or in our face, but was there to pour our water and wine all night long. She offered helpful suggestions but did not force our hand to order anything. Dishes were run and bussed in a timely and very well paced manner (sign of a great support staff). We ordered over 8 plates, and everything came as we hoped and right as we wanted it. Our bottles of wine were poured before dishes were brought to the table, which is not necessary, but certainly a really nice fine dining touch.

    Wine/Beer/Cocktails - ***

    The cocktail list, while very interesting, was a little short in the tooth. The drinks however were boozy and clever, though the bartender took his/her sweet time making them. The wine list is understandably short given the expected confines of storage, and though it was well priced, it lacked any middle ground. A list of all low end or high end bottles forces the diner to either spend beyond their means or sacrifice. A few middle of the road vintages would go a long way.

    Food - ****

    The biggest player in the show, the food, rarely disappointed. While a few dishes did miss the mark, most were phenomenal. Marcie Turney changes the menu frequently, so not all these dishes will necessarily be available in the future, but if they are I will highlight the highs and lows.

    Highs: Sheeps Milk Ricotta and Toast, Smoked Shrimp and Polenta, Truffle, Asparagus and Poached Egg Pizza, Meatballs, Caramel Budino

    Lows: Chicken Liver Mousse and Foie Terrine, Octopus Salad, Branzino  

    Overall - ****

    A really fantastic experience in a great spot. There is no question why this place is always packed as the ladies of 13th St. are simply killing it. What I liked potentially the most was the feel that one could come to Barbuzzo for a drink and a pizza and not feel pressured to eat more. I went for the all out dining experience, but I will undoubtedly be back to enjoy a cocktail and snack on a plate or two. The value is tremendous.

    Note: Reservations are not required but I would certainly make one unless you are content to eat at the bar or chefs counter.

    Met my cousin at Barbuzzo for a midweek dinner; we both found ourselves in Philadelphia the week before July 4.  Barbuzzo sounded like a great place for a kick-back-and-catch-up dinner.  We wanted to avoid the Garces and Starr establishments that we had enjoyed but seemed formulaic by this time in our dining lives.  We were not disappointed and thankful for the efforts of Marcie Turney and Valerie Safran.

    (Arrived a bit early so actually decided to hit Jamonera across the street for some sangria and then claimed our table at Barbuzzo)

    They sure pack people into the small dining room here.  It was not unlike many places in SF that I am used to, but you definitely have to "make room" for the fact that you will be sharing your conversation with your neighbors (and maybe even your dinner if they are friendly!) .
    Having said that, the service was attentive and professional, which was really nice given that Barbuzzo could fit into the "hipster/trendy" classification, but I suppose it embodies the true hipster spirit by bucking the hipster convention of  "I-don't-care-I'm-laissez-faire."  Pushing my late thirties now, so I have every right to be cranky-old-mannish whenever I want.  My point, though, is that the service was great.  Anyway, back to the food:

    Started with the chicken liver and foie gras mousse (which was all mine as my cousin is vegetarian!) and the cheese board.  Ended up being a lot of baguette for our little two-top table, but we ended up enjoying all of it.  4 cheeses nicely-paired with the appropriate accompaniments, and the mousse was served in a mini terrine that was perfect for just me.  Would have hated to share it, especially as I live in the land of no more foie.

    We also split a roasted beet salad, which was served with a nice goat cheese and kale.  Didn't really taste the pistachio pesto, but I like the idea of it.  We then shared an asparagus pizza, minus the guanciale - sacrilege, I know, but such is life when dining with a vegetarian.  Funny thing is that my cousin remarked that the pizza needed "a little something else for zing," to which I blithely replied, "Yep, that's what the guanciale gives you."  The light definitely went on for her at that point.  She's not a convert yet, but definitely suggestible, I think.

    For dessert we shared the salted caramel budino, which was, well, awesome.  We also had the watermelon prosecco sorbet which tasted, well, exactly like watermelon and prosecco.  A very nice way to end our meal.  

    All told, including a couple of beers (decent draft selection and a really nice selection of bottled beers, by the way) and gratuity, we were in for around $110.  Well worth the money and I will definitely go back when I am in town.
