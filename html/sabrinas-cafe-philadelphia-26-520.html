    My favorite restaurant. Brunch all day long,  BYOB, evening dinner options.  A creative-genius-concoction of a menu featuring contemporary cuisine infused with serious flair.  

    Weekly special themes vary from Mean Girls to Halloween to the presidential elections.  Don't let the server take the menu away until you've read the entire gag.

    The French Toast (yes, it deserves to be capitalized) is serious food porn.  In fact, every Sabrina's dish is food porn.  Take pictures to keep you sated until you return.

    My go-to dish is the Ultimate Caesar Salad - no dressing, but rather a side of spicy marinara for the blackened chicken.  Go for the spring roll appetizer: a perfect medium of crispy-chewy salty-sour-sweet.  If you are craving comfort food, lay your fork into the meat loaf with garlic mash.

    Call ahead.

    Huxley and I are big fans of eating and drinking in places we've never been before, so on a whim, we drove up to Philly for the weekend. We got in late, had too much wine too fast, and passed out all drive-tired and squirrely on her cousin's floor. Before we lost consciousness, LC made us promise to do brunch the next morning at Sabrina's.
    Tucked away in the Italian Market area, Sabrina's Cafe is a cute little shanty, a home turned restaurant, buried within all the bustle and kitsch of that area's shopping effluvium. We got there around 9am on a Saturday, and the place was hopping live with the chatter of its posh patronage. We were led through a series of small dining rooms, between rows of plain-tables situated with eclectically mismatched seats, above rises in the floor, through low-clearance doorways, and over simple single stairs to nowhere before being seated at little two-person cornertop. I angled in my gangly legs and knocked them against Hux's thighs. She gave me a one-eyed squirm. Our waitress, a plain-faced oscine with tight golden hair chopped short around her skull, squinted and curled her lips at my unlikely ranginess. "You might be better off down there," she said with a gesture toward a more open table on the short landing beneath us. Huxley nodded in agreement.
    We shuffled down and I tossed my scarf and hat to the floor in the corner and slammed down my bottle of cheap Biltmore champagne, which in North Carolina is quite routine but felt somehow exotic having traveled to Philly in my trunk. Our waitress nodded accordingly. "Orange juice and a couple glasses?" she asked.
    "You know it."
    Our waitress wilted from view behind the open-wall counter of the small kitchen behind us. Huxley flipped her dirty blond wisps around, clutched the strands tight between her spindly fingers, twisted and curled and in one fluid gesture managed that whole big mop of gorgeousness up into a prim bun atop the bulb of her head. "I love BYOB," she said as she secured her bun with a pin. I jittered like a little kid. "I know, right?"
    Our oscine returned, carrying delicately between her tiny hands a fat marble cylinder, smoking lightly in the early morning light. She plunked it down in the center of the table and I could see residual frost lightly misting up upon its gray and spotted surface. "These things are really cold," she said, rubbing her fingertips together to return their warmth. Hux poked at it playfully and recoiled. "Yeesh." Our waitress poured our first mimosas and then slipped the bottle down into the marble chiller.
    We browsed the brunch menu, an unexpectedly deep assortment of options, both routine and wildly inventive. We locked in on the daily specials, a list of stringently named dishes that each seemed a little more elaborate than the last. My eyes affixed to two particular options, the Eggs Benedict Florentine (with avocado and tomato) or Jenni Rivera's Mexi-Special.  I couldn't decide. I looked up to Hux; she had one hand angling her menu, the other gently stroking the tiny fuzz along the nape of her long and narrow neck. I had to close my eyes for a second to focus. "What are you thinking?" I asked after a moment of self-conciliation.
    "I dunno," she said, that hand still wandering that neck... "I'm torn between the eggs benedict and this mexi special."
    I shook my head violently from side to side and widened my eyes. The waitress returned and asked us if we were ready. I said I'd have the Eggs Benedict and the lady would go with the Mexi Special. Hux grinned and shot me an understanding glance. "And then we go halfsies?" she asked. I nodded.  "Oh, and no ham on the mexi," I added. "We're pesky."
    The food came fast and hot and done up in an array of colors and textures not often seen. "This is beautiful," Hux said with a lilt of sincere enthusiasm. "Almost too pretty to eat."
    I looked to the waitress. "Sriracha?" I asked with some hesitation and trepidation. I wasn't sure if that was a common thing in Philly. Without hesitation she produced a bottle seemingly from nowhere. Maybe she had that thing in a holster on her belt or something. Hux and I were impressed.
    The Eggs Benedict was flawless. Perfectly cooked, just that right amount of yoke golden and flowing out into the muffin. Barely together fresh ripe avocado, a smattering of herb and cream, flush red tomatoes juicing and lining the plate, and a healthy portion of roasted potato wedges stationing the whole thing. Hux's Mexi-Special was just as ornate: scrambled eggs over spicy grits, buried beneath cheese and cream and beans and salsa.
    We ate with mess and fervor, her eyes gleaming and my heart racing, and all that food just going down our gullets. Hux just managed the one mimosa; I drank all the rest. Half-drunk and happy and spewing silly words of love and fondness from my lips, a sinewy brunette next to us saw our enjoyment asked which of the two dishes we recommended.
    "Get both," Hux said without a thought. "You deserve it."
