    On the window, I read: "Authentic Mandarin and Szechuan" and thought, "why not?" and walked in. It was around noon, and crowded.

    Julia, Empress Garden's friendly owner, greeted me. We had a conversation on what was tasty and she ultimately recommended a Taiwanese noodle dish (C71 on the menu) to me, which I ordered to go. I mentioned that I've never been in her restaurant before, so when she said it was 24 years old, I was floored!

    My dish of "spicy" (they weren't really spicy), dry Taiwanese noodles set me back $6.75, but kept me fed for hours. It was a generous portion of shredded string beans, salty soybeans, tofu, pepper flakes, pork (meat), bean sprouts, and white noodles. Did I mention it came out right away? Worth a try, stop in!

    Best place for Taiwanese food in the area, in my opinion. I always stop by for a big bowl of the Taiwanese Beef Noodle soup, and treat myself to a freshly-made, real-fruit mango smoothy (watermelon in the late summers, too!).  Their three-meat dishes, Chinese watercress with garlic, and fish dishes are all delicious, and remind me of eating in Taiwan.

We've also ordered larger dishes for catering (rolls, rice, noodles, and meat dishes), and they were swift, and gave us a discount!

    This place knows Taiwanese Food!

    Among the things of MUST GETs:
    - Taiwanese Beef Noodle Soup - as others have mentioned, not too salty, perfectly seasoned, tender meats, noodles with nice bite, lots of pickled mustard greens (my favorite).  Great portion, doesn't go overboard on the star anise.  Actually, the Beef Noodle Soup here is better than most I have tried in NYC's Chinatown.
    - Porkchops over rice (Paigu fan) - these porkchops are perfectly seasoned with that five spice powder and the juicy suckers are fried in a super crispy batter and fried to perfection.
    - Jia Jian Mien (the homestyle noodles with the black bean sauce) - what I love about these noodles here is that there are plenty of bits of meat and it is saucy.  I hate it when you go to places and there is not enough meat.  And it's got some sprouts in there to give it that nice wet crunch.
    - Mah Wan (massive Taiwanese vegetable dumpling?) I don't know what it is in english, but it is made of that glutinous rice and inside it is filled with assorted Taiwanese veggies and it has that pink/red sauce on it.  When I was growing up, I always called it a jellyfish, but it really has nothing to do with a jellyfish, it just kind of looks like one. The one here is GREAT.  No tuff spots in the dough.  The sauce is the perfect consistency and has great flavor and tang. Not overly sweet like some places.
    - Scallion pancakes:  the ones here are crisp and thin and comes with the seasoned dipping sauce.  Delicious!

    Service is super friendly.  I'm a fan and can't wait to go back!

    once in awhile a taiwanese food crave will creep up on me and engulf me until i am lumbering down chinatown looking for my childhood favorites.  a handwritten poster on the window reads "taiwanese snacks" in chinese, listing some taiwanese favorites which was enough for me to venture in.

    for those that do not read chinese, unfortunately, the authentic dishes are only listed in chinese on the back of the menu.  some items will appear on the english pages but not much.

    here is what we ordered:  

    1.  taiwanese oyster pancake ($6.95). it definitely did not come close nightmarket quality.  it was passable and more "homestyle."  it seems like no one really knows the secret behind the red sauce so most places outside of taiwan use a ketchup/soy sauce mixture.  empress garden is no different.  the oysters i thought were non-existent.  but the starch mixture had a decent crisp.

    2.  taiwanese crystal meatball (ba wan) ($3.95).  the outershell was ok.  the meat filling could use bigger chunks of meat and some bamboo shoots.

    3.  scallion pancakes ($3.95).  one of the best scallion pancake i've ever had.  it's more deep-fried than pan-fried tasting.  i'm not sure why they bring out a vinegar dipping sauce with it though.

    3.  steamed rice with braised ground pork (lu rou fan) ($6.95).  eh...  it was okay.  really overpriced for a bowl of rice, even if it's a big bowl of rice.  don't get me wrong, it is quite tasty but just not authentic enough (meat is too lean and sauce is not greasy enough).

    4.  mango smoothie and red bean smoothie ($3 each).  both were delicious!  not too sweet and full of flavor.  i liked how they served the drink in a glass rather than a plastic cup.

    overall, i am only commenting on the dishes i tried.  i thought they tasted fine but were not quite authentic.  my friend who grew up in taiwan was pretty disappointed but comparing taiwan with empress garden is not really fair.  on its own, i think empress garden does a nice job offering something different in philadelphia.  

    besides the food, service is really warming.  the owners are really attentive and like to chat with their customers.  they are enthusiastic about recommending dishes and keep the water coming.

    Dear people of Chinatown (and those who venture into it), Mandarin and Szechuan does not mean Taiwanese. I'm not even sure if the staff here is Taiwanese. I had asked them if they had "Dan Bing" and they looked at me like I wanted an enchilada. Back me up here, my fellow Taiwanese! But, they do have some dishes from Taiwan on the menu. With that settled, let's get to the good (and bad) stuff.

    Definitely Order:
    + Peking Noodle (Ja Jiang Mien) - This is the reason I love coming here. This is the Chinese version of Spaghetti alla Bolognese. Salty, sweet, spicy, crunchy bean sauce over thick Chinese noodles. The noodles are on the softer side (old school Asians don't believe in al dente) and mix very well with the sauce.
    + Scallion Pancake - The best scallion pancake in the city. It's not thick like other places, and it's thin enough where it's not a cracker, either. The best part is the dipping sauce. Instead of the standard black vinegar mixture, the sauce is clear! I think it tastes better than its dark counterpart.
    + Ma Po Tofu - I love ma po tofu. When I can't have my mom's, I come here for it.  

    Maybe Order:
    + Taiwan Beef Noodle Soup - The noodle soup tastes pretty authentic, although it is on the blander side.
    + Pai Goo Fan -  Fried pork chops w/ pickled veggies over rice. I like this one, but the pork chops need to be crispier.

    Don't Order:
    + Soup Dumplings - Tastes like the frozen kind. There are other spots in town if you're craving these little exploding dumpling balls.
    + Chinese-American Dishes - General Tso's chicken, beef and broccoli, orange chicken, and shrimp in lobster sauce? Umm... no.

    I just noticed that all of the dishes listed under "Maybe" are the Taiwanese dishes, weird.

    The service, food, and prices at Empress Garden are great. Now if I can only find a place that makes Dan Bing....
