    Unpretentious, great tasting food, and very low prices.  Old recipes that take a long time to perfect, but not a long time at all going from the pot to your bowl (or incidentally, from the bowl to your stomach).  Simple food to bring your spirits up without tearing up your budget.

    These are all phrases that describe Nan Zhou.  I was lucky enough to get out of one of my catering training sessions an hour early yesterday night, though unlucky enough to be suffering from a bad case of a sinus-cold.  While I stumbled into the door at 9:05 wheezing and dripping snot and brain-juice, one of two women who seemed to be working the shop alone hurried me into a seat by the door.  I knew from researching the place prior that the closing time was 10:00, so I felt a little bad... but this was my last chance to get a square meal before hitting the booze and the bed before work tomorrow!

    I quickly ordered the lamb soup with hand drawn noodles, cuz' hey - it's a hand-drawn noodle shop, ya'll.  There are much more exotic selections, but I thought if I am just coming here for the first time, something I know to be delicious and comforting is exactly what I needed to clear the snakes out of my brain.  Worthy of note is tripe, tendon, and stomach.  I'm not particularly scared of these selections, so I'll probably have to give them a go another day, perhaps with some friends to sample a lot of stuff family style.  There are also more expensive offerings labeled 'appetizers' which I assume correlate to the (what I thought was only Sichuan) practice of offering plates of particularly flavorful cold and warm/roomtemp preparations for a group of people before the real grub-get-down.  Usually the intention of such plates is to offer an assortment of textures according to Fuchsia Dunlop's book, "Land of Plenty."  That would explain why the widest variety of organ meats and such seems to be available in the appetizer section, I suppose.

    Let's talk turkey... or lamb, as it would be.  The broth was fantastic, with great body/mouthfeel, depth, character, and the perfect amount of seasoning.  There is what looks like a basic homade chile paste on every table to adjust the spice to your favorite level as well.  If probed, I'd guess the broth mostly consists of pre-boiled meaty bones, as it is quite clear, has little sediment, and doesn't have the aromatic qualities that charring provides to broths of the pho family.  The noodles are beautifully irregular, and have a much better texture, bite, and secondary flavor than dried noodles, or much more significantly, fresh noodles from a Chinese market.  The color of the noodles did not fade at all, though they did sit in the hot broth for at least 20 minutes to my knowledge.  The broth was littered with about a handful of stewed lamb bones with meat attached that were very flavorful, and easily yielded from the bone with no effort whatsoever.  There was also a scattering of typical Chinese mustard pickle and cilantro which gave little 'palate refreshments' between different bites in the soup.

    The total price was only $6.50, so I will most definitely be back.  The service was notably fast and efficient, but sent off a rather cold vibe to me that drastically changed after I offered a tip while paying later.  I'll have to see if they remember a face.

    Why haven't we tried Nan Zhou before?! We love all kinds of Asian cuisine, and my bf loves loves LOVES not only really good soup, but also really amazing noodles.

    We went on a whim around 2pm on a Saturday, and boy was it PACKED! The nondescript room, which is sort of open to the kitchen in back, has a number of chairs and tables but it quickly became clear that they fill seats regardless of who else is at the table. After waiting about 10 minutes we were seated at a large round table where a group of 4 was already chowing down.

    We ordered our soup, opting to try out the hand drawn noodles instead of the shaved noodles, from the rather short menu that's just a list of soups with different proteins, and a few appetizers that those in the know seem more likely to order. Service was very nice and FAST, and we quickly had a large steaming bowl in front of us. NOODLES. That was all I could think with each bite. OODLES OF NOODLES. They were perfect, just the right amount of bite. I could eat those endlessly, and I'm not someone who likes pasta. And I can't believe how cheap these bowls are! Prices don't seem to go above $8, with most being around $6 (unless you just want soup, no noodles, and then its even cheaper!). We will be coming back here many, many times in the future - whenever I don't feel like cooking!

    This place is going to become a staple in my restaurant mix, especially in the winter. I was totally anticipating something like ramen when I ordered the veggie and egg noodle soup, but was presently surprised by a broth that was so much more aromatic and noodles that were so much more satisfying and flavorful. I got the hand drawn noodles and preferred them a lot more over the shaved noodles, which my boyfriend got in his soup. The texture was more delicate, and I found that they soaked up some of the broth's flavor a little better probably because of their large surface area. I also like the slinky texture of them as you scoop them up with your chopsticks and slurp them into your mouth.

    The broth is salty and slightly sweet and smells a lot like Chinese five spice. And if you're thinking this soup is like the Vietnamese pho because it's so aromatic and nourishing, it isn't. The broth is richer, and more robust here. If you get it with egg and break it up so the yolk swirls all throughout, the broth becomes unctuous and glossy. My only complaint is that the egg and vegetable soup could use a few more veggies. The sprinkling of green onions and the five pieces of Chinese greens in my soup was a little underwhelming, especially since my boyfriend's beef brisket soup came with several large chunks of meat. But for the price, it was hard to complain. Oh, and don't forget to hydrate before and after this meal as it is a tad salty.
