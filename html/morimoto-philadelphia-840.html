    Warning: my review and rating are for the Omakase experience.

    Drinks: Cilantro is a love/hate herb. If you love it like I do, order the Cilantro Gimlet and thank me later. The White Pear is a sweet drink for the ladies and was quite delicious. The Morimoto Hazelnut Ale brewed by Rogue Ales was also  good with a noticeable hazelnut aftertaste. For a really strong drink, go with the Morimoto Martini.

    Food: Because it was a celebration, I decided to splurge on the $150pp Omakase. Overall, the food and quality were good but definitely not worth the price of admission.  At that price and for the $120pp price, I would much rather order 2 entrees, 1 cold appetizer and 1 hot appetizer because their regular menu has some delicious sound dishes.

    Now that you know how I feel about the Omakase. I'll give you a play by play of what I ate if you're still interested in trying it.
    1) Toro (fatty tuna) Tartare with a light soy sauce and gold flakes
    2) Three raw Japanese Oysters, each with a different topping
    3) Hokkaido Scallop Carpaccio with Uni (sea urchin) on top
    4) Five types of Sashimi (2 pieces for each type). Tuna, Amberjack, Yellowtail, Scallops, and I forgot the last one
    5) Half of a Grilled Lobster
    6) Wagyu Steak on a hot skillet (cook to your desired temperature by none other than you) served with a pesto sauce and a tomato puree
    7) 5 pieces of Nigiri (sushi)
    8) Goat Cheese Cake served with Peach Jelly and sprinkled with Black Sesame

    I will definitely never order Omakase from Morimoto's again but I may someday return to order off the regular menu. I'm a sucker for Japanese inspired dishes and everything on the regular menu is making my mouth water right now.

    I've been wanting to try a Morimoto restaurant since I recognized him as an Iron Chef. An original Iron Chef (Japan). His dishes are without fail the most artistic, amazing, awe-inspiring creations ever to grace the Food Network or any other aesthetic tableau. The ponytailed maestro is simply the greatest chef working in America today, and what better location to finally taste his creations than in his original United States restaurant in Philadelphia.

    After procuring reservations a month in advance, we were incredibly excited to learn via his Twitter feed that the Chef himself would be in the restaurant the night of service. Awesome. Best case scenario in fact.

    So in we strolled, group of six ready to dine finely until our heart's content. The first observation was hilarious - my confidante said, "this feels like a Pinkberry." And he was right - neon green, pink & purple rotated against the stark white walls and the minimalist decor; the only notable exception to the yogurt-addled blandness was the phallic mini-lights adorning each table. "An interesting choice," I thought, "but don't be harsh." My hopes were still high.

    As the server approached our table, notably annoyed, we inquired to whether Morimoto himself had graced his original U.S. spot with his presence as his feed indicated. "I don't think so," said the server. My immediate thought was: "this is an easy yes or no question, why would he need to think about it." The answer, obviously, was no. Clearly concerned about tip slippage based on the lack of the celebrity chef, he only lost points for honesty - not the lack of presence of Iron Chef Japan.

    We proceeded to order - the five other guests ordering entrees while I ordered the omakase. As the food arrived, I noticed a complete disarray of menu and service. I'd receive two dishes at a time and then wait (literally) fifteen minutes for the next. My hopes were high an I'm an optimist, so I decided to overlook the inconsistencies: "well, either the entire table gets omakase or nobody does," I thought, forgiving the shoddy service. As the dishes arrived, however, something else stood out - the quotidian nature of the food. Everything I had at the restaurant was a derivative of food I've had a Nobu, or worse - the myriad sushi spots in L.A. that serve fine cuisine. Nothing subpar, per se, but nary a dish that altered my life. Nothing akin to the beautiful pastiche created on ICA; nothing close to the ice-smoked fish he's been known to assemble. Worst of all, the closing dish - the ode to sushi, hand-chosen by the most renowned Japanese chef east of Nozawa, was average. Amberjack, skipjack, o-toro. . .fine, but not buttery. Good, but not buttery.

    I was honestly disappointed. And for $120, I felt that perhaps Masaharu Morimoto saved his best work for his more upscale, metropolitan locales in NY & LA. I made an incorrect assumption in hoping that his return to the original U.S. restaurant would make this a bastion of true Japanese cuisine. I suppose there is a reason he didn't show, however - the food just isn't up to the Herculean standard created on television, and he didn't want to let his most ardent fans leave with a bad taste in their mouths. So instead, he settled for mediocre.

    For shame, Morimoto-san. Thank goodness Jose Garces remains true to his Philadelphia roots; in this Iron Chef 'Battle,' the clear winner was the local that still has his heart in the City of Brotherly Love (tbc: Amada review).

    We squeezed this place in at lunch, which is a great time to go since they had a total of six tables the entire time we were there, including us. Service was impeccable. Our server was friendly and informative but not in the slightest bit pushy, and the busboys working with him were sweet and enthusiastic. They have a great list of very reasonably priced lunch specials, plus an omakase lunch offering. We also had decadent dinner plans, so we decided against omakase. The bf had the sashimi lunch special, and I had the buri bop, which is seared yellowtail in a hot stone bowl with veggies, much like the Korean dish, bibimbop. Lunch specials also come with miso soup and salad with a great yuzu dressing. The sashimi was good and beautifully presented, but we're from LA, so for us it wasn't a standout in terms of quality. I would say it was a B+. Good, but not great for the price. My own dish was truly great. The portion of rare yellowtail that accompanied it was generous (my boyfriend ate several pieces and I still had plenty), and the shiso dressing really brought out the flavours. This dish is mixed for you tableside in a stone bowl that is heated to 300 degrees so the rice develops a deliciously crunchy crust. Our server was especially enthusiastic in his mixing and with his intricate play by play explanation of what he was doing, from placing the slices of rare fish on the side of the hot stone bowl to sear it to drizzling seasoning on top to taste. I rounded out my meal with a glass of Morimoto's own sake and the matcha tres leches with burnt honey ice cream, both of which were delightful. Would definitely return and do the omakase.

    The surf and turf special comes out on an oversized plate with the tuna being tossed in a sauce table side.  When mixed with the cherry tomatoes and avocado, the subtle flavor of the yellow tail was completely lost.  The "kobe" steak was seared with a peppercorn like crust which was again so overpowering, the steaks flavor was ruined.  If you're ordering a kobe steak, you want to taste it; it shouldn't end up tasting like any other steak house cut.

    Salmon sushi dish appetizer had a nice flavor with the glaze, portion size was nice and the table enjoyed it.

    Individual sushi piece sizes are nice, although the rice could be just a tab bit sweeter.  Prices are per piece not per 2 like most places, so although the price doesn't look bad,, you're paying twice as much as anywhere else.  The rolls were assembled nicely, however there's nothing really that unique that stands out that you can't get anywhere else.  Nothing worth coming back for, there are much better sushi options in Philadelphia.

    We had some form of bowl of tuna-seaweed concoction that was again tossed and dressed tableside, but I wasn't impressed enough to write down the name of it for this review.  It was ok, but not something I would ever order again.

    Oh yea, be prepared to pay for royally for this experience.  $400 with tip for two couples sharing 2 entrees, a few roles and a couple of drinks.

Stephen S. Business Owner

Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District. STARR Restaurants expanded to Atlantic City, NJ, with Buddakan and The Continental located in the Pier Shops at Caesars in 2006, and to Florida with Steak 954 at the W Hotel Fort Lauderdale in 2009, and Makoto in the Bal Harbour Shops in 2011.
