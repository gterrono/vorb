    Thank goodness for Famous 4th Street Deli in the neighborhood - what a great addition to Rittenhouse area brunch spots!

    The space is tiny and quarters are cramped.  Unlike the original, the bakery items are in the back and there isn't much physical space for queuing to occur during peak dining hours.  

    My party of two had no trouble getting a seat in the late afternoon on Saturday.  We did have some difficulty fitting all our plates onto the table - complimentary relish & pickles, a roast beef sandwich, grilled cheese and side of fresh cut fries.  Proportions are enormous and the delicious, fresh rye bread was sliced over  1/2" thick.  Our waitress missed our order of fries so we didn't get to enjoy them at the same time as our sandwiches, but they were totally worth the wait.  Unsalted and plainly seasoned it was the thickness of the cut that made them stand out.  Crispy on the outside, after 15 minutes they were still hot and steamy on the inside.  

    Famous 4th Street Deli sandwiches & platters are so large, unless you're splitting a dish plan to bring home a doggie bag.  Because most people wrap up food to go, I'm especially disappointed that they use styrofoam packaging.  

    Ending on a positive note, the freshly baked, soft chocolate chip cookies served with our check were a delightful treat.

    I agree with Jessica T., this is a great addition to the neighborhood. I'm constantly searching for good brunch places. Famous 4th street is a no nonsense old school jewish deli perfect for a casual weeknight dinner or weekend lunch/brunch. This new location has the physical trappings of the one on 4th and Bainbridge - white walls, black and white checkered tile floors.

    The sandwiches are huge and the portions are more than generous. Be prepared to doggie bag just about everything. The pickles and slaw salad they give you free when you order is great. It really helps cut through the rich meats in the sandwiches like the killer pastrami.  

    I recently visited this place for brunch and had no problems getting a table even though the place was more or less full. It was great to be seated at a busy brunch place in Rittenhouse and not be around scenesters. It was a good mix of grad students, young professionals, families, and older clientele. No one was here because they thought it was hip...rather is was all about the food.

    The matzo ball soup comes with one ginormous matzo ball. The corned beef hash platter is a mountain of corned beef, potatoes and sauteed onions and peppers topped with two fried eggs. The platter is a thing of beauty and the perfect hang over cure. The service is efficient, warm and friendly. And at the end of the meal they send you on your way with two free cookies. The prices may seem steep at first glance but once you see the portions they make sense.

    I would recommend this place with no reservations to anyone who likes to chow down on some good jew food. I know I crave a pastrami sammie every so often and it's nice to have this place so close by.

    Amazing breakfasts? *check*
    Monster sandwiches and portions? *check*
    Dr. Browns soda? *check*

    For a Jewish kid raised around these types of food, when I walk in to Famous and see their long glass cases filled with huge pastries, cakes, followed by smoked fish and deli meats, I can feel it in my heart. No, that's my my heart preparing for the artery clogging goodness that awaits it, that's love that I'm feeling. Famous runs the show for the Jewish deli scene in the Philadelphia area.The closest thing to Famous between here and NYC is Harold's deli in Edison, NJ, though Famous is obviously much closer.

    Everything at Famous is huge, from the challah french toast, to the bowls of soup, to especially the sandwiches. The sandwiches are easily enough to share (unless you have an endless pit of a stomach like me).

    Famous is perfect for breakfast, especially on Sundays, then it's the place to be. Ask for a booth "by the windows", which is a line of maybe 15+ booths that run along Bainbridge so you can watch all the action.  

    My personal recommendations:

    Breakfast:
    *The traditional challah french toast
    *Kippered salmon platter. Ask for a scoop of whitefish salad to accompany your platter, and mix both on your bagel. This is the breakfast of champions

    Lunch and Dinner
    *Roast beef special on rye (russian dressing and cole slaw)

    If you're a fan of NYC Jewish deli's, you'll fall in love with Famous the second you walk inside. As you're eating your leftovers the next day, you can't help but fall in love with Famous all over again.

    The glowing reviews made this place a must-try, especially since it's a quick stumble from home. Inside, the delicatessen was trying very hard (and mostly succeeding) to ooze 1930s New York deli charm. Maybe it was a bit brightly-lit for a Sunday late morning, but hey, it did help wake me up.

    We had a bit of sticker shock with the menu: $15 for a pastrami sandwich? $11 for a side (side!) of nova? Hm. But nobody said this place was cheap (I just wasn't quite braced for an average $15 entree at a "mom-and-pop"-feeling place) and we figured, hey, maybe that means it'll be really damn good.

    I ordered eggs with a side of smoked whitefish (my freebie potato side was latkas), my gf got the eggs with beef hash (and a freebie side of hash browns), and we both got coffee and OJ. I'm used to tiny glasses of juice, but these were really small--like, 3/4 the size of an Old Fashioned glass. We also weren't brought water, but the place was busy: maybe it was just overlooked.

    It took a while for the food to come (maybe 15min or so), but the place was pretty much packed, and we entertained ourselves with watching the GIANT sandwiches roll past. We also checked out the take-out counter (cookies, cakes, etc--also a savory section that we didn't look too long at).

    When the food came, it was sort of epically sized: I think there must have been four eggs in my scramble, though the whitefish piece, for $7, was 3" long. Bagels (or toast, if you preferred) came with both our dishes. My gf's hash was huge. There is no other word for it. You could have split this thing between five people, easily, and nobody would have been hungry. Seriously. It was stacked at least 7" high and came on a 10" oblong plate, with the sunny-side up eggs balanced precariously on top.

    Sadly, it all looked better (and boy, did it look good) than it tasted.

    My eggs were fine. I would have liked salt and pepper, which were on some tables but not on ours, and the waiter was too busy to make eye contact. The whitefish was definitely too dry. The bagel was ok, but not, you know, a "New York deli" bagel: it had the same texture as a Dunkies bagel rather than the more compelling bite of a really top-notch one. The latkas were a little heavy on the grease, but had very good flavor (I like mine a bit heavy on the pepper, but I get that that's me, and don't expect them to have enough pepper for my taste). They should have come with sour cream (or apple sauce, if that's your shtick); they came with neither.

    My gf's pastrami hash was pretty good. She got ketchup with it, and it needed it, but the ingredients all tasted fresh and simple. The eggs were undercooked (underside pale, white not set within 1/2" of the yolk)--we both like soft eggs, but that just seemed like a rushed batch. The hash browns were alright: no complaints, no compliments. Could have used more salt/pepper (had none, like the eggs).

    Brunch consisting of two coffees, two (tiny) orange juices, eggs + whitefish, eggs on beef hash + hash browns came to $42. That seems ridiculous, in retrospect. Add tip to that and you're about at $50 for "just ok." This place would have gotten 2 stars if not for the value for your dollar, which really, really fails.

    The take-out counter did call, and we got a (giant) eclair and a 4" slice of apple strudel, which came to $14. At home, the strudel was mushy without a distinct apple flavor, too much cinnamon, and a general lack of texture; the eclair had promising if thick pate choux, but a weirdly chemical-aftertaste highly-oversweetened filling.

    In retrospect, what upsets me less is blowing $60 on mediocre food; what upsets me more is that giant pile of hash going to waste, because there's just no way even a hungry college football star could tuck the entire plate away: it seems unethical to make portions so big that you're guaranteeing waste, or forcing people to come in a group of 3 or 4 to split a single entree (in which case, your dollar value improves significantly).

    For a better meal at a better price, try Rachael's Nosheri just a couple blocks away. For a fancier meal that's still half the price, try Day By Day, also just a few blocks away. Don't bother with this overrated, overstuffed, uninspiring wannabe deli.

    You'd expect a Jewish deli to be able to make the perfect smoked-salmon bagel sandwich. So I went there with high expectations. Although I'm not Jewish, my favorite breakfast meal is smoked salmon on a bagel with cream cheese and onion. I usually make my own at home, with ingredients from Whole Foods. In the '90s, though, I lived near the original Famous 4th Street Deli, so I'd occasionally have the sandwich there. I now live near the new Famous on 19th Street, so I thought I'd try them out this morning. So I ordered the nova version of the bagel sandwich (lox is also available), and headed to Rittenhouse Square to sit on a bench and eat it.

    What a disappointment. The main problem was that the nova did not seem to be fresh. It had a strange taste -- not a fishy taste, but rather a slightly "sewery" taste. Now, it's assumed that this morning's batch of nova was simply on its last legs, and as soon as this batch is finished, the deli will have a fresh batch of nova to work with, and therefore the problem should not be there in the future. However, the fact that this particular batch would continue to be used beyond its apparent "sell-by" date indicates that there is a deeper problem here. The products need to be monitored more closely for freshness, and when something (especially something as highly perishable as fish) doesn't taste or smell right anymore, it should be disposed of, not served to customers.

    Beyond that, though, whoever made the sandwich just didn't take enough care to put it together properly. There was a big hunk of cream cheese in the center, without much on the edges of the bagel. It was as if the maker was too lazy to spread the cheese evenly around the whole bagel.

    It was also a somewhat odd choice to use red onion rather than white or Vidalia (the latter being my favorite on a smoked-salmon bagel). I think of red onion being more a salad onion, not a sandwich onion.

    The bagel itself was good, so no complaints there. And the slice of tomato was fresh.

    One pet peeve, though -- why do some places insist on slicing a bagel sandwich in half? Slicing a bagel sandwich does not make it easier to handle (as it does with a hoagie-type sandwich), it makes it more unwieldy, with the contents of the sandwich more apt to fall out. So please, at least ask before slicing.

    I give two stars rather than one because: 1) The place is pretty and clean and pleasant inside; 2) The wait-staff is attentive and friendly; 3) I want to give them the benefit of the doubt that this was just an anomaly and was simply my bad luck that I got something yucky on my first visit. I'm not sure, though, that I would give them a second visit, so I'll probably never know.

    My mom saw Famous 4th Street on the Food Network and couldn't wait for me to try it out - I'm so glad I took her suggestion!  It was definitely one of the best Jewish delis I've been to, including those in New York.  It was actually a much nicer experience than your typical New York deli because it wasn't ridiculously crowded and I didn't feel rushed to leave.  

    The prices are a bit shocking at first glance ($16 dollar sandwiches and $10 pieces of cake), but the portions are enormous.  My corned beef sandwich literally lasted me three meals!  We started off the meal with a plate of pickles and coleslaw, and a plate piled high with different kinds of bread.  The bread is AMAZING - I couldn't stop eating it.  The rye was great, but what I really loved were the rolls - a perfect skin of toasted brown with a sweet, buttery center.  I could have eaten five without blinking twice.

    Be sure to come with at least three dining companions because the chicken matzo ball soup is a must have, and only comes in a big portion for "2-3 people" (it could easily serve 6 people if eaten as an appetizer).  It's like chicken noodle soup with matzo balls, and the combination is far superior to either soup separately.  It's easy for such a soup to be too bland or too salty, but Famous 4th Street's version is perfection.  

    As I mentioned, I had the corned beef sandwich, which did not disappoint.  It was piled high with meat (I had to eat about half the meat on its own before I could fit the sandwich in my mouth) and was very authentic - nothing on it but rye bread and corned beef.  

    We ended the meal with complimentary chocolate chip cookies, which were heavenly - warm, crispy on the outside and chewy and gooey on the inside.  The service was also great - our waitress was very patient with our indecisiveness and checked on us frequently (another nice contrast to most NY delis).  Definitely my new favorite place in Philly!
