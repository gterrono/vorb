    Soooooo good!  Morimoto is a beautiful restaurant with a 'flowing' wood-planked ceiling and designs on white plaster (?) walls that evoke being inside a roiling wave.  The seating -- raised two-person booths along the sides and tables of assorted sizes down the middle -- is separated by short plexiglass walls that are lit internally by richly colored lighting that changes over time.  You can also get most of the full menu in the small bar/lounge upstairs that overlooks the restaurant.  The staff is friendly, helpful, efficient, and knowledgeable.  The pacing of the meal is perfect, even when people at a single table are getting varying numbers of courses.  The food is outstanding across the board.  (However, to me, the seafood -- every bit I tried -- soundly trounced the beef.)  Everything is flavorful and beautifully presented.  Eating at Morimoto is definitely a splurge, but it's well worth it - I look forward to saving up for another visit someday!

    A couple of weeks ago, the BF and I decided to come here spontaneously for a couple of reasons: it had always been on our bucket list, and both of us wanted sushi (or some variation of it).  We finally decided to cross this upscale, and dare I say, decadent hot spot off our list.  

    I'm always a bit apprehensive with Stephen Starr restaurants (they're very hit or miss), but Morimoto was a surefire hit.  Definitely worth the time and the money, but not with some exceptions.   Needless to say, it's an experience everyone should do at least once, and the high quality food and service makes it worthwhile.

    What we liked:
    -Those interiors. Like seriously. It might be a little late 90s-early 00s but they are still gorgeous
    -The sashimi appetizer was incredible.  Some of the best raw fish I've ever had in Philly.  Variety and flavors were on point. I could eat that for days.
    -Foie Gras and Grilled Eel with Sake Meyer Lemon and Asian Pear. Let that sink in.
    -Duck Duck Duck was unreal. I was also surprised at how much duck breast they put on your plate. Definitely more than enough food and justifies the price point.
    -The tres leches cake for dessert was unique and incredibly creative. Additionally, it was sweet without being too sweet.

    What we didn't like:
    -The bar service needs work. Our server informed us that they had someone new starting. It took forever to get our cocktails, and my yuzu gimlet tasted like water (luckily, our server was happy to replace it with an alternate beverage)
    -The Black Miso Cod was nothing to write home about

    Overall, it's a pricey exercise to do dinner at Morimoto, but the food is wonderfully crafted and beautifully presented with some original creative twists here and there. Definitely worth a visit.

    Morimoto offers an a la carte menu and two tasting menus (120/150).  My partner and I opted for the 150 tasting menu, ending up spending $470 for the tasting menus, four total beverages (we did not get the pairings) and tip.  Price wise this is comparable to the dinner seating at Momofuku Ko in NYC.If I could only do one, I would choose Ko, but this is in no way on slight on Morimoto. The major difference between the two is that that emphasis at Ko is on creative, expertly crafted dishes, whereas the emphasis at Morimoto is on fresh fish of exceptional quality. Our meal, while not the best of my life, was well worth the money spent, Morimoto is worth a trip, not for the kitsch value of a celebrity chef, but for the most authentic sushi, sashimi and maki I have had since leaving Japan.

    The courses:
    Amuse Bouche - the amuse bouche is an excellent way for the chef to present his offerings in a bite-sized portion and to sort of tease the pallet.  At Morimoto it was largely unremarkable and seemed more a perfunctory offering than an attempt to demonstrate his skills.

    1st - Tuna Tartare with edible gold - this was outstanding both in presentation and in flavor profile.  It included a lovely wasabi (of which they have three different varieties), crispy shallots and was finished with a Japanese peach. I do not know all the elements that mark the difference between the 120 and the 150, but it may have been partially the opulence of the edible gold. Because Japanese cuisine is as focused on aesthetics as on taste, the edible gold felt more appropriately worth an increased price than it does in other locations.  The dish was beautiful and delicious.

    2nd - Scallops - the scallops were perfectly prepared and cut razor thin.  Paired with caviar and sesame oil, they were one of the best dishes.

    3rd - Oysters - I do not like oysters, but will eat almost anything on a tasting menu.  The Oysters presented had the typically beautiful presentation. There were four oysters each with a different seasonings that were increasingly spicy beginning with a lemon preparation and ending with a seeded jalapeño.  The spiciness seemed to cut down on the briny taste the first had, and while not a stand out dish for me, they were palatable, which is, for a non-oyster eater, a rare feat.

    Amuse Bouche - cucumber cilantro gelee. This was the best dish of the night in terms of presentation and flavor profile.

    4th - Sashimi - a selection of six different fish.   A few reviews have noted that the fish did not taste fresh; however, my experience was that the fish was exceptionally fresh.  And I would go further to say that you can skip everything else and will be, if you have had sushi/sashimi in Japan, completely satisfied.    This (and the sushi course) are the reason to go.

    5th - Sushi and Maki - Having lived in Japan for a while I am typically dissatisfied with the sushi I have in the states.   This sushi tasted completely authentic to me, but I couldn't identify what it was that made this sushi excel where others had failed.  I mentioned it to our wonderful waiter who told me that the reason was probably that Morimoto polishes their own rice.   Once he said it, it made sense.  Other places have wonderful, fresh fish, but at Morimoto the rice is also right.  The sushi was perfect.

    6th - Lobster with bok choy and heirloom carrot and creme fraiche custard.  it is difficult to go wrong with lobster, but this was a delicious hot dish.

    7th - Wagyu beef - one of the differences between the 120 and 150 tasting menu is that at $150 you will get Japanese wagyu as opposed to Australian. Japanese wagyu is something that should be eaten at least once in life and really as often as money permits.  

    8th - dessert - chocolate cake with matcha and cassis sorbet.  The dessert was good though not particularly interesting (although asian cuisines tend not to have desserts that appeal to western pallets); however, our waiter brought us a small dish of coconut sorbet that was excellent. In fact, if you order the tasting menu I recommend ordering a small portion as it complements the cake beautifully.  

    Service - Our waiter, Andrew, was the perfect mix of professional and friendly.  With a meal that takes around 2.5 hours it is important to feel a rapport with the waiter.   I can't speak to the service for ordering a la carte, but the tasting menu experience was made complete by the excellent service we received.

    Drinks - The drinks had surprisingly light pours for the type of restaurant; however, the cocktails are unique and balanced and, thus, worth it for me. However,  my male partner found that they lacked a little of the robustness he was hoping for.

    In sum - this is a meal worth experiencing.  If you go with the expectation of receiving quality foods , carefully prepared (and not inventive combinations) and enjoy authentic sushi, this will be an enjoyable and memorable meal.

    Overview:

    Morimoto was a mixed experience for me. While I vacillated between giving 3 or 4 stars, I ultimately settled on 4 because this is still a restaurant that did nothing overtly wrong and is a safe bet for a special occasion. I think that you won't regret coming here, but if you've already been, or are going with a group that's already visited other nice restaurants, you should think twice. This is a solid place but the outdated decor and the overseasoned food take away from its sophistication. Especially for a 4-$ restaurant, there are places that feel more evolved in being elegantly subtle in both food preparation and ambience.

    Details:

    Food-wise, I was not super hungry so did not opt for the omakase. Instead, I ordered the kumamoto oysters (half-dozen for $18, which is a steal for kumamotos at a nice restaurant), a daily special bone marrow appetizer, and the sea bass, which many people on Yelp had already recommended.

    My gripe with the kumamoto oysters and the bone marrow is that these two dishes are already delicious with minimal seasoning/sauces. Yet, Morimoto masked their inherently unique and wonderful flavors by overdoing it. Kumamoto oysters are my favorite because you can eat them pretty plain and still get a rich, sweet, and buttery flavor. Morimoto doused them with various toppings (jalapeno, citrus, and one more that I can't recall). These were good, but detracted from the whole reason you eat kumamotos - while more standard/briny oysters might need flavor distraction, kumamotos do not. The bone marrow was also fine, but again, was prepared with extra seasonings that made it overly salty.

    However, the sea bass was phenomenal!! The texture was perfect - it fell apart easily but still gave good chew. I also tasted the Asian influence in the savory/umami flavoring.

    Decor-wise, I actually disagree a bit with the general consensus and think it detracted from the experience. The shifting lighting, goggle-shaped motif, massive curved lines, and the hologram at the entrance felt gimmicky and outdated. It reminded me of what trendy restaurants were supposed to look like in the late 90s/early 00s. I think many restaurants now eschew that aesthetic for something more natural/rustic (think ABC Kitchen), or just go with a more traditional clean and elegant style.

    Value-wise, it's not cheap but is decent. My three-course meal, which left me very full, was close to $90 after tax and tip. Had I been hungrier, the omakase at two price points ($120 or $150 before tax and tip) would have been good options for the large amount of food and courses.
