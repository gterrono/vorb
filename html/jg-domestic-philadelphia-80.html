    Listed in Drinking in Philly

    I've been to JG Domestic a few times now and am very pleased with its consistently good food and its relaxing environment. I actually set my expectations fairly low the first time I came, as I had heard mixed reviews, so I was pleasantly surprised that we've turned out to enjoy it so much.

    The first time I went was for a celebratory lunch. I got the pappardelle with black kale, Oregon chanterelles, and kabocha squash, which was exquisite. The whole dish was so thoughtfully executed, beautiful to look at, and full of flavor. It still ranks as one of my favorite dishes since moving to Philly. The portions were not too large (not too small, either, but just right), so we had room for dessert and got the bourbon beignets, which are to die for - light and fluffy, dusted in sugar, and served with Maker's Mark butterscotch and a bourbon vanilla mousseline. We enjoyed these with some wonderful coffee, and I'd have to say this dish, too, ranks among our all-time Philly favorites. Overall I enjoyed the focus on local/regional and farm-fresh ingredients, as well as the open, airy space in the Cira Centre lobby. A little pricy for a regular weekday lunch spot, but great for something special.

    We've been back for lunch, but still hadn't been for dinner until the other week, when we had the pleasure of attending a special bourbon dinner. We were really looking forward to finally being able to enjoy some drinks here, since usually we're here during the workday and can't partake. I've been to my share of wine & beer dinners at various restaurants over the years, and I thought this dinner was executed very well. We started off with some complimentary cocktails and charcuterie, which were just right for setting our palate for the dinner to come. The dinner itself was held in the back room, at two large communal tables. All of the dinner dishes were served family style, which was a really interesting approach for this kind of dinner, but it worked. The setup was excellent for ensuring that the guest bourbon distiller could speak to the whole room at the same time (unlike some dinners where the speaker has to make the rounds to a lot of different tables and repeat him/herself over and over), and we could all hear fine, as well as engage in some very interesting discussion. I even learned from a fellow diner that Chef Garces has played a significant role in bringing some specific bourbons to the state, which is much appreciated. We enjoyed all our food, from the buttery dinner rolls to the chicken chowder, fresh greens, pickled vegetables, pork spareribs, cornbread, macaroni & kale gratin, and of course, those sublime beignets. The friendly servers were generous with the food and kept the bourbon flowing, which incidentally did pair well with the food, especially the meat. I think the whole meal was a fantastic value - it felt like we easily ate and drank twice as much as what we were charged. We even got some leftovers to take home! It was definitely a night to remember, and really makes me respect the Garces Group even more.

    I really look forward to coming here more often and continuing to enjoy all it has to offer.

    Every paradigmatic American supper should begin with two things: 1) a nice cool, tall glass of honey-colored domestic pilsner and 2) classic pull apart dinner rolls. Said rolls must have amber brown tops, glazed with sweet butter and a pinch of sea salt for gettin' fancy. The bread itself must be slightly yellow with layers lending themselves to vertical peeling.

    Butter should be whipped, clean, and nearly white, and just a snatch should lend moisture enough to the roll on which it finds itself melting. If you have dinner guests, be sure to serve apple jam, which, in its vague tartness, intensifies the roll and butter duo.

    During the awkward interim between spring and summer, a sun choke soup, sprightly, and textured with enough puree such that your palate doesn't confuse the admixture for a lobster bisque, is the perfect appetizer to quell any stomach as it grumbles before the main course.

    Beets served over whipped ricotta must be cooked such that enough crunch remains without a raw molecule to chip a tooth. The whipped ricotta must be ethereal enough to please the gods, but earthly enough to offer a more substantial sponge over which the maroon sheen of the beets may flow.

    If serving a pizza, mushrooms are all a-twitter among dilettante diners, and must be seared flawlessly. They must not be too chewy, and if adding truffle oil, epicures would advise against dousing any fine cheddar smothered with egg-yolk too liberally. After all, the triumvirate of umani, sharpness and cream is a delicate one, but when executed heedfully, creates a beautiful crescendo of flavors not to be missed.

    J.G. Domestic is the place to indulge in a multifarious, yet quintessential, American meal. With no region left untouched, J.G. has wittingly unearthed the finest foods of the United States, from sea-to-shining-sea and served them with such splendor that no 'merican (nor worldly fellow) could contest the greatness of American cuisine.

My best friend (not the bday girl) is a vegetarian and pregnant and we were not aware until we got there that the only menu would be the Dining Days menu. The main entrees were all meat and the appetizers had either meat raw egg, or cheese that a pregnant woman can't eat. They did give her lettuce salad without the goat cheese and made her a noodle dish with peas. Overall, there just wasn't anything special about anything we had, especially not the substituted vegetarian noodle dish. I tried all 3 desserts- the chocolate sandwiches, the bourbon donut thing and the blueberry custard. The fingerlings were not warm when I got them and pretty blah, not any better than a diner. The desserts were probably the best part and the bourbon was very interesting and unique. Our 4 course meal was $30 a person since it was Dining Days so we were all pretty stuffed.     If ambiance is most important and you aren't into anything unique with your food, then you will probably like this place. But if you are looking for something unique with your food, you may be disappointed. But it is all a matter of preference.

After a few courses, he commandeered an empty 2-top next to ours and proceeded to make extra space for us. Much appreciated!

    Tasting Menu ($55 pp)

    I've noted the a la carte pricing for those who may think this tasting is simply too gluttonous.

    Parker House Rolls

    But on with our tasting! As with all meals at JG Domestic, you start with delicious Parker House rolls. These come fresh out of the oven in a cast iron pan, topped with sea salt. Apple thyme jelly and whipped butter accompanied the quartet of rolls, of which I gladly ate three. Sorry babe!

    Hickory Smoked Georgia Pecans ($7)
    Maple, Bacon

    These are the perfect bar snack. The smoky bacon, the sweet maple, and some cayenne added a hint of spiciness to the delicious molasses-y nuggets.

    Landaff Cheese ($7)

    The Landaff was sourced from New Hampshire and offered a tart creaminess which was complemented well by the accompanying mango raisin chutney and accentuated by the tart Granny smith apple slices. The requisite baguette slices were fresh and appreciated.

    GTC Salami ($9)

    The house-made salumi had a gaminess with prototypical highlights of fennel. But the real star here? The egg salad - creamy with textural accents of raw onion, which offset the intensity of the salami. And who can say no to more bread?

    Roasted Beets ($13)
    Whipped ricotta, smoked maple syrup, micro arugula, pistachio

    There was a mix of sweet purple and yellow beets, with highlights of smoked maple syrup. The slightly tangy fruit and the subtly sweet ricotta melded the dish together nicely. The pistachios added a nice textural element as well.

    Wood Oven Flatbread ($14)
    Hen of the woods, egg yolk, black truffle, cheddar

    While many can say that flatbread with an egg and truffle is also commonplace, it's hard to say it's pedestrian. It's easy to see why this is a favorite and a house specialty at JG Domestic. With a bechamel base and a crispy, buttery crust, the additions of maitake mushrooms, or hen of the woods, is a substantial addition. The umami of the egg yolk, black truffle oil, and cheddar push it over the top. A must-order.

    Day Boat Scallops ($24)

    Sourced from New Bedford, MA, these scallops were sweet, with a slight chew. The outer crust was cooked well, though slightly salty for my taste, though the spritz of the lemon wedge served to temper this. Simple, but delicious.

    Jumbo Shrimp ($22)

    One of my favorite dishes at Tinto was the gambas. So natch, I was excited when I saw these bad boys from the Gulf Coast of LA. Smoky with a nice snap to the tail/torso end, which the GF indulged in, I was happy with the heads. Creamy and deliciously savory - most people don't know what they're missing. Another must-order, provided you know what to do with the heads.

    Organic Poussin ($21)

    Sourced from Grisstown Farm in Jersey, these young chickens were expectedly moist and juicy, with a nice outer char. From top to bottom, the accompanying trio of sauces included a herbaceous chimichurri, a creamy jalapeño aioli, and a romesco sauce. My favorite of the three was the aioli, which had a nice spicy kick to it on the finish.

    Brussels Sprouts ($10)
    La Quercia proscuitto, Allagash honey, roasted chestnut, orange

    The proscuitto added the smokey undertone that is usually offered by bacon, but in contrast, the proscuitto had a sweet undertone. The contrast between the bright orange and the raw red onion was nice. The greens themselves were slightly charred and offered a bright accompaniment to the aforementioned poussin. The sauce was highly reminiscent of what us Koreans dip roasted pork belly in - a soy/sugar sauce with raw onions.

    Bourbon ($9)
    Beignets, bourbon vanilla mousseline, Maker's Mark butterscotch

    For the finale, we had a French Quarter staple - crusty beignets with fluffy innards, meant to be dipped in that delicious bourbon butterscotch (in place of coffee). Powdered vanilla bean stems topped a mousseline, which was thick, with nice undertones of bourbon and subtle hints of bourbon.

    JG Domestic is a great option if you're looking to try some of Iron Chef Garces' dishes through his tasting menu gauntlet. It's nice to see fresh ingredients that can be rendered into inventive and more importantly, delicious dishes. The location could be better, but I guess it makes sense to have one of your restaurants below your main offices. What better way to keep a pulse on food that is being put out under your name?

History

Established in 2010.

Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a refined, Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, a contemporary American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago, IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale, AZ and Palm Springs, CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the landmark Revel Resort. Today, in addition to our much-lauded restaurants around the country, Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
