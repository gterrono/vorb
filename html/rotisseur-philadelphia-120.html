        For my first Rotisseur experience, I decided to go all in with a whole chicken and two large sides for $28.  It provided a perfectly A-OK dinner on a cold winter night, but I will probably do some things differently on my next trip.

        Mine will not be the first review to suggest that Rotisseur's prices are a little high; however, I would say they are totally worth it for the chicken.  This was the BEST rotisserie chicken I have ever had.  It had slightly charred skin, ample amounts of moist and succulent meat, and came pre-rendered into manageable dark and light meat pieces.  The chicken was cooked to perfect "just done-ness" so it wasn't dried out or stringy like most rotisserie chickens.  Next time I will plan my evening a little better to eat the chicken right after I get it and enjoy the skin while it's still crispy.

        For as amazing as the chicken was, I was a little disappointed in my sides of confit roasted potatoes and beets with feta.  The potatoes were fine, but not any better than what I could make at home - not even better enough than frozen french fries to justify their price tag.  The beets were a lot more pickle-y than I was expecting, which completely overpowered the sweetness of the veggies, and the feta mixed in with them was excessively salty.

        As far as service goes, the store was pretty empty around 5:30 on a Tuesday and the young men at the counter were very nice.  My chicken was cut up and packaged (along with the sides) very safely for travel.

        I want to love this place, I really do. It serves some of my absolute favorite foods - roasted chicken, bbq chicken, mac n' cheese, roasted potatoes? Mmm... But, Rotisseur doesn't really deliver. The chicken is meh. The BBQ sauce is meh. The rolls are okay. The condiments are not that great either.

        It's a little pricey as far as Philadelphia goes, too. I'd expect to pay the amount they charge if I were in, say, the heart of Williamsburg. But, charging me $3.50 for literally 7 potato wedges is kind of ridonkulous considering that an entire 10 lb. bag of potatoes probably costs them that much money.

        What is also a little annoying about this place is that when I stopped in on Friday night, there were literally 7 employees floating around behind the counter, and it still took a goodly amount of time to re-heat the pre-prepared food I ordered. One employee was in training, another two employees were in the very back of the shop, and there were an additional three making food and serving customers, and to top it off, the seventh employee present was not even on the clock - he was just hanging out for fun, occasionally dipping back behind the counter.

        Everyone working there is nice and friendly. They were extremely well dressed, too. I guess charging $3.50 for 7 potato wedges allows them to pay their employees decently enough to have a nice wardrobe? But, I was put off by the fact that the girl working the register was wearing her long, thick hair down, when this is clearly a violation of the health code.

        The food wasn't terrible, but it wasn't great. The prices left much to be desired given average costs of food in Philadelphia. Mostly, I was put off by the fact that there were more employees than customers and they were a little too laid back to provide quick service. Clearly, this place is striving for Brooklyn levels of hip, but falls utterly short.

        Every day, around 11 am, my stomach grumbles and my mind shifts focus for the next hour to the most important decision of my day: what do I want for lunch?

        The answer is often Rotisseur, primarily for 3 reasons:
        1) Chicken Banh Mi
        2) House-made Red Wine Vinaigrette
        3) House-made spicy hoisin-like sauce

        It goes without saying that this place has damn good rotisserie chicken, which you better if that's your namesake. Their juicy, expertly-roasted chicken is local and free of cages, hormones, and antibiotics, making it 1000x healthier for you than that cheesesteak you WERE going to order for lunch.

        Now, you have a couple of options. Essentially, you can order chicken and sides or you can order sandwiches. The sandwiches, however, are a summery, spicy bite served on crunchy French bread and bursting with flavor. It looks small but it's satisfying. I'm not sure if I'd liken it to the more traditional banh mi but hey, a rose by any other name yadda yadda.

        Speaking of great sides though, they have fantastic salad. Yes. You heard right. I said "salad" and "fantastic" in the same sentence. Why, you ask? Well, the flavor artisans at Rotisseur makes some of their own sauces, one of which happens to be their INCREDIBLE red wine vinaigrette.         Couple their honest, good food with their fantastic service and super convenience and you'll never wonder about where to go for lunch again.

        Okay, let me preface this by saying I am a vegetarian and I suppose am mostly writing this for other vegetarians who might be interested, since this place boasts two different tofu sandwiches. Basically, my advice would be to take the sides, which are good, but leave the tofu.

        I got the BBQ Tofu sandwich once. It's a long roll with sliced tofu, mayo-less slaw, pickes and BBQ sauce. Everything else was fine (although the pickles leaned towards the thick end), except the tofu. Here's the thing, in my experience, with trying to BBQ tofu. First, you really need to press it. Here, it was decently firm, okay. It wasn't chewy (usually, to get that, you need to freeze and thaw it). Overall though, I would not get it again for one major reason - no crust. BBQ tofu generally isn't marinated like other tofu dishes, so a lot of people drench it in sauce (this wasn't) and broil it for a few minutes. It'll get a delightful crust that is similar to the crust on rubbed meats or chicken skin. It's actually pretty good. There wasn't any crust on the tofu here, which felt a little sad to me. If I am totally honest, tofu isn't really the "best" meat substitute out there for BBQ - most veg people I know argue that seitan is better, but I get that seitan is more labor intensive and tougher to work with. Only one BBQ place I ever went to offered it, and they aren't in business anymore. I am sure that the BBQ chicken is probably light years ahead of this.

        Okay, on a positive note, I do love the sides. They offer Kimchi (which will please many a vegetarian), little corn bread muffins (which are Amazing, seriously), mac and cheese (which I admittedly have not confirmed is totally vegetarian, but I really, really hope because I love it so), beets with feta cheese, roasted potato wedges (really, really good), pickles and others sides. I will certainly be back here to indulge in one of vegetarianism's greatest traditions - gorging on sides. (and maybe getting a salad). The sides are a good size, reasonably priced and certainly worth the trip all by themselves.
