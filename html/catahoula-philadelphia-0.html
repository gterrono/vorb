        First to Review

        Holy cow Catahoula. First of all, I understand that there have been many regime changes and renovations that have gone into Catahoula, and I am not going to get into those because I am just not qualified to comment on the history. That being said, I am going to comment on the amazing meal I had last week.

        Catahoula is brand new. It is a diamond in the rough in Queen Village. A friend of mine read about it on Thrillist, and suggested it for happy hour. Normally I would have deflected to something a little better known like Tinto or Tria, but I let her talk me into it. She promised me Cajun, and Cajun I had.

        I don't make it down to Queen Village very often, for whatever reason. But living in Old City, I really should. Catahoula was about a 15 minute walk, and definitely worth the trip. It's a teeny little restaurant, and beware, the awning out front still says "Saute." You walk in, and it screams "new restaurant," from its polished bar to stylish and sleek high tops lining the windows from the front door to the kitchen. It's not a big place by any means- its about the width and depth of your standard row house. It's sparsely decorated, without the kitschy southern decor of a place like NOLA. It doesn't "look" like it is going to serve you down home Southern cooking. It is deceiving.

        The menu is set up like tapas, with additional entrees (Big Papa Plates) and sides. My friend and I shared the New Orleans BBQ style shrimps with summer corn, scallions and griddled corn bread; and a side order of hushpuppies with truffled tarter sauce. I was expecting heartburn and overly spiced everything. I received a gift from God- that sounds melodramatic, but it's the truth.

        The shrimp came in a long, narrow white plate (the presentation screamed Asian fusion or Italian bistro), and there were about a dozen of them, plump and nestled among chunks of corn and a gorgeous sauce. One bite and I was hooked- the shrimps were juicy, and the corn sweet and crisp, and the sauce sang with butter and spices. It was so delicate that I knew I wouldn't have heartburn, and yet also homey and authentic, like someone's grandmother was in the kitchen cooking little plates for me. The cornbread was a fat chunk cooked in a slather of butter. Dipped in the BBQ shrimp sauce, it made my heart sing (and my arteries clog, I'm sure).

        Let me tell you a little bit about hushpuppies. My mom went to college in Charleston, SC, and when I was a kid my family went to Charleston and Folly Beach each summer. I am from the Midwest, but I feel that a part of my soul is Southern. I certainly love the food. I haven't been down to SC in many years, but I can still taste hushpuppies if I close my eyes, and sadly I haven't been able to find too many places that serve them up North. A good hushpuppy is a mouthfull-or-two-sized orb of sweet, cornmeal-ish dough, deep fried until the outside is brown and crispy and salty, and the inside fluffy and doughy and sweet. Ideally this should be served in a basket with some sort of breaded seafood (fried shrimp, oysters, clams, etc) on a dock overlooking an ocean or bay. They make me want to cry out of beauty and joy.

        The hushpuppies at Catahoula are a little different from the ones that I have tried. For one thing, they have chunks of corn in them, so they aren't the same smooth consistency. They are also served with truffled tarter sauce. These differences make for a spectacular hushpuppy. They are perfectly crispy and the inside texture is just right. The corn is an unexpected additional crunch and chew that goes well with the flavor and texture. They are not as sweet as the ones I have tried, but still have a hint of sweetness. The sauce. Ohmigod. The sauce. I love truffles. I love tartar sauce. This is such a good idea.

        Basically, it was an incredible little snacky meal. The waitress was wonderful, and very concerned and wanted to make sure that we were enjoying our meal. So she encouraged us to try their peach cobbler (that has some sort of crazy name that I wish I could remember). Oh dear god.

        Butter. Butter is what binds this place together. The flaky, creamy, buttery consistency of the dough part of the cobbler, combined with the delicately spiced, roasted peaches, topped with "too much" whipped cream (Their words not mine- really, too much whipped cream? These folks clearly have not met me). Words cannot describe.

        Drinks are 1/2 price before 7pm. All in all, we each paid $17 for enough food and beverage to fill us up and leave us happy. I will be going back, many times- mostly because I need to have an oyster po' boy and a Big Papa catfish basket in order to complete my life. Yessssssss.

        Listed in Under the Radar, Top Restaurants, Sarcone's, Of Course

        Have the fangbangers discovered Catahoula yet? When word gets around of its down-home Louisiana bites, True Blood soundtrack and stiff drinks, this place could be overrun with wannabe Sookehs and Beehls.

        It's only because the staff is so charming, and I dread the thought of them closing up and depriving me of my new hushpuppy addiction, that I am even yelping Catahoula.  Formerly the BYOB Sauté, the narrow room has been transformed with flatscreen TVs, a heavy wooden bar running along its length and just a few high-top tables.  A simple, even humble setting for some of the best Cajun food being plated north of  Morehouse Parish.

        Oysters fried to what only can be described as a fluffy texture were nestled in a nest of shredded lettuce and spicy remoulade; a soft-bite baguette finished off this po' boy worth a thousand shocked yummmmmms. A catfish version was just as nice, and the Phillies game-time special of a half-po' boy "slider" complete with fries for $5 is an amazing deal.

        The confited quacker leg topping the duck jambalaya ($18) recommended by the staff was absolutely luscious, its rice and sausage bed cooked to an ideal "al dente" bite. My virtuous chopped salad ($10) was generous with the goodies: soft goat cheese clouds, piquant pickled apples, halved cherry tomatoes, fresh corn and an herby viniagrette. Hushpuppies studded with more fresh corn kernels are a requirement of dining here; the truffled tartar sauce that accompanies them possibly the condiment of the Southern gods.

        A postprandial Sazerac cocktail screamed to be accompanied by something sweet from the dessert chalkboard menu.  We zeroed in on the "white chocolate bread pudding bananas foster"($7) after confirming with our server that it was indeed a single dessert.  

        Served hot in a gratin dish, this custardy glory was topped with a heap of caramelized bananas in their signature sticky fosters syrup and fresh whipped cream.  Beneath was one of the best bread puddings I have encountered, and I've had my share and yours too.  Based on brioche, this was an almost ethereal texture, especially compared to the dense bready bricks that pass for pudding elsewhere.  White chocolate does not normally hold much appeal for me, but this sugar bomb transcended preference.  It was, like Catahoula, a shockingly singular pleasure.

        I'm feeling as sense of nostalgia for time I've spent in Charleston, SC.  5 years of living on the coast gave my wife and I plenty of time to enjoy all the down-home southern food joints.

        Catahoula is a big hit - and it's on the "coast" = Front St.

        Great service, even in the midst of a Phils game  (one flatscreen tv).

        Happy hour from 5-7 (Order the hush puppies, you won't be disappointed)

        My burger (that's the name of the burger) tops my charts of burgers.  A brioche bun smothered in goat cheese and stacked with smokey bacon, the "icing" on the burger was the fried green tomato sealed in an envelope of cornmeal.  Truly delicious.

        My fellow foodie order the oyster po-boy, which failed to disappoint.  You could still taste the salt on the plump cornmeal fried oysters, like they had just been pulled from......the gulf?  Er, maybe PEI.  Nestled inside a lightly toasted french bread roll with a shmear of brick-red remoulade and shredded lettuce with tomatoes.  This, too, was a big hit.  My wife talked about finding the next flight to Charleston because she was so homesick.

        You can never go wrong with a neat Jameson, but a martini, on the other hand, can be a disaster.  Not at Catahoula.  What they lack in olive allocation they make up for in heavy handed pours.  I'd say a solid 8-10oz.

        If I had room for desert, the white chocolate bread pudding bananas foster would have been the choice.  

        I'm reserving one star for the day Catahoula announces an oyster roast - seeing as how you can really only have an oyster roast in months that have the letter R in them, this is prime time for that announcement.

        Arrive hungry and thirsty and leave stuffed and stumbling.

        Don't sleep on the drinks

        Front Street is rarely my first thought when planning a night out, and Catahoula is just far enough off the beaten South Street path that it seems off the radar. One look at the menu here and I figured I'd have to come back a few times. After eating, I knew for sure.

        The smaller eatery is all high tops, with two large televisions above the bar. It's a cozy feel, with big windows to let in the night and dimmed track lighting. Phoebe the bartender (more on that momentarily) mentioned that they're usually playing Pandora's Tom Waits Radio.

        There are a few great spots in Philly to grab a perfect cocktail, and Phoebe's drinks have definitely landed Catahoula on that list. Alongside the signature French 75's and Sazeracs, she offers a farmers market cocktail on Tuesdays. Recently it consisted of fig jam, bulleit and a few other magical ingredients that all added up to a sweet, bitter and savory drink I could have sipped on all night. She also obliged when I ordered 'something with rye,' creating the perfect post dinner cocktail. Don't sleep on the Sazerac too. It's just about perfect.

        So the drinks are deft and worth the price, but the food is solid too. Started out with a crawfish bisque which was smooth and spicy, followed by some oysters.
