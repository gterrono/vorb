    Disclaimer: My ratings are based on value compared to other restaurants of the same cuisine and of a similar price point.  A $10 meal could very well score 5 stars if the restaurant delivered excellent value.  Similarly, a $100 meal could score 1 star if the food was dismal compared to its similarly-priced brethren.  For the most part, my ratings will be based on the quality of the food, unless I explicitly comment on the service or the decor.

    This review is for lunch for two on September 6, 2011.

    In this case, my reference points will be the following:
    - dinner at Talula's on September 5, 2011 (the Morimoto meal ended up ~$140 before tip while the Talula's meal was ~$120 before tip)
    - my countless visits to Morimoto Philadelphia in the 2002-2006 time frame when I was studying in the city

    I hadn't been back to Morimoto's in 5 years since I graduated.  I had fond memories of the place and, on a weekend trip back to Philly, decided to stop by for old time's sake.  This visit exemplified the phrase "resting on its laurels".

    Let me start by saying that the waitress was wonderful.  She made the best of a terrible situation.  We ordered the omakase and sat at the sushi bar.  The mistakes were numerous:

    - the tartare to start was swimming in sauce; not an auspicious start but maybe most of Morimoto's American clientele like it that way.
    - seared carpaccio was okay, probably one of the better executed dishes that day
    - salad, oversauced (I actually liked the sauce but there was just too much of it)
    - scallops, overcooked
    - dessert was child's play, especially compared to the wonderful desserts at Talula's the night before

    But the two cardinal sins, the ones that really made me angry:
    - The chef lied to my face and said that they didn't have hon-wasabi that day; I was used to paying an extra $10 before for the treat and had resigned myself to the fact that they were out-of-stock... later, I saw the chef pull out a wasabi root (!!!) from under a cloth in the sushi bar, inspect it, and put it back on the sushi bar under the cloth.  We were sitting right in front of him.  Did he think I didn't know what a wasabi root looked like???
    - The sushi course included a broken piece of fish (see the photo).  No self-respecting chef would have served that piece of nigiri.  Usually, one would just scrap it to include in a tartare later.  The chef served it directly to us with a straight face.  He must have noticed the broken fish.  He didn't even attempt to cover it.  That was pretty shameless and a clear symbol of everything that was wrong with the meal that day.  

    Conclusion: The kitchen lacks standards and discipline.  I don't care that Morimoto has a half dozen restaurants to his name now and Philadelphia is playing the role of a forsaken wife in favor of newer mistresses in Hawaii and Napa.  I don't care that this was lunch and the "B-team" was playing.  The kitchen clearly lacked even the most basic pride in its work.  

    Compare this to Talula's, where we had dinner the night before.  The lamb was only slightly overcooked, a fact that was more than compensated for by the wonderful vegetables.  The waiter apologized profusely, offered to have the kitchen remake the dish (which we refused) and comped one of the desserts (which we accepted though we were going to order two desserts anyway).

    Morimoto probably still has the freshest fish in the city but I can't recommend the restaurant anymore.  Head to NYC for your high-end sushi fix.  The Morimoto here is but a sad shadow of its former self.

    The review would be horribly long if I went into the details of every course of the omakase, but the highlights included oysters in the shell three ways (perfecto), sashimi (octopus is normally something we avoid, but this left us wanting more) and the Kobe beef (everyone at the table tried this at some point, and there was stealing involved!).  The beverage omakase highlights for us were the "Cabin in the Woods" sake and the Sangiovese.  This is exactly why we chose the omakase - we found ourselves liking and wanting more of things we would never, ever order normally.

    The diners in our party range from hard-to-please picky teenagers to "rustic" fried food lovers, to poultry-only and so on.  Who would have thought a single restaurant would please us all?  The only disappointment was when the experience was over.

    I feel like I didn't do this place justice when I reviewed it in two sentences last time. I've been here about 4 or 5 times now and each experience has topped the last one. I don't t know if it was the increase in alcohol, or the company I was with, but the enjoyment I had there most recently remains clear and distinct in my mind.

    I can outline for you every dish that's worth eating and those that aren't even worth your time. See it's important at this restaurant to spend your allowance wisely, you could be spending $42 on the most delicious dish you've had in a long while (Ishi Yaki Bop w/ Kobe Beef) or you could be wasting it on decent sushi that one could find at any other restaurant.

    I feel the best way to describe my experience would be to outline my Top 5 dishes. So here goes:

    1) Ishi Yaki Bop w/ Kobe Beef: Don't skimp out on this dish, it's packed with flavor, finished with a soy dressing and includes nori and a nice fresh egg on top.... and then SLATHERED with nice cuts of tender filet

    2) Black Cod Miso: $24, the most wonderful, flaky, miso-glazed piece of cod. Perfect texture and seasoning. Simple dish in comparison with the others, but always a favorite of mine

    3) Kobe Beef Carpaccio: $25, this isn't like any other carpaccio you've tasted. And it's certainly not Italian by any stretch of the imagination. These thin cuts of raw beef are dressed in a subtle yet tangy dressing that makes your mouth water and crave for more. Pure.

    4) Duck Duck Duck: $33 It's difficult to prepare duck without making it taste "gamey," but with this dish you get it cooked perfectly in three different ways. It might sound repetitive, but the difference in flavors and textures makes it seem like you're eating three different dishes.

    5) Rock Shrimp Tempura: $18.5 awesomely sweet and sour glazed shrimp pieces that have been lightly fried in a japanese batter. Eat it with the leaves they provide u with. It dulls the richness of the fried flavor slightly.

    As for dessert, there are two that seriously blew my mind. They are:

    The Chocolate Pot de Creme: OH my god... i can't begin to describe the pure joy in eating this at the end of a meal. It's creamy, not overly sweet, chocolatey, and HEAVY as hell :P

    Match Tres Leches: A great asian spin-off of the classic Spanish dessert, with the colors and flavor of japanese Matcha (green tea)

    So there it is. When you decide to take a trip over to this Iron Chef's restaurant, you'll be fully informed as to what will be sure to make your night ;)

    5/5

Meet the Business Owner

Stephen S. Business Owner

Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District. STARR Restaurants expanded to Atlantic City, NJ, with Buddakan and The Continental located in the Pier Shops at Caesars in 2006, and to Florida with Steak 954 at the W Hotel Fort Lauderdale in 2009, and Makoto in the Bal Harbour Shops in 2011. For more information, please visit www.starr-restaurant.com.
