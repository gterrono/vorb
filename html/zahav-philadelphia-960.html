        My wife and I adore Middle Eastern and Israeli food. Our favorite restaurant at home in New York is Balaboosta, an establishment similar to Zahav. So we knew this was where we wanted to go for our one night out in Philadelphia.

        It was quite good. Not great, but quite good. Not as good as Balaboosta, though, and it was hard not to compare the two places.

        We really enjoyed the salads that came with the hummus and lavash. I especially liked the spicy chick peas. The hummus had a wonderfully smoky flavor that went great with the bread straight from the oven. I probably would have been happy making hummus, bread and salads my meal.

        None of the small plates quite lived up to that beginning, though everything was certainly was quite tasty. We really liked the zucchini served over baba gannoush, and special bonus points to the smoked sable on challah. It reminded of the Yom Kippur break fast, but much, much better.

        All in all, we'd definitely return. But our heart remains with Balaboosta.

        A restaurant whose name is (the Hebrew word for) gold creates the specter of high standards and higher expectations.  In this case, even overzealous expectations were most definitely exceeded, as nothing we tasted (and we tasted roughly half of the items on the menu) fell short of sublime.   What's more, the day after our visit, a well-timed column by Frank Bruni of the New York Times shed intriguing light on Michael Solomonov, the culinary genius behind Zahav, whose intense passion, exuberant creativity, and abundant skill are ingredients in each dish.

        Zahav offers a "tayim," essentially a tasting menu that allows you to rove ravenously from one section of the menu to the other.  A no-brainer for our party.  The first arrivals were six fresh, gloriously colorful and insanely delicious salads, along with perfectly creamy and nutty hummus, and two hot spheres of laffa bread, a chewy, understandably addictive delight popular in street food in Jerusalem.  All of the salads were superb, but the three that stood out most were the Moroccan carrots, the tabbouleh, and especially the salt-roasted beets pureed with tahini, lemon juice, and mint.  We couldn't get enough.

        Our mezze selections were similarly divine.  Our favorite was the fried cauliflower, which somehow managed to be crisp on the outside and piping hot on the inside with virtually no residual oil.  The chive, dill, mint, and garlic labahen (a creamy yogurt) on which the cauliflower rested was lovely.  The colorful heirloom tomatoes were refreshingly light, set off by crispy black lentils, and served over tzatziki made with corn and cucumbers.  The magical, mysterious alchemy of this dish came from the spare and subtle application of herbs and spices, as it did with countless other dishes we tried.  The most unique mezze was an extra-thick piece of toasted challah crowned by tender smoked sable and poppy seeds.  Our server dramatically sliced into the challah after setting down the dish, releasing the yolk of a soft fried egg buried somehow inside.  

        The menu proceeds to a host of Al Ha'esh dishes, grilled to perfection over coals.  The branzino done in this fashion was perhaps our favorite single taste of the night.  The bright white fillet, crisped skin facing upward, balanced elegantly atop chickpeas and peas with more labaneh and turmeric.  Senses were catapulted instantly into overdrive with the complexity of this dish, an inestimably terrific interpretation of one of our favorite fishes.

        Then came the struggle: first, to pick only two of the magnificent desserts, and then, to create room for even a couple of bites of each.  We settled on a traditional Israeli dish consisting of a square of layered crispy filo threads stuffed with ricotta and surrounded by strawberries and white chocolate crumbles.  The second dessert was a flour-less brownie topped with almond mile ice cream and accompanied by a cigar of almond baklava.  Both of these dishes could have been award winners, and then a third was brought our way courtesy of our diligent and charismatic servers who heard us debating our choices.  The grand finale was chocolate halvah mousse, a decadent, thick but creamy pudding with a chocolate quotient sufficient to send even hard core chocoholics into a trance.

        This is one of those places where you look to reserve your next meal before you actually surrender your seat.  I am also eager to try Solomonov's new hummusiya across town:  quite the little empire.

        I had been dying to go to Zahavs for a long long time as I always have a craving for good Israeli food after being in Israel. I brought my mother here and we were certainly not let down.

        The food is served tapas style so you can try a variety of different offerings. My mom and I both ordered the Tayim which is essentially a sampler meal deal (appetizers, entres, dessert). We started off with a grilled pita type bread and warm chickpea hummus (there were about 6 you could choose from) which was unbelievable. So creamy and fresh.  Next we got the salatim which are salads. There was a cucumber tomato salad. A tobouleh salad (bulgur wheat, parsley, tomato, onion), some sort of beet salad, spicy green bean salad, a carrot salad, and a roasted eggplant salad. My favorites were eggplant, the spicy green bean, and the tabbouleh. Everything was so fresh and well flavored. Emphasis on the eggplant though, outstanding.

        For the next course, we each ordered 2 mezzes. We got fried cauliflower which came with a creamy mint sauce (outstanding and a highlight on their menu). I don't even like cauliflower and I couldn't stop eating it. We also tried the crispy grape leaves (unbelievably delicious). I looks like a grape leave but it tasted to me like a sausage. We also got the house smoked sable that came over challah bread with a fried egg. Awesome awesome awesome. If you're a bagel and lox type of person you need to get this. Lastly we got the Yemenite beef stew. The beef was cooked perfectly and melted in your mouth. Simple flavors but delicious nonetheless.

        We then each ordered one al ha'esh menu item. (Off the grill) we got the kofte which is a lamb/beef meatball and the lamb merges. Both were very good but salty. I normally love very salty things but the kofte was a bit TOO salty. We spoke to our waiter just to give him a heads up about it and the chef even came over and apologized and thanked us for letting him know.

        Finally we made it to dessert,
        My mom ordered the cantelope sorbet which was so refreshing and tasted like you were actually eating the fruit. I ordered the konafi which was a ricotta, rhubarb, and strawberry dessert with white chocolate walnut bark on top. It was out of this world outstanding. I've never tried anything like it. A must get. The chef also gave us a complimentary dessert "for something sweet to balance out the salty". It was a coconut cake soaked in something ( maybe honey) but very very good. Reminded me of a rum cake.

        Overall one of the best meals I've had not to mention the wait staff are so polite and attentive. Cannot wait to come back again soon!!!

        Zahav hosted my rehearsal dinner a few weeks ago and everything was PHENOMENAL! I have always loved the food at Zahav, so I was very excited about booking them for the rehearsal dinner. They have many rooms available for reservation at no additional cost. Our room was able to seat up to 50 guests, and to reserve it there is a food & drink minimum of $3,000. This may seem like a lot, but almost every other restaurant I looked into for the rehearsal dinner had a hefty fee to reserve a private room (some required several thousand to book the room, plus the cost for food and drink!). I had 30 guests for dinner, and then an additional 20 came for drinks afterwards. They let you book the room for the entire night (6pm-closing), and they let us use their projector and screen.

        Everyone was incredibly impressed by the food and exceptional service. We had several guests with dietary restrictions (some vegan, gluten-free). Before the meal they asked each guest about dietary restrictions and were EXTREMELY accommodating and pointed out to each guest what they could eat. Many of our guests said it was the best meal they have ever had!

        The size of our dinner ended up being perfect for the food & drink minimum. Everyone ate tons of food (and we had lots of leftovers, which they wrapped up for us) and drank as much as they wanted, and the bill ended up being very close to $3,000. At the end of the night, when paying the bill I failed to notice that they had already included gratuity in the total. I was a bit overwhelmed (which happens the day before your wedding...) and tipped them an additional 20% on the final balance. They actually called me later that night to clarify to me that the bill already included gratuity, and they let me modify my additional gratuity-- thank goodness, that saved me $500!!

        I would highly recommend Zahav for any private event-- I am sure your guests will be very impressed!

        We brought my parents here to have an early celebration of Jewish New Year, and I ordered some of my previous favorites for the table: Haloumi with dates, and the Cauliflower, and of course the salatim and hummus.. The spread of interesting salads is always a great way to start and get people excited about the meal. This is my favorite part of Israeli cuisine: the little salads with fresh produce and exotic spices.

        Among our other dishes (and the restaurant week menu allows a table of four to try basically 20+ different things), the favorites were the potatoes, the sable, the lamb merguez, the duck kebabs, and the halvah mousse dessert.

        Zahav is really a dining experience, and I love that it shows the diversity of Israeli food. I think you could come back here 10 times and still be excited by the surprising flavors, delightful ingredients, and of course the friendly service.

        Hummus with Laffa

        A flawlessly smooth hummus with a nice balance of tahini, citrus and garlic. The laffa was also so much more than a tasty eating vessel. It was crisp around the edges, chewy in the middle and deliciously seasoned with aromatic spice.

        Yemenite Beef Stew
        Roasted Short Ribs, Potatoes

        A blessedly tender short rib in a comforting rich broth. This dish reminded me the stews my mom raised me on.

        Pastilla
        Spiced Chicken, Apricot, Almond

        After having pastilla in Israel, I literally dreamt of the dish constantly ever since and was determined to find it done well in North America. Well, Zahav, you did not disappoint. This was definitely one of my favourite dishes of the night because I loved the balance of savoury and sweet. The phyllo was crispy and light, was packed with wonderfully flavourful meat and was absolutely delicious smeared through the apricot puree.

        Fried Cauliflower
        Labneh with Chive, Dill, Mint & Garlic

        Another favourite dish so I'm not surprised our server said it's a top seller. The cauliflower were seasoned generously and fried to a light and crispy consistency, absolutely brilliant when swooshed through the herbaceous tangy labneh.

        Crispy Haloumi
        Dates, Pickled Onions, Walnuts, Urfa

        Imagine a salty firm cheese but fried to golden crispy nirvana like crazy-addictive chicken nuggets. But wait, it gets better when paired with the sweet date puree and the slightly bite of the pickled onions.

        House Smoked Sable
        Challah, Fried Egg, Poppy

        Do-not-miss-this-dish. Holy hell. Some of the most fantastic challah I've ever tasted, I wished I could just order a loaf to go. But pair that ethereal goodness with a smoky sweet fish and a luscious runny yolk and I officially prayed to G-d for a second stomach.

        Duck Kebab
        Dirty Rice, Rhubarb, English Peas, Summer Squash

        My favourite of the two main courses, only because I apparently cant get enough of that Middle Eastern combination of savoury and sweet. The duck meat was juicy and beautifully seasoned and irresistible with a smear of tangy rhubarb and the pop of the fresh sweet peas.

        Lamb Merguez
        Carrot Puree, English Pea Harissa

        In contrast, this dish offered a nice level of heat to cut the richness and fat, tempered down by the comforting carrot puree. A wicked choice for anyone who likes a little kick.

        Konafi
        Ricotta, Rhubarb, Strawberry, White Chocolate

        My favourite dessert of the three. I loved the creamy mild filling against the light crispy texture of the shredded phyllo, and the sweet fresh fruit and white chocolate bits. We were at the end of the meal, so I gave myself permission to polish this baby off.

        Vanilla Custard
        Poached Banana, Pistachio, Cardamom

        I usually don't order custards or panna cottas as sometimes they have gelatins or too much gelatin, which I find unpleasant. This version, however, was an absolute pleasant to let melt into my tongue. The custard was satiny smooth with a nice aromatic level of fresh vanill....
