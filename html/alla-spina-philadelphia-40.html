    Alla Spina is a great introduction to the Vetri experience, without the high price tag.  The tonnato stuffed bell peppers are a great opening bite.  The smooth tuna filling is rich but gets offset by the roasted peppers slight bite of texture and sweetness.  Caper berries top each pepper to add a hint of saltiness.  I opted for the sandwich route, with the new sausage cheesesteak served on a pretzel roll.  The sandwich is extremely cheesy and quite delicious, if not just a tad greasy.  I also had the swordfish "blt" with basil and guanciale.  The fish had a nice firm texture, but did get slightly lost with the rest of the ingredients.  Overall it was a delicious sandwich, but i wouldn't have known it was swordfish without the menu.  Stick with the salt and vinegar chips, they're amazing.  Alla Spina offers beer flights as well (half pours of a number of beers) which allows you to sample a nice variety.  They seem to group them by region (east coast flight and west coast flight) for a reasonable price ($13). I didn't try and of the sweets, but a few came past while i was eating and they looked fantastic.  Reservations were at 7:30, and the restaurant wasn't quite full, so it looks like Alla Spina might still be a hidden gem.

    My boyfriend had the fried chicken and that was solid. Had a maple glaze on it, and it was really crispy. I had the gnocchi, which was surprisingly good (the menu is so hap hazard that I wasn't sure how it would go). They were pillowy and melted in your mouth the way gnocchi should.     I think this restaurant has the potential to be great. The neighborhood is slowly turning around (Starr is right next door...so it's only a matter of time), and the food is good.  The menu is all over the place, so I think after about a year of business they can hone in on what is selling, and find a better focus.     I went to Alla Spina with friends on Saturday night and was really looking forward to seeing what Vetri was up to with the whole "Italian Gastropub" idea.  That task turned out to be a bit more complicated than I thought it would be: my table spent a good amount of time breaking down the menu, trying to figure out how to build a meal from the variety of options presented.  It seemed like the selection of items lacked a consistent theme or trend.  This is kind of hard to explain.  

    There were plenty of good items to choose from, but  I feel like if you heard about AS from two different people, without knowledge of the 'concept', you'd think that they ate at two completely different places.  This wasn't a big deal to the group, but it just struck us as odd as it didn't seem like there was something holding the idea together.

    In the end we went with the beer pretzels, beef fries, baked oysters, and truffle/mushrooms for apps.  The fries and pretzels were barely warm and  the oysters were rubbery... not the best start.  For entrees we had gnocchi, the swordfish BLT, and the friend chicken... The gnocchi was delicious and the friend chicken was very good, but the BLTs were sub par.  It just seemed like they had sat out for too long before coming to our table, as they were pretty soggy on the bottom loaf of bread.  

    Throw in the fact that our service was less than stellar (long waits for drinks, missed drink orders, long waits between courses, sub-par apologies for said waits by the manager) and it was a pretty disappointing experience.

    I'd like to give it a second chance, as its a cool bar and they had some great sounding items on menu that might be better, but considering the price for what you are ordering, it will probably be after I make the rounds of some of the better gastropubs in the city.

    The poutine was memorable - the fries were excellent, with a sumptuous sauce that left us scraping the plate with the (pita?!) bread they served with the meal.

    My last review of Alla Spina was right when it opened, and the food warranted three stars.  In good news, the food has improved.  In bad news, the improvements were not as enormous as I'd hoped, and the service seems to have seriously devolved.

    THE FOOD:
    So first let's talk about the high points:  The sweet, crispy, incredibly good maple-glazed fried chicken had me in heaven.  The fries cooked in beef fat were liberally salted and incredibly good.  I definitely did some damage to that cone of deliciousness.

    The poutine is middle-of-the-road for me.  Maybe I'm a poutine purist, but to me the guinea hen gravy is way too much; the richness completely overshadows the cheese curd and fries.  People love it, and I understand why, but I want this to be a french fry dish and not a vessel for hen gravy.

    On the negative side: I did not enjoy the involtini with pig trotters, hot sauce and onion jam.  This was quite spicy, which was unexpected based on the nonexistent heat on the rest of the dishes I've had in my two trips here (and I like heat).  I didn't finish this and I wouldn't order it again.  I also was not in love with the pork belly and melon salad.  The pork belly itself wasn't fatty enough for me, and just didn't have that richness that comes with all those delicious layers of fat, which made it feel overcooked and dry, even if it was not.  It went well with the melon, but the belly itself hurt it for me.

    THE SERVICE:
    By far the worst part of our meal was the service.  I've never seen anything quite like it.  Our table of six ended up waiting for about an extra half an hour at the bar, watching food go by.  No problem, we had a drink and made do.  But by the time we sat down and ordered... the dish two of us ordered (and had seen parade by us 10 times as we waited) was completely sold out (the sausage cheesesteak on a pretzel roll, since you asked).  Not the end of the world, but not a great start to the night, to run out of what we wanted while we were waiting to sit down.

    And then things got bad.

    Our appetizers came out, and then immediately (maybe three minutes later) out came two of our five main courses (one fried chicken and one burger).  WAY too fast, we hadn't even gotten into the apps yet.  The other three main courses?  They didn't come until we'd finished all the appetizers AND both of the main courses that had already been served.  The waitress stopped by to ask if we were waiting on any more food.  Well, yes, these three people with nothing in front of them are waiting for quite a bit of food.  Worse: two of those three missing entrees were another fried chicken and another burger, exact duplicates of what had been delivered far too early 20 minutes earlier!  So we ate in uncomfortable shifts.  Then, when the last three entrees were almost gone... out came our last appetizer. What?

    Bizarre, terrible service.  What's worse, our server said nothing about it.  Did not acknowledge the terrible timing of everything all night, did not apologize for clearly forgetting four of our nine dishes until everything else was just about finished, and did not do anything to help compensate us for the awkward weirdness of the whole night.  Maybe don't charge us for that appetizer that came out after everyone was done their main courses?  Toss in a round of drinks?  Anything at all to acknowledge that I could have eaten a more orderly meal at an IHOP without dropping hundreds of dollars on it?

    So sadly I am left giving a worse review on the my second visit.  The stars above are for an *excellent* fried chicken and some great fries... but I'm sad to not have been able to give more.  The service had a seriously negative impact on the night.

    Alla Spina is Marc Vetri's latest and most accessible venture yet. It is also his least impressive. It is difficult to categorize where the let down occurs. The space is bustling and cool and very put together, which is perhaps why it doesn't feel that cool to be inside. It has a Stephen Starr sense of cool, one which is forever destined to not actually be cool at all, simply because of its creator. Starr has some beautiful restaurants but they all feel so researched and surveyed and designed to appeal to a certain type of feeling. And Alla Spina suffers a similar fate, it should be interesting and funky, but because the goal was to be interesting and funky, it just feels fake.

    Perhaps it is in the service which on a Friday night was poor, though on a Tuesday was much more attentive. It is hard to judge a restaurant in a time of crush, but given that most diners are only free to eat out on the weekends, the ability with which a staff can handle a crush is of some importance. A full house clearly weighs heavily on these blue tee shirt clad hipsters, as water was scarcer than our server, who took our drink and dinner order at the same time after twenty minutes of waiting, and delivered the drinks with the first course. Was there no one else available to run some wine and beer?

    The beverage list is interesting and long and offers a decent value. It is put together with care and a nod to the birthplace of the restaurant, and offers a nice array of interesting local and imported beer and wine, all meant to be paired with the bar-centric food menu.

    On one visit I sampled smaller offerings including the baked oysters which were overpowered by toppings and they're crusty exterior. The tiny shelled creature was lost in dough and crust, and flavors to powerful for it to fight. An oyster is best alone, served in his own home. The pretzel was a pretzel which is hard to argue with though it had a nice crispy exterior. I was driven to wondered if they doused it in lye to achieve it old school style, or if they used the newer, safer baking soda trick. The spicy beer cheese was novel and delicious, if not a touch overpowering and heavy. I am one to always sample the deviled egg offerings,with the knowledge that none will ever measure up to my all time favorite belonging to the Spotted Pig in New York City. This porcini endeavor at Alla Spina was a valiant effort but was in the end bogged down by over salting and a too oily texture. A deviled egg with no texture makes for a rather unsatisfying mouth feel. The mortadella hot dogs, were as promised, magnificent in their simplicity and honesty; a simple, well done product meant purely for enjoyment and not awe.

    On the next visit, my fateful Friday evening, I went in for the larger items. For a starter my date and I shared the taleggio stuffed, fried polenta which was severely disappointing.     The struggles of Alla Spina are perhaps heightened by the name on the front door. Vetri has such a following and reputation now that even his low end gastropub is held to higher standards. If Alla Spina was the first try by a new chef or restaurateur it would be judged under a completely different, and notably dimmer, light. But Alla Spina is run by Marc Vetri, and for that reason for good and bad, it is always packed. It does not live up to his name, but it also doesn't have to necessarily. The hoards will come no matter what and get lost in the catchy soundtrack and good if not great food.  They come and they come, and eat and drink, and maybe they leave disappointed, but really thats not the point, the point, though I was disappointed, is to experience a bit of Vetri without having to shell out a paycheck for it. Its good food, in a good atmosphere with nothing horrible and nothing great. Alla Spina is Marc Vetri's latest, and most accessible restaurant. Sadly, it is also his least impressive.

    I love that great restaurants are bravely opening in this area. It gives me hope that one day North Broad will finally crawl entirely out of its current blightness.

    I've been here several times for happy hour, and a couple of times to eat. The late night happy hour here is a real winner. The draft list always has at least three things that I REALLY want to drink and have trouble deciding between, and they do bar totally snacks right. The appetizers are unique - my boy and I usually can't resist ordering the little bowl of straight up delicious sauteed mushrooms that they offer. It's so good, it doesn't need anything else. Happy hour puts some of the really good craft brews at $3, and some really GREAT ones at $5-6.

    There is a super freaky face staring down at you if you sit in the lounge area. Check it out. Outdoor seating is very dog friendly and not overrun by pedestrians or overwhelmed by the din of traffic on Broad, somehow. There's a more formal dining section, a bunch of booths, and a really comfortable, roomy bar.

    Service was excellent during all but one of my visits. The bartender was having a particularly sassy "let's ignore these customers while staring straight at them for 20 minutes" kind of day. But that was only once. Usually the bartenders and servers are prompt, informative, and unintrusive. I'd also like to high-five whoever has their ipod on - the background music is always spot on.

    So glad Vetri decided to open a place this quality that's still accessible to, you know, working class lushes like me on a regular weeknight.
