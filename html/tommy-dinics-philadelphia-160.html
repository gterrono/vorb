    [nostalgia]

    I used to love Tommy DiNic's.  When I lived in Philly, this was one of my regular pit-stops in Center City.  Smack in the heart of Reading Terminal Market, DiNic's carnivorous concoctions were the source of much gastronomic delight (and elevated cholesterol levels).  I'd stop by DiNic's at least once per month.

    [/nostalgia]

    In the intervening years, though, a lot has changed.  When I stopped by other other day for a quick lunch, I saw that my plans for a speedy meal were averted by a line stretching halfway across the entire market.  The friendly, cheery staff were gone, replaced by folks who barely look up from the cash register while taking your order.

    I wasn't going to let that stop me from having one of their sandwiches, though.  

    After muscling my way to a seat at the counter, I ordered a roast beef sub with provolone and broccoli rabe -- two of my favorite traditional fixins.  The turn-around was prompt but gave a clear "eat faster and get the hell out of here" vibe.

    The sandwich itself was completely average.  The roast beef was flavorful and juicy, but it was a little tough.  The broccoli rabe was bright and fresh, but I thought it was bitter and undercooked.  A generous dollop of spicy horseradish helped temper the bitterness, thankfully.  It still didn't taste anything like the DiNic's I used to know, though.

    This begs the question: What changed?  DiNic's food or my palate?

    Maybe both?

    I'll leave that question to the philosophers.

    When asked to name the food that is most representative of Philadelphia, most people will say cheesesteaks, and, prior to coming to Philly for college, I must say that I would have said the same. However, after spending a couple of years going to school there, I learned to embrace the roast pork sandwich and all of its meaty goodness. While there are plenty of roast pork purveyors in Philly, DiNic's is one of the most acclaimed. Based on the positive reviews from the media and my fellow Yelpers, I decided to check it out for myself, and I must say that I was not disappointed. I went with the standard sandwich of roast pork, broccoli rabe, and sharp provolone, and I must say, I was completely blown away. The roast pork was ridiculously succulent and juicy, and, when combined with the salty, garlicky greens and the sharp provolone, it created a balanced, harmonious sandwich that was more than the sum of its parts. It's a pretty big sandwich (I'd estimate it as being about 14 inches long), but the sandwich was so delicious that I put it away in less than 10 minutes.

    Because of both its reputation and its location in the touristy Reading Terminal Market, DiNic's often draws a long line of people, but don't let this deter you, as the line moves pretty rapidly. And if you are intent on getting a sandwich, make sure to arrive early, as I have heard that DiNic's often sells out their sandwiches by the late afternoon. If you are ever in Philadelphia, definitely give DiNic's a visit; I assure you that you won't be disappointed.
