    Few places combine value, taste, and ingenuity like Resurrection, and few restaurants leave me this excited to go back.  Situated down a long dark stretch of Grays Ferry Ave, Resurrection is located a ways away from the bustling restaurants around Rittenhouse and Fitler Sq.  Good thing too, the dark and quiet environment dissuades the less adventurous well-to-do crowds from clogging this place, and the neighborhood keeps the prices down.  

    The restaurant itself isn't too big.  With a nice bar and about 10 tables total, it's more conducive to intimate dinners and drinks than other establishments like Parc.  Resurrection has a bit of a Brooklyn hipster feel, but it doesn't take itself too seriously.  While soccer plays on the TV, the music was definitely 90's top 40's.  The combination makes the place feel almost homey and definitely welcoming.  I didn't want to leave.  As the name suggests, there is a great selection of beer here.  The waiter was very knowledgable about each one, and we tried two we'd never heard of - Baltic Thunder and V12.  Both were delicious and only $5 each!!  

    For our meal, we chose the pan-seared cod and the tagliatelle with fried duck egg.  I was served two large pieces of perfectly cooked cod.  It was moist, flaky, and extremely fresh.  It came on a bed of lightly sauteed celery and citrus slices, with a creamy almond sauce on top.  Well-balanced, subtle, and delicious.  My bf's pasta came tossed in brown butter, duck fat, pine nuts, and sage.  It was so aromatic and decadent, my bf really cleaned his plate (no pine nut left behind).  

    I really fell in love with this place.  For those willing to venture out of the main Center City stretch and walk down some less than well-lit streets, the rewards are incredible.  This is definitely my most exciting culinary discovery in awhile.

    Burger (with swiss and pickled onions, on a challah roll).  I disliked the challah roll, the burger didn't need any extra richness.  The pickled onions were nice, BUT it didn't come with anything else - no tomato, no lettuce, no raw onion.   I asked for raw tomato and they didn't even have any.  It came with a sauce made from ketchup, mayo, mustard, and curry seasoning.  It tasted ok, vaguely interesting.  The meet was very nice, slightly over-seasoned.  When you're served a burger like that you get the impression that the chef believes he knows what's best for you.  No toppings.  It only comes with swiss cheese, they don't bring standard ketchup or mustard unless you ask.  It's like the chef is saying "you don't need to modify this burger because it's amazing and perfect the way it is."  WRONG.  Burgers are made to be modified, that's what they are, they are personalizable devices for a reason, and everyone has their own well-established set of preferred burger modifications.  To simply refuse to accommodate these preferences is the height of egoism, and in this case, misguided egoism because the burger wasn't even that good.

    Fries.  The fries were really good - shoe string and crispy.

    Deep fried chick pea sandwich with roasted peppers, fennel, and green goddess dressing.  The dressing needed more acidity, and I would have smushed up the chick peas - it was an open faced sandwich and they rolled off the bread.  The bread was nice and chewy.

    Halumi and arugula salad with fig jam, pickled beets and mint.  This was good, arugula was fresh, the fig jam was perfect.  Nicely balanced dish.
