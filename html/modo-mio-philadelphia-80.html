    This is the place in South Philly for delicious Italian food.  The seared scallops and prosciutto appetizer (sesame vinaigrette gave it a tangy yet nutty twist) was wonderful, as were the mussels in red sauce (slightly spicy giving them an extra bite).  The bucatini amatriciana (hollow spaghetti with pancetta and tomato, topped with Romano) was very bold and slightly spicy - the pasta was very tender and the Romano complemented the tomato-based sauce perfectly without making the dish too cheesy.  The gnocchi was a perfect blend of spice and cream, with rock shrimp as an addition.  I had the skate wing braised with caper butter, which was delicously creamy, and tried the pork loin, which was succulent and flavorful (topped with gorgonzola).  The panna cotta was very rich and creamy.  I was stuffed but had to finish it because it was that good.  For the appetizer, pasta, entree, and dessert, it was ~$33/person.  Service was very attentive yet not overbearing.  Definitely recommend.  Just make sure that you have reservations.

    This was a great BYOB cash only spot just outside of Northern Liberties that also has a great authentic Italian style and feel. Modo Mio made me feel like I was eating in Italy again, with its 4 course "turista" style menu. It is uncommon to find a place in America that bases their restaurant around this longer, more enjoyable style of eating, with smaller portions and more courses.  The food was modern Italian with a little French influence, and it did not disappoint. The menu had alot of different options, including several vegetarian/ non-meat entrees for all you herbivores.  AND the best part is that its only 33 bucks per person.

    I had scallops with carmelized strawberries, proscuitto and arugala to start which was delicious (the woman next to me was complaining too salty but she was full of it). My date had an italian style crabcake that was also very good.

    For the pasta course i had homemade ravioli stuffed with rabbit and veal which melted in my mouth. all the pasta and the bread was made in house which is another plus in my book.

    for the secondi i had the sautee'd sweetbreads (the waiter asked me if i knew what they were which i thought was hilarious) that were doused in an orange almost chinese style duck sauce and pancetta. they were delicious coming from someone who really enjoys sweetbreads, and has definitely had some bad ones. My date had the eggplant stuffed with tomato and ricotta that was enormous and delicious.

    The desserts were the only thing that were sub par. We had this strange watermelon-mint panna cotta concoction that was not good at all, but the chocolate tart was decent.

    Overall awesome place to take a date for a nice meal that is affordable where you will get a lot of good quality italian bang for your buck.

    Listed in Philly Foodie/Drinky List

    Modo Mio is my star of Italian food in Philly. While I'm the kinda guy who tends towards quantity over quality, the dishes I ordered at Modo Mio brought me to my knees despite their rather small portions. The food was well prepared, presented, and also delicious -- I've never tasted Italian cuisine with so much hidden flavor and zest inside -- well worth a try, and not too expensive for a college kid.

    The place is a BYO, fyi. The bread they served us went really well with the olive oil, and helped fill me up ahead of time. The service is also on point there -- fast and friendly -- our waiter was the man.

    I'm a scallop lover usually, but the crab and ricotta cake for the antipasti was beyond words. I've never had such a perfectly balanced and tasty crab cake in my life. It was rather small though, so if you're looking for more volume, I'd go with the mussels.

    For the pasta I got the veal and rabbit ravioli -- this was, again, the tastiest pasta I've had in my life -- noodles cooked just right, sauce wasn't too chunky or heavy, and my tastebuds flipped when I first tasted it. Wish there was more veal (or rabbit) in it and the dish was slightly larger, but that's alright.

    Last of all, I got the grilled skirt steak for the second entree. Not the best steak I've ever had, but it was still great -- juicy and marinated really well. My friends there also had the pork loin, which was also good -- tender, but with a unique taste (they add an orange twist to it) -- and the skate wing was also delicious.

    It can get noisy in there because of how popular it is, so perhaps not the best place for an intimate dinner, but it's a great BYO for friends. I wouldn't go there every week, but worth showing off to food connoisseur friends when they come to town.

    8/12/2010

    I visited Modo Mio on 8/11/2010 with 4 others.  I was really torn about how many stars to give it and wound up giving it 3 because of some service issues.  Decor was not particularly memorable, fairly casual.

    Service:  Our waiter made good suggestions.  We asked for additional bread 5 or 6 times (we really like bread), and they never called us fat or greedy.  They also brought out more olive oil and ricotta once after the first round and didn't charge us, which was a pleasant semi-surprise.  However, the timing on bringing out plates was off.  My appetizer came out at least 5 minutes after everyone else had gotten theirs.  The same thing happened with someone's pasta.  My main course came out after everyone else's, although this time I only waited a minute or so.  They brought out red wine glasses at first.  We asked for white wine glasses, too, and waited probably 10 minutes.  We asked a second person and got them, although we still waited a couple minutes after asking the second time.

    They started us with a tasty complementary amuse of toasted bread with a bean spread, tiny slivers of pear, and rosemary.  The bread was good, although they'd very liberally sprinkled salt in a line along the length of the loaf, so one section of the bread was bordering on unpleasantly salty.  The ricotta with the olive oil was a very thoughtful element of the bread service.

    Antipasta: The octopus was incredibly tender, almost melting inside, and had great flavor.  The tripe cake had been recommended by the waiter to someone who didn't like tripe.   The tripe was ground, mixed with, uh, stuff, and breaded, so it didn't have the tripey texture.  Without the texture, it may as well not have been tripe, but the sauce and breading on the cake were still very good.  I didn't get to try all the components of the snail bruschetta, but I tried a snail on its own, and it was garlicky and chewy (in a good way).  The liver was my least favorite (I'd ordered it for myself, unfortunately)--it was good, but not as good as the others.  I didn't think the sharpness of the gorgonzola, the sweetness of the cherries, and the richness of the liver worked perfectly together.

    Pasta: The gnocchi was light, although the sauce was a little pedestrian (in the words of the person who ordered it).  It was supposed to have crab, but I didn't really see or taste much.  The rabbit and veal agnolotti were nice--the pasta was very good and I liked the brown butter sauce.  I could taste meatiness in the filling, and I feel like filling in stuffed pastas is often lost.  That said, it could have been any meat--I couldn't make out rabbit and veal.  The buccatini a l'Amatriciana (I'm probably butchering some spellings) was a good version of a fairly simple dish, nothing earth-shattering.  My pasta had chickpeas and scallops, a sauce that I think was made with pureed chickpeas, and noodles that were like pappardelle but a tad wider.  It was very good, and I was glad the waiter had recommended it instead of the gnocchi.

    We all felt the main courses were less strong than the first two courses.  They were fine, but not great.  I had the skate with brown butter and capers, and I felt it really needed some more acid and a little bit of salt.  The pork chop with prosciutto had a really nice smokiness when I ate all of the components together, but the pork on its own was a little boring.  The duck meat in the duck saltimbocca with orange sauce was cut too thinly for me to get as much rich duckiness as I wanted.  I didn't get any crispy duck skin, but I don't know if it wasn't on the plate or I just missed it.  The sweetbreads were good--these seemed smaller than the ones I've had at Bibou, so they were nice and caramelized, but there wasn't a creamy interior for contrast.  Lastly, the veal stuffed with mozzerella was well prepared but not terribly original or prepared so well it really stood out.

    Desserts:  Chocolate buddino, which was fine, not great.  I would've liked a little bit more.  Tiramisu was a good rendition.  Vanilla panna cotta was a little muted in flavor but very very creamy.  And a peach crostata, which was good, but tasted more of nuts than of peaches.

    Lastly, we were offered complementary sambucca.  I'm not a big licorice fan, but hey, it was free.  In very very small sips, the licorice flavor was mild, and it had a very cooling feeling in the mouth (sort of like menthol).  It was less pleasant when I took bigger sips, and I think most of us left the shot glasses mostly full.

    If it hadn't been for the service issues, this would've been a 4-star review.  I appreciated the amuse, bread service, and sambucca.  The antipasti and pasta were good to great and, while none of the main dishes or desserts were as good as the first two courses, they were solid.  This was a good meal ($45/person including tax/tip), and I feel like with the right ordering, Modo Mio could be great.

    P.S.: I wish the menu on the website were updated more often--it's outdated.

    Served in a large bowl and easily big enough for 2 (and as it turned out a 3rd,) Lamb Tripe and cabbage stew with parsley, olive oil, basil plus grilled Tellagio bread was my first experience with Lamb Tripe, but ideally not my last.  Flawlessly cooked the offal was minimally gamey and mildly pork-like in flavor.  Cut into thin strips the preparation was raw and rustic with the flavors reminding me of my Grandmother's Hungarian Style Stuffed Cabbage.  With my companion appropriately impressed but already starting to feel full we offered a sample of the dish to our neighbors and they graciously accepted.  When the biggest complaint about a dish is that it is "too much good food" and "now I have to come up with a different antipasti to try" that is saying something.

    The cheesemonger's first course would've been my choice had he not ordered it.  Entitled "Crocante" with prosciutto wrapped chicken livers, cherries, gorgonzola, and sweet red wine reduction this dish was perhaps my favorite of the evening.  Lightly fried pancetta encompassing mild chicken liver was served over warm artichokes and chopped cherries tossed with a creamy gorgonzola.  Topping the whole amalgam with a thick and aromatic red wine reduction and spices lent an almost haute-Marsala flavor to the whole dish - it was beautiful.

    Faring well amongst the second courses would be "Lingue" with braised calf's tongue, pizziola, portabello, plum tomato, and capers.  Inspired by the Italy trip our server stated that the concept here was to "introduce" diners to tongue by creating a pizza-esque flavor without the dough.  Delicate and thinly shaved the protein was surprisingly light while it was actually the mushrooms that lent heft to the dish.  With ample notes of basil, tomato, olive, caper, and cheese I can say their attempt to replicate a pizza flavor was spot on and I enjoyed the dish much more than I had expected.

    My secondi selection (trumping the duck on our server's suggestion) would be "Animelle" with veal sweetbreads, artichoke, pancetta, lemon, and thyme.  Bearing in mind the fact that I'd already eaten substantially during the day and was now eating for two I was lucky this was so good - it was worth being "stuffed" for.  Ample in portion, crispy on the surface and creamy within the offal was paired inspiringly with crispy artichokes - similar in texture yet vegetal in composition.  Topped with chopped pancetta and a lightly lemon accented olive oil the entire plate came together nicely and ranks amongst my favorite dishes in Philadelphia - an assessment that Phillymag also seems to agree with.

    Our third dessert and the last taste of the evening would be Gorgonzola Panna Cotta, White Figs, Honey - it would also be (by far) the best dessert of the night.  Like a composed cheese plate arranged into a stunning cheesecake the panna cotta would prove to be impossibly light despite its hefty cheese notes while the pungency and bite of the aged cheese was tempered beautifully by the lavender accented honey and sweet poached figs.  

    Bordering on a genuine food coma at this point we were asked if there is anything else we'd like and while I'm sure I would have liked to try almost everything on the menu we decided to take a rain check.  Settling the tab - a mere $45/pp with a hefty tip

    Looking back on my visit to (and receipts from) Philadelphia Modo Mio would invariably be my cheapest lunch or dinner - less than 1/2 the price of Le Bec Fin and less than 1/3 the price of Vetri...it was also cheaper than recent Italian jaunts in New York, Las Angeles, and Columbus.  While not every plate was spot on, the dishes that did excel did so to an incredible degree and not a single flavor fell short of at least being good.  Speaking with fellow diners (and waiters) at Zahav, Vetri, Supper, and Le Bec Fin I can't say I was surprised by the fondness with which people speak of Modo Mio - it truly is a special place that I was lucky to enjoy with a fantastic fellow gourmand.

History

Established in 2007.

Peter McAndrews established Modo Mio restaurant first in a fabulous line of Italian themed restaurants.  Following Modo Mio and included in the Modo Mio restaurant group are both Paesano's Italian Style sandwich shops, Monsu restaurant, and Popolino restaurant.  Peter specializes in home cooked Italian cuisine with each restaurant representing a particular region in Italy with his interpretation attached.  From Northern Italy down through Rome and finally ending in Southern Sicily Peter evokes the essence of tradition recipes with a new wave approach.  Come in, eat, and be happy!

Meet the Business Owner

Peter M. Business Owner

Peter McAndrews has been involved in the Philadelphia Restaurant scene since before their was a Philadelphia Restaurant scene.  After working in a number of kitchens under different ownership Peter left for Italy to fine tune his skills.  He began cooking on his own with Modo Mio located in Northern Liberties.  A comfortable BYO with emphasis on Northern Italian cuisine.  He soon followed with his Paesano's sandwich shop brand focusing on flavor and ingredients with each bite.  Monsu located in the Italian Market was next which focused on Sicilian cuisine.  Finally Popolino was last which evoked a traditional Roman style Trattoria !  Each restaurant represents a different part of Italy which in turn represents a different part of Peter McAndrews
