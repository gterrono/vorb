    This is my kind of restaurant.

    Husband-wife chefs, local and seasonal menu that changes daily, focus on the highest-quality ingredients, and everything is made in-house. The ambiance is beautiful -  large architectural pillars, antique lighting, and naturalist decor.

    My fiance made reservations for me as part of a birthday gift. He knew I had wanted to check this place out for some time. We found ourselves there on the first night of restaurant week with an amazing menu to choose from:

    they started us out with a bread basket of wheat bread made in house. some of the most delicious bread i've had to date. the crust was perfect. it came with creamy delicious butter and salt for seasoning. we ate two baskets worth.

    we then opted for the artisanal cheese plate, which was a supplemental $10 but well worth it. the cheese plate was served with slices of pears, candied walnuts, and delicious crackers.

    the three cheeses on the plate were:
    - laughing lindy - bloomy rind goat's milk cheese from linden dale farm in rinks, pa)
    - pecorino ginepro - raw sheep's milk cheese from Italy bathed in balsamic and juniper berries and aged in oak
    - moody blue - a holstein cow's milk blue cheese from Wisconsin smoked with wood fruit

    all were delicious. the laughing lindy worked great on a cracker with a slice of pear and a candied walnut on top. i was popping the pecornio in my mouth as is. and the moody blue was delicious with the sweetness of the pear.

    first course
    - *roasted golden beets (horseradish, chicory, dill, sherry gastrique)
    - *john tau's bresaola (house-made pickles, purple mizuna, crostini)
    - goat cheese ravioli (marinated heirloom tomatoes, oregano)

    we decided on the roasted golden beets and the bresaola. both were deliciously flavorful and well balanced.

    second course
    - *sockeye salmon en cartoccio (wild mushrooms, leeks, beurre rouge)
    - *braised lancaster pork shoulder (creamy polenta, braised kale, chili oil)
    - hubbard squash alla parmesan (tomato fondue, sage, ricotta)

    we ordered the sockeye salmon and the pork shoulder. both were delicious. the salmon was the standout. everything was perfectly cooked and flavored in the beurre rouge. the pork shoulder was the perfect dish for a cold evening. it reminded me of a deconstructed stew.

    dessert
    - *local apple cake (rosemary sabayon cream, cider syrup)
    - *raspberry triple cream cheese tart (lemon marmalade)
    - nectarine-pinot gris grape sorbet (lillet-peach granita)

    for dessert we again went for the first two options. they blew us out of the water. they were the perfect size and immensely fresh. the flavor pairings were perfect. we had a hard time picking our favorite, but decided on the apple cake. the rosemary sabayon cream and cider syrup were so delicious, we were using our fingers to get the last bit off the plate.

    service was impeccable.

    we will definitely be back!

    A cozy, comforting gem.  What a delicious meal.

    I've heard great things, but I was not prepared for Russet to completely blow me away the way it did.  The space feels like you're in someone's home, but is at the same time very spacious, with tons of room between tables.  Portion sizes were larger than expected, especially in today's "small plate" boom.

    We had an absolutely delicious meal, and I'm already excited to go back.

    - Chestnut Pappardelle with guinea hen ragu, bacon, and native chestnuts. Whoa.  This was like nothing I've ever had before.  The papardelle is paper-thin, and the combination of flavors from the hen and chestnuts just blew me away.  I wish I was eating it again right now.  Familiar yet entirely new, and perfect for the holiday season.  The chef came out towards the end of the meal and explained that his chestnuts come from 150-year-old American trees which only ripen in December (as opposed to most chestnuts which are from Asia and ripen in the fall). Russet makes their own chestnut flour in-house from these chestnuts.  I'll just repeat that again: they make their own chestnut flour in the kitchen.  That's the kind of attention to detail that makes food like this possible.

    - A "salad" of Duck prosciutto, duck liver on toast, a soft boiled egg, a little arugula, and some aged balsamic.  Wow.  This is my kind of salad.  Rich and decadent, with every component serving a wonderful purpose.

    - Foie gras on toast with apple mostarda.  It says a lot that this was not my favorite first course.  Foie gras typically doesn't have to fight hard for the number one spot, but this time around it had a real struggle, not because it was bad but because the other appetizers were so interesting and flavorful.  Still great, and that apple mostarda was bangin'

    - Second Course: Goose leg confit.  Good god.  Crispy and tender and rich, served on a bed of spaetzle that lit it right up.  Plus very generous portion which I was glad to be sharing.  Another spot-on dish for a cold night.

    This place really blew me away.  Fantastic food, fascinating yet familiar flavors, incredible attention to detail, locally sourced everything, excellent service... plus it's BYOB!  It's not the cheapest menu in town, but two can easily split an entree and two appetizers and end up spending $50, which is practically a steal for food this good.

    Um, hello Russet! Where the heck did you come from?!

    I've heard quiet rumors about Russet over the past few months but absolutely not enough to be as blown away as I was with my meal and dining experience. I'll start by saying that this place was adorable. It's nestled in the living/dining room of beautiful townhouse with high high ceilings and simple/inviting decor. Next, all of the menu items are based off of the ingredients the chef gets that week...which leads to a constant changing menu with the freshest of ingredients.

    On to the food...
    The appetizer winner had to be the chestnut pappardelle with guinea hen ragu. The pasta was paper thin and melt in your mouth. We later learned from the chef that not only did he use these native chestnuts mixed into the dish but he made his own chestnut flour to make the pasta. Talk about impressive. Not to mention, he shared the most incredible story with our table about how these chestnuts came from a 150 year old american tree which is very rare of chestnut trees. This story just made me love the restaurant even more.  
    The main course winner was the goose leg confit. I had never had goose before and I am quite certain that I will never have it any better than at Russet. It was cozy and rich without being too heavy. I loved every part of this dish including the spaetzle served underneath.

    I can not wait to get back to Russet. I know the dishes I loved so much won't be there, but I am absolutely looking forward to trying a new creation on the menu that week.

    One more thing, it's BYOB so make sure to bring one of your favorite wines and enjoy!
