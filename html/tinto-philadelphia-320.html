    I had high expectations when I visited Iron Chef Garces' Tinto, his Tapas restauraunt near Rittenhouse Square.  The food was reasonable and tasty, but I had other problems here.  First the waitstaff, particularly after experiencing the excellent waitstaff at 10 Arts, was not so well-trained.  I asked my dishes be staggered.  They were not.  I felt I was being rushed even though it was early and only a few others were eating.  I had the Crispy Boquerone Special which was quite good with some lightly candied orange peel, a good aioli, a really bright nest of microgreens dressed perfectly, but not very crispy boquerones.  The lobster came with a grilled cheese crouton and roe on top, but everything got lost in the tub of melted butter underneath it all.   The Shortrib Bocadillo was served on a small tough hard roll and the contents kept spilling out with every bite.  This might not have been a problem if I could have let the contents spill and eaten the sandwhich on the plate, muching the roll separtely, but the sandwhich was served on paper, so cutting with knife and fork was not an option.  Hard to eat should not be the take-away here.

    I have never had a bad meal at a Garces Restuarant, so I am glad that my high expectations were met whilst dining here.
    I went here with my girlfriend this month for her birthday, so we elected to try the tasting menu (They have 2 options, a regular one for 55, and a more upscale menu options one for 75. We opted for the regular one.)
    With the very dim lighting in the restaurant, it was much easier to just go with the chef's tasting, than to try and figure out what all the different menu options are. Plus, if you choose it for tapas, you know you won't go home hungry. With 16 different items that followed, my intuition was correct.

    Things that came that were wonderful:
    Charcuterie and Cheese Plate. - Probably some of the best charcuterie I've ever
    had. I don't love ham, but the Jamon was chewy and delicious, Lomo (like a thin-cut beef teriyaki) was superb, and the Chorizo matched perfectly with several of the cheeses we were served.
    Chicken on skewers in garbanzo bean puree. These came served in tall shot-glasses (neat-touch), and the puree was good enough that I drank the sauce (not sure if I was supposed to or not, but I didn't care).
    Green Bean Salad, w. almonds, and sherry vinaigrette - Warm green beans tasted much better than the canned stuff I ate growing up.
    Prawns with shrimp and chorizo - Very large. Nice touch adding chorizo to the shrimp. Whats not to like.
    Patatas asturianas - Very similar to patatas bravas served at most tapas restaurants, but delicious all the same.
    crispy potato, tomato compote, la peral cream
    Pork Belly open faced mini sandwich - who doesn't love a good pork belly.
    And for dessert: a chocolate mousse, flourless chocolate cake. I could have ate many of these had there been any room left.

    Things that were good, not great:
    Smoked almonds. Olives. -Both regular tasting, but nothing special.
    Bomba rice with mushrooms in them. - I don't really like mushrooms. Others in my party liked this very much.
    Moules Basquaise - Mussels with chorizo and fries. Mussels tasted kind of sandy. Not the taste I want in my mouth.
    Lamb chops - One of the seasonings they used (rosemary I believe) coated it, and made it difficult to eat without choking on it.
    For dessert there was also a toasted brioche, that didn't do anything for me, and couldn't stand up to the chocolate cake.

    Things that I didn't like:
    Turbot and Sea Bass - Both fish were too salty, and salt was the only flavor you could taste. I would not order either of these again.

    To eat 16 things, and 14 of them good or delicious is a winner and my books. I would soon come back to grab a glass of wine and another tapas or two that we didn't get to try last time.

    The boy and I tend to be fans of Jose Garces establishments, so we were excited to try out Tinto.  We were not disappointed that night!

    The ambiance is casual and clean with interesting decor. The service was attentive and friendly throughout the meal.

    The menu has a lot of choices, and it's a great place if you're like me a love to try a variety of flavors in one meal without having to eat too much of one thing.  We ended up choosing:

    - Bomba rice: Soft yet chewy, flavorful, aromatic, and delicious. Highly recommended!
    - Chicken brocheta: The most generic and predictable of all the dishes.  It was still good, but nothing special.
    - Ajo Blanco: The broth had a wonderful almost sour punch, which the almonds and grapes helped to contrast and sweeten.  The hamachi was soft and tender.
    - Roast Pork Sandwich: The pork salsa was a flavorful punch on a crispy baguette.
    - Pork belly:  The actual meat was much leaner than many other places, which I love.  This dish tasted very fresh, and the pork belly paired well with apple!

    Overall, great meal that I have come to expect from yet another Jose Garces restaurant!

    To echo many other reviewers here, the food is fantastic, yet pricey.

    However, I was disappointed in three things: the wine list, wine service and the atmosphere.

    Wine list: While there are some great bottles here (with triple-digit prices) there is hardly any depth in the lower price range ($35-60). The wines offered in this range were not only light in number but also in flavor and authenticity. This is unacceptable - especially at a Spanish wine bar. Not only do values abound from lesser known wine regions (Montsant, La Mancha, Priorat...), but simple, modestly-priced wine should be the cornerstone of any authentic Spanish wine bar. It's absence makes diners feel like donors to a Philadelphia revitalization fund - the fancy new restaurant may be good for business, but it is not faithful to it's culinary roots

    Wine service: If a restaurant aims to maintain a top-flight wine list (with top-flight prices) the knowledge of the wait staff must keep pace. We were confidently informed that a fruit-forward wine matured exclusively in stainless-steel represented traditional Spanish wine-making. Figuring we'd go along with it, we ended up with a juicy red a la Australian Shiraz. Basic wine-fundamentals should be a key piece of the training for wait-staff at any type of wine bar.

    Atmosphere: My beef with the atmosphere mirrors the above description of the wine list. I felt a bit like I was at the Cheesecake Factory when the waitress "upsold" us. It's really not necessary to tell guests how many tapas they are supposed to order - our stomachs can do the math.

    All in all - the food is fantastic, but the overall experience served the heighten my longing for the tapas bars of Madrid.

Specialties

Tinto features a mouth-watering selection of "pinxtos" (pronounced PEEN-choes), the Basque equivalent of tapas.  Along with pinxtos, Tinto offers a tempting array of charcuterie, cheeses, mariscos (shellfish), brochettes and bocadillos (sandwiches).  

Some of the inspired dishes include:  Duck Confit Montadito, with wild black cherries and bleu de Basque;  Gambas Brochettes, shrimp with grape tomato and sherry espelette chili sauce;  Roasted Rack of Lamb Au Jus, with truffled artichoke puree and candied lemon;  Marmitako de Ventresca, tuna toro, parsley oil, migas and shaved mojama;  Txanguro Montadito, Dungeness crab, avocado and espelette;  and Mousse de Queso, with goat cheese, calabaza, orange, olive oil, caramel and lavender tuille.

History

Established in 2007.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
