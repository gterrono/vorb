    Listed in Culinary Colonialism, Ritten-hungry, Burgers are Delicious, There Is Nothing Wrong With Drinking In The Morning, Baby, You're a Starr

    Thumbs up, Starr. I've always thought you were a bit pretentious and overrated, but your recent enterprises are getting me to rethink my opinion.

    The physical space here would be worth coming for alone. It seems to have taken over two adjoining houses, so there are series of small rooms, each with "theme" decorations. I particularly like the "dog room" on the second floor. The main bar area on the first floor was also very cute, and there is a large table by the hostess stand that overlooks 18th St. that looks like it would be a great place to hang out and drink and snack with a bunch of friends. I do think we had the best seats in the house, though - a big table tucked into an alcove between the dog room and another dining area, with a long bench and comfy pillows. The little details will keep you entertained. I went with Lauren H., and as she says, "it's like walking into your grandmother's house, if your grandmother is a wealthy alcoholic taxidermist." I'm pretty sure I recognized some things in this place from my own grandmother's house, so there you have it.

    The food was solid. I wasn't super-impressed with the menu, although there are a lot of options there wasn't much I was very excited about. The drink list is a good size and has lots of variety, and some unusual choices - I tried an elderberry soda, which was surprisingly refreshing and delicious. Bonus points for showing me something new. The bread and butter that they brought us was absolutely phenomenal. As far as food goes, I started with the winter root vegetable soup, which was incredibly thick and delicious. I'd definitely get that again. The house blend burger was pretty good - nothing to write home about, but cooked the way I wanted it. I was not a big fan of the fries, but the table was divided on this point. We also shared the honey roasted parsnips, which I really liked - but apparently I'm the only one. Poor sad bastard of the root vegetable family. In terms of food, Lauren H. won the table with her choice of the slow roast pork belly. Mmm.

    I'm planning to head back because it looks like they're already working to tweak their menu. Like, by adding Welsh Rarebit. I *KNOW* this wasn't on the menu when I went, because I have never in my life given up an opportunity to eat Welsh Rarebit. I'm also pretty excited by their brunch menu, this has the potential to be a stellar brunch spot. It's in a great location and Starr has done a great job with the physical elements of the space; if they keep up the quality of the food and the service, this place has the potential to turn into a go-to spot in Rittenhouse.

    Before I start my review, I must mention that I am bothered by the name of this place; it doesn't have the right British Pub feel to me.  So for the rest of this review I will refer to it as "The Dandy Lion Pub". Which I find to be so much more pleasing than a bar named after a weed that I don't even think exists in England...

    I have a tip for anyone trying to get into this place: that super cool door on the corner of 18th and Sansom? Not the actual door.  I felt like a fool tugging on it, only to find out the real door looks like a side entrance on 18th.  Anyways, the decor in the Dandy Lion was somewhat odd, but enjoyable. As I waited for my friends to arrive, I sat near the fireplace in what felt like some old man's living room. I was afraid to touch anything until someone on the staff came over and told me it actually was for sitting not just looking (it resembles the random furniture you see thrown into some museums, with ropes in front of the display telling you not to sit). Side note: the staff here is fantastic: super nice, helpful, and very cheerful but laid back. We sat upstairs in a very homey feeling sun room which i just loved.  I could sit up there all day.

    I came here for brunch and was really pleased with the menu. I find most brunch menus to be very overwhelming, with way too many options. There was a nice range of choices but not so much that I couldn't choose a meal. I went for the Eggs Royale and they were fantastic. Just the right amount of Hollandaise and smoked salmon, and really well cooked eggs. The coffee was really delicious but heads up, its like $3.50 (a bit steep if you ask me).

    Also, the bar area looks lovely. I can't wait to find an excuse to come here for happy hour!

    After reading reviews and hearing various mentions from friends, we decided to stop in for lunch this past Saturday. Walking in through the heavy red velvet curtain once past the entrance door, you're immediately greeted by friendly staff, a bar to your left and English décor. We had our choice of being seated downstairs or up and chose upstairs. Plus, I was also secretly hoping to be seated in the Dog Room and experience what everyone seems to mention after a visit here.

    Sadly not seated in the Dog Room, however, into the room facing the street we went - cozy, English cottage décor adorned the walls and the room was interspersed with smaller tables for two near the beautiful fireplace or larger to accommodate four plus people along the window line.

    Greeted by our waitress with a smile, she took our drink order - I decided on a Pils - imperial size, hoping to compliment my lunch choice - that it did. Perusing the menu and from what I've heard, I was automatically drawn to the fish and chips; line caught and tripled cooked chips. I was excited! Wasn't too adventurous on our first visit as two orders of them went in and the only other thing ordered was the Mac & Cheese. Lunch arrived and couldn't wait to dive into the beer battered fish that was a light golden brown and crisp as could be, side of tartar. The chips, hand cut potatoes in their own serving cup were served with ketchup and seasoned with sea salt.

    First bite of my fish was delectable; it was flaky and full of flavor and easily separated. Adding a tiny bit of tartar sauce, so as to not take away from the fish, I was set! Next onto the chips...I enjoyed those more with ketchup than without. I'm not a huge fan of thick cut fries, but these were a nice compliment to the fish in traditional fashion. The macaroni and cheese was rich and creamy, piping hot served in the dish it was baked in. The English Cheddar cheese accompanied by the braised ham hock was an incredible choice to mingle - uniquely flavorful and kept you wanting more. Not huge, but would think enough for a single lunch portion and was perfect to share. Didn't take long for me to clear the wooden plank my lunch arrived on. I was pleasantly full but not uncomfortable.

    Only downsize to mention, was the price tag. Fish and chips are $21, while the Mac & Cheese was $9.

    Although the Dog Room was not in the cards for me to dine, I did walk through it to get a gander.  Definitely different, with dogs everywhere - even ones with red eyes staring back at you, it was an experience.

    It was somewhat crowded when we arrived, but not packed to warrant a wait time. By the time we left, the only seats available was the tiny round table for two upstairs by where we sat. They also serve Saturday brunch which I found to be equally intermingled throughout the patrons specifically there for lunch. Brunch, BTW, looked delish and curious to try on another visit. Great precursor to the Franklin Institute...or any afternoon plans in the city.

    This place was extremely disappointing.  I wanted to love it (the decor is fantastic) but I don't think I'll ever go back.  

    My boyfriend and I went there for a celebratory drink after my first day of work at a new job.  We were both excited, since he went to school in England and has a great affinity for English pubs.  When we walked in, the people in the second floor couch area were just getting up, and there was no wait, so we got the huge couch all to ourselves!  Hooray!  We settled in and began admiring the authentic and well-executed decor, and that's where things began to go downhill.

    The most obvious thing to criticize is the absurd pricing of local brews that one can get at MANY other establishments for literally half the price, but I'm not going to go that route.  Instead, I would like to comment on their less-than-quality service.

    Now, I will admit that when my boyfriend and I go to restaurants, we frequently have lots of questions.  In this case, he wanted to know what temperature the beers were served at (in America, all beers are served ice cold, in England ales are served almost room temp whereas lagers are served cold -- he prefers the former).  Our server seemed very confused and frustrated by this question, and stood there and stared at us in a pissy way while it was being asked.  We had a couple of other questions too, and she continued to be vaguely snotty while acting like she had WAY better places/tables to be (at).  Bad, bad, bad.  

    After we finally managed to order our drinks, we started to settle in when I got a tap on the shoulder.  I turned to one of the hostesses hovering over me.  Said she, "Hey... would you guys mind moving down on the couch?  I mean... this is first come-first serve in this space, but I have these two people who want to sit down."  I looked around me, and there were at LEAST three available seats.  There was no reason whatsoever to ask us to move.  So we didn't.  I mean, really... I've worked in the service industry for at least 6 years out of my life.  I NEVER  would ask people to move if there is another option, especially when they've already been served their beverages.  Stupid, stupid, stupid.  

    As we attempted to continue our conversation, she sat two men (one of whom had the LOUDEST most OBNOXIOUS voice EVER) near us.  On top of this, the shitty music slowly rose in volume, becoming ever more distracting, and forcing us to almost shout over the din.  Not fun.  

    We continued to try to ignore Dandelion's many faults by drowning our sorrows in our miniscule beers and eating an overabundance of admittedly tasty pate.  After finishing off our first half-pints, another snooty hostess came over to take our glasses away.  She started off very smiley, pleasantly asking us if we wanted another beer.  When my boyfriend politely and pleasantly responded that we might just have one, her face visibly fell.  She looked very pinched, and responded, "Oh." -- as if only having one beer and a bit of pate was some sort of preposterous thing.  

    That was pretty much the last straw.  The rest of our time there was a blur of irritating interactions and overly-loud music.  

    Basically, I think it comes down to this: The Dandelion Pub is an English pub for the snotty Rittenhouse set.  And maybe for tourists.  It COULD be a neat, friendly, fun place to go have a pint after work, but instead it's a prettily wrapped-up nest of bad attitude.  

    We would have probably gone back there for dinner at some point, but considering the crap service, I'll take my money to Garces Trading Company, Nodding Head, and Tria, thanks.

    The Dandelion isn't Starr's best restaurant, but it's a solid addition to the scene, save for the lackluster brunch.

    The dinner menu is heavy on comfort food and rich British classics. Skip the underseasoned and thin shephard's pie in favor of the rich and unusual rabbit pot pie. The black sea bass, at $26, was underwhelming and the most expensive thing on the menu. Steer towards the meats instead, especially the exceptional pork belly with its amazing crispy skin. Mustard haters read closesly: it's in a lot of dishes.

    Desserts were all around tasty, but the winner is most definitely the warm chocolate cake. The figgy pudding was too dense and the tropical dessert (the name of which escapes me) a bit too sweet.

    The brunch selection is entirely underwhelming. There are the same standards and only one notably british addition: English Breakfast. It's a ludicrous amount of breakfast meat. The french toast is an anemic portion, perhaps as a parting shot to their continental rivals?

    The atmosphere can't be beat and service is adequate to great. Dine later or early if you haven't booked a table in advance. The Pimm's cup deluxe is a must for drinks.

    Met some friends here for a quick bite and drinks before a play at the nearby Adrienne.

    This was January, soon after opening and pre-OpenTable, so I tried calling for a reservation but they were full. They suggested just walking in and asking, which I did, and the hostessing crew was very nice. Sadly, with a party of three, we got the worst table in the joint, the round 4-top directly in front of the door -- and the bitterly cold winds that the curtains couldn't do much to stop with every entry or exit. That sucked, so fair or not, I'm taking off a star for that.

    Side rant: By 2011, there should be a way to make any dining room experience consistently comfortable. We have winter and summer here. We all know what they're like. The heavy curtain is a nice effort, but can't we do better? I'm not looking for levitating cars or jetpacks, but I know design solutions are out there.

It was a lot better than the versions I got in at least two pubs in Ireland, and it set the new standard for me for this dish. Plus, the whiskey cocktail was great, and our server was fast and attentive. The Irish gal liked her bangers and mash, and the non-Irish gal liked her glass of wine.

    Since we had to bolt, it wasn't a complete meal or experience like those second- and third-floor dining areas, which I'm thinking could be an even stronger four stars. Prices are what they are, and as I say with any Starr or Garces or other signature chef review, you already know what you're going to pay before you ever see the menu.

    It's a good pre-show option, and I'll surely stop back sometime when the weather is right, but never again on a windy winter without a firm reservation. Fortunately, they're on OT now.
