    Today is my birthday so why not try a brand new restaurant?  Visited for lunch at 11:30am.  The place is (and looks like) a genuine Cuban diner so I sat at the counter.  .  There was plenty of space so  I was ok with someone sitting next to me later in the meal.  To my direct right, they were interviewing Chef/Owner Garces,  so I can't say it's a typical experience.  I refrained from giving him "bunny ears" during the interview.  He was a wrestler in high school and could prob deliver payback.    

    The ceiling is high, w. cool metal fans that give it a design touch, but without hitting you over the head w. "design". It feels like my favorite diner on Collins Avenue in South Beach.  

    Dan was my server/bartender and I also talked to Lynn--they knew a lot about the menu and seemed to have each others backs w. taking care of customers.  He explained all the drinks to me and it's a list worth working through, I had a classic mojito, delicious, and there's a mojito further down the list w. some heat that I want to try next time.  Amazing rum selection and plenty of bottles of wine in the $40-$55 range.  

    I had a cheese empanada, flaky, crisp, burst w. cheese flavor.  I ordered peccadillo which is my go-to dish when I'm at a Cuban diner--it comes in a ramekin and the server takes the lid off it when serving.  I was thinking, "ok, it's pecadillo, it will be garlicky, taste good, not great,"  I took the first bite, yelled "holy granola," spun around four times on my counter stool and took a swig of my mojito to calm myself.  ...Well, that's what will happen in the movie.  I did think, "Oh my God, this is so good!"  It was just spicy enough, potatoes were cooked perfectly and the rice was fluffy. Tons of flavor.  Comes w. two sides and I had spinach and yucca fries.  The crispy (airy) yucca fries come in a ceramic cylinder w. a tiny side dish attached which holds the dipping sauce.  Nice playful touch, very Disney meets Chestnut Street.  I am easily amused and something like this can make my day.  .  

    Great lunch and will be back to dig deeper into the menu.  Another patron smiled at me from one of the booths early on w. a "come hither" look.  That was a nice birthday surprise!  Not bad for 11:30am on a Tuesday.

    I want to preface this review in saying that it was opening night, and I realize that it's always a bit hectic. I also have extremely high expectations of Garces places due to my experiences at the Trading Company, VIllage Whiskey, and others.

    That said here's my experience at the place-

    Upon entering, the atmosphere is exactly what you'd expect from a Garces place. Classy and laid back, and very friendly. I was very excited upon entering. We had a reservation but were asked to wait either standing, or at the bar until our seat was ready. The selection of liquor is phenomenal and the beer selection is mediocre, but enough to get by.

    Once we were seated (in the bar area seating) - it was incredibly loud. Between the hosts, the music, and the guests at the bar, I had to yell at the single other person at my table to hold a conversation. We waited about 15 minutes for the server to come visit the table to ask us if we wanted drinks, and another 10 minutes afterwards before we received them. Our waiter was very obviously flustered but again, I get that - it's opening night. He was still very pleasant and responsive.

    We ordered the marinated octopus and cod fritters for our small plates. They came in sequence and both were good. The octopus was my favorite, make sure you get some of the pickled vegetables in each bite or you'll end up eating them all from the bottom at the very end. The fritters were good by themselves but the dipping sauce they were served with completed the dish - creamy and smoky, very tasty. The chef was also kind enough to send us a complimentary 'queso empanada' which, while tasty, was described between the two of us as a 'Posh mozzarella stick' - I would not order them by themselves.

    We ordered the Picadillo, or 'Ground Beef Hash' with plantains and black beans and rice as our sides. The sides were fantastic, they were the only redeeming parts of this meal. I would absolutely not recommend anyone I know order the Picadillo. It tasted like a can of Sloppy Joe had been put into our ramekin. I was shocked when I tasted it and we didn't even finish eating it. It may have been an opening night issue, or something happened in the cooking, but it was so disappointing I almost sent it back.

    As I said before, I have very high expectations from anything Garces, Rosa Blanca is no exception to this. I plan to go back in the future to see if it was a once-off, but I am not looking forward to that visit.

    I cyber-stalked this restaurant for many months leading up to its opening, pretty much from the day it was announced that Chifa would be replaced by a Cuban diner concept. Chifa had a special place in my heart, and it was a great place to take gluten free folks like myself. Rosa Blanca is a fine replacement in that respect, with a majority of the items on the menu being based around rice and corn rather than wheat. The food all around was really great, with a few slight misses, but I had to deduct a star for value.

    $4 for one small empanada, no matter how good that empanada might be, is criminal. Make it $6, and put two on the plate. Then I'd be a bit more satisfied. That being said, the picadillo empanada was pretty much perfect (it maybe could have used one more minute in the oven). The ham and cheese empanada I didn't like as much. It had a different style of pastry and was fried instead of baked. Cuban ham and cheese empanadas are usually made with cubes of ham and cheesy super thick bechamel, much like the croqueta filling, and use the same type of pie pastry that the picadillo empanadas had. These were solid cheese and had serrano ham layered on top of the cheese. It was just a bit too rich and salty for me.

    The salt cod croquetas were also a bit too salty for my taste, and I really love salt. The croquetas be jamon were absolutely perfect, however, and the dipping sauce was also delicious. You get 4 to an order. Get these. They will make your tummy happy.

    For entrees, E went with the picadillo, his all-time favourite Cuban dish (he's Cuban), and I went with the vaca frita. You get to pick two sides from the list to accompany your entree. We both got the arroz moro (rice with black beans mixed in), I got the maduros (fired sweet plantains), and he got the tostones (smashed discs of fried unripe plantains). His entree had white rice underneath the picadillo stew, and had he known he probably would have selected a different side than the rice.

    The picadillo was absolutely delicious, even better than my own and I make awesome picadillo. My vaca frita was everything I hoped for and more, tender with a perfect crisp exterior and a sizable portion, but it needed a hot sauce of some kind to bring some moisture to the plate with the rice and beans. There is no hot sauce on the table, and this needs to be rectified. A bit of the house salsa from the complimentary chips and salsa, though, did just the trick! Those chips and salsa, by the way? Worth coming for just themselves. The salsa was absurdly delicious. There was definitely some balsamic vinegar hiding in there...

    We washed all that down with a mojito temparado each, which was crazy delicious and really refreshing, and I also tried a rum cocktail made with blackberries. I'm not usually a big rum fan but I loved both of my drinks. E also had a cafe cubano at the end, which was excellent- I stole a sip.

    Total damage was $101 before tax and tip. We racked up the bill quite a bit with drinks ($10 each, we had 3) and four appetizers, so a couple could easily get away with spending closer to $40 or $50 for a nice midweek meal with more careful ordering. The entree portions were plenty filling. Service was more than capable, the food and drinks were a bit slow to come out but it was very busy when we got there. Noise level tends toward deafening when it's a full house. I liked the update in decor, they really managed to change the vibe.

    On our way out, we spied a surprisingly well-stocked pastry case for a late Tuesday evening. We snagged two guava and cheese and two just guava pastries, which was another $12.50. I just had mine for breakfast. It took all my willpower not to steal E's while he slept. Puff pastry so flaky and perfect you can't help but make a huge mess while eating it. They were heaven. Can't wait to come back for a lunch and breakfast!

    Weekday Breakfast Review. I've been so excited to try this place ever since I heard of the demise of Chifa I had to stop by before splitting town for the holidays. I'm a big fan of Garces and all his restaurants, I love Cuban food, and have a high affinity for diners. Can't lose with Rosa Blanca, right?

    The good - the atmosphere is exactly what you would expect from both Garces and a Cuban diner, colorful, vibrant, lively, sophisticated yet laid-back. The front of the restaurant really has that Cuban diner feel, big silver fans and bright colors; there's a long bar which is a welcome sign along with big booths up front accommodating six or so people. The server was very friendly and knowledgeable about the menu and I thought the prices were extremely reasonable for breakfast. We opted for two breakfast plates which comes with two eggs and your choice of two sides; we went with the chorizo, pork belly, potato hash, & grits. Also had to order the sweet plantains as well...how could one not? All the sides were spot on, very flavorful, and this plate of food for $7?! The coffee was, as you'd expect in a Cuban diner, very bold/strong. They also had soursop nectar - if you can't have the real fruit, might as well go for the Goya nectar - so refreshing!

    The not-so-good - we were the only people in the restaurant at 8AM on a Thursday. That's not the issue though, the issue is how long it took our food to come out and when it finally did my eggs were overcooked although my wife's were perfectly fine. I could see this being a great meet-up spot for a business breakfast but the slow turn time will keep me away if I'm in a hurry. It was also pretty loud, which would mean lively at night, but in the morning it was a bit jarring having loud Cuban music blasted into my ears (although I did love the music itself, just turn it down a notch or two...especially if the restaurant is empty!)

    All in all though Rosa Blanca is going to be great, I can't wait to return to sample the sandwiches and other Cuban delights. The overall concept and food are great, just need to work out a few kinks here and there.

    Some really good dishes

    Cons:

    Nervous and weird waitstaff
    Feels like a Disney theme park version of Cuba
    Irritating music
    Stupid use of space / layout / seating

    Details:

    I love that this place serves food until midnight on a weeknights.  Their ensalada verde was outstanding.  When I crave salad I will come here for a huge mound of super fresh greens - some sweet some bitter with the perfect amount of dressing and really fresh beans.  So damn healthy and delicious.  Plantains, beans and rice, and cubano were fine, nothing to write home about but not bad either and priced well.  Guacamole was a little too she-she for my taste but the chips served with it were homemade and outstanding.

    First, let me start off by saying I was a little bit indecisive in my star rating.  I was on the fence whether to rate it 3 or 4 stars.  Can I do 3.5?  Until Yelp allows it, I'll keep it at 3 for now.  I finally got a chance to try Rosa Blanca after it opened a couple months ago, which is a bit overdue considering I work one block away.  It's part of the famed Garces Group of restaurants so you know the bar is being set high.  It's very clean inside (as it should be since it's so new) and decorated appropriately in this 1950s themed Cuban diner even Castro himself would feel a bit nostalgic about.  It boasts over 70 different types of rum-- not too shabby for a diner, huh?  Since it was my first time there, my decision was an easy one: the Cubano sandwich.  When in Rome right?  Or, in this case-- when in Havana.  My middle-of-the-road rating was based on this sandwich.  The Cuban sandwich is a very simple sandwich.  It's almost difficult to screw it up.  On the other hand, it's difficult to set yourself apart from another.  While it was good and by no means a bad sandwich, for me it's just one of those run of the mill sandwiches that anyone with the most basic culinary training can duplicate.  If I am wrong on this, please let me know.  The portion size was good, but my one issue with it was that it looked a little lonely on my plate.  The only thing that accompanied the sandwich was a little Cuban flag sticking out of it.  Now, Rosa Blanca considers itself a diner.  When's the last time you've been to a diner and not had a sandwich come with fries, cole slaw, or even the dreaded bag of chips? I did order the Yuca Frita though which were amazing.  Perfectly seasoned and a nice,sharable sized portion, it comes with a delicious dipping sauce.  Don't bother asking for ketchup with these fries!  I'm looking forward to going back and trying something new on the menu, and hopefully updating my rating.

Specialties

Cuban comfort food, from Chef Jose Garces' dream diner. Combining the laid-back, all-day appeal of a diner with the innately comforting character of classic Cuban fare, Rosa Blanca is the best of Latin and American culinary culture from Philadelphia's top Latin-American chef: Jose Garces.

History

Established in 2005.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
