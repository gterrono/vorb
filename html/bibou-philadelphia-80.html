    I've been trying to get a reservation at Bibou for over 6 months now. A spot finally opened up on Thursday night allowing us to the sample the French fare that has been causing such a stir.

    The dining room is intimate, as is the norm with the standard "front room" style BYOBs that seem to define the Philadelphia eating experience. Decor is nice, you don't feel too shoved on top of your fellow guests and there isn't an issue with noise.

    The wait staff are quick and efficient, be it pouring wine when your glass is running low or explaining the menu and the specials. The chef is friendly, makes sure to come out and talk to each table at least once during the evening and often rushes out of the kitchen when you are leaving to wish you a bon soiree and encourage you to visit again in the future (if you're ever lucky enough to get another reservation).

    But lets get down to what's most important, the food. Exceptional, and in a Philadelphia eating scene that now has a number of excellent French restaurants, putting Bibou at the top of the pile tells a story in itself. I started with the Foie Gras. Served two different ways 1: seared with peaches 2: as a creme brulee. Both were beautifully prepared, the sear on the Foie Gras was just right and all the flavors melded. I can't imagine coming to Bibou and not ordering this. For my entree I went with the Bone Marrow special. Presented on the half bone with the marrow melting into the stuffing and with a simple potato and green salad side, this was absolute heaven. I can't report on dessert as I was so stuffed from the richness of the Foie Gras and the Marrow.

    Philadelphia may just have the best French dining scene outside of France right now.

    If there's one place I wouldn't mind eating every week, it's Bibou. The food is outstanding, the servers are very nice, and it's a BYOB so you can bring your own alcohol. It even has free parking! Plus, how many restaurants give you the opportunity to talk to and thank the chef after your meal? Chef Pierre Calmels and his wife and hostess Charlotte are the nicest people and they're always genuinely happy to see you. Be forewarned, though. Because Bibou is so good, you will likely not get a table without a reservation and, even if you do, you'll have to be happy with whatever table you get because the place is small, cramped with too many tables in its space, and is as popular as Le Bec-Fin used to be. Why? The obvious answer is Chef Calmels. He is possibly our area's finest French chef having been executive chef at Le Bec-Fin for five of his eight years there. His sous chef at Bibou was also a chef at Le Bec-Fin. Bibou serves extraordinary French food at reasonable prices and without any of the pretentiousness that turns off a lot of people from French restaurants. My boo and I each ordered the Foie Gras Duo for our appetizers. Can you believe it? Two preparations of foie gras! Foie gras port wine crème brulée and seared foie gras with sautéed fig in a red wine, campari gastrique sauce. The taste of the crème brulée might be a turnoff for those expecting their crème brulée to be the usual boring sweet vanilla variety, but this was a marvelous version made with my favorite organ meat. The seared foie gras sat atop a small piece of sweet toast that was very much like French toast. The accompanying sauce tasted very much like maple syrup. Both preparations reminded me of a decadent breakfast. For our entrées, I had the braised veal cheeks, rutabaga purée, turmeric spaetzle, fava beans, and sundried tomato, and he had the poached red snapper roulade with pasta au gratin, baby arugula, and almonds. I sampled the red snapper and it was delicious, prepared in a porchetta style. Add Bibou to the list of places that serve items in an elegant porchetta style! My veal cheeks were simply the best entrée I've ever had the pleasure of eating at Bibou... and I've had quite a few meals here. I have to say that their Pied de Porc, which is braised pig foot stuffed with foie gras, has gotten plenty rave reviews, but is not my thing. There was just too much gelatinous fat for me and too many lentils in the dish. I've had the distinct pleasure of enjoying Bibou's soft shell crabs, a summer dish, on three occasions with marks of good to excellent each time. I've also had their bone marrow, a divine entrée that only a foodie like me will understand. Craig Laban of the Philadelphia Inquirer calls it "caveman cuisine at its most decadent, and so primally good, like eating Satan's stuffing." As good as all of these entrées were, none compared to the gorgeous dish in front of me last night. Oh, it is an ugly-looking dish by most standards, this braised veal, but it is gorgeous in its flavors, textures, and aromas that simply define good comfort food for the soul. Chef Calmels told me he wanted to make it more stew-like for the season. I like his thinking. If only I could eat this while sitting in front of my television watching the Eagles on Sundays!

    What a night!  We were celebrating with our son, who is home for the weekend, and is an avowed foodie.  

    At Christmas, when I tried to make a reservation:  Mission Impossible.  So when I saw an opening at 5:30 PM, I grabbed it!  Subsequently my diners disagreed, so I went back to Open Table...and VOILA- a 10:00 PM reservation opened up!  So I happily changed our reservation to 10:00 PM.

    Charlotte called the next day to let me know that there were 2 reservations in my name!  Agh!  So open  Open Table, cancel 5:30 PM, save!!

    We had a short wait when we arrived.  Bibou is so intimate and tiny that of course diners were lingering over their food, their cheese plates, their wine and their dessert.

    When we were seated, our server apologized profusely for the wait.  He and all of the other staff were very friendly and polite, not to mention efficient.

    We shared a yummy Turnip Soup with jam and the Creme Brulee Foie Gras Duo, served with a pan-seared Foie Gras over sautéed fig in a red wine, campari gastrique sauce.
    I had a tough time wrapping my head around the idea of a Foie Gras Creme Brulee, but any doubts disappeared after my first spoonful.  it is so difficult to describe the rich texture, and the savory flavor with a hint of sweetness on the tongue.  Well that didn't come out right, but it is the best I can do to describe such heavenly food. (I basically didn't want to swallow it).
    I was contemplating licking the little casserole dish, but my son recommended using a small piece of french bread to mop up every morsel.
    Which I did, and enjoyed every bite.  The seared Foie Gras was perfectly cooked, and like butter in my mouth.....

    For our entrees we shared the absolutely amazing bone marrow, the veal cheeks, and the hangar steak, cooked to a perfect pink, well paired with the Cabernet we had brought to drink.  All of the dishes were superb.  The Bone Marrow stuffing over the half bone was ..... Exquisitely indescribable.

    We got a little bit overly enthusiastic in our sharing, there was a crash, and red wine was suddenly decorating my white blouse, the tablecloth, my pants and the floor.  The staff were quick to supply copious napkins.
    Oh well, c'est la vie.

    Our server apologized again for our wait.  We ordered creme brulee and a fruit tart for dessert, but alas...they were gone. So we had yummy pineapple and limoncella sorbet, and a floating island as well. Cookies accompanied them.

    Charlotte came to visit our table right after the wine spill, and she was sympathetic.  Pierre came out as well, and visited every table.  He is so affable and approachable; not my idea of the "stuffy" french chef.  Pierre was so kind as to give us a restaurant recommendation for a Paris visit, and also to describe his daily routine at the market.  

    When we finally said bon soir, I was already thinking about what I wanted to try on our next visit to Bibou.

    We luckily, miraculously managed to get a reservation for 4 only two or three hours before the reservation was made. I originally planned to go to Fond but when I saw the chance to dine at Bibou, we had to jump on it!     Anyways, we ordered I believe three appetizers and four main dishes. My guests ordered the artichoke soup and the chicken liver mousse and I had the foie gras duo.     Buddakan or Bibou?  I was leaning towards Buddakan because I had a wonderful experience the night before at its sister Steve Starr restaurant, "Morimoto".  However, upon doing some due diligence about Bibou, I discovered that Robert Parker of the Wine Advocate, arguably one of the two top wine connoisseurs in the world (along with Stephen Tanzer), made the claim that this was the best bone marrow dish he had in his life.  Hearing this, I had no choice but to cancel all my dinner plans and go over to this establishment.

    Wow. Wow. Wow.  From the moment my bone marrow dish came out till the moment I left the restaurant, I chanted these words...perhaps around 100 times..and I was completely awestruck..to the point where I almost lost consciousness eating  the food. Arguably, this may be one of the dishes where I will not find a better dish like this for the rest of my life.

    Food:   So what is so special about this dish?  For those who may be misled like myself, the dish is not really "bone marrow," but is more on the lines of  a "stuffed marrow".  I didn't quite get the full instructions as to how this is cooked, but from the bits and pieces I gather, the blood in the bone marrow is drained overnight and is filled with stuffing and baked.  The marrow melts and gets absorbed by the stuffing and viola! You have one helluva crispy stuffing.  Of course it's easier said than done ^_^

    The other dishes that my coworker and I had were the Escargot, Foie Gras.  I found the Escargot to be a bit on the bland side and the edamame that accompanied it reinforced such thoughts.  Foie Gras ..well is a typical foie gras dish, but was accompanied by a Creme Brulee side that was an interesting version of what we normally know of...the dish was not sweet and was a tad bit burned on the top.  However, sadly to say, these dishes were all overshadowed by the wonderful bone marrow dish.

    Service:  Fantastic service.  The chef and his wife goes around the entire restaurant and has a conversation with each table for a good 10-15 minutes.  I heard the stories of Robert Parker, the history of the restaurant, and a detailed preparation process of the bone marrow from the chef.  Needless to say, I was enthralled by the service.

    Regrets:  It is a very non-pretentious atmosphere for a place that caters to people who are serious wine connoisseurs.  $500-$1000 French bordeaux wines everywhere...yep that's what people bring to drink here (BYOB).  As for myself, being an out of towner who never expected this, I had to go to the local wine shop to pick up a $12 bottle of Argentinian Malbec.  If anything, that was the biggest regret of the night. I wish I had a better bottle of wine sigh.

    I was extremely fortunate to snag a reservation 1hr before my meal on a Wednesday night and I am absolutely, positively certain that I will return here with a very very nice bottle of wine to accommodate my meal in the future.

    6* for the bone marrow dish and for the restaurant.  This is a must-go restaurant in the Philly area.  Just remember to place your reservations ahead of time and request a bone marrow.  They only serve 10 a night and if those go, that's it.
