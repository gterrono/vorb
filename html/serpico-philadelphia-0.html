    Came here with a party of 4 last night and had the most amazing time!

    We all kind of operate with the "go big or go home" mentality, so we promptly decided to order everything on the menu (minus the lettuce salad and plus the special dry 44 oz rib eye).

    Everything was truly delicious, but the standouts for us were
    - diver scallops
    - market ceviche
    - fluke crudo
    - steamed egg custard
    - the hand torn pasta and ravioli
    - caper brined trout (my personal favorite!)
    - seasonal vegetables

    We ladies disagreed with the gents about the wagyu beef - we liked that it was well cooked and thought the sauce was really tasty; they thought the dish was too dry and didn't meet its full potential.

    The lamb rack for two is really for two people with gigantic appetites. If you don't want to break the bank (and like lamb), this with just a few of the smaller, lighter dishes would make a pretty substantial meal.

    Service was impeccable - the food was paced comfortably, the check-ins didn't feel intrusive, and every dish was presented beautifully (seriously, everyone's food instagrams are going to look awesome).

    The space is elegant and the open kitchen is mesmerizing. I think a two-seater at the chef's counter would be a fantastic date.

    With drinks and 1 rocky road dessert to share, the final bill came out to a little over $400 - admittedly extravagant for a random Tuesday night dinner, but not shocking when you consider how much tasting menus go for.

    I hope the Serpico menu is a continuously changing one - we can't wait to go back again!

    We were counting down the days until Serpico opened and I was thrilled to snag a reservation before it got crazy busy and impossible to get into. We were seated promptly at the chef's counter, which was exciting. I love how big the chef's counter is - it was incredible to watch Serpico and the other chefs work. They all remained calm throughout dinner service, which was super impressive on their second weekend and with a full house. Serpico himself served some of our dishes, which was very cool.

    We started with cocktails - the cocktail list is fairly short (only 4 or 5 cocktails) and I really wasn't happy with the one I ordered. Our server must have sensed that because when I mentioned it was very strong (basically just pure gin and vodka) he immediately offered to get me a new cocktail. He even had the bartender come over to make recommendations for a new drink, which I thought was very nice. I still didn't love my second drink, but it was way better than the first and I appreciated the fantastic service.

    We weren't starving so we ordered on the "light" side (5 dishes). We enjoyed the raw fluke, ceviche, dashi soup, corn ravioli, and fried duck leg sandwich. I think our favorite was the fried duck leg - truly phenomenal. All of the food was very good, seasoned well with complex flavors. I think our least favorite was the ceviche - on the menu it seemed more exciting than the fluke, but the fluke flavors were much more complex (ceviche was good but a bit boring). Some items were a bit heavy on the salt, but still delicious. The goat cheese sorbet was the perfect end to our meal - perfectly refreshing and just the right amount of sweetness with the rhubarb compote, nuts, and crushed shortbread cookie.

    While the menu prices don't appear particularly high, keep in mind that the dishes are very small. We ordered 5 dishes plus dessert and while it was enough food for that particular evening, we likely would have left hungry had we gone into the restaurant starving. This is not the place to take a big eater who doesn't want to drop $150+ for 2 on food alone in order to be satisfied.

    I wouldn't say it's the best meal I've ever had, but was very good. I can't give 5 stars simply because I wasn't particularly thrilled with the cocktails, and the portions were extremely small for the price. But, we had excellent food and service with 0 kinks, only the second weekend after opening. Quite impressive.

    I really wanted to like Serpico.  Having been to most of David Chang's restaurants in NYC, and living within a 5 min walk of its South St location, I eagerly awaited its arrival.  I snagged a 6:30pm reservation a few days after it opened and went there with my wife and another couple who also loved David Chang.

    After sitting down, our server recommended 7-8 dishes for 4 people so we went with that. In summary, it was a slow and long decline.  I present the dishes we ordered in order along with our comments on each.

    1. First, 2 smaller dishes:

    Raw Diver Scallops: This was one of our favorite dishes of the night.  The combination of the raw scallop in the buttermilk along with the chives made for a light and very flavorful dish.  We wanted more of this dish.  It was excellent.  

    Chilled Dashi Soup:  We were confused by this dish.  The small creme fraiche cube seemed out of place and the overall flavor profile was strange.  The sugar snap peas were the best part of the dish, providing a nice sweet note to the dashi broth that was otherwise a bit bland.  Perhaps our inexperience to dashi broth was our downfall?  Underwhelming though.

    2. Next, 2 (or really 3) more:

    Deep Fried Duck Leg:  Having heard that the duck leg roll was a bit small, we ordered two of these to share among 4 people.  This was a good call.  The meat of the duck was moist and a good bit tangy, and the potato roll paired nicely with the flavor.  Also, the picked vegetables that came on the side (daikon, carrots) provided a refreshing complement to the fried duck leg.  It paired like korean fried chicken and picked daikon.  This was second most favorite dish of the night.  Recommended.

    Hand Torn Pasta:  Who doesn't like crispy chicken skin?!  When we saw this, we knew we had to order it.  The dish, however, was pretty meh.  The pasta itself felt a little thick.  The snails were great in the sauce, but the chicken sausage didn't add much to the dish.  And the chicken skin was good, but aside from providing a contrasting texture to an otherwise heavier dish, it didn't do much to enhance the flavor overall.

    3. So having taken down 5 dishes, we waited for our "entrees" to come next.  And waited.  And waited some more.  About 40 minutes later, a random waiter came over and looked at our table puzzled.  We thought he would remedy our lack of food problem, but then someone dropped a plate in the kitchen and he rushed back.  So, we waited some more and our waiter cleared our plates and brought us new silverware.  We all finished our drinks and ordered another round, and still nothing.  My friend looked down at his watch, and it was 7:55, almost an hour and a half after we sat down.  

    Finally, more than an hour after disappointing pasta, the servers came with the next dishes.  No apology, no explanation, nothing.

    Wagyu Chuck Flap:  The best part of this dish was the mustard sauce.  Otherwise, the chuck flap was tough and needed some salt.  Also, we didn't understand why Jason Sheehan of Foobooz went crazy about the fried potatoes that came on the side.  They were barely better than Trader Joe's oven-baked tater tots.  Perhaps Chef Serpico added a little extra somethin' in Jason's potatoes?

    Lamb Ribs:  This came on 2 plates: one for the lamb ribs, and one for the eggplant under yogurt in a bowl and charred spring garlic.  We actually thought the kitchen was apologizing for the long delay by giving us a free second dish.  Anyway, the lamb ribs looked amazing and there were 6 lamb ribs in total so we each took one and dug in.  The sauce on the lamb ribs was great, but who knew that lamb ribs were so fatty?  My wife's first bite was all fat.  And it seemed that about half of the ribs were just fat.  Don't get me wrong, I like the fatty part of meat, but this was mostly fat with a nice sauce on it.  The eggplant under the yogurt was pretty tasty and paired with the charred vegetables made for a nice side to the lamb.  But we were disappointed for a $55 dish.  

    4. Now, having had shaved foie with lychee dish at Momofuku Ko (and two bowls of it since my wife doesn't like foie), I was extremely excited by the foie dessert.  We ordered that and Apple Cake to share:

    Foie Gras - Salty.  It didn't quite come together like the shaved foie and lychee dish.  There wasn't enough peanut brittle and grapes to balance the salty foie powder.  A real downer.

    Toasted Apple Cake - A great dish, kind of like a bread pudding.  Burnt apple sauce was unique.

    Total (7 dishes plus dessert and drinks) = ~ $70 each; not NYC-expensive, but a bit on the pricey side for what we got.  

    So, not sure how experience was so different from the other viewers, but there you have it.  Palate aside, we were also underwhelmed with the service.  After the first dishes, no one asked us how the food was, which boded poorly, given that we were there on opening weekend.  Honestly, we left a little hungry and more than a little disappointed.

    4.5 stars.

    My fiance surprised me with a dinner reservation at Serpico during their opening weekend. Having had one of the best lunches of my life at Momofuku Ko, I was beyond ecstatic to try one of the most anticipated restaurant openings of the year. I really don't know how he managed to get a res, but I am so thankful that he did!

    Just off of 6th and South, Serpico definitely stands out from the crowd. Gray bricks line not only the exterior, but the interior as well. Blackboard walls along the perimeter show off the menu, as well as childrens' drawings from the friends and family soft open. Dark and intimate, Serpico's decor also has a touch of mysteriousness to it. You can't help be feel intrigued. Kids, we're not on South Street anymore.

    Upon walking in, we were promptly seated at the chef's counter (#13 and #14). The focal point of the entire restaurant truly felt like it was the kitchen - they succeeded in their design. When we finally peeled our eyes away to look at the menu, it was pretty tough to make decisions because we wanted to try everything... we finally settled on:

    1. Raw diver scallops (buttermilk, poppy seed, green chili paste, white soy, chive) - Subtly sweet, the chili paste and buttermilk complemented the scallops very well.
    2. Chilled dashi soup (sugar snap peas, charred pea tendrils, shiso, cucumber, mustard oil, creme fraiche) - I enjoyed the soup, but could have done without the creme fraiche.
    3. Market Ceviche (oysters, razor clams, pineapple, seaweed, roasted fennel, olive oil) - Definitely for the oyster lover. The Market Ceviche was absolutely delectable.
    4. Egg custard and caviar (siberian sturgeon caviar, brown butter, crispy potato, cauliflower mushroom) - Only one word can describe this custard: divine. This was definitely one of the favorites of the night. The custard and caviar excited every sense.
    5. Deep fried duck leg (hoisin, scallion, pickles, potato roll) - Delicious. As soon as we saw "deep fried" and "duck" on the same line, we knew we were ordering it. I loved the play on the hot dogs using the potato roll. My only suggestion here would probably be to use a smaller or more proportionate plate... so that the duck leg doesn't look so lonely.
    6. Hand torn pasta (chicken sausage, braised snails, garlic, crispy chicken skin, pecorino, Italian parsley) - This was very good. The pasta was perfectly al dente and the flavors from the chicken sausage and braised snails were enveloped in every bite. (I was so thankful that the pecorino was not too heavy as I'm not a cheese person.)
    7. Caper brined trout (smoked potato salad, pepperoncini, blue crab, trout roe, chive oil) - Holy smokes. Every trout in my future should include blue crab and trout roe. This is the new standard.

    I really wanted to try the Toasted Apple Cake for dessert, but was completely vetoed. We had:

    8. Foie gras (green grapes, elderflower jelly, candied peanuts) - This was a pretty "big" dessert. The texture of the foie gras is so interesting. The elderflower jelly reminded me of lychee.
    9. Rocky road (chocolate pudding, toasted marshmallow, candied walnuts) - For the chocolate lovers.

    Overall, service was good. Our server was sweet, but we thought she could have checked on us a little more. Being seated at the chef's counter, it would have been nice if the sous chef was more interactive. She was all business - which, I understand it's her job - but we were seated literally right in front of her. It was as though she was trying to avoid eye contact at all costs.

    Nonetheless, the food was amazing. Being able to watch Peter Serpico and his crew create our meal in front of us was a real treat. He served some of the dishes himself, which was pretty cool. He seemed very comfortable in his kitchen.

    We definitely look forward to coming back. It's been hinted that the menu may change seasonally, so we will see.

    I was very excited to go to Serpico - not only because of the chef involved, but after anticipating its opening, seeing the menu list and some pictures, and reading the initial Yelp reviews, it just seemed like a restaurant that would greatly appeal to my inner foodie. My fiance and I, however, ended up being disappointed with our overall experience.

    We had spent the afternoon out and were HOT, and a bit tired, so we went early to Serpico, arriving around 5:30pm on a Sunday, and it was pretty empty but patrons were slowly entering. The heat had gotten to us, so we weren't very hungry - which given the menu prices, was a good thing. We ordered 3 dishes to start, figuring if we were hungry enough we could order more. The Market Ceviche (different name on their current menu) with oysters, razor clams, seabass, seaweed, fennel, and olive oil came out first, and was refreshing and delicious, the raw seafood tender from marinating and the flavors mellow. This was my fiance's favorite dish (he flat out said so at the end of the meal). Then came the deep fried duck leg, which the kitchen had nicely cut in two for us to share...but it did highlight how freaking small this little sandwich is. It was delicious - the duck was moist and tender, the bbq sauce sticky sweet and smokey, with cucumber slices for crunch and the potato roll we agreed was the right bread. The sriracha sauce on the side allowed us to add as much spice as we wanted, and the small pickle jar had fresh, crunchy assorted pieces of pickled veggies to cleanse the palate. But it was seriously small, my fiance could've put the whole thing in his mouth, but thankfully he is too much of a gentleman to do so (in public). The corn ravioli with chorizo slices, sour cream, cotija cheese, and pickled/roasted onions was next, and although the flavors were delicious, it wasn't WOW in your face, nothing particularly interesting or special, which is what I expect from a chef like Peter Serpico.

    I had to try the foie gras dessert given what everyone else on Yelp has said, and my fiance was at least willing to humor me but he said that he didn't have high expectations for it after the other dishes. To be honest, I wish I hadn't had my high expectations because it didn't live up to it. The foie gras flavor is mostly lost when constructed in this powdered form. The grape pieces, elderflower jelly, and fragments of peanut brittle all together formed a flavor similar to peanut butter and jelly. It was fine, but it wasn't delicious - and its probably best to share it given its rich and salty component.

    Our main gripe is the prices given the portion sizes. They just seem too expensive, and the way the menu is laid out, unless we had asked our waitress to explain the portions we would have had even less of an idea of what to expect with each dish. But more importantly, with its location on South Street, Serpico makes little sense amongst the other businesses and restaurants. Restaurants like Amada and Morimoto seem like much better deals/more appropriately priced, and their location within CC has allowed them to maintain pricier menus - but their portion sizes at least make sense and don't feel like close to a rip-off. The interior design is nice and I love the open kitchen format with the bar overlooking the chefs at work, but I think that there are still necessary changes to be made before Serpico can flourish.

    The meal, in order of appearance:

    - Duck Liver Mousse:  The pomegranate jelly was just not enough to cut through the duck liver.  This needed to have something brighter and sweeter to really make it shine.  But the presentation, which looked like a single brush stroke of duck liver across the plate, was gorgeous.

    - Egg Custard and Caviar:  This was a delicious, surprising dish, a little pot of egg "custard" with brown butter, crispy potato breadcrumbs, and little slivers of cauliflower mushroom, which I'd never had.  Very tasty.

    - Deep Fried Duck Leg:  This variation on Peking Duck made my day.  Our foursome was smart enough to order two of these so we could each have a half sandwich.  Crispy fried boneless duck leg on a Martin's potato roll with hoisin, scallion and pickles, with a sriracha dipping sauce.  I could have eaten four of these and called it a night.  Wow.

    - Hand Torn Pasta with Snail Sausage:  The snail sausage was interesting. Cubed of firmer-than-you'd-expect "snausage" with perfect, house-made pasta shredded to all different sizes, with flecks of crispy chicken skin and pecorino cheese.  I loved every bite.

    - Cope's Corn Ravioli:  It's a shame for the hand torn pasta that this corn ravioli is also on the menu, because it blows the other pasta out of the water.  Wow.  Thick ravioli with corn centers that are somehow firm enough to jab with a fork, plus some chunks of chorizo, all in a bowl of... honestly I have no idea what that sauce was.  The description says there's white cheese, sour cream and lime, but I have no idea what of that is in the ravioli and what's around it.  It doesn't matter.  Truly delicious.

    - Caper Brined Trout:  This was an excellent dish, nice and salty, especially with the trout roe on top of it.  Not a world-changing dish but quite good.

    - Duck Breast:  This was probably the prettiest piece of duck I've ever received.  It glistened like the fish in Jiro Dreams of Sushi.  It was perfectly cooked: crispy on the top, perfectly juicy and tender through the rest of the breast.  The honey takes the sweetness up a notch, which is just how I want my duck.

    - Frozen Foie Gras:  This is a dessert, and to be fair it's listed as "semisweet"... but it's just a little too far towards "semi" for me.  I would have been much happier with this as an appetizer, and even so I wanted more fruit and sweetness to balance out the enormous foie flavor.

    It isn't often that I leave a restaurant feeling high from meal-induced endorphins, but after dinner here I was positively giddy.

    My first impression upon walking in was that it was much more casual than I had anticipated, and that's a good thing. It's very Philly. Serpico has a gorgeous open air kitchen surrounded with bar seating at which you can watch the food be prepared. If you're in a small group or on a date, take the bar seating. It's like edible dinner theatre. Specials are written in chalk paint on the walls and the lightening is dim and sexy. You can definitely see Stephen Starr's handiwork here, but he didn't go over the top at all. It's a really attractive space.

    As for the food, everything I tried was fantastic:
    The chilled dashi soup made me want to cry it was so good. There were so many things going on it was like a game trying to figure out what the hell I was eating. "Are those peas? I think they're tiny melon balls. These aren't tofu cubes they're crème fraîche. WHAT IS HAPPENING."
    Raw diver scallops. Amazing and simple. This is going to sound nuts but with the fresh fish, poppy seeds, and tangy buttermilk sauce, the flavors immediately made me think about a really solid New York bagel. Maybe I'm crazy.
    Fried duck leg. Clearly an homage to the Momofuku bao bun. Excellent. Love the potato roll.
    The duck liver mousse is awesome and rich and livery and fabulous.
    The hand torn pasta was definitely good, although I found the pasta slightly on the thick/al dente side.
    The only thing that didn't totally blow me away was the egg custard and caviar. It was good, but for the price I expected more complex flavors. Plus the custard was kind of broken. It made me feel like they just slapped some caviar on top of a very standard savory custard to jack up the price. It's not that it's BAD, but I felt a little duped to be honest.

    As for cocktails, The Fall Guy is a must for any scotch lover. It's sweet and smokey and boozey and perfect.

    Service is wonderful, food is fantastic, and I want to live here. The end.
