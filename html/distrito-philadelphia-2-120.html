    It seems that I am alone in hating the décor of Distrito.  I mean, maybe it's just me, but the combination of the Volkswagen with the masks on the walls and the pinkness makes me think that MadLibs were involved in its design.  (Name a color... Pink...  Ok, that's the wall color!)

    At any rate, décor does not feature heavily into my rating of a restaurant.  I've been to Distrito twice, and had two somewhat different experiences with the food.  The first time was right after the restaurant first opened, so perhaps everything hadn't quite made it to its proper place yet.  Nonetheless, I found the food to be somewhat underwhelming.  Nothing stuck out as particularly good or bad, but in a tapas-style place I always hope to have at least one dish that blows me away.  Not that time.  Given the price and, again, the ambiance, I'd probably give Distrito two stars based on this first visit.

    The second trip, however, impressed me much more.  This time it was UC Dining Days menu, and if anything I would have expected the food to have been worse.  Thankfully, the ceviches, tortilla soup and huaraches were all delicious, definitely up to Chef Garces' standards.  The masks were less irritating when the food was so tasty.  This trip would have gotten at least four stars.

    So, average the two out and you get three stars.  Assuming the kinks have been permanently ironed out, a meal at Distrito is unlikely to disappoint.  The food isn't quite as good as Amada's, but that's a pretty high bar, and you might like the vibe more than I did.  But I can't speak with any certainty about the consistency.  Maybe experiences like my first one still happen.  Guess we'll have to see.

    I've visited downtown Philly a few times now, and by the time I hit Distrito, it's become evident that a lot of the hot spots in the area are decorated to a degree that even restaurants in LA (which is where I'm from) don't approach. In LA, for example, it would be enough of a novelty for a restaurant to have chairs that hang from the ceiling. At Distrito, they have chairs that hang from the ceiling, AND a wall lined with luchador masks, AND a full sized car in the front window that you can dine in, AND movies projected on the wall, AND a unisex bathroom sink area, AND a secret karaoke room accessed when you push on a wall lined with soda bottles. A bit much? Maybe. But there is a sort of amusement park feeling when you first walk in that makes you want to run up and down the stairs to check out everything.

    Luckily, and unbelievably, the food actually lives up to the hype created by the heavily themed interior. As you might guess, the food is mostly Mexican. Our party, seated in the aforementioned karaoke room, had the tasting menu, and pretty much all the small plates we tried were good. Some of the dishes were more adventurous than others, like sushi topped with sorbet. A lot of dishes were the melty cheese type (pizza, nachos, etc), not very interpretive but you can't go wrong. And some were just more straightforwardly plain, like refried beans. My favorite of the night by far were the fish tacos garnished with some type of red veggie on top (ginger? radishes?). Couldn't get enough of it in this very refreshing combination. The chips and guac were also excellent, and the mushroom tartine was very juicy.

    The item that I had the most hope for but found a bit disappointing was the duck taco. The duck meat just seemed a bit too aged, and I've had better versions of duck taco at my neighborhood taco spot in LA. Still, they get points for serving relatively adventurous meats like lengua (the cheapest taco on the very expensive menu, perhaps to get people to try it?). The order of the tasting menu came out in a weird sequence, with a lot of the heaviest dishes being presented at the end when you're expecting something minimal to close out the night.

    The karaoke room, which can be booked for private parties, was actually really well laid out for both eating and singing. The karaoke selection was also solid, my only complaint being that you had to switch out the CDs for different selections. If Distrito invested in one of those machines that can house an entire library on a central drive, they'd be golden. Our server was excellent and, ahem, hot.

    Listed in THE PARENTS ARE COMING TO TOWN!!!, Date Night

    Immediate observations: Small plate (order 2-3 dishes per person), the decor is as much of the experience as the food, expensive.  This is definitely a special occasion restaurant!!

    Obviously a world class chef, and owner worked together to create Distrito.  I've been twice, both times the food was amazing.  Distrito uses high ingredients that are in season and fresh, the flavors were wonderful.  Each ingredient was distinguishable yet they complimented every other ingredient.  Flavors were not conveyed using grease and the food is not overly salted like most Mexican cuisine.  A lot of attention is payed to presentation at Distrito, but never at the expense of quality or taste.  The service was very good, not excellent.  This leads me to my only complaints, small plate restaurants should be experts at timing.  Dishes should come out one after another at perfectly coordinated intervals.  There were long lags during both of my visits.  Last, servers shouldn't have to ask who ordered what when the food is brought to the table, that information should be conveyed if a server is going to delegate the task.
    Expect to spend ~$35 per person. Cocktails seemed to be ~$12.  Visited 7pm and 9pm, Sat and Fri, respectively.

    My mother and I popped into Distrito for a late lunch today after being turned away from nearly every other restaurant on the 40th block of Chestnut that decides to close its doors to customers between the hours of 3pm and 5pm (we must have tried at least four with no luck). While we ended up being the only people in the place, the atmosphere of Distrito proved vibrant and enthralling.

    Three words: TRANSLUCENT NEON CHAIRS
    In my opinion, this is the only attribute a restaurant must possess in order to gain my devout patronage. Not only did Distrito pass the T.N.C. (Translucent Neon Chairs) test, but they somehow succeeded in integrating WICKER banquettes into the mixture... God knows how. Achieving this caliber of ambiance takes superior interior design prowess. However, if you're not a fan of lime green and pink, you might want to take your "Loco Por Los Tacos" self elsewhere.

    On to the meal- My mother and I split a Skirt Steak Quesadilla, Chorizo Fundido, and a side of some kind of roasted corn with cheese and creamy picante lubricant (how would YOU describe it?) that came in what seemed to be the miniature version of an old-timey rootbeer float glass. It was rather strange but quite delicious. The Skirt Steak Quesadilla was also delicious, and the Chorizo Fundido kind of reminded me of dog food, but it was my first fundido ever, so i'm not yet sure if all fundidos are like that...

    For dessert my mother and I split the Churro Dish (fresh churros, coffee ice cream, and thick mexican hot chocolate for dipping) and La Bomba, which is a big ol' ice cream sundae with plantains, bananas, fudge brownie, hot fudge, caramel sauce and roasted pumpkin seeds. Devouring plantains and bananas in the same bite seemed a little sacrilegious to me, but it was tasty! My only criticism is that the churros were a little too fresh and poofy. I've grown to prefer my churros crunchy (and kind of old-tasting... like the ones you buy from a street vendor in NYC when it's cold and raining outside and when they pluck the churro from the steamy underbelly of their chrome hotdog cart, it's immediately rendered ice cold and stale). Now that I think about it, perhaps my own personal preferences could use a little charm school...

    All in all, I'm glad Distrito was open for lunch at 4pm on a Thursday, since it seems that nowhere else within shooting distance follows suit. The food was quite savory and memorable. However, I was a little put off by the lack of side dishes. Maybe it's just how they do lunch, but you can't go wrong with an ice cream scoop of refried beans on the side of any Mexican meal. If you crave legit Mexican (or New York City Taco Cart Mexican), Distrito might not be your bag of tortilla chips.

    I'd call their style a little more "Mexipolitan."

Specialties

Distrito is the new restaurant concept by Jose Garces, the chef behind the acclaimed Philadelphia restaurants Amada and Tinto. Distrito is a high-energy restaurant inspired by the spirited culture and cuisine of Mexico City. The Distrito menu features Chef Garces' fresh interpretations of classic Mexican fare and beverages include over 65 tequilas and margaritas made with fresh seasonal fruits. A striking decor includes a hot pink and bright green color scheme, a wall constructed from over 600 colorful, campy masks of lucha libre, or "free fight" professional wrestlers, and a booth crafted from a Volkswagen Beetle, a typical Mexico City taxi cab.

History

Established in 2008.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
