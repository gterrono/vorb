    First off, let me say that the food was delicious. Tuna pizza, whitefish carpaccio, and sorbet for dessert were among the standouts. For the quality and portion size, the price also seemed fairly reasonable.

    The service, on the otherhand, turned what could have been an extremely enjoyable evening into a slight dissapointment. The table was small to start, and there is a (rather fallic) lamp screwed (that's right, you can't move it) right to the middle of the table. This made sharing dishes nearly impossible, as there was only enough room for one dish, two plates, cups, tea kettle, and a bottle of water.  When the waitress brought the second dish before we finished the first, we asked her to wait until we had finished and had more room. She actually refused and proceeded to push dishes around the table to try and make room. This happened a few more times with little or no sympathy from the wait staff.

    On top of that, there seemed to be almost no pacing from the kitchen.  With multiple hot dishes coming out at once, we were forced to eat everything as fast as we could so we could enjoy it all before it got cold.

    If you are looking to have a nice evening, try to request a table larger than your party size, then you might be able to eat without rushing through everything to keep up with the kitchen.

    Don't miss: Exotic sushi choices, extensive dessert list, relaxing bathrooms

    Try to miss: "Made tableside" course (delicious, but there is simply no room), two person tables that line the walls

    During my Philadelphia visit, I met with my friend whom I haven't seen in 3 years. He told me ahead of time that he was going to take me to a nice sushi joint. When he pulled up in front of Morimoto, I was just so excited.

    It was Saturday evening without reservation. Since the sushi bar was available immediately, we decided to take the sushi bar, rather than wait 3 hours for a table.

    As soon as we sat at the sushi bar, the sushi chef immediately greeted us in Japanese. Yes! A real Japanese sushi chef... We don't have those in Cleveland, so I felt like I was home already.

    Our server was this Asian dude who had some weird accent. I think he just wanted to sound cool. He explained their specials for the night but he explained really really fast, so I had no clue what they had.

    Anyway, we decided to order a bottle of Japanese white wine, Koshu. It was not too sweet, but had a crisp tangerine/grapefruits flavor. Kinda like Pinot Gris.

    For appetizers, we ordered hamachi with jalapeno pepper slice with ponzu sauce, and kobe beef with yuzu fused hot oil. We watched the chef prepared both appetizers and I was so impressed by his knife skills. How can he slice anything so paper-thin? The ingredients were so fresh and the presentation was elegant.

    For entree, we decided to get sushi chef's selection ($75). It came with 2 kinds of maki rolls (unagi and spicy tuna), 8 different kinds of nigiri sushi (2 pieces per fish), including chu-toro, salmon, red snapper, etc... Every sushi was so fresh and just a right amount of rice to fish ratio. Addition to sushi, there was a homemade kasutera (like sponge cake), which was so delicate and tasty. It was plenty for both of us and we were actually full and happy.

    The sushi chefs were very friendly and even though they were busy, they explained everything they were making.

    I think my friend was trying to impress me by taking me to Morimoto. He always jokes about how crappy Cleveland is and we don't have super fancy restaurants like in Philly.

    Well, I think he did a good job and yes, he impressed me with Morimoto.

    Oh yum. Morimoto definitely lived up to the hype last night.

    First off, the ambiance was lovely even though my husband and I walked right past the restaurant initially. Really interesting wavy wooden ceiling, with an apparent separate dining area upstairs. I could do without the phallic light in the middle, but feh. The chairs are a bit too high even though I'm not particularly tall, I'm not really short either.

    Service was great. Right off the bat, Ben and his bar manager were very helpful and attentive. We opted for the mid-range tasting menu, and split a very nice bottle of wine that was recommended to us by the server.

    With the tasting menu at ($120 each) we received (please note some courses might be missing or out of order, remember the aforementioned delicious bottle of wine):

    1. Toro minced with shallots, served in a cool soy based sauce, with caviar, wasabi and a japanese peach on the side. It was delicious, although maybe a bit much on the sauce.
    2. Raw oysters, one with an orange salsa, the other with cilantro and the last in spicy jalapeno and soy sauce. The jalapeno overpowered the oyster, but the other two were very well balanced, and the oysters were obviously very very fresh.
    3. My husband's favorite: a snapper carpaccio served with a citrus soy sauce, topped with fried ginger and garlic and micro cilantro. YUM. Really delicious.
    4. Yellow tail sashimi with a microgreens salad. The sashimi was delicious and freshness was obviously the name of the game.
    5. Palate cleanser of a blackberry soda. Namely blackberry syrup with sparkling water. Did it's job!
    6. Half lobster served rubbed in "Morimoto's special seven spice blend" or some such. It's lobster, it is hard to screw up unless it is overcooked. Again it was delicious, although I generally prefer a plainer preparation of lobster, and I missed the tomalley and roe, but I know not everyone eats it, and it certainly wouldn't have looked as pretty on the plate.
    7. American Kobe beef, served rare. It was certainly tasty. Very beefy with a nice buttery undertone, although not as delicious as the Wagyu we were able to get in Tokyo.
    8. Selection of five sushi pieces including toro, squid, red snapper, yellow tail and one I'm forgetting. The rice was very fresh, and the fish all good. The squid though was amazing. It had a wonderful fresh flavor and great texture.
    9. Dessert of a honey cake with peach sauce and white chocolate mousse.

    Every thing was small but beautifully presented. I saw that a previous reviewer indicated that he left hungry, but we both left very very full, and neither me nor my husband would be considered thin and we can both eat.

    The service was great, as mentioned. The courses were all well spaced and at one point the husband ran around the block to feed the meter and our server made a point of not serving the next course until he was back, seated, and situated.

    Certainly Morimoto won't be someplace we frequent often, but man, it would totally be worth it to really splurge at least once, and we have plans of sidling up to the sushi bar for some more snapper carpaccio and maybe some more sushi choices which seemed reasonably priced from our review of the menu.

    Morimoto was Great (Back in 2002)!

    I went to Morimoto after it first opened.  I was broke college kid back then so our party could only order appetizers, but it was delicious.  It was the first time I tasted Gorgonzola cheese, and was impressed that it blended well in the dipping sauce for tempura.  This was in the age of where Iron Chef Japan was immensely popular (before the American version even existed) and when nobody could really pull off a Japanese fusion restaurant, until Iron Chef Masaharu Morimoto came along.
     
    The decor was impressive, from the mood light equipped benches and the wave flowing ceiling to the terraced sinks and hidden hand dryers.  It was the first nice meal I could afford on my own, and I relished that memory.

    The man himself was working there, and was kind enough to join in pictures with us.  He was friendly and nice.  I later bought his autographed book, and I still remain a fan of his.

    Morimoto no longer works at his Philly restaurant on a day-to-day basis, so I'm not sure how much the level of quality has degraded.  I just tried the Morimoto in New York for a Restaurant Week lunch and was very underwhelmed.

    Atmosphere is very chic and modern minimalist. Price is very Le Bec Fin. Ambient noise is loud, but does not effect tableside conversation, which is nice since no one appreciates eavesdroppers. Food is creative, exotic, exciting, full of life, full of flavor. Despite full stomachs, our appetites were insatiable...just could not wait to see what the next dish would look, smell, and taste like. I have been here twice, both times exceeding my expectations. Wait-staff was above and beyond accommodating and knowledgeable about the cuisine and drinks, especially when it comes to sake and beer pairing.

    Here's a rundown of our culinary adventure:

    We kicked things off with some drinks. I started with Morimoto Martini (Junmai sake, Smirnoff Vodka, and Japanese cucumber slices). Tasted of honeydew melon. Crisp, fresh, delicate, and enjoyable. Others had the Pomegranate Shiso-Jito, and the Morimoto Imperial Pilsner. I tried the Morimoto beer, produced by Rogue brewery, and it was very good. Pleasantly bitter, fruity, heavy alcohol.  

    Sushi:
    Yellowtail-Scallion roll: Super-fresh fish, very tasty. Sushi rice was light and sticky. Not heavy. Soft Shell Crab/Spider Roll: Small crabs lightly tempura'd. Crab was meaty and flavorful, a bit sweet. Very nice.

    On to the Raw Bar:
    Kumamoto Oysters: I have not had a huge array of oysters in my time, but these were fantastic. Small oysters, packed with flavor. Each oyster was splashed with either Japanese salsa, Ceviche, or Thai fish sauce. Not overpowering and certainly a nice complimentary addition.

    We threw in an order of the Soba Carbonara noodles with edamame, bacon, and scallions. An odd choice since this was a heavy creamy pasta dish, but very tasty nonetheless. I say it is a decent interpretation of the Italian carbonara, but let us leave that to the Italians.

    Out came the hot dishes:
    We threw edamame in the mix just for the fun of eating the little salty bean pods. Then out came the rock shrimp. Little pillows of batter fried shrimp dressed in a mildly spicy  Kochujan Aioli. Great flavor. We kept this on the table and picked at it throughout the meal. Next up: Spicy King Crab that is broiled on the half shell. Dressed in a sweet, spicy, and creamy sauce. Huge, meaty, and flavorful crab legs. Literally hunks of meat in each leg. Sauce was a perfect pairing.

    At this point we are bursting at the seams, but out come the entrees:
    First off, we ordered the IshiYaki 'Buri Bop' rice dish just for the table. If you are not familiar with this dish, it is a tableside preparation. Waiter comes out with a super hot stone bowl filled with rice, cooks either Kobe beef or yellow-fin tuna on the sides of the hot stone, and mixes an egg into the bowl, which cooks off the residual heat. A fantastic sensory experience. We tried both the beef and fish on separate occasions. Both excellent. Also had the Black Miso Cod which blew my mind. I personally never tasted Miso cod, I have only heard of how good it is at Nobu. Well I finally realized what all the hype is about. This fish tastes incredible, Buttery, velvety, firm, and sweet. You have to remind yourself you are eating fish. I was definitely impressed. We also tried the Duck, Duck, Duck. This was Madras roasted duck breast with a duck confit risotto, and duck egg. Very flavorful. However, I do not recall any crispy duck skin, which I love. Probably why I was not so into this dish. Duck risotto was great though. The Dry Aged NY Strip steak with Wasabi dipping sauce was outstanding. A great cut of beef, cooked perfectly to medium-rare. Very flavorful. I thoroughly enjoyed the Seafood Toban Yaki with King Crab, Shrimp, Scallops, New Zealand Mussels, Oyster mushrooms, and citrus butter. The broth in this dish was outstanding. Buttery, with a hint of citrus and sake. This combined with the meaty chunks of fish was dynamite. Also tried the Morimoto Surf and Turf with Grilled Kobe Sirloin and raw Hamachi ribbons. This dish, while very good in the general sense, was a bit bland compared to everything else. I probably would not go this route next time. Other entrée's brought more wow, for sure.

    I have no clue how we made room for dessert, but like they say; there is always room for dessert. We tried the Chocolate Pot de Crème. Rich, chocolate-y, and very good. Morimoto brownies, these were just Ok. Coffee was good. I would say dessert was the weakest for us. I would certainly try something different next time around

    This is an excellent restaurant and a memorable dining experience. You will not be disappointed. It is very expensive though, so keep that in mind as you salivate over the menu and yearn to try everything. Go with a bunch of people so that you can broaden the array of items ordered. Go hungry, leave satisfied. Money well spent.

    Holy crap, this place is spectacular. I have been here for the omakase many times over the years and am always blown away. I have been to comparable (and sometimes more expensive) restaurants in other cities and with the exception of Nobu in NYC, which is pretty similar in its awesomeness, there is just nothing that competes both in terms of food quality and quality of service.

    If you are ordering a la carte, I highly recommend the ishi yaki bop with kobe beef (or, if you are feeling really really decadent, upgrade to wagyu). It is a singular dish - extremely yummy, and something you can't get anywhere else. I have also heard good things about the Sea Bass. They have a great selection of sakes, some of which are Morimoto house sakes, and they are great at matching you with a sake based on your preferences. Morimoto is not really known for its dessert, so I wouldn't recommend coming just for dessert, though.

    That said, let's get on to the important part: the omakase. First of all, paying more really does make a difference. The most expensive omakase grade is completely worth it - I find the $80 to be a bit lackluster by comparison. The same goes for the beverage omakase. I have tried both the general beverage omakase and the sake omakase and in both cases, they have been superbly matched with the courses (this is particularly true for the more expensive courses paired with the more expensive beverage omakases - at lower levels, it's more like some tasty drinks and some tasty food but they aren't perfectly matched up). Even better than there just being fantastic food, I have almost always had superb waiters who are familiar with the food I am eating, as well as the drink pairings (they either selected the pairings themselves or have tried them) and can talk with us at length about what we are eating. It is worth noting, for the lady who gave the place 3 stars because she didn't love one of the dishes, if you are honest with the waiter when he asks you how you like each course, HE WILL BRING YOU ANOTHER COURSE.     Had another terrible trip to Philadelphia... All my my trips involving Philadelphia go awry.  I like the city itself, but for some reason it doesn't love me back.  City of brotherly love, my foot!  Anyways, I digress...

    ISHI YAKI BOP!  That was the highlight of my trip...Went for lunch by myself because I can't be visiting Philadelphia, be within walking distance of the master, and not plop myself down for a spell.  Well, I did make reservations.  Service was fine, my waiter was nice.  Ishi Yaki Bop (hamachi) was yum...and generously sized.  Next time I would mix it up myself because I like it when the rice sits and gets crunchy.  Because my waiter efficiently mixed everything I didn't get as much crunchy rice bits.  Also, I would probably keep the fish towards the top.  The later pieces on the bottom were not as yummy because they cooked for a longer peried of time. The accompanying salad was very good.  Tender and not ice cold, exactly the way I like it.  The soup, miso was fine, but nothing iron chef-y about it.  Bathrooms were cool, but the cheapy plastic ikea-like waste basket kind of downgraded the feel.

    Wish i could go for sushi and/or dinner, but I was on the company dime and wanted to stay within budget.  I skipped breakfast and dinner that day, but was worth it.
