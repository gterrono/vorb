    m not totally sure why I hadn't been to Eulogy in so long. Sure, it's in a spot that annoying to… m not totally sure why I hadn't been to Eulogy in so long. Sure, it's in a spot that annoying to drive to, and weekend night here are more crazy than that of the zoo. But my love of beer and food should bring me here more often.

    Eulogy has a lot in common with Monk's: Excellent Belgian bars with great tap and bottle lists. They both have great food, including mussels, and also sport multiple bars with different taps. So far, everything is great. But I'm not just going to write a comparison between the two, because it might just be boring.

    I've been on a quest for my favorite burger in Philly, but after eating too many fist-thick pub burgers, I decided to scale back a bit. Still, I've heard good things about the burger here, so I couldn't resist. The result? A perfectly cooked, shaped and seasoned burger. Burgers come with your choice of cheese and one topping. Cheddar and leeks did me very well. It's easily one of my top burger spots in the city. I also tried the white wine mussels. These are also a delicious choice.

    Now throw some of the best beer selection in the city on top? Eulogy makes good use of their bar to serve up a Belgian heavy menu, while also keeping a few excellent domestic beers. I started out with Kwak Amber. It was a delicious beer, and could only be outdone by me next choice, Gulden Draak. These are some of the premier beers in the world, available only a short bike ride from my house. The Draak comes in at %10.5, but is so well crafted you can't even tell. Sure, it's $9 a glass, but that doesn't change my recommendation.

    I visited over lunch, so it was pretty slow and the staff was restocking a bit and moving things around. But the food still came out quickly, and the bartender was on top of the service. To boot, he was also really cool. I'm sure it's a trend with most who work here.

    Bottom Line: Come here ready to eat, drink, and have an excellent time. Make sure you have your wallet, because the bill can add up quickly, especially with the beer. This is one of the top spots in the Olde City, and my favorite Belgian spot in Philly.     Read more

    I decided to come here after hearing they had the largest beer selection in the city. You can get some good beer at places closer to where I live, like the POPE, SPTR, or Lucky 13, but I wanted to try something different. Also, they have Piraat on tap which I thought was cool as shit.

    I did a little research before I went so I would have some idea of what to order. I chose 3 things:  Duvel Green, which was on draught, because Duvel is one of my top 3 favorite beers; Jolly Pumpkin's Oro de Calabaza, which comes in a 25 oz bottle and was selected #1 in a NY Times article on Belgian-style beers; and a 3rd which I would leave to a bartender's suggestion.

    We were the only ones in the place when we got there. It was what Devil's Den wish it was. It had a cool vibe, very authentic. And by authentic I really just mean it feels like it has been there a long time, doing its own thing long before the age of the hipster.

    A similar thought struck me after the place started to fill up, around 9:30. There's something strange about this crowd. I couldn't put my finger on it, until I realized -- no hipsters. It took me a minute to notice it was what WASN'T there, like the way you don't really notice when a headache goes away.

    I ordered the Jolly Pumpkin I wanted to try, but the guy brought out the La Roja instead of the Oro De Calabaza. I didn't really care because I was trying something new either way. It was a nice beer, tasted like cherries -- probably too subtle for my brutish taste.

    For dinner, I got a large order of mussels in red sauce which was very good. Not the best, but very good. She had the steak sandwich with gruyère cheese which was delicious and I wish I had gotten instead.

    After dinner I asked the server guy for a suggestion. He knew a lot about beer. He suggested Chouffe Houblon Dobbelen IPA Tripel, which they had on tap. He told me the difference between Duvel and Duvel Green, which made me decide not to get it (basically a lighter version). We got the dessert (there's only one -- chocolate mousse cake with caramel) which was good as well.

    All in all I really liked it here. At first, I didn't really feel like I got a deal, per se. I had a good time, but I paid for every bit of it. Then I saw that they have a lot of the same beers at Devil's Den -- but at higher prices. A 25 oz Jolly Pumpkin goes for $15.99 at Eulogy, but at Devil's Den it'll set you back all of $18.00. The Chouffe Houblon I had would have cost me a full dollar more at Devil's Den -- same with the Duvel Green I didn't get, which is in a 10 oz rather than 12 oz at Eulogy. A dollar more for 2 oz less.

    I look forward to coming back for another mini-vacation from hipster land that is the Passyunk East area.

    Three times I have brought individuals who were self-avowed beer haters to this place.  They all claimed that there was no beer that they would enjoy.  Three times I left with a newly converted beer geek.

    Each time I have evangelized the greatness of beer, I have brought the heathen to this church of brewing, and had one of the bartending pastors of beer guide the newcomer down the path of goodness.  They usually begin with asking what kind of foods the person likes, if they like wine, and if they do, what kinds, and if they prefer things that are sour, bitter, fruity, or sweet, or a combination thereof.  They never hesitate to pour samples of different types for the person to try, and it seems as though they always have a few opened bottles sitting around just for this reason, so you're not necessarily limited to the draught varieties.  Eulogy manages to get beers that are not to be found anywhere else on the east coast, such as the Russian River Consecration that I had last time I was in the pub.  Many of their brews are quite expensive, with some bottles pushing $30 or $40.  But not all.  There are plenty of $5-$10 beers for those who wish to just try a few different types.  It's not a place to go to drink to excess though, as that will get expensive very quickly.  It's a place to go enjoy a favorite beer, or discover a new bottle.

    Now, Eulogy is first and foremost a beer bar.  The food here is obviously designed to complement it's beer centric attitude.  Heavy, Belgian type dishes that aren't going to be overwhelmed by a sour Lambic or strong Tripel.  One of my favorite foods here is the Brugges chicken sandwich.  They have the standard double-fried pommes frites with different dipping sauces, which are pretty tasty in their own rights.  Of course, one cannot talk of Eulogy without mentioning their nicely varied selection of mussels.  

    Eulogy is easily one of my favorite places in Old City Philadelphia, and my favorite beer bar in the United States to boot.  Check it out, you won't be disappointed.

Specialties

This is Beer Heaven, give a "high praise" Eulogy for your favorite beer after you consume it.  We also prepare our food in the authentic Flemish Belgian Style as opposed to other Belgian restaurants who choose the French Walloon Belgian style.  This means our fries are thick enough to truly hold a double fry technique, our fries go "crunch, goo" because they are the right thickness, the wimpy skinny fries elsewhere go "goo goo goo" because they are too skinny and the double fry technique does not work.  Also our mussels are larger than the PEIs other restaurants use, this is because our mussels are the authentic North Sea size like in Belgium.  The more meat the more flavor the mussel can hold in our ala minute pan technique (all ingredients added fresh, never ever ever any pour over sauce on mussels).  Also our handburgers are award winning based on seven secret spices and our fish and chips and other entrees are as good as the beers ! Make a reservation at www.eulogybar.com

History

Established in 2002.

My family is from Brugge, Belgium, I am passionate about Belgian beer, food and heritage, this adds an authenticity to the business.  I got the building in 2002 and built the restaurant myself using antique stain glass behind the bar from an old Philadelphia church and a chandelier from an old church as well.  The bar was constructed in Fishtown in Philadelphia by a local carpenter using Honduras Mahogany I imported and the design of the bar was based on a blueprint I drew.  
We take our beer very very very serious, our beer starts in a beer cooler and is then delivered via dual gas blenders using co2 and nitrogen blends for the proper carbonation technique.  Most of our draft beer never travels more than six feet but along that journey there are glycol filled lines keeping the beer at 40 ish degrees until it hits your glass.  We have about 200 different glasses we keep in stock because most of the taste for beer is accomplished through your smelling it, yes your nose!
