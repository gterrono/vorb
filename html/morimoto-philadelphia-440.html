    I have eaten at Morimoto's several times over the years since it has opened but I have not been there recently since starting to Yelp.  My rule for Yelp is simple, no Yelping from memory (at least over a few days), so I have had to wait until this return to Morimoto to drop a review.

    Went in for dinner with some friends and tried a variety of dishes.  We began with some edamame for the table, delicious and well made.  The beans were succulent and juicy with a  generous sprinkling of sea salt.

    Drinks consisted of a sample of Choya Umeshu, a particular version that I was not familiar with.  it was smooth and sweet, much smoother then the usual choya umeshu.  We then went on to the Oyama nigori for a few glasses.  Very smooth and slightly sweet, perhaps not as good as Rihaku but close.

    Appetizers was a chef's selection of sushi and sashimi.  For the sushi the rice was well cooked with evenly formed nigiri.  The sushi-su was a complex sweetness with a hint of vinegar.  One of the higher level su formulations I have had.  I still stand by the thought that a great deal of the sushi quality can be told by the quality and flavor of the su, and Morimoto is excellent.

    The fish selection was broad and fresh.  The saba was succulent and firm without mealiness that sometimes mars lower quality fish.  The toro, maguro, and aji were firm, well cut, and deep of flavor.

    I went with the ishi yaki with the wagyu beef addition.  The rice was well made for entree as with the sushi.  For the night I stopped by there was a special with Japanese Wagyu beef rather then the usual USA wagyu.  The beef was tender, juicy, and flavorful with nice marbling.  In the hot pot it sizzled to a nice medium rare state that was excellent.

    The pickled cucumber and mushrooms with the rice and soy based sauce was a winning combination.  The sauce was thin and light to mix with the contents and highlight them without overpowering them.

    The desert was a miso honey cake, apple topping, and shiso ice cream helped to end the meal.  Flavors melded well and was an excellent choice for an ice cream flavor to match the miso honeycake.

    Overall as in the past my meal at Morimoto's was awesome and worth the trip and price.  It is a 5 star restaurant and menu without a doubt.

    Listed in Hungry Visitor in Philadelphia

    Another upscale and trendy Iron Chef establishment in Philadelphia, this is the original Morimoto for all Food Network fans to try.  During my week visit, I was happy to have the opportunity to dine here twice - one for the happy hour and the second for dinnertime.  Happy Hour is from 5-7pm, Sunday - Thursday and served in the upstairs lounge.  With $6-7 appetizers and sushi, $6 drinks, and $5 desserts, happy hour is a steal for anyone who wants to sample part of Morimoto's menu.  

    To try:

    Sparkling Ume: ume (plum), orange blossom, champagne.  A delightful fruity bubbly to start or finish a meal.
    Rock Shrimp Tempura: Morimoto's glazed popcorn airy shrimp served in a spicy "kochujan" aioli.  
    Spicy Salmon: fresh chunks of salmon in a slightly spicy aioli sauce
    Spicy Lobster Skewer: lobster served "dynamite" style on a stick, with delicious Japanese sweet potatoes.
    Mary Jane Brownies: A dense brownie topped with Kinako-Kuromitsu - toasted soybean ice cream glazed with Japanese molasses and candied hemp seeds.  Delicious without being sugar overkill.      
    Black Cod: buttery fish with a crisp skin and glazed in a sweet miso sauce.  Melt-in-your-mouth goodness, served with sweet black beans.
    Salad with crispy seafood, Belgian Endives, and tasty viniagrette dressing      
    Duck, Duck, Duck: Juicy duck served with flavored rice.  
    MIso Happy Cake: Dense miso cake topped with vanilla ice cream; thick but still an interesting treat.  

    To pass on:
    Warm Mushroom Salad: chopped mushroom mixed with herbed grains, served with endives.  The texture was like a dry stuffing and slightly bland.  I was hoping for a stronger mushroom flavor.
    Angry Chicken with cellophane noodles: Spicy tender chicken - a pretty simple and not very impressive dish.  
    Sushi Chef's Special: depending on the amount of sushi, the $30 gets you about 15 pieces of various sushi.  Other than the salmon (which my friend claimed melted in her mouth), I wasn't pleased with the sushi's cold and dry rice.  The sushi I sampled, while good, was not mind-blowing.  This makes me pretty skeptical about the Omakase special.  

    Service upstairs during the Happy Hour was great.  The waitresses are friendly and quick to bring you orders and drinks.  During dinnertime, though, I found our waiter off-putting and pushy.  We had told him a few times that we were going to be sharing our dishes, but he kept asking each person whether we'd like to order both an appetizer and an entree.  He didn't look happy when we reminded him we were sharing and when we chose to split dessert at the end.

    The setting for Morimoto immerses you in a cool, futuristic setting.  It's part crisp white, igloo-like, then merging into wooden walls with peculiar shapes, and the crazy florescent lighting at the tables which seems to make the restaurant glow in hues of blue, green, and purple.  Although for such a trendy vibe, I almost laughed at the disposable "take-out" chopsticks as part of our table setting.  Really?      

    My impression is mixed because I loved many of the dishes but also wasn't too thrilled about a few other aspects of the meal, including the service.  Overall though, it's definitely a fun foodie experience and worth trying.  The pricing (except for the expensive chef's choice Omakase option), quality of food, and portion sizes offered at this high-end restaurant are pretty reasonable for anyone who wants to eat like a Celebrity Chef.

    Started out the evening with a glass of sparkling ume (plum wine). It came in a cute flute and had a drop of actual ume on the bottom. The glass itself was insanely small, but I did manage to get slightly tipsy off it since I downed the whole thing before dinner started. It was happy hour, so it was a little cheaper than expected.

    I started my meal with the appetizer-sized soba carbonara, which was quite delicious, though distinctly very non-Japanese. This is to be expected from Morimoto, whose specialty is Japanese-fusion. I really love the flavor and texture of soba noodles, and the sauce really sticks to it quite well. And you definitely can't go wrong with bacon. It could have done without the edamame, and perhaps they should have given me a fork for that dish to help eat them, but I can't complain. The entire bowl costs as much as a single piece of oh-toro.

    My sushi selection was basically a dream team of expensive stuff I've been wanting to try for years now. The oh-toro costs twice as much as the others, but it was definitely the best. It's extremely fatty and gives the tuna a cloudy appearance compared to regular maguro. The taste is exquisitely buttery and had to be the best piece of sushi I've ever eaten in my life. The shima aji (striped jackfish) was also extremely good. It's not a fish you will find at most restaurants, but Morimoto claimed on Iron Chef it is one of the best types of fish for sushi, so I had to try it. I wasn't disappointed. The anago, I got on a friend's recommendation. It is sea eel (as opposed to unagi, which is fresh water) and possesses the same fatty characteristics of unagi. It was not as charred as unagi usually is and it lacked a sauce. It makes any comparison difficult to make, but it was tasty. I'm just not sure if I would order it again under the same circumstances. I'd be willing to try it one more time if it were prepared more closely to unagi because I really love that eel sauce. Lastly, the uni, I had to try since so many people hyped it. It was good, but nothing to write home about. It was slightly sweet with a kick of saltiness at the end. It was tasty, but I definitely prefer the taste and texture of raw fish.

    For the entrees, I tried some of my cousin's angry chicken. It tasted extremely Indian, and we think the sauce is similar to tandoori. I didn't try the noodles or the giant red chili peppers that my cousin was munching on. I don't think I liked his entree much, but again, it might be better with the rice noodles and the chili peppers.

    My entree was wild sea bass with black bean sauce. It was a bit disappointing for me. The black bean sauce was overwhelming on the sea bass. It was way saltier than the stuff I'm used to in Chinese cuisine. By contrast, the sake sauce was extraordinarily light and loaned itself quite well to the delicate flavors of the sea bass. The fish itself wasn't particularly special. I was hoping for some giant lumps of fish fat, but the entire filet was completely lean. What a disappointment. My friend completely cleaned off his plate and even eat up all the sauce with the leftover rice. He really loved it. Personally, I would skip the entrees here and just go for the sushi.

    The desserts were both really awesome. My friend's dessert was a delicious sesame mousse cake. The mousse was made from black sesame and was quite silky smooth. Its relatively light flavor contrasted well with the thin layer of chocolate cake on the bottom and the soft chocolate ganache layer on the outside. The presentation of it was also beautiful. I didn't get to try the little cookie on the top, but oh well haha. The dessert I split with my cousin was fantastic. As good as the mousse cake was, the green tea trio (matcha tres leches) was even better. The little cracker on the top was infused with massive amounts of green tea flavor. The ice cream was really light and had the taste of honey. I've never had honey ice cream before, but now I will always crave some. The ice cream contrasted really well with the bit of green tea cake on the bottom. The cake was super moist and had the texture of a piece of bread soaked in way too much syrup. Green tea flavor was bursting out of every single bite. I am guessing that the cake must have been soaked in some kind of strong green tea syrup. It was amazing.

    All-in-all the food was pretty good. The sushi was top-notch and well-sized. The appetizer soba was excellent, though I think next time I go (if I ever go again) I will skip the entrees and either go straight sushi, or get a bigger bowl of noodles. The desserts were a nice finish. My meal cost me ~$90 (w/ tax and tip) even with the discount. I can't say it was worth it, but it was definitely an experience. Go big or go home. I doubt my cousin and my friend enjoyed themselves as much as I did, since they pretty much only had entrees... which to me was the least enjoyable portion of the meal. They said I should have gotten omakase, but meh. Maybe next time.
