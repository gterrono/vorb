        How you feel about this place will depend a great deal on your attitude. This is probably one of the cheapest places to enjoy a Garces meal. It is also one of the most expensive places to enjoy Mexican food. I prefer to approach this place from the former perspective, and as such, I loved it.

        The menu was large and kind of short on description. So rather than try to parse it all out, my wife and I put ourselves in the hands of the kitchen and opted for the $35 tasting menu.

        Here' what they sent out for us:
        - Chips / Salsa / Guac: perfection -- I feel that this is one of standard measures of quality at Mex places and Distrito's version was as good as any I've ever had.
        - Shrimp ceviche: tasty, but a little too sweet by the time we finished it.
        - Chop Salad: Perfectly fine...for a salad.
        - Mushroom flatbread: perhaps my favorite dish of the night (although it seemed very similar to the flatbreads at all the other Garces places)
        - Hamachi taco: picture a fishstick with pickeld cabbage on top -- doesn't sound all that great right? Yet somehow, this was delicious. It made me happy that we ordered the tasting menu because I never would have ordered this if I was left to my own devices.
        - Carne asada: Pretty good spice rubbed NY strip - texture could have been a little less sinewy, but all was forgiven because of the rice that came with it.
        - Black Bean side: too full by this point to really gorge on heavy dark beans, but they were tasty for a bite or two.
        - Flan: Sounds boring, but was delicious. Tasted like pumpkin pie. Portions only consisted of about two bites worth, which was perfect for me because I'm not a fan of giganic sweets after a big meal, but others may be disappointed by the portion size.
        - Drinks were fine, but I'm not much of a drink snob. They were tasty and had booze in them -- good enough for me.

        The atmosphere was very festive. If you want a place where you and twenty of your sorority sisters could roll in wearing bunny ears, pinching waiters' asses, and screaming  "woo" without being out of place, Distrito fits the bill. If you want sophistication, go to Amada or Tinto. It seems like some people see the loud gaudiness as a fault, but the place accomplishes what it sets out to accomplish (high-end zaniness). It's not my style, per se, but I can't really hold it against 'em.

        So let's tally it up! Here's a new scale that I devised for my stars from here on out:
        5: I would drive across town for this.
        4: I'd go again if I happened to be in the neighborhood.
        3: I might return if I was in the neighborhood and everything else was closed.
        2: I'd rather go hungry than eat here again.
        1: I need to evangelize against this for the good of humanity.

        Based the quality of the food and the reasonable prices (a dinner for 2 for around $100) Distrito gets a 5 on this scale.

        :: the birthday kid, part IV, ie the next birthday kid ::

        my special day(s) came and went....and a month later, it was the boy's turn to celebrate. since he had treated me to a starr dinner for mine, i chose to return the favor by taking him to a garces locale. reservations were slim pickins as his birthday fell during restaurant week, but distrito wasn't participating, so we were able to secure prime time seating sans limitation of a set menu or being forced into 3 courses we hadn't the room for.

        surrounded by abundant shades of pink - thankfully more swank than steel magnolias - and luchadors aplenty, the scene was certainly festive. we steered away from the pink for our drinks, each ordering a blue demon mojito, made with mixed berries. rounds 2 & 3, however, we both opted for regular mojitos, which we agreed were superior in their simplicity. in lieu of bread, distrito provides a basket of spicy nuts - a welcome alternative to the typical pre-meal munchies.

        having recently been dazzled by the yellowtail ceviche at alma de cuba, the boy was instantly set on ordering the hamachi to start his birthday feast at distrito. sadly, it didn't hold a candle to his previous yellowtail love-fest....or so i took his word for it. i began with the chilango chop salad, a colorful mix of baby arugula, watercress, green apples, dried cranberries & spiced pecans with a honey-lime yogurt dressing. it was crisp, refreshing and a perfect size for two...which worked out well since the boy was digging my salad more than his ceviche.

        while sharing of small plates is the M.O. at all garces establishments, our different dietary preferences led us to each order our own entrees, along with a side of tasty sweet plantains for the sharing. the birthday boy selected tacos (pollo? carnitas?) which he enjoyed, but didn't find especially memorable. and clearly, neither did i.

        much to the chagrin of my funghi-fearing flame, i chose the los hongos - described by our server as a mini mexican pizza - which featured forest mushrooms, huitlacoche sauce, queso mixto, black truffle and corn shoots atop a thick corn tortilla. the presentation was lovely...the aroma enticing...the taste divine. so much so that mr. mushroom hater even cut himself off a piece - and liked it!

        too full for dessert, i paid our [not-too-outrageous] bill and we headed out...but not before inquiring with management about the impressive luchador wall. for anyone who's ever wondered, they have had to replace about 25 stolen masks over the past year, and have had even more thwarted attempts. dangit, there went my idea for a memorable bday present!!

      Specialties

      Distrito is the new restaurant concept by Jose Garces, the chef behind the acclaimed Philadelphia restaurants Amada and Tinto. Distrito is a high-energy restaurant inspired by the spirited culture and cuisine of Mexico City. The Distrito menu features Chef Garces' fresh interpretations of classic Mexican fare and beverages include over 65 tequilas and margaritas made with fresh seasonal fruits. A striking decor includes a hot pink and bright green color scheme, a wall constructed from over 600 colorful, campy masks of lucha libre, or "free fight" professional wrestlers, and a booth crafted from a Volkswagen Beetle, a typical Mexico City taxi cab.

      History

      Established in 2008.

      Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

      Meet the Business Owner

      Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

      Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
