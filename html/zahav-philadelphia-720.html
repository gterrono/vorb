    My sister was taking me out to dinner for my birthday, so I turned to Yelp to see what's raving.  I told her Zahav has great reviews and I've always wanted to try it.  I had Michael Solomonov's 10-course tasting menu when he was at Marigold Kitchen and it blew me away.  I was hoping Zahav would do the same.  It kind of did.  I would go back...

    We ordered the Tayim.  Probably the best way to go for any first-timer.  I'm glad we did.  I told my server about my allergy to eggplant so they made sure to keep everything separate for me.  I don't break out into serious hives and sweats or puff up into a blueberry with eggplant unless I have a lot, so I DID allow myself just a taste here and there.  The hummus-tehina is the best hummus I have ever had.  Airy, smooth, and velvety. Laffa bread with the Za'atar seasoning is beautiful, and was replenished when we ran out.  I really wasn't too crazy about the salatims.  The eggplant salatim (the one I can only have a teeny tiny bit of) was the best one.  The fennel with Serrano chilies was good too. I felt the beet salad didn't have much flavor and had an usual mush texture, and the Tabbouleh maybe had too much parsley? Strange, I know.

    I got to taste a lot of mezze.  Some were perfect bites. The fried cauliflower (fried and grilled are my favorite cauliflower cooking methods) was a no-brainer and near perfect.  The labaneh gave the cauliflower a wonderful burst of freshness, but the cauliflower could have stood on its own too.  Delicious little bites. The best mezze I had though was the House smoked sable. Pretty much the ultimate brunch item if they had brunch.  An egg oozing inside crispy fluffy challah bread topped with the fish and poppy seeds.  O.M.G.  Next favorite was the Kibbe Naya, a lamb tartar that has sweet crunchy corn complementing the soft texture. I had a love-hate relationship with the chicken liver mousse, though I would probably order it again.  The bread it came on was too greasy and made the bite richer than it needed to be.  The mousse itself--brilliant. The watermelon salad was fresh and fun and the crispy lamb's tongue was good.  Glad I got to taste both, but would not order both in the future.  The biggest let down was the sweetbreads.  Solomonov gave me a flawless nugget of thymus at Marigold Kitchen, and the one at Zahav was sugar-cube shaped and overly seasoned and kind of tough.  Sorry, but a no-go for me.

    For my Al Ha'esh, I ordered the hanger steak. It was good, but not great.  I tried my sister's Branzino.  Same sentiments, though I felt the fish was a little heavy on the salt.

    For dessert, I ordered the white chocolate cake. Presentation was marvelous.  Pieces of cake dusted in sugar to look like marshmallows, sitting on top of some yogurt, and paired with blackberries and peaches.  This dessert was well balanced, though I felt the cake to be a little dry.  My sister got the basboosa - pistachio cake with strawberries and strawberry ice cream.  This was very tart and bold, but I also felt the cake was a little dry.  The big homerun of the night: Tehina Semifreddo. HOLY MOLY. I am glad I sneaked a bite from my fiancee before it was all gone.  It was paired with an almond pastry and a cherry reduction.  Near perfect dessert - 4.9/5. A 5 if there was more of it. For free.

    As far as drinks go, I stayed non-alcoholic. The Mint Lemonade was good, but sour.  It would have been better had the mint been crushed, so I just kept doing it myself with my straw.  I also had the Ginger Spice - strong flavors, and it would be a good pairing with gin.  While both are very creative, I will stay with water next time.  Also had the French press coffee to round out our meal.  It's a very strong and dark coffee, and I tend to like more mild blends.

    The other reviews were right: the service was impeccable.

    So... when I go back, I'm going to order the Hummus-Tehina, House-smoked Sable, and the Tehina Semifreddo and call it a day.  We were VERY full after the tasting menu.  No snacking beforehand!

    I've been pining for a trip to this restaurant for months now, and my fiancee happily obliged for my recent birthday. Tucked away on a path near the Society Hill Towers, Zahav is unassuming and clean on the outside, opening into a larger room with stone floors and wooden tables as light filters in from the nearby windows.

    The kitchen sits across from the bar, separated by a layer of glass - allowing you to watch the chefs without the residual noise, steam, smell, etc. After sitting down our server promptly came over to greet us, explain a few details about the menu and get us set up.

    First off - a tasting menu at a top-rated restaurant for $40? I'm sold already. Starting off with a series of Israeli salads, humus and freshly baked bread, we were already diving into some serious flavor. Carrots, green beans, peppered fennel, tabbouleh, beets and more adorned our table, as a literal taskforce of servers swiftly delivered and removed plates with ease.

    Our small plates included crispy lamb's tongue, sweetbreads with chic peas, heirloom tomato salad with house-made ricotta, fried cauliflower and crispy okra with peaces (compliments of the chef). I'm scrimping on some of the finer details, as each dish was remarkably paired with just the right amount of sauce, portioned well to allow us to enjoy each plate without overdoing it.

    Hanger steak with baba and branzino with wild race awaited us next, both cooked to perfection and wonderfully tender. We hung onto every bite, sharing everything until the plates were literally wiped clean.

    Before dessert we opted for a drink, diving into some Blanton's bourbon. For $12 I expected a reasonable pour, but the bar at Zahav hooked it up, delivering a bang-for-your-buck sized glass of whiskey that lit my eyes up at first glance.

    Dessert closed out the experience with pistachio cake and strawberry mousse, along with an Israeli pasty (the name escapes me) and espresso ice cream. A waiter even delivered a small plate of house-baked cookies with a candle impossibly mounted on the rim, having noticed it was my birthday.

    From the welcoming atmosphere and top-level service to the extraordinarily flavorful food and reasonably priced tasting menu, Zahav was one of (if not the best) dining experience we've had in Philly. Congrats to the chef, Michael Solomonov on a job incredibly well done.

    Many people have told me that Zahav is the number 1 restaurant in all of Philly. Naturally then, I had to visit during my recent weekend visit. While I'm not sure I would personally consider it to be the best restaurant in Philly, it was certainly a great place offering interesting Israeli fare that you don't typically find everywhere. Perched atop the park, the space is spacious and beautiful as well, suitable for a hot date or a fun night out with friends.

    The bf and I actually happened to catch the tail end of RW when we visited on Saturday evening, which was totally unintended. Even though it was close to 10pm, the dining room was still full with a few small walk-in parties waiting to be seated. Not sure if RW brought the crowds out, or if Zahav is consistently that busy on the weekends. Much to my surprise, the RW menu at Zahav was a great value; they actually offered the regular menu (minus the mesibah meal with the roasted lamb shoulder) - a leisurely paced 5 course meal for just $35 a person.

    They started us off with the salatim and hummus with laffa within seconds of placing our drink and food order. All of the salatim were flavorful and refreshing, my favorites being the cucumber, slightly spicy green beans, pickled fennel and crunchy carrots with a strong kick of cumin. The hummus was beautifully creamy and smooth, and not too thick, which the bf really loved with their slightly chewy and thin warm laffa that came straight out of the oven.

    Moving on to the mezzes - we had 4 mezze plates between the two of us, including the fried potatoes, chicken liver mousse, crispy haloumi, and house smoked sable. I was actually quite disappointed by the crispy haloumi, which seemed to be one of their highest rated dishes; I found the gently flavored cheese to be too rubbery and bland while the edges took on a slight bitterness from being burnt. The house smoked sable with fried egg, poppy on challah was amazing, which I totally was not expecting from an Israeli restaurant. The sable was tender, taking on a delicate smokiness that was perfectly tempered by the gooey egg. The chicken liver mousse on Ramadan bread with pickles, gribene and sumac was surprisingly intense in liver flavor. Not something I minded at all, but potentially off-putting for beginners. I do appreciate that they showcase some off-cuts on the menu, as the grilled duck hearts, crispy lamb's tongue, shabazi sweetbreads and kibbe naya (lamb tartare) all looked very intriguing.

    We also had two al ha'esh dishes - the branzino with walnut pilaf, green beans and tzaziki and the kofte with cumin, peppers and carrots. I enjoyed both dish greatly, as the branzino was nicely cooked, seared skin down, with nice crunchiness and acidity from the accompaniments. The kofte had great cumin flavorful, exactly what I like from middle eastern meat, only much refined. The robust flavors also somehow made me feel comforted and cozy on a chilly autumn evening - as did the earthy Tempranillo that was poured.

    Finally we finished our dinner with two desserts - the pistachio cake for me and malabi with white chocolate, rhubarb and melon sorbet for the bf. Both desserts were good and quite light, although we were admitted pretty full at this point to fully enjoy them. All in all, I enjoyed my dinner at Zahav. While not every dish was a homerun out of the park, I totally appreciated the innovativeness behind the cooking and the ambition to elevate Israeli food to a higher level.
