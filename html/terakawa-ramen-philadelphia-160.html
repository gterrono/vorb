        The people working here are gems, I tell ya. So super nice and accommodating. The place is relatively small so it would be hard to have large groups in here, but they're fast getting your order out.

        I got the Shoyu Ramen and the broth wasn't overly salty. It says on the menu that they can make adjustments if need be, which is great. My noodles were cooked perfectly, and the pork on the ramen was delicious. It's a good place to start for people who are used to only the instant ramen noodles because the broth is very mild but tasty. A little oily, but I gather that's from the pork. The Tan Tan noodles were good-- the broth was creamier because of the sesame paste and a spicier than the Shoyu (but not what I would consider very spicy). Delicious. It was a hefty bowl with lots of ramen and probably a cup of minced pork.
        Pork buns are definitely good-- might as well as go for the steamed versus the crispy (fried)-- the fried pork buns weren't that much more spectacular than the steamed. Good all around though.

        I liked that you can order different sizes for the ramen too. Kudos for that!

        Freakin' solid place.  A place in Chinatown to bring your mother, not just your weird dirty friends.  A place in Chinatown where the tables are clean, the kitchen open, and the servers speak proper English.  A place in Chinatown that could easily be plopped elsewhere in the city and fit in just fine.

        Mind you some of the above points could be taken as negatives too.  Sometimes I want to go to Chinatown to be challenged, feel wrong eating somewhere.  Just painting a picture with words here.

        Now that we've established that it's nice inside, how was the food?
        - Takoyaki - Mixed feelings on these little guys.  You get two skewers with three balls on each.  The balls themselves have a nice enough taste, pretty gooey, few pieces of recognizable octopus in each.  But a few of these pieces were tough.  Like chewing on that floppy part of your ear.  The lobe.  Or what I'd imagine that to be like.  And those fish flakes (of which there were a TON)?  Like the flakes I'd dump in my fish bowl growing up.  For the fish.  Not for the Brian.

        - Gyoza - didn't eat one because my girlfriend ordered only these from the start and I didn't want her to starve.  But she liked them I think.

        - Tan Tan Ramen - I dug this, although could've stood to add some heat which of course you can do yourself at the table.  Didn't blow me away but was very satisfying, a nice portion for ten bucks.

        - Edamame - had never heard of this before, nervous to order initially but really enjoyed.  Little green things with some sort of saltiness on top, not sure exactly what it was but an enjoyable snack for the girlfriend after realizing gyoza alone weren't enough.  Could see this becoming popular.

        Mochi - God I love these things.  Could tell they were fresh, much better than the boxed version you buy in Asian supermarkets or Whole Foods.

        Go eat here!

        In short: good ramen, great atmosphere, and awesome waitstaff.
        I've had the dandan, signature, and shoyu ramen. All of them were very good. Dandan was nice, rich and filling, it was perfect for a below-freezing day. The signature ramen has nice toppings but I prefer thicker noodles, which you can specify although I am not so crazy about the broth. The shoyu ramen was flavorful even though it is really simple. Also...I LOVE THE CURRY. Even though I am not Japanese, I would imagine that being Japanese soul food, it's not like curry I am used to; this curry is a thick gravy. It comes with a salad which had a dressing I didn't care for (I don't care much for dressings in general). I had the pork katsu curry plate; the katsu wasn't flavorful but it went well with thickness and spices in the curry.
        I have tried the appetizers. Karaage were pretty good, pork buns were alright, and the takoyaki had too much mayo and bbq sauce for me. I find the appetizers a bit too expensive compared to what you get for the ramen so it probably evens out.
        The service was great. The waitresses were attentive and always there to fill up my water. It is entertaining if you are seated right across from the chefs surrounded by 50-100 gallons of boiling broth and water.
        It's great if you are looking for lunch during a weekday afternoon when it isn't too busy. I wouldn't advise on having groups over 4, but there is good seating if you are alone or have a friend with you.

        Listed in The Highlight Reel, Top Restaurants, Caliente!

        With the great Pho VS Ramen debate (yelp.com/topic/philadelp…) alive and well on the talk threads I found myself drawing the conclusion that I needed to expand my ramen horizons outside of Nom Nom (yelp.com/biz/nom-nom-ram…) and my crap filled college days. And while I might still find hand drawn noodles to be the more suitable of competitors to pho (yelp.com/biz/nan-zhou-ha…), I left Terakawa with a full and warm belly and being impressed by what they did with the old Ramen Boy space.

        From lightening quick delivery , to friendly attentiveness service was spot on. while nowhere near the best I've had and kind of on the plain side, the steamed pork gyoza were nice and filling. But the main attraction by far was namesake Terakawa ramen, which I added extra pork belly to like a boss. from the chewy and earthy bits of mushroom, to the small and robust egg with a gooey yolk center, and the fatty and tender bits of pork, everything were in the right place of complimenting the plentiful noodles. And the broth was a warming combo of spice and miso.

        I might still find Pho to be the winning contender after my outing. But I'll definitely be back for more bowls to come.Especially when I can add in delicacies like donburi, pork buns, and takoyaki to the mix!

        Because Brian A. could live on ramen, we frequent a variety of ramen joints in the city.

        Nom Nom Ramen is our "we're too tired to walk to Chinatown" ramen. Terakawa Ramen is our top choice go-to spot. (I actually also adore Ramen Bar in University City because of its vegetarian option--I just like the vegetables offered, even though I always get the pork-based broth--, but we're hardly ever over there).

        One of the nicest things about Terakawa Ramen is that because of how often we go, the staff recognizes us. They're always very pleasant and service is quick, even when all the tables are full (gotta love ramen's turnaround time)--the restaurant itself is pretty small, but there is the traditional bar along the open kitchen, which is usually faster if you don't mind not facing your dining mates. The bathrooms are always very prettily decorated (part of my judgment of a restaurant is formed by its bathroom!).

        I prefer Terakawa Ramen to Nom Nom Ramen because of their hard-boiled egg. Terakawa Ramen has the marination and seasoning of the egg down to a science. (Nom Nom Ramen is just kind of a hard-boiled egg in your soup.)

        Appetizer-wise: the Pork Buns are great, as are the Gyoza. They also have more adventurous offerings, such as Takoyaki (a/k/a octopus fried into little balls) and, apparently, a very nice Seaweed Salad.

        I like the variety of types of ramen you can order and how you can request one type of noodle over another (there's the usual thicker noodle and then there's a thinner, slightly tougher--I think--noodle). My usuals are the Miso Ramen, the Terakawa Ramen, or the Spicy Ramen (slightly Korean-style with kim chee galore).

        Now that I've written this, I think we'll have to go again tonight, 'cause the ramen buds are singing!
