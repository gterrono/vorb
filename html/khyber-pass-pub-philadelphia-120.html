        A great "new" spot from the fine folks behind Royal Tavern, Cantina Dos Segundos, etc.  I was… A great "new" spot from the fine folks behind Royal Tavern, Cantina Dos Segundos, etc.  I was pleasantly surprised to enter the Khyber and not be slapped in the face by the famous stench of stale beer...instead I was welcomed into a low key pub with a stellar beer list and matching menu.  I love the beer menu which lists what's currently on tap and a section below with what's on the way.  As we were there for most of the night we watched as the menus were updated/stamped to show what was "kicked" or "just tapped."  The menu is great outlining the style of beer, alcohol content, brewery location, and a brief description.  As with all fine bars our waitress gladly brought over samples throughout the night as they tapped new kegs.  

        My party sampled a few things off the, New Orleans low country-leaning, menu - hushpuppies, fried okra with an Alabama white barbecue sauce, smoked cheddar fries, sweet potato fries, and my wife and I split the special sandwich of the night, a smoked duck sandwich with cashew butter and pepper jelly (made famous by chef Susan Spicer at Bayona in New Orleans).

        The fried okra were great, crispy but not greasy and the sauce was superb.  It's not often you find an Alabama white sauce, especially "up north" (which could be considered Tennessee if you're from Alabama.)  The fries were all superb as was the sandwich (as for remoulade to dip those fries in...it would be good slathered on an old boot!)  The hushpuppies were just okay - being from the south I'm a bit picky about my hushuppies and no matter how good they aren't served with maple butter and pepper jelly...but...they were tasty nonetheless.

        The service was awesome as well, as you would come to expect if you visit Royal Tavern with any regularity.  Love this place and a great addition to Old City.  I'll have to say - I hadn't been to Old City in quite awhile so this might actually make me go back!         Read more

        We ended up at Khyber Pass because Eulogy had an hour wait - I'm so glad we did!  We waited 25 minutes (not bad for a Friday night at 8:00), and while the actual length of the wait wasn't bad, the waiting situation is a bit challenging.  The bar area is pretty small, and the only place to wait is sandwiched between the door and the area of the bar where the waiters grab their orders so you're constantly in the way.  That said, the waiters were never impatient with us for being in the way so it was all good.

        Once seated, the atmosphere totally changes - the seating area is spacious and really nice.  I had the Pulled Pork sandwich with mac and cheese - absolutely amazing.  The Pulled Pork had a delicious vinegary barbecue sauce, and was topped with coleslaw and a buttery, toasted bun - simple, but perfect.  As practically all the other reviews note, the mac and cheese is amazing.  It's baked and has a great layer of crunch on top.  It's served very, very hot (it was still warm at the end of our meal, 30 minutes later) so the cheese stays gooey and delicious and you can take your time eating it.  We shared a slice of pecan pie for dessert, which was also heavenly.  To top of the seriously delicious food, our waitress was incredibly nice and attentive, and didn't rush us at all.  I'll definitely be back!

        The Khyber is a great pub.  I love it as much today as I did 20+ years ago when it was a great little rock club featuring up and coming  bands.

        The bands are gone, the stage area / dance area has been filled with a row of pub tables and the approach has turned from rock n roll to gastro pub.  The bar area has not changed a bit in the past two decades and the staff is still as cool as ever.  The place features NOLA inspired dishes and one of the finest beer selections in the city.

        Here's how I would break down my most recent visit:

        Food, 3 stars.  I've had 4 star meals at the place but decided on this visit to try the pork ribs.  The ribs were fine but I have had more tender and succulent at other places.  My girl felt the same about her fried chicken, good but short of heavenly.  We both would note that the sides were excellent.  I went with the baked beans and the Big Ass Biscuit and my girl loved her collard greens and mac and cheese.

        Service / Atmosphere, 4 stars.  It's just a great, hip hole in the wall type place with cool twenty somethings working the bar and floor.  I've always been impressed with the level of attentiveness the bartenders and servers have given myself and my fellow patrons.

        Beer list, 5 stars.  This is reason enough to hang at the Khyber.  Choose between an excellent selection of ever changing draughts or thumb through about 8 pages of selections in the bottles menu.  Easily one of the best beer selections in the city, fully capable of being spoken of in the same breath as Monks and similar pubs.

        I love the Khyber.  It's a great Philly hangout with nothing pretentious about it.  I can't wait to go back.

        The word hero gets thrown around a lot. Used to mean something. Now anyone who safely lands a jet in a river gets the term hung around their neck like some best in show county fair medal. But after a recent meal at Khyber, I've got a worthy addition: me.

        While catching up with a high school friend over dinner--a who's fat, facing drug charges, or both conversation--I noticed a growing flicker of light at the table next to us. Just as I turned my head I realized it was our neighborhood's napkin lit aflame and that flicker had exploded into a thundering roar. Someone had been a little too lax in their passing-a-napkin-over-a-candle policy and now half the bar was on fire.

        A gaggle of shrieking hipsters in skinny jeans pushed their way past the women and children. A few Jersey Shore types did the same, but I think that was benevolent given the flammability of their hair. Some Occupy kids stuck around to steal food off people's plates. Me, I ripped off my shirt, looked at the fire and said, "let's dance, Sally."  I blacked out at this point but was later told what I did most closely resembled "beating the shit out of fireworks." The restaurant saved, I was hoisted up by the crowd and asked to become intimate by several patrons. I obliged all.

        It happened this way, or I just quickly dumped water on it to the table's astonishment. Still: hero, right? Right.

        4 stars for the food, led by the small army of po' boys and a Confederate-loyal militia of Southern fixins. The fried shrimp and oysters po' boy ($15) practically glistens with fryer oil off the metallic ceiling while flakes chip off the crusty Leidenheimer roll, blotting up the grease stains on your face like specks of tissue on a freshly nicked shaving job. A little pricey, but get this if you're thinking po'.

        The fried chicken po' boy with ham and swiss ($12), regrettably named a "sammy," was fine but the ham was particularly dry and dragged the dish down. The hush puppies, even with their fun dipping sauces, are a bland nightmare. Mac-n-cheese, onion rings, and collard greens are legit and recommended.

        5 stars because few bars match Khyber's food, beer list, and atmosphere.
