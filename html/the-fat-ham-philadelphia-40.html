    I've been to the Fat Ham twice now.  Once, right after they opened for my birthday with my parents, and once after work for some drinks with friends.  

    IT. IS. DELICIOUS.

    I love trying new restaurants, and this one did NOT disappoint.  Not only was the atmosphere comfortable, despite being a smaller space, the décor is so interesting and eclectic.  You eat off of a variety of plates, and some dishes even are slices of trees.  It's such a creative way to serve food.

    I HIGHLY recommend the ox tail, trout, hummus, ricotta, spicy chicken... hell, get everything on the menu - that's basically what my parents and I did.

    The cocktails, yes are a little pricey, but nothing totally outrageous, and definitely worth it - I recommend the classic Old Fashioned - it's not made too sweet, and the liquor they use is delish.  I also like the Fat Apple - I'm usually not a fan of sweet drinks, but I think the green apple gives it enough bite to offset the sweetness.

    I can't wait to go back!  It's such an easy place to grab a quick drink and some small plates after work as well.  Since the food comes out quickly, you can get in and out of there before the dinner rush!  I recommend getting a reservation if you're planning to go for dinner :)

    Listed in Center City's 5-star Dining

    Wow.  Wow.  Wow.  We had heard good things about the Fat Ham, so we went in with high expectations for lunch.  There are nine "small" plates on the menu.  Perfect lunch-sized to maybe split one and then have another to yourself.  Or so that's what our waitress said.  Well, the two of us decided to go all out and have 2 plates each.

    Ha ha.  After eating all four plates greedily and hungrily -- the hot chicken, the mustard greens salad, the oyster sliders and the ham and egg, we decided that the food was so good that we would order two more.  So we got the pork sliders and the smoked ricotta cheese.  At this point the kitchen took note and sent us the trout dish compliments of the house.  We were so impressed we dived into that one as well.  We were up to seven dishes now and decided, hey, why not order the mac n cheese?  So we did.  As we started to slow down a little, the kitchen sent the last item on the menu out to us, the cheeseburger.

    Wow, so we had EVERY ITEM ON THE MENU!  Two people!  And finished them all!  And both of us not your typical gluttons!  I swear I was more full after this lunch than I was on Thanksgiving.  And now I am in the rather unique position of being able to comment on every menu item!  Sweet.  So here we go, quickly.

    Hot chicken.  Rivals some of Han Dynasty's heat, but not quite.  Absolutely spicy and crispy and fabulous.  A must.  Please never change this, Kevin Sbraga!
    Mustard greens salad.  A winner, nicely dressed. The nuts add some depth.
    Oyster sliders.  Wonderful, great balance, incredible crunchy batter, the oyster is huge also.  This is a must have also.
    Ham and egg.  Good.  Not my favorite, but tasty.  Needs more of that gravy!
    Pork sliders.  Carolina style so lots of vinegar.  Tender pork.  Good.
    Smoked ricotta cheese.  Definitely not a main course.  Better to share than eat alone.  Wonderful smoky flavor and probably a good counterpoint to the hot chicken.
    Trout.  Much lighter food than any other dish.  Nicely composed, lots of flavors, colors and textures.  Fish cooked perfectly.  Even the skin melted in your mouth.
    Mac n Cheese.  Baked in an iron skillet.  Yummy, not too sticky, a hint of bacon flavor.  Bread crumbs on top for crunch.  Nicely done.
    Cheeseburger.  Fabulous.  Medium, so there was pink on the inside.  Soooo juicy and tender and the house made pimento cheese was fantastic.  

    Service was fantastic, and the maitre d was a very cool guy.  The bartender even ame over and helped us with our after lunch glass of bourbon.  Yes, they have about 100 American whiskeys.  Very upbeat staff.  Plates arrived quickly and cleared away quickly too.  But the crowning touch was when Kevin Sbraga himself, chef and owner, walked in with another chef from his other restaurant, Sbraga, and sat down to lunch.  Hey, when you want to eat your own cooking, that's fantastic, what better endorsement is that?

    What made the food so good ultimately wasn't the fanciness, it was just the marriage of flavors and the elevation of simple Southern cooking into something memorable and just very very good.

    Maybe it's the fact that I associate southern food with more reasonable prices and larger portions, but I thought that the value-proposition offered here isn't all that great.

    I had the oyster sliders, fried lobster tail, collard greens, mac & cheese, carolina rice, and hot chicken.  I was really surprised but the lobster turned out to be the best dish.  The vegetable hash tasted fantastic, and it really added lots of sophistication and depth to the lobster (i mean, a battered & fried lobster tail is going to taste good no matter what, but the complexity and flavors of the rest of the dish really made it special).

    The rest of the dishes were definitely good, but none of them exhibited enough interesting flavors or textures to justify the price hike in comparison to other good southern restaurants.  I loved the oxtail on the rice, the bbq chips on the mac & cheese, the dill/cucumber with the hot chicken, and the little bit of spice on the collard greens.  But those little things only made slight interesting improvements to the dish - they were little flourishes and didn't "transform" the dish or anything.

    Don't get me wrong, it's definitely good.  I just don't know if it's worth the high price tag & smaller portions, given the fact that the dishes being served aren't really all that different from other southern restaurants.

    p.s. My wife had their blueberry mint julep.  It was terrific, and they don't skimp out on the bourbon.

    The fried chicken dish at Sbraga was my favorite fried chicken in Philly.  Unfortunately, as of late, it appears to be on hiatus.  I was thrilled when Fat Ham opened and a similar, but spicier fried chicken dish was on the menu.  When I was finally able to wrangle a friend to go with me, I was thrilled.  

    She drove into the city to meet me.  Luckily, even at 6 pm on a Friday, parking on the Walnut Street bridge is abundant.  You only have to pay until 8.

    The first thing that struck me about the Fat Ham was how tiny it was, like only seats probably 30 people tiny.  We did not know this before our arrival.  We went 30 minutes before our reservation to grab a drink at the bar, but I probably would not do that again--at least on a Friday.  The bar was packed, but the drinks were flowing, so after our first drinks (she had the Sauvignon Blanc on tap, I had the gin and aperol cocktail which was delicious and well made by friendly bartenders--I highly recommend it, if you like gin and aperol, of course).

    We were seated promptly at reservation time and were quickly approached by our server.  We had spent the past 30 minutes at the bar salivating over everyone's dishes, so we were ready to order.  Our server explained that the food was served tapas style (smaller plates) and recommended three plates per person.  My friend and I both ordered three plates of our own and waited for the food to arrive.  I would recommend, if possible ordering plates for the table to share.  Our plates were all served at different times, which resulted in my friend eating my appetizer b/c she thought it was a bread dish for the table and us staring at each other while we ate our plates.  

    The FOOD:

    Southern Hummus--this is a peanut-infused hummus topped with bits of whole peanuts.  I thought it was a nice twist and the grilled bread it was served with was delicious--both crunchy and airy.  There was just enough bread for the amount of hummus served (I hate the dreaded out of bread, cup of hummus scenario).  

    Baked Mac and Cheese--The "mac" was actually shells of pasta that were well cooked, slightly al dente.  The cheese was a creamy mix that included a smoked cheese, perhaps gouda, which went very well with the crumbled BBQ potato chips that topped the dish.  It's hard to go wrong with mac and cheese and this dish does not disappoint.

    Hot Chicken--This dish is very similar to the Sbraga dish, with the addition of a spicy hot sauce coating the chicken.  The large piece of chicken tops a dill-ranch soaked thin piece of white bread and a few pickles.  The ranch cools some of the heat and really helps you taste the chicken and the flavors of the hot sauce.  Make no mistake, this is a hot dish, so if you do not like spicy food, stay away.  The chicken and skin are incredibly crispy.  The breading is not too thick.  

    Hot Sauce--every table is topped with a bottle of homemade(?) hot sauce that is different than the hot sauce on the hot chicken.  It is mild and garlicky, and perhaps includes tomatoes.  It was delicious.  I used it to accompany my mac and cheese and it added delicious garlicky flavor.

    All the food was delicious, the cocktails were creative, the servers, hosts and bartenders were friendly, helpful, and knowledgeable--which was super impressive so soon after the opening.  The Fat Ham is another gem from Sbraga that I will add to my rotation.  My friends on the other side of the Schuylkill will be excited to know that I definitely found an excuse to take more trips to University City in the near future.

    The first thing you'll notice when you walk into the restaurant is how tiny the space is. There are 30 seats in the restaurant and an additional 12 at the bar, but the seating is creatively arranged to allow guests and servers to move through the restaurant with ease. The decor is an odd juxtaposition of rustic meets modern. You notice the variety of whiskey bottles on the shelves behind the bar and immediately acknowledge that you're in a Southern-style restaurant. Service moves at a slow, steady pace. The first of our dishes arrives, a bed of mustard greens tossed with benne seeds and peanuts. The dressing is fiery hot and spicy good! Spicy without being unbearable, the dressing accents the nuts and greens nicely. What a crazy flavor combination! The shrimp and grits were utter perfection. Big, juicy, grilled shrimp sitting on top of a small helping of creamy, buttery grits with diced pieces of country ham strewn for good measure. Who would think grits could be this good? The buttermilk coated fried oyster slider with its burst of flavor in the middle was just sooo good! Move over crabcake and filet sliders, this oyster slider has found a place in my heart! The blackened catfish was one of the best pieces of catfish I've ever eaten. It was firm like rockfish, but didn't have that characteristic catfish taste or odor. The Carolina gold rice is one of the best accompaniments I've ordered in a while. It pairs really well with the hot chicken, which is OH. MY. GOD. everything that the hype says it is. It is indeed fiery hot, as in spicy hot. It is indeed crispy on the outside and juicy in the middle. My friend Chris W and I were ready, willing, and able to dive right into the alleged chicken that saw fit to win the heart of local food critic, Craig Laban. In our two orders, our server stated how fortunate we were to have one dish with white meat and one dish with dark meat. Without hesitation, I went for the dark meat. I tasted a small sample of the breast meat on Chris's plate and it, too, was juicy good. Almost too good. Was there crack in the chicken? The pecan pie was a delicious ending to a fantastic meal. You can't ask for a better meal anywhere. I felt like I was at Morimoto or Vetri. For this reason, I can honestly give The Fat Ham my highest rating.

    As to the decor, the South-meets-Anthropologie theme was galling.  All serving and hosting staff wore plaid shirts.  The inside of The Fat Ham has the feel of a manicured, early twentieth-century butchery.  Crescent moons adorn the bathroom stall doors--perhaps a cheeky reference to outhouse architecture.  The plates, wood stumps fashioned into oversized bowls, encumbered the visuals of the food.  The check arrived on a plank with a clothespin.  A clothespin.  Apparently making upgraded Southern food means you impose gimmicky caricatures on your customers.  At the least, there were no drinks served in Mason jars, a personal pet peeve of mine.

    Taken together, our experience at The Fat Ham was a real bummer.  Nashville has many of these nouveau-South cuisine and reclaimed-wood aesthetic restaurants, and it's just as tacky in Music City.  Sixty dollars later, and in a city with as many fantastic restaurants as Philly, I expected much, much more from The Fat Ham.  This Southerner will not be returning.

Overall we really enjoyed dining here and are excited to go back. We're already hooked on that Hot Chicken which will be a must-order every time we go back. The plates are small but we thought for the quality it was worth the prices. We left very happy!
