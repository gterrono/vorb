    When I heard that Terakawa had quietly opened over the weekend, I basically sprinted the three blocks from my apartment.

    Living in Northern California for a few months had me utterly spoiled with fantastic ramen joints and since I've returned to Philly I've been seeking the perfect bowl of porky, eggy, noodley goodness. And Terakawa's signature ramen has quite a bit of goodness.

    Noodles are cooked perfected and the broth is crazy-flavorful. It's topped off with all the standard fare: slices of tender pork, kombu, bamboo shoots, and [half of] an egg. I recommend adding fish cake for 50 cents.

    My only complaints were that the broth seemed slightly oily and they played that horrendous "Keri" song twice on loop. That said I opted to keep my leftovers and they eventually switched to J-Pop.

    Best ramen of my life? Nope. Solid noodle spot? Totally.

    When I first heard of Ramen Boy closing shop I was upset because they were one of the few ramen joints that didn't equate salt with flavor.  Upon entering Terakawa, I liked the upgrades to the decor and I was so happy to see some of the ramen boy staff still working the house.  The menu immediately caught my attention because not only did it have some classics such as miso, shoyu, and tan tan ramen, but it also boasted a house special Terakawa ramen as well as the visually stunning mayu ramen.  Needless to say, I had to to try the mayu and it did not disappoint.  The broth was creamy with a strong garlic infusion throughout.  The noodles were nice and chewy and the egg was brilliant (a perfect soft boil with a think and runny yolk).  This is a ramen joint that is aiming to please and I will be returning soon to try the other soups.  I gave Terakawa 3 stars but I hope to upgrade that to 4 or 5 in the future because I am not sure if there are any spicy menu options.  I was in a hurry and didn't have time to really ask about possible extra spicy options.  If the super spicy is possible, than I could have a new favorite ramen joint because the staff is wonderful, the atmosphere is chill, and the prices are fine.  To be continued....

    TL;DR: I think this place is way better than Ramen Boy ever was and slightly better than NomNom ramen. Hope this sparks further competition between them.

    I had the Mayu "smoke" broth ramen.

    (1) Broth: very decent recipe that isnt overly salty, yet still missing that extra flavor kick that Im looking for. Still satisfying overall though

    (2) Noodles: I like the thinner stringier white noodles rather than the chewier yellow noodles. Terakawa uses the thinner kind that I prefer, but to each his/her own.

    (3) Cha-Shu: Just right in terms of thinness/tenderness and could only be improved by either offering more slices or lightly searing with an acetylene torch a la Hide-Chan or Toto Ramen of NYC.

    (4) Other: Egg was half cooked/raw/creamy/awesome. Bamboo shoot was good and better than NomNom; Ginger I didnt really care about;

    (5) Ambience/service are essentially unchanged and previously rated as good.

    (6) Price was $10 for a fairly decent sized portion IMO, but you can ask for the kaedama at a reasonable price $1.50 if you need more noodle carbs.

    This place is a welcome addition to the area, but my only worry is that Ramen Boy had so tainted this space previously that I might be overestimating this newcomer. We'll see what other yelpers have to say.

    A post-op Ramen Boy is now out on the town, with improved management and a sexy new name!

    Though you might not realize it when you first walk in. Same décor, same layout, same backless, ass groove seats. The menu, however, is completely reinvented (and designed to look like a flashy ad in an Asian newspaper).

    The Terakawa ramen comes with seaweed, bamboo shoots, scallions, red ginger, and half a marinated egg (two halves if you decline the pork). I am a fan of the ginger (though they might give too much), and the egg is super tasty, even if a bit overdone. The noodles are noodles, and otherwise nondescript. The broth is where it really takes a downfall - far too fatty and not enough flavor. We took to adding chili oil, dried garlic, and white pepper to spice it up (thankful that they provided so many of these options on the table!).

    The quality of the food is definitely better than it was pre-op, but given that Hand Drawn is just a couple blocks away, I don't see myself coming back soon.

    Thank god for Terakawa Ramen for finally bringing some decent Ramen to Philly.  This is the first place I've been to (sorry Nom Nom) that truly feels like a NY-style Ramen joint: small space with no frills service that makes a quality product where all the aspects are enjoyable.

    The two of us came here tonight before a show at Electric Factory.  I opted for the signature Terakawa Ramen while he went with Mayu Ramen.  They're the same dish except the Mayu's broth is darker and tastes more bitter.  I thought the ramen was very good for Philly: delicious rich broth with noodles, juicy slices of roast pork, and savory vegetables that accentuate the soup's flavor and composition.  The noodles and bamboo shoots could have been better, but that's the Ramen purist in me coming out.

    Terakawa Ramen is a huge step up from the previous Ramen Boy. The interior looks great with the wall decals and it feels very authentic. The staff is friendly and is always ready to answer any questions. My personal favorite is the Shoyu Ramen. The broth is light and flavorful.

    The pork buns and home made gyoza are killer.
    Overall, Terakawa has trumped Ramen Boy in every category. They're even open on Mondays!
    If you want a true Japanese Ramen experience, make your way over to Terakawa.

    Listed in Noodlin'

    Finally, a real ramen place in Philly!  From the moment I walked in and was greeted by the friendly staff, the cute wooden tables, and Utada Hikaru in the background, I had a good feeling about it.  I ordered the mayu ramen and the takoyaki.  It's almost impossible to find takoyaki in this city, especially good ones, and this one did not disappoint.  There are 6 per order with a perfectly browned outside and molten inside.

    The ramen came in a large enough bowl that my husband and I were able to share it and still come out full.  The broth was a peppery, blackish grey color and was rich, almost creamy, and porky without any overwhelming saltiness.  The whole thing--the broth, the vegetables, the pork, the noodles and the egg, was very good and reminiscent of the ramen I had in Tokyo.  I usually prefer my ramen-egg to be a bit more runny, but what they did here was pretty interesting.  It seemed like a soft boiled egg, but the yolk was sweet and gelatinous and must have been processed separately.

    I would love to see them add a spicy tonkatsu ramen to the menu.  But either way I will definitely be back!

    - Food is prepped very promptly.

    - Service is decent and friendly.

    - Menus aren't slick or minimal at all. It's got big ass pictures of food, little to no descriptions, colorful fonts and just outright bombastic graphics plastered all over the menu.

    - Food choices are kind of diverse. They have curry and tonkotsu for the uninitiated hillbillies who don't want "smelly" ramen. They may have appetizers outside of the obligatory gyozas, but can't say I noticed.

    Don't think there's too many gimmicks here like kara age or chashu buns or whatever.

    - Decor is acceptable. It's no hole in the wall, but there ain't nothing hip going on here.

    - Space/size is on the smaller end, but as it stands (due to poor location), the capacity matches the demand (almost)

    ******  On to the food

    Broth - I usually go with the tonkotsu broth. It doesn't have the characteristic whitish/beige color that I expect - perhaps shoyu is mixed in? Whatever the case, it works and is delicious and just rich enough.    I also had the mayu ramen as well, which I presume was tonkotsu with a hit of the garlic/leek? oil. Very satisfying and addictive.  7/10.

    Noodles - Been a while, but I think I've only gotten straight noodles. Taste is pretty standard. Undercooked slightly more chewy than I like, but not as undercooked as in other places. This is a highly subjective category, either way  5/10.

    Cha-shu -  Pork here is good. It's thin cut, tender, and just fatty enough. The biggest downer was that it's a little on the cold side. Quantity of cha shu seemed generous if the chashu ramen is ordered.         7/10

    Toppings - Came with red ginger, nori, complimentary egg/half egg (can't remember), and *maybe* menma. It's been a while. 6/10.

    Price - NYC prices, but the quantity seems slightly more than average so overall it's a decent enough deal. But the high prices amidst the cheap eats land known as Chinatown could be a detriment.  5/10

    ******

    Would I come back again? You bet your ass I would - helps that I live very close by. This is the best tasting ramen in my opinion and they score well on all the food elements. It falls short on decor/ambience. Moreover, don't be discouraged by the smell and squalor of 9th St and Chinatown itself. Give this place the chance it deserves. Otherwise, it'll close and I won't have a place to indulge in when I feel sad =\
