    Had a wonderful dinner here with friends! It really reminded me of being on a cruise actually. We were a little confused on what the dress code was suppose to be, but they're not strict about it at all. You can pretty much wear anything from a nice dress to a t-shirt and shorts.

    We started off with the Artisan Cheese Plate and the Hawaiian Ahi Tuna Tartare as our appetizers. The cheese plate came with three different types of cheese: a Humboldt Fog goat cheese, a Rogue Smoky blue cheese and a 24-month aged Grafton cheddar. They were complemented by morsels of strawberry jam, vintage port syrup, sun-dried figs, candied pecans, quince paste and honey. The tuna tartare was recommended to us by our waiter and it was absolutely amazing - a lovely concoction of fresh yellowfin tuna, citrus soy dressing and avocado.

    For our entrées, we had the Berkshire Pork Tenderloin, Barnegat Light Diver Scallops, Herb Crusted Rainbow Trout and Crescent Farm Duck Breast. Apparently, these are pretty much the four best things on the menu (in our waiter's words), and I would have to agree. The pork tenderloin really lived up to its name (it was tender, juicy, tasty, etc.) and came with a wild mushroom and chevre crepe that I thoroughly enjoyed as well. I only got to try a little bit of the other dishes, but if I had to select the one dish that made me wonder if I should have ordered another entrée, it would be the duck breast. I'm REALLY picky about duck (the only other place I have had amazing duck at is Tangerine), but Moshulu prepared it perfectly.

    Just got back from an anniversary dinner at Moshulu.  First impression: I liked the decor.  I thought it was very appropriate for the age of the ship, so I don't know why so many reviewers did not like the decors.  It's not a "hip" and "trendy" place but it isn't trying to be.  It's more like "timeless."  I also like the mood lighting which was a little bit dim but not so much that you can't read the menu.  It was quite soft and romantic.

    We got there around 7pm, and the place was half empty.  After we were seated next to one of the windows, the place started getting filled up.  We weren't sure if the guests were local or tourists -- it didn't matter.  It did started to get really noisy but not so noisy that we couldn't carry a conversation without yelling.  We probably did have to speak louder than we wanted to though.

    Our waitress was friendly and bubbly.  We didn't have to wait long for her to fill our glasses with water or the lemonade we had.  

    There was complimentary bread but they were very tough.  The inside was soft, so I know it isn't because it was old and stale, but the crust was very chewy.  The butter that came with the bread was in little balls.  It was a cute presentation but the butter came straight from the fridge so it was hard to spread on cold bread.  So the complimentary bread didn't score high from us.  My husband says it made him feel like a cow chewing the bread.  

    The waitress (Mia) told us what the appetizer specials were and we ordered the lobster ravioli.  I liked it, hubby didn't.  It had less lobster in the raviolli than I would like, but I liked the sauce.  It came with "Rainbow Swiss Chard."  It's a bit like collar-greens but it wasn't bitter.

    My husband ordered the Crab cake and I ordered the duck breast.  I really enjoyed my meal, although, the duck's skin could be a little bit more seasoned.  The flavour seemed to be a little lost with the sides.  However, the breasts were very tender and I thoroughly enjoyed it.  Hubby enjoyed his crab cake too.  It was not breaded heavily (which he detests) and it had a nice texture.  I only had a bite of his crab cake.  I was surprised to see only one on his plate, but it was a big ("jumbo") crab cake.  However, I much preferred my duck.  It was so good!  The duck was plated with more swiss chard and topped with a mixture of swiss chard and pine nuts.  There were probably other things too... but all I can think of was: "wow... that's a lot of swiss chards I'm eating tonight."  I'm sure it's just because of the dishes I ordered came with it, but I did wonder if I could have switched out my veggies for something else.  If I could, there was no where on the menu that said I could change my sides.  I supposed I should have asked, but I didn't think of it at the time.  Maybe someone can enlighten me as to whether or not I can substitute sides.

    We didn't get a dessert because we were quite full.  The food portion looked small on our plates because the plates were big and the food were plated in an elegant way.  But the food was just enough.  I don't like leaving a restaurant feeling like a stuffed pig and can barely waddle my way out the door.  I don't like eating like that at home and I don't like eating like that when I go out.  I think Moshulu has the portion right: Enough to fill the belly but not enough to make me want to puke because I'm so full.  They also have the portion just-so so that one would want to go: "Yum... I wish I can have just one more bite."

    After dinner, we took a stroll throughout the boat.  Wow!  What an amazing view on deck!  Night was falling and the boat lit up... it was so nice and romantic.

    Yup, so, everything was really nice. The only con was the complementary bread.  Otherwise, it was a great anniversary date. :)

    Oh yes... in regards to parking.  We didn't park in the parking lot which was $15 per car!  We parked on the street which was $1 an hour at the meter.  I know it's a hit-and-miss with the street parking but we went on a Tuesday night (which I am guessing is technically a slow night although, it seemed pretty busy to me) so there were ample street parking.  Yes, we did have to walk about a block but it saved us some $$ in parking so we can buy an appetizer.  So, I think it was worth it to walk it.

    The Moshulu was built in 1904.  It sailed around Cape Horn 54 times.   It was used to haul supplies in WWI.  It made coal, coke and nitrate runs to Mexico and Chile.  It was captured by effing Nazis at one point.  It is the largest 4 masted sail boat that is afloat.  It was named by Woodrow Wilson's wife after a Seneca word that means "Dreadnaught."

    So what does Philadelphia do with it to honor her service and remarkable history?   It turns her into a themepark restaurant.  Now, I love a good themepark restaurant, but if you're gonna do it.... DO IT.  Make everything nautical.  Do it up with fish themed tablecloths.  Make your servers wear an eyepatch.  But please, please do not put up inappropriate Gauguin murals and a four foot tall plastic Buddha on the buffet table.  Do not cover poor Buddha in shiny purple fabric.  

    If you're going to go to the Moshulu for brunch, you've got to just go with it:  you're going to be presented and asked to consume the strangest mix of foods: Asian pasta salad, apple danishes, bacon, lobster bisque, lemon curd pancakes, madeleines, spinach salad, and not one but two chocolate fountains... all of this in the same meal.  

    The best thing about brunch is the unlimited champagne.  I would also say this is also the worst thing about brunch, because two hours later the four glasses of cheap champagne will, most definitely, not seem like such a good idea anymore.

    I have to say that I was really impressed by the service here.  Within seconds of drinking the last of my coffee or champagne-flavored champagne, it was immediately refilled.  The servers were extremely nice.  Almost creepily nice.  Despite the strange assortment of foods, it was similar, quality-wise, to what you would get at any vanilla brunch restaurant.  So this begs the question:  why am I paying so much for it?

    As always, after reading varied reviews about a place I haven't tried yet, I was second guessing my decision about giving the Moshulu a go. Only this time, I was taking my out-of-town guest out for a $35 Sunday Champagne Brunch and I didn't want to disappoint going somewhere I'm not familiar with. But then I remembered, "Oh, right. It's the freakin' Moshulu, a unique experience on an historic boat with unlimited champagne, accompanied by a girl friend who just wants to do something "fancy." Of course we go to the Moshulu! I live here and still haven't been, so off we went.

    Now for the part you readers actually care about:

    ~Coffee:

    Tasted just fine and was refilled to my heart's content.

    ~Champagne:

    I'm going to be bold and say (after tasting the mimosa and bellini) it's probably an inexpensive Extra Dry champagne. This is totally fine with me because I prefer that over too-sweet Asti and macho overtly dry Brut varietals anyway! So whoever complains about the champagne being cheap, I'm sorry but I suppose you'd have to be a connoisseur to really care since it's mixed up with fruit juice anyway. We dug it.
    (Also, no hangovers were experienced, as we drank our waters like good girls.)

    ~1st & 2nd course buffet food:

    Mostly hits and a few misses. But at least it's a buffet, right? The croissants (I know there's nutmeg in there!) and danishes got winning marks. The other pastries were pretty good too. Mini quiches wonderfully seasoned, but the pastry shell was lacking freshness. The bagels were cold, hard and awful. Genuardi's has better bagels, but the lox cream cheese was lovely. Who needs the carbs anyway?
    The sausage & cheese antipasto was a bit too greasy and potent in the wrong ways and didn't really go with all the other stuff around it, but the bowtie pesto was just right: light and fresh tasting with mozzarella balls and grape tomatoes. The lobster bisque was wonderful (although I secretly wished there were some solid bits of seafood in there like a chowder). Incidentally, I actually tried the bisque after I was already stuffed from the main 3rd course, but it counts as part of the 2nd so I'm adding it to this FINE paragraph.

    ~3rd (Main) course:

    I selected the filet mignon omlete [sic] which was served with jalapenos and provolone cheese and a side of wedged roasted potatoes covered with a sparkling rainbow of sauteed bell peppers and onions. The meat was great with the peppers and cheese, but I have to say I didn't bother eating the egg part of the omelet because they must have cooked them separate with no seasoning AT ALL and just covered up the meat, peppers & cheese like a lazy taco. The filet and potatoes were tasty and satisfying, it's just a shame they didn't actually cook the steak with the eggs mixed together. Flavors & ingredients = WIN, potatoes & steak = WIN, execution of "omelet" = FAIL.

    My friend had the pumpkin pancakes and they were just what you want when you order pumpkin pancakes, complete with spiced butter, cranberry garnish & a cran sauce on the plate. Just add syrup and, oh yes, it comes with sausage and bacon on the side. I tasted & was pleased. "Consume all the pumpkin things!"

    ~Dessert:

    Somehow I managed to make it to the dessert table (which should not be missed) and enjoyed the fare there. The highlights for me were the lemon tartes, the not too potent tiramisu (I don't like it too bitter and this one was delicate and fluffy), the butterscotch budino (a fine whipped pudding with crunchies at the bottom and whipped cream w/ chocolate shavings at the top), and of course the fresh strawberries dipped into the chocolate fountain. I am not really a rich chocolate person, but if you are, I can tell you the opera torte was delicious as well.

    Our fine waiter, Bora, was very attentive to our needs and was just flirtatious enough to keep us giddy. (He was really pushing those extra bellinis too lol) I daresay the other tables that were doing the girlfriend brunch thing were quite happy with their male waiters too!

    So despite the noticeable flaws in some of the items served (and the slight ache in my back from sitting at a 15° angle (it's a boat) in an overly-cushy chair that swallowed my ample rear like a failed puff pastry) I would still TOTALLY go back here to enjoy brunch and gorge myself on culinary delights for 4 hours while a cute waiter brings me coffee and fruity champagne. I bat my eyelashes like Mae West, saying "No, I simply couldn't have another...*pause* Bahaha! Ok, bring me another!"

    I had a great time brunching but I don't think I'd choose Moshulu for a dinner date.

    If you're trying to impress foodies that can't hang with a few mistakes, then by all means, forget the old timey charm of the ship and go elsewhere, but I think this was absolutely worth it and if you haven't been here yet, stop flip-flopping and give the Brunch a try. You WILL be full and you there's a good chance you WILL find SOMETHING you like. Perhaps a croissant... Perhaps a waiter! XD

    We arrived before our reservation and took a stroll around the deck.  It's pretty cool, and in very good condition.  There's also a few tidbits of info relating to the ship's history mounted along the second floor walls.  

    Dining room is a little stuffy, and it takes a bit to get acquainted with the slant of the floors.  Our server was a pretty interesting guy with an obvious passion for food.  He knew his stuff and was helpful.

    I thought they'd have a larger wine list given the size of the restaurant and more formal atmosphere.  Dogfish Head 90 Minute and Midas Touch on tap as well.

    Started with the best part of the night, the PBJ Foie Gras.  Seared atop a griddled brioche with pistachios, cherries, ginger roasted plums, and a port wine foie gras jus.  One of the best I've ever had.  Fruit flavors threatened to overrun the foie gras at times, but overall everything balanced together pretty fantastically.  Extremely good.

    Entrees were kind of rough.  I've had far better pork tenderloin.  I probably should have ordered something different, the pairing with fruit and yams / vanilla is too standard.  Midas Touch went nicely with it though.  Wife was pretty disappointed with the scallops, there was way too much salt.

    S'more was good.  Flourless chocolate torte was a good substitute for a Hershey's bar, and the smoked salted vanilla ice cream worked well with it.  Berry sundae was also a hit.

    So it was a little disappointing due to the entrees.  Somewhere between 2-3 stars.  They are often running some sort of promotion, so that brings the price down a bit into more forgivable territory.

Specialties

The Moshulu, the world's oldest and largest four-masted Tall Ship offers unsurpassed views for intimate to extraordinary dining aboard this Philadelphia landmark. .  Enjoy delectable, award-winning contemporary American cuisine by Chef Anthony Bonett using the freshest, local ingredients available from the land and from the sea paired with an extensive wine list hand selected by Sommelier, Dusan Vranic.

History

Established in 2003.

The Moshulu was purchased in 1968 in Naantale, Finland for restoration and conversion into a restaurant. In the fall of 1974, she was towed to Philadelphia and opened as a restaurant on Philadelphia's Penn's Landing in 1975 until she was damaged by fire. In 1994, the Moshulu was purchased by HMS Ventures, Inc and restored in the style of a turn-of-the-century luxury liner. The Moshulu was re-christened by Philadelphia Mayor Ed Rendell on July 24, 1996. In 2002, well known restauranteur, Martin Grims, purchased the ship as she is today.
