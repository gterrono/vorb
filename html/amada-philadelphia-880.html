    For those who don't know about Amada, the restaurant is one of Jose Garces (Iron Chef's) restaurants.  He's a recipient of the prestigious James Beard award in 2008, and word has it that this restaurant is one of the best places to get Tapas on the East Coast. At 4.5* reviews with over 900+ reviews in Philly, you can't go wrong right?  

    I've been to 3 regional winners of the James Beard Award:  first was Michelle Richard's Citronelle in DC, the 2nd was Cucharamama in Hoboken NJ, and the 3rd is this.  Unfortunately, this is strike 3 of 3 and I feel cheated for wasting money on subpar restaurants that serve mediocre food.  FYI my combined average yelp rating for all 3 restaurants is roughly around 3*.  I find yelp review are far more reliable than listening to the likes of the James Beard judges.

    Today, I had high expectations for Mr. Iron Chef's restaurant and I tried 6 dishes for brunch.  I decided to go with the traditional tapas dishes and dishes from the grill. The sixes dishes are:

    1.  PIQUILLOS RELLENOS-4*-crab stuffed peppers:  the peppers were very well cooked and soft.  It was stuffed with a creamy sauce smothered crab stuffing.  This was the highlight dish of our meal.  This was a great dish (not mindblowing).   I cannot give top accolades to this because the taste was very, very predictable: the taste was very uniform from the moment the food entered my mouth until the tasting was finished.  I really think that a bit of spice and flavoring to this dish would have worked wonders.

    2.  CROQUETAS DE JAMÓN-3.5*-we received 5 small pieces of croquettes.  The sauce was an interesting hot pepper and carrot based sauce.  It had the typical fried exterior with a small piece of ham at the center of the croquette.  Unfortunately, this dish was extremely salty. If that issue is resolved it would be a near 4* dish.

    3.  ALBÓNDIGAS-3*- reminds me of Swedish meatballs. It was in a creamy, sauce similar to what was served with the crab stuff peppers. However, there was no crab to add flavoring to this dish.  To top it off, this dish was way too salty.

    4.  Lobster-1*- for $16, I ordered 1/2 of a lobster (very small) that came with a parsley sauce that was prepared in a similar manner to a pesto sauce.  The smell, however, was similar to kale.  The lobster was...tough.  Obviously, this was very very low grade lobster.  How bad was it? I'd rather go to Red Lobster...yes you heard that right.

    5.  Calamari-2*- this was sauteed and had no flavors (oil sauteed) in the same parsley/pesto sauce as above.

    6.  Scallops-2*- low grade scallops that was very chewy and tough...it was in the same parsley/pesto sauce as above.

    Honestly, I was appalled.  About a year ago, I went on a tirade about a very highly rated Italian restaurant in Philadelphia and how they used "Ragu" spaghetti sauce for multiple dishes that were served in their pre-fixe course menu.  Bad Karma or what not, the same thing happened today at Amada with the multiple dishes and their "parsley/pesto sauce".  

    What was so disappointing about the experience is that 3 dishes used the identical sauce (the parsley/pesto sauce).  How lazy are you to recycle the same sauce for identical dishes or did I hit the lottery and happen to order the 3 dishes on the menu that used the same sauce? I wonder.

    To add to my grief, the service was subpar.  The waiter really really did not seem to care.  As a matter of fact, he did not ask once how we felt about our food.  The other servers seemed the same based on my observations.

    In preparation of those who will try to derail my credentials, send ad hominem attacks (which happened with my previous review about a particular Philly restaurant), and who think I'm a Philly hater:

    1. I''m not a Philly hater.  There are lots of places in Philly I like: Bibou's bone marrow (which I think is one of the 5 best dishes I tried on the East Coast) + Capogiro Gelato(which I think beats out on any gelato I tried in Europe or the US)

    2.  My knowledge about tapas.  I had at least 50 selected meals all with 4-4.5* ratings from European review sites and Tripsadvisor in Barcelona, Madrid, and Southern Spain including Torremolinos, Cordoba, Sevilla, Malaga, Andalucia, Granada, Marbella to name a few).  First,  Amada hits below average for a restaurant in all the aforementioned cities.  Second, while Amada may sit "ok" or "good" with most people who ate in larger Spanish cities such as Barcelona and Madrid, tapas in the larger cities don't even come close to what you can get South of Madrid.  

    For those in DC who want to gauge the food at Amada, it ranks a star below Estadio in DC.  For those who think I'm being a bit unfair of comparing Amada to tapas in Spain, I'll throw a recommendation: Seabra Marisqueria in Newark NJ.  While not all dishes are spectacular, their grilled octopus at $23.50 is the closest seafood tapas dish that resembles what I had in Southern Spain.

    We visited Philly in November on a whim, and stayed in a hotel two blocks away. I ate at Distrito this past Summer, and loved every bite, so I figured we would try Amada...

    We went there on a Monday, and we were able to get a table without a reservation - only because we came early. You should play it safe and make a reservation, because the place was packed by 6:30... on a Monday...

    Our waiter was polite, attentive, and helped us assemble our order nicely - we brought our 3 year old daughter with us, and everyone was very accommodating. Another restaurant site lists this place as a bad place to bring kids... I disagree wholeheartedly!!! They had a high chair, and all the wait staff were very sweet to her. What they did not provide for her was the one thing we absolutely cringe at when we go to restaurants with her - an awful kid's menu. No, there's no grilled cheese, chicken tenders, or Mac and cheese to clog your child's coronaries with here, and thank God for that! She rather enjoyed the gambas al ajillo, and we enjoyed introducing her to this treat!

    We decided to order in the true tapas fashion: gambas al ajillo, pulpo a la gallega, boquerones, lamb meatballs, calamari in ink sauce... And then we went back for a second round of gambas! We finished it off with their sorbet plate (chocolate, mocha, and the not to be missed lemon-rosemary sorbets) and espressos. The plates were properly portioned for tapas, and were not oversized - they were just right for tasting, without overdoing it.

    We found this place to be a delicious, fun place, and definitely recommend it to anyone going to Philadelphia. If we hadn't found Chifa, we would have definitely come back for a second visit that week - and we were only in town for 4 days! Gracias Chef!

    Will definitely be back.

    Listed in The Yelp 100 Challenge, The Splurge

    For my 75th review, I'm going to tackle one of the culinary giants in the city of Philadelphia, Amada. Now, tapas is a hot business these days and the novelty has worn out on me, personally. Tapas has been imported by the boatload and in recent decades, restaurateurs have even begun bending the rules, blending the Spanish-inspired dish with menus from other nationalities. I've been to Japanese tapas, often called 'Japas,' which would sound offensive if it wasn't titled that by the owners. And Stephen Starr's Continental is something like American tapas, just a stone's throw from Amada in Old City.

    Amada, however, is a cut above most things in the city. Jose Garces is a heavyweight contender in the city, his eyes on the belt all the time. His "Garces Group" runs some of Philadelphia's hottest tickets, from Amada to Garces Trading Co. to Village Whiskey near Rittenhouse. He has a lot of people in his corner of the ring, just as Starr has his own spartan following. If they ever go head to head, that would be a sight to see.

    On to business. I visited Amada for Philly's Restaurant Week at the end of January and the prefix menu provided by the restaurant was generous and impressive. I had high expectations, and even then, I found myself really taken aback by the quality of the food. Without a reservation, we had to settle for the Chef's Table, overlooking the food preparation area. It was a nice little spot, though, and we enjoyed watching our plates being made and then delivered. The Manchego, apples, and homemade savanella honey, along with a small helping of Tortilla Espanola, were good starters. The Espanola was, to be honest, nothing to write home about.

    The main courses included ham croquettes, short rib flatbread, traditional grilled chicken with a fried egg, and chorizo briquettes. The chorizo was only so-so, but every other item - particularly the flatbread - was incredible. We closed the night with a Spanish rice pudding with berries, nuts, and ice cream as well as a cake resembling flan, which was for all intents and purposes, flan.

    The moral of the story: the creativity of Amada's menu, even during the stripped down Restaurant Week, is nonpareil. The execution is imperfect, but such is to be expected a place that aims as high as Garces' tapas jewel. I would not hesitate to place Amada in the top 5 or 10 restaurants in the city.

Specialties

Stepping into Amada's lively 30-seat bar area, guests feel as if they have entered a typical Spanish bodega, complete with hanging jamones, a display of whole cheeses and shelves brimming with imported canned goods for sale.  

At the Charcuterie Bar, guests can create a casual meal from a selection of cut-to-order meats and cheeses served with complementary garnishes.  Offerings include:  Salchichon, a mild, flavorful sausage made from a combination of pork, garlic and black pepper;  Lomo Embuchado, a cured pork loin seasoned with smoked paprika and garlic;  Cadi Urgelia, a Spanish cow's milk cheese paired with a homemade chocolate hazelnut puree;  and Fresh Queso de Cabra, a fresh goat's milk cheese paired with balsamic-marinated strawberries.

At the bar, guests can enjoy Amada's Red and White Sangrias, served in handsome earthenware terra cotta pitchers or by the glass.

History

Established in 2005.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
