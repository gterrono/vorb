    12/31/2009

    With a hankering for a burger and prompted by a discussion on duck fat fries, I wanted to try both at Village Whiskey.

    As others have mentioned, this place is small and still pretty popular, resulting in a 30min wait (no waiting area) for a seat at the bar at 1:45pm on a weekday. It started to open up around 2:30pm.

    I ordered the top of the line, Whiskey King - an 8oz burger patty w/maple bourbon glazed cipollini onions, applewood bacon, foie gras and  rogue bleu cheese (which I recommend getting on the side so that it does not overpower the foie). When the burger arrived, it had a nice piece of perfectly cooked foie gras on top, but the bun was sitting in a pool of liquid - not ideal for the brioche bun. The liquid seemed to be a combination of the braising liquid from the onions, melting of the foie gras and oozing from the burger. Now, I don't mind a juicy burger, but I don't recall ordering a sloppy joe. Nonetheless, I brace the ginormous burger and in my best Adam Richman impression from Man vs. Food, I dive right in.

    Juicy - check
    Fresh brioche bun - check
    Subtle flavors of the foie (if you put blue cheese on the side) - check
    Best tasting burger I have had - errr.....not so fast.

    Yes, the burger was juicy and the foie flavors just speeked through on certain bites, but what was missing IMHO is the ever so important flavor of the meat itself!! The best burger I have ever had is the 40 day aged prime burger from David Burke's steakhouse in Chicago. Not because it was the most opulent burger I have had (that honor goes to the DB burger at Daniel Boulud in NYC - 36 hr braised short ribs with foie and truffles), but rather for the umami that the meat exuded. I would rate the Whiskey King burger a 7.5 out of 10 for the price. Personally, I think they can loose the blue cheese which can easily overpower the other flavors and offer it as an additional topping. Perhaps next time, I will go a more minimalist route their regular burger topped with caramelized onions and fried egg.

    On to the duck fries, which are a separate order. Nice but not particularly memorable. The seasoning on the fries makes it next to impossible to  pick up any subtle flavors the duck fat might have imparted.

    Service was friendly and efficient. The selection of whiskey and bourbons were good, but as others have pointed out on other threads, the markups are too high (900%-1500%) given that spirits have less waste than wine. Many of the higher end bourbons and single malts they offer like the Pappy Van Winkle (for $50 / 2oz) I can enjoy at home for much less.

    So in short, if you have not been, try it out but if you are a burger purist, skip the Whiskey King.

    As visitors to the city of Philadelphia, my fiance and I carefully chose which places to go to, knowing that there are so many great options. We knew Village Whiskey would be a must, because he is a big fan of Scotch, and I wanted him to be happy in spite of being dragged to a literature conference in the dead of winter.

    We waltzed in the night before New Year's Eve, and the place was packed. We told the woman at the door that we'd go to the "standing room" section, which was the BEST IDEA EVER.

    Seating tips...
    Standing there, we checked out a menu, and then a seat opened up at the "bar" (a small ledge alongside the wall next to the bar). We grabbed it (the fiance stood near me), and soon, a nearby patron left, so we both got seats! By the way, as we sat there, at one point our waitress asked if we wanted a table, and we said no-- so if you're not in a big group, I think this is the best way to "wait" for a table.

    Waitstaff
    The "waitress"/drink connosieur was EXTREMELY friendly and knowledgable about Scotch and Bourbon. We admitted that we were overwhelmed by the selection, and my fiance threw out some things he generally liked-- smokiness, a bit sweet-- and she gave us 3 solid choices for scotches and another 3 for bourbons, and didn't just steer us to the $60 glasses, either. We ordered one Scotch, one Bourbon, and shared them, and had a great time. We ordered some pretzel sticks to stave off hunger, and enjoyed ourselves, while watching the bartender make "fish sauce."

    It IS possible to go there and be served (alcohol) within 20 minutes, and if you want to sample some yummy drinks in a fun, European-esque atmosphere, go here!!

    Listed in Help! I'm stuck in the 20's and I can't get out!

    Delicious food, great cocktails, VERY limited seating... but beware: a bit overpriced.

    I finally got to check out Village Whiskey last night and I have to say it was pretty well worth it.  Diners on a budget will cringe when they see the menu...  the 2 cocktails, an appetizer, a salad, 2 entrees (the cheapest on the menu), and a dessert... the final bill was up to about $85.   However, I can say that everything was delicious... and you'll not likely often have a burger or fried quail as tasty anywhere else in Philly.

    The joint is in the same vein as the Franklin Mortage Co. - prohibition era setting/menu w/ bar seating and raised booths w/ subway tiles lining the walls.  The whiskey menu here is excellent, and is just as impressive as the juicy burgers that are quickly making this place famous.  There was a short wait on a wed. night around 7pm... but we stood in the back and ordered a coctail and appetizer (fried pickles!).  I'm sure it's nearly impossible to get a seat on the weekends.

    The food selection is limited, but well worth the visit.  I opted to try the fried quail, which was crunchy and delicious.... although the biscuit that came with it was the saddest excuse for a biscuit I have ever had.  My date went for the Village Burger... the cheaper model, which was thick and juicy.  The taste was perfect.  For those of you familiar with Five Guys (which is great, IMHO)... it was about 10 times better than that.  For dessert, we tried the apple and cranberry pie a la mode which was tart and delicious.  

    Coming here was definitely a treat... there are things on the menu I would like to try, but I can't justify the price right now ($28 lobster roll).  The duck fat fries w/ cheddar and short rib are calling me back right now, since I didn't actually get to try them... but instead had to lustfully watch the table next to us devour them.  They will be mine... oh yes.  they will... be.. mine.

    Great bar selection (especially for whiskey lovers) and tasty simple food offerings. Depending on how extravagant you are (truffles on your burger), it'll cost you but it'll be worth it.

    Ambiance: Small intimate and dim. Can be a bit tight. But this is an ideal setting for when you just want to wind down and relax with a nice glass of scotch. Either by yourself or with friends.

    Service: Speedy (they know this place is PACKED so they are helping to move things along on their end) and still friendly. Just remember you are looking at a wait of ~40min in the peak hours of post-work/weekend drinks & dinner.
    TIPS to getting a table: 1. Go early (circa 4:30/5pm) 2. Put your name down & grab a drink nearby 3. Nestle into the bar for a drink and keep an eye for any free spots at the bar / side wall spots towards the back.

    Food: Village burger has a very unique and moist texture. Juicy, flavorful & large. The meat grind/grade is really loose and fluffy. Hard to describe, best to experience it. The bun became a bit soggy for me but that meat was so darn memorable. Duck fat fries were not as crisp as I would have liked but the flavor was nicely balanced and really quite hearty.

    Listed in I Can Has Cheeseburger?, Beer: Drink, Drank, Drunk!

    First off, this place is small. Like, teeny tiny. Even at 9 pm on a weekday night, there will be a wait for a table. In a Friday night, you're looking at about an hour and a half wait for two people. But rest assured, your patience will be rewarded.

    Chad has declared Village Whiskey's duck fat fries the best he's ever had, which is a big deal since french fries are his favorite food so he orders them everywhere.

    Indeed, they are wonderful, so much so that ketchup seems like an insult. Since my first visit, the fries themselves have been scaled down in size. The portion is still the same, and they're still relatively thick cut, but the fries are no longer reminiscent of The Flintstones. I think this has has ultimately improved them, they seem crisper and less starchy. The cheese sauce is great, if you're in the mood for it, but, again, these fries don't need anything.

    The burger has remained consistently amazing and perfectly cooked. This seriously might be my favorite burger in Philly. The fat to meat ratio is perfect, as is the bread to patty ratio. Yes, I consider these things. The bun was an obviously fresh brioche, topped with sesame, I need to figure out how to make these at home. But the dressing is what takes it from excellent to incredible- it is a type of thousand island with a hint of smokiness. Seriously, I wanted to order some to dip my perfect-on-their-own fries in. And it comes with a pickle, which also tasted a bit different, in a good way, from the last time I was here. The burger starts at $9 and the price goes up as you add toppings, so it ends up about what a gastropub would charge for an inferior product. Although, the cheddar seems a bit overpriced at $3.

    For drinks, Chad stuck with Ommegang. They do have a good beer list here which had pretty much every major variety you could desire covered. I stuck with the mixed stuff, the Ginger Rogers was the highlight. Not too sweet, not too floral, not overwhelmingly gingery. It was perfectly balanced and far more potent than it tasted. Still, booze is never cheap at a Garces establishment, that seems to be where he gets you.

    The only weakness I've encountered is the desserts. Why even put something with strawberries on a winter menu? Then again, I probably wouldn't try very hard if I opened a restaurant up across the street from a Capogiro either...

    I'll definitely be back to try more items from the menu and the bar.

    My experience of Whiskey Village is definitely one marked by highs and lows.

    The good:  If you are a fan of whiskey, then this is a place made for you.  Far and away the most inclusive list of whiskeys I've seen anywhere in the Philadelphia region.  The cocktails, the two I tried, were amazing.  The starters are unique (deviled eggs, tater tots, pretzel sticks, etc), tasty, and very reasonably priced.  The atmosphere is casual and relaxing with an awesome mix of music -- some reminiscent of the 1920s, some blues.  The waitress was very nice and quite knowledgeable about the whiskeys.

Specialties

A ground-to-order Angus Beef Burger, a Veggie Burger, a Bison Burger or an Vienna All-Beef Hot Dog with all the trimmings are each served alongside duck fat French fries and Sly Fox cheddar sauce.  Burgers are topped with Village Whiskey's house-made thousand-island dressing, Bibb lettuce, beefsteak tomato and a potato-beer bun, with optional toppings including bacon, caramelized onion, truffles, artichokes and a selection of cheeses.

As one of the most comprehensive collections of its namesake spirit available in Philadelphia, Village Whiskey features a veritable library of 80 to 100 varieties of whiskey, bourbon, rye and scotch from Scotland, Canada, Ireland, United States and even a few Japanese options.

History

Established in 2009.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
