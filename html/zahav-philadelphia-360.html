    This is the perfect place if you're dining with a group. The portions are like tapas and served family style. Get the tasting menu -- it's a steal for the quality and variety of food. Another bonus -- the wine list is fairly reasonable. ($50-$70). We went as a group of 5, and with how the tasting menu works, we pretty much tried everything on the menu. The standouts? Fried cauliflower, beet salad, kugel, smoked sable, and duck hearts. The duck kabob was fabulous, as were the trumpet mushrooms. Do not skip the complimentary pickled eggplant and olives -- some of the best olives I've ever eaten.

    The point is you can't go wrong with the food. But the atmosphere puts this place at the top. How many restaurants play Jay-Z *and* Golden Earring? Out of this world. Service was fabulous, too. The manager came over to say hello, ask how we liked it, and what - if anything - they could do better. Phenomenal place to dine.

    Clearly, this place is awesome. I'm just here to confirm.

    On a FREEZING cold night, the lights and warmth of Zahav were a lifesaver. We perused the menu while hummus and laffa were served. I could have eaten about five more servings of laffa but I managed to reign it in. The hummus had a smooth, buttery consistency and had an amazing, earthy olive oil drizzled on top. We ordered drinks (The New Jerusalem Cocktail was to die for and very strong) and our meal and then the salatim arrived. Everything was incredible. My favorite was the beets. I didn't even know I liked beets but whatever they did to them rocked my world.

    After that we were served a quick succession of dishes: stuffed grape leaves with tender mushrooms; potato latke with salty salmon and paper-thin cucumbers in a cool, creamy yogurt sauce; haloumi with thin slivers of apple on top of a sweet fig sauce (my favorite dish of the evening); fried kibbe, a sort of Israeli meatball; hanger steak with eggplant and trumpet mushroom with lamb bacon. It was never ending and who would want it to? I gobbled up everything. The spices and flavors were so different and wonderful.

    For dessert we both had the chocolate baklava which is good because that meant I didn't have to share. The one thing I didn't like about the meal was the pumpkin ice cream. I didn't think it tasted like anything and I wish I'd substituted the coffee ice cream that came with the rugelach. But no matter. The chocolate baklava rolls promptly disappeared and I'm hoping to try the rugelach and coffee ice cream next time. Yum!!

    To sum up: Our waiter and service in general was fantastic. The atmosphere was romantic. The food was delicious. Mad props to Zahav.

    Listed in FYI: How to Make Some Fall in Love with You

    True story: I'm slightly envious of my Jewish friends. The whole Birthright free trip to Israel to learn about ancestry, lineage, and culture is really an awesome idea. While this ordinarily really wouldn't or shouldn't bother a prototypical gentile, I've got a problem. I'm - and forgive the reference I'm about to make - a Mischling...a diluted half-breed of sorts.

    Zahav has, unfortunately,further tantalized my desires to learn more about my Grandfather's people. I'm convinced that only the "Chosen People" could create cuisine so amazing as to be dubbed the "Chosen Food."

    The Turkish Hummus (yeah, geniuses, there ARE Jews in Turkey) is perfectly pureed. It is served in a small skillet, which ensures that the butter melted on top flows through the entire chickpea blend. The laffa bread is fluffy and warm, worthy of its own dish...and perhaps a second order.

    The Fried Cauliflower looks enough like Cauliflower to convince yourself its healthy. (This of course, goes along with the whole butter on hummus theme...) It's crispy on the outside, and soft with a buttery inside.

    For dessert, the Havlah Mousse is a God-send. No seriously. The interspersed hazelnuts (?) offer a texture that accentuates the slightly coffee-meets-chocolate whipped delight.

    So, thanks Chef Solomonov for further enticing me into visiting Israel - though I doubt any of the food there could be as delicious as yours. It's only fitting that the restaurant is called Zahav - after all, this is the gold standard of contemporary Israeli cuisine.

Every dish had a unique variety of textures, colors, and flavors.
