    I have been drooling over Yelp reviews for Honey's for months.

    After a quick ride on the El, my friend and I arrived at Honey's a little after 2 pm.  We waited 25 minutes for a small 2-top next to the entrance.

    I love this place.

    When we were considering buying a house, the proximity to this place was a factor.

    There is a lot that you can tell about a place when you consider the wait time of brunch. The wait at this place can be hours long. And we'll stand or sit or lean. It's that good.

    This place is comfort food at its best. The menu is huge and expansive. There are always specials and always stand bys and the sidedishes... oh, the sidedishes. The amazing thing is that no matter what kind of delicious sauce, or deep fry batter, or sour cream your food is smothered in, you never leave feeling gross. You never hate yourself for over-eating, even though the portions are pretty substantial. I think that's what happens when you use really good ingredients.

    And brunch is wicked busy. Try going for dinner. It's the same food and you can relax. (Bring a sweater. It gets chilly in the winter.)

    Some suggestions:

    Try the huevos.... they're lighter than an authentic huevo, but really tatsy and fresh.

    Eat the soup. They're all good.

    The rueben. You'll only need half.

    Don't leave without trying the potato latke. I'm not sure exactly what they do to it, but you'll crave them later.

    Spring for the carrot cake. You don't want to miss the cream cheese frosting.

They've manage to create a veritable Frankenstein dining experience by combining elements such as southern comfort food, all-day breakfast, locally sourced ingredients, BYOB, a diverse and varying menu, bottomless coffee, vegetarian friendly items, and economical pricing. Not only do they do all of these things but they do them very well.

    I'd really give Honey's a 3.75 rating, but since I can't, a four star is only fair if you round up, which I learned in grade school.

    If Abraham Lincoln opened a diner in his log cabin, it would look like the Honey's Sit-N-Eat's interior. Going on a Saturday morning for brunch, Honey's was crowded, and as there is really only a bit of standing space for the hostess to take your name, most eager diners-to-be wait outside for their names to be called. But during winter, Honey's has three metal patio heaters to make the wait more palatable. If you can tear yourself away from the outdoor heating, there is a mural painted on the 4th St. façade. Once you finally get inside, everything seems to be made of wood, except for the plastic tables and metallic chairs, and the traditional diner counter.

    Honey's serves breakfast, brunch, lunch, and dinner. The diners themselves were an eclectic bunch; it ranged from families with young children, college-aged students, young professionals, to senior citizens. Meanwhile to complement their diverse clientele, Honey's also plays eclectic music during your meal. Our waters and settings came immediately after we were seated, and the extensive menus were set before us. There is a slight Jewish nuance on the food, which was subtly accented by the Hebrew-like lettering of "Sit-N-Eat" on the menu. I rather liked that detail. We ordered the pumpkin pancakes with spiced pecans, scrambled eggs which came with a buttermilk biscuit and a cheese and onion potato latke, a mushroom-avocado omelet, and the peaches and berries French toast. The buttermilk biscuit came lightly buttered, and it was flaky and slightly sweet; it was easily the most enjoyable item I had. The latke was deep-fried, which was off-putting, and the filling was more like a latke quiche, so it was much denser than I preferred. The pumpkin pancakes were not as fluffy as I had expected, but they were delicious nonetheless. They were not overly sweet, but had a strong hint of pumpkin that did not overpower the palate. The French toast was the perfect amount of eggyness, but the berries came in a light compote style topping, so it made the French toast soggier faster. Finally the egg dishes (both the omelet and the scrambled eggs) were nothing special, but there were no chief complaints either. My friends had the bottomless coffee, which the waiters did come by to refill often. The service was exceptional. Though there were only three or four waiters that I could see making their rounds around the diner, they were very attentive and cheery. Overall, Honey's had decently good food across the board, but nothing really astounded me gastronomically.
