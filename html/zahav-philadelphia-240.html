        This was my first time trying Israeli food and I had a wonderful experience at Zahav!

        I really liked the interior--although the walls and tables were dark wood, the restaurant had plenty of natural light coming through large windows overlooking Society Hill. As the sun began to set, candles were lit at each table.

        All of the employees were friendly and helpful, and our waiter was very eager to answer any questions about the menu. I noticed that there were quite a few employees in general so there was no problem in getting our water refilled.

        As others have mentioned, the tasting menu is the way to go. You can share the salads, bread, and hummus, and then choose 2 small plates and 1 large plate.         I'd definitely recommend trying Zahav when you're in Philly

        Sadly, I missed Seder this year because of the crazy work schedule, but here comes Zahav to the rescue with a Seder dinner special! And who could resist, especially after Chef Michael Solomonov got a nod from the White House with his recipe contribution to their annual Seder? (tinyurl.com/4ynql9u)

        It started off with hummus and freshly made matzo - we couldn't get enough of the matzo, and we got seconds as we tore through the hummus and the next course, the fresh, flavorful salatim. This was then followed by smoky, full, fluffy matzo ball soup that was one of the best I've had (and as a former NYer, I know my matzo ball soup).

        The gefelte fish and the hamachi carpaccio were fantastic. The gefelte fish reminded me of the rich fish cakes used in Japanese Oden stew, especially since it was topped with tempura fiddleheads. Just when I was starting to feel like I could be full, the brisket came out - encrusted in matzo as if it were a beef wellington in disguise, and with a dark flavorful jus of coffee and morel base, plus sweet fresh charoset to balance the richness of the meat. I may have scraped the plate to get every last bit of the matzo crust and brisket bits.

        Just when I was considering a nap at the table to sleep off the food coma that was rapidly approaching, out came the crispy, fluffy, chewy, toasty goodness that was their giant macaroon, smelling of yummy coconut and sitting atop a pretty little pistachio ice cream (frozen custard?) and sour cherry sauce mountain. Along with the freshly pressed decaf french coffee (with a sand timer to let us know when it was ready to be pressed, a nice touch), this too became consumed in a frenzy, as I forced my stomach to expand a few more belt sizes to accommodate the sweet happiness that was the final end to our meal.

        And the service was fantastic - each plate cleared without being rushed, but without delay, and after asking if we were ready; table wiped off after each course, plates and utensils refreshed; servers that checked in, kept glasses full, and explained each dish with knowledge and detail, without being overbearing in the process. They were clearly proud of their food (as they should), and interested in customer's preferences. Our server found a bag left by diners at another table, and she ran out to catch them. First class service and food without the stuffiness, a perfect combination in my book.

        I love Seder for the gathering and the story telling, but with food this good, Zahav may have become my other Passover tradition from now on. And it won't be too long before I'll be posting an update on their regular tasting menu...

        If the world really were ending (as it didn't on the 21st) I would remember my meal at Zahav's as being one of my most delicious meals to date.  Set in a large bustling dining room in Society Hill, Zahav might appear like any other decadent fru fru restaurant that graces most metropolitan cities.  However, this hip Israeli joint serves reasonably priced tapas that are nothing less than downright sensational, and worth every penny!

        I went here two weeks ago with my family, and we had the opportunity to try a lot of things on the menu.   Originally, we were all going to get their tasting menu, which is $36 per person, but realized it was too much food.  So we all shared a hummus and veggies plate, and each got a mezze, and an al ha'esh (grilled item).  

        We had a great server who helped us carefully select our items.  Here's what we tried:

        Turkish hummus with butter & grilled garlic, served warm
        House-baked (8/12)

        Add garlic, butter and creaminess..and add some warm white beans and you're in heaven.  Eat it with freshly baked pita bread, and you'll be sure that there IS a second coming, it's just happening in your mouth.

        Combination of veggie dishes (?)

        The beets and eggplant were particularly good.  Everything tasted fresh, and combined well with the hummus

        Mezze

        Crispy haloumi dates, mustard greens, almonds (7)

        Lightly fried cheese swimming in date jam with a touch of cinnamon flavor = the perfect sweet/savory dish!

        Mina smoked brisket, matzah, coffee (9)

        Tender meat bathed in chocolate and espresso (Not really, that's just how it tasted)!

        Kibbe naya raw lamb, bulgur, black harissa, baby romaine (8)

        So soft and fresh, it's like a newborn, but not in a creepy way!  The accompanying vegetables were perfectly cooked adding a slight oniony flavor with the texture of silk.  Talk about an over all wonderful mouth feel!  Probably one of the best raw meat dishes I've ever eaten.

        Al Ha'esh

        Kofte ground beef and lamb, cumin, peppers, carrots (9)

        Fresh, light, and elegantly spiced.

        Duck breast barley, almonds, pistachios (10)

        So fresh and non-fatty we didn't think it was duck.  Something truly different.  I would say more, except that I think my family ate most of this dish.  I only got a little bite (I think that is testimony to its popularity.  Not may people can out eat me).

        Royal trumpet mushrooms chickpea puree, lamb belly "shawarma", allspice (10)

        One word, Lamb bacon...no wait....mushroom love!  Ok, go with both of those.

        So as you can see, I really enjoyed my food at Zahav's.  I can't wait to go back!

        Two or three stars, that is the question. I've wanted to eat here for quite some time, and I was excited to finally get my chance. The staff was very accomodating, finding us a table when we walked in at 5:30 on a Saturday. They first gave us 3 seats at the Chef's counter, which was fine but quite hot, due to its proximity to the kitchen. We were then offered a table, if we could finish our meal in an hour, and we accepted. The restaurant is very attractive, with stained glass windows, and a giant picture of the Shuk HaCarmel to make Israelis feel at home.

        Disclaimer: This is not Israeli food. It's an Israeli owner's vision, but the food is absolutely not representative of what we eat in Israel, in portion size, combination, or flavor.

        Much of my displeasure with Zahav had to do with our server. He attempted to explain the cuisine to us, and I say "yes, we're Israeli." To which he replied "right, but this isn't really Israeli food, it's....." Now would you do that to anyone else? I can't imagine a Sushi restaurant telling Japanese people what the food should be like, a French restaurant talking down to people right off the plane from Paris, etc. If our server had known what he was talking about, ok, but he couldn't even pronounce Zhrug, the ubiquitous Yemenite hot pepper paste. And yes Mr. Server, I know what zatar is.

        We started with the mint lemonade, which lacked any real flavor, and the salatim (salads in Hebrew), a selection of bite sized portions that were one of the better offerings of the night. A notable exception was one of my favorites, the Moroccan carrot salad that had none of the spicy flavor that I am used to. Now on to what I came here for, the hummus, or in my case masabacha, which is hummus with whole chick peas. It's usually served warm, but this wasn't, and unfortunately this was a really inferior dish. The ratio of tahini to chick peas was really off; masabacha isn't 30% tahini, more like 5%. To make it worse, the bread wasn't even good, far too crispy, almost cracker like in places. Unevenly cooked bread in an Israeli restaurant that talks about being inspired by Abu Lafia and the Ali Caravan?

        As our server reminded me (which I interpreted as an attempt to get me to order more food), the food is tapas style. My wife ordered kofta that came on a bed of spicy pepper relish, and this was a very good dish. I'll give credit where credit is due. My daughter loves eggs, so we got her the mezze of fried challah and egg with smoked sable. Honestly, the sable was good but very very salty. It could have been whitefish for all that I could taste it. The rest of the dish was unremarkable.

        Now we journey into the twilight zone. I wanted the merguez sausage with shakshuka, but not the accompanying couscous. I asked my waiter if I could substitute something else for the couscous and he told me that if I didn't like the couscous, I could just push it over to the side. Yes, this really happened. I could laugh that off in Israel, but in an expensive Philly restaurant? I ordered the hangar steak instead, which came on a bed of eggplant puree, and topped with some kind of diced vegetables. It had little flavor, but the meat was well cooked.

        The rest of the wait staff was very attentive, bringing more bread, putting up with my 3 year old daughter's shenanigans, etc.

        So, to end the night, I tracked down the Israeli Maitre'D and told him how disappointed I was in our server, but that everyone else was great. He apologized, and gave me some discount tickets for our next visit, as well as a free (but incredibly underwhelming) serving shakshouka.

        No dessert, no wine, no coffee for us. The bill was $73 with tip, and I paid $11 to park 2 blocks away.  

        What a shame. I had high hopes for Zahav, but I wish that I had saved my money and gotten a cheese steak instead.

In retrospect, we should have ordered the fried cauliflower- it looked good at the table next to us and I read it was delicious, but we honestly didn't know how the food would be served (as it was ready, not in any particular order, and it didn't matter who ordered what because it's served tapas style and meant to share- perfectly fine by us!); Grilled Chicken Livers (with barley mujadara, gribenes, and sumac) were so smooth and seasoned to perfection. I don't know what the stuff it came with is really, but it was basically like a well-cooked bulgar wheat or close to a pearled couscous- it was delicous, that's all I know!

        For the Al Ha'esh, we had the Kofte (ground beef and lamb meatballs with cumin, peppers, and carrots) and they were okay. Not that flavorful. If they'd used the carrots from the Tayim, they would have had a real winner! The Hanger Steak was just as good as we hoped it would be and came with eggplant two ways, sesame, and pine nuts. It was also a great dish to share and was cooked to perfection. The Crispy Branzino (fish) with braised legumes, green beans and spicy tomato was just okay- I did eat the crispy skin, much to my sisters dismay, and the fish was left mostly uneaten. We would have liked more of the veggies and less of the fish in this one.

        Now, on to dessert! We had a round of Turkish coffee, which was strong and slightly sweet (maybe?)- it was fun! My dessert was the best of the bunch- Cardamom Vanilla Custard with carmalized banana and phyllo crisp. We also had the Kataifi, which was a little strange but still good, and one that doesn't seem to be on the menu, which was a rhubarb dessert with something like white chocolate granola and some pistachio ice cream- it was pretty good, but some busser came by and snagged the plate before I could finish off the rest of the rhubarb- NOT COOL!

        All in all, a fun tapas place to try. Like most, you will end up spending more than you thought you would. I'd say it's worth it though. Especially a nice place for a date with an adventurous eater!

        Zahav asserts that the soul of the restaurant is found in its Laffa Bread, which is made to order in the restaurant's taboon oven. I can recommend the Turkish Hummus, which is made with butter and grilled garlic and served warm in a skillet - it's magical.

        As for the Al Ha'esh section (for grilled proteins and veggies), I enjoyed the Kofte, which is a dish of tender beef/lamb meatballs. The Duck Breast and Spiced Eggplant were fine, but nothing to write home about.
