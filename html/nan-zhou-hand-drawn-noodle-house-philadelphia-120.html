    The first time I ever saw the place, believe it or not, was after a fire had torn it apart back in August, 2009. There were bowls of noodles sitting on the tables, all alone, and water dripping from the ceiling. This was the only time I saw the restaurant empty.

    Like a phoenix, the noodle house arose from the ashes.

    Having lived in southwest China, I can testify that the bowls served here are authentically delicious. I am partial to the yangrou mian, the lamb noodles, and I prefer the thicker, shaved noodles.

    You get a sizable bowl of noodles and meat (on the bone, real Chinese style...) in a savory broth, replete with pickled vegetables and scallions. For around six bucks, this bowl is filling and satisfying in a way that defies simple description.

    The staff is pretty friendly, too. Overall, my favorite restaurant in Chinatown--though the cash-only policy is a little inconvenient.

    NOOOODLES!!!!!!!!! IN SOOOUUUUP!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!!

    My favorite thing in the world!

    First lunch in Philly and first official food stop on Philly food crawl brought us through an especially animated Chinatown (4th of July apparently makes us Chinese break out the....Dragon dance? Chinese operas? Cooking demos????). Ten minutes of politely wending through the crowds and we finally made it in here.

    The boyfriend and I are avid noodle soup fans and he was just plain delighted by the opportunity to watch real noodle-makers making noodles...by hand! I love this place for being so Taiwanese. The staff speak Taiwanese Mandarin, they serve pickled vegetables on top as garnish the way only Taiwanese people do and the menu has a big emphasis on beef noodle soups. Ahhh! I'm in Taiwan heaven!

    I got the beef brisket and the boy got the sliced beef. Now, you have to understand that beef noodle soup is the comfort food, the slice of pizza, the pint of Ben & Jerry's, the mac 'n' cheese of Taiwanese cuisine. And just like those wonderful items, beef noodle soup can come in many forms. At its most unhealthy, it is thick and hearty, spicy and slightly greasy with fatty chunks of tender beef. That's my favorite version of it.

    But I am not averse to its more healthful cousin. The light, almost clear, flavorful broth, the low-fat cuts of beef, the healthy spinach, the utter lack of grease or spice of any kind. This is what Nan Zhou serves. And it's so delicious. It's like chicken soup for the soul. The Taiwanese soul. And I can't believe it's under $6 for a piping hot bowl of this. Oh God, I wish I could bring this restaurant back to Boston!

    I have a "bucket list" of places that I've been meaning to try in Philadelphia for a long time. This place is one of those mysteries, because it's pretty unknown and doesn't have a web presence like so many other places.

    I ordered the Roast Duck Soup with Shaven Noodles. I witnessed them shaving the dough in the back and I have to say, the noodles were really fresh and delicious. Unquestionably, the best noodles I've had in a Chinese restaurant in Philadelphia. When I received the soup, the total came to $6.20. For that price and the freshness of the noodles, I think they earn 3 to 4 stars on the Yelp scale.

    I will say that when it came to broth and vegetables, that this place underperformed for me. Sure, the broth had great clarity and lightness as others have described, but it really lacked the meatiness (umami) that I expect from say, for example, the soup at Sang Kee. Additionally, the broth had some pickled vegetables and a ton of cilantro in it. Within about 2 minutes, my broth went from pretty good to totally overpowered with vinegar and cilantro flavor. I love cilantro and pickled vegetables, but it just doesn't fit quite right in this broth, which is why I think I have to give 3 stars. I did forget to mention that there was a generous amount of roast duck given the cost and that was quite tasty.

    Will I be back? Yes, I think I'll give them another shot, though next time I'll try the meatball and vegetable dumplings and I'll ask them to hold off on the pickled veggies at least, maybe even some of the cilantro.

    My favorite soup is Roast Pork Wonton Noodle Soup with Vegetables at Sang Kee. If they could have these hand shaven noodles in that soup, it would undoubtedly be a powerful combination.
