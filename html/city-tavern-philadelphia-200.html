    We decided to stop here for lunch after a morning spent exploring the historic sites nearby and had a very enjoyable meal.

    Our server was friendly and very attentive and the atmosphere was touristy but fun. Our daughter loved the costumes and unique dishware.

    The meal was bit pricier than we are accustomed to - the lunch menu prices were more comparable to what we usually pay for dinner, and the dinner prices made me blanch a bit - definitely a date night destination and not for everyday dining. However, the portions were generous.

    My husband ordered the veal sausage, which he said was good (I don't eat veal) and which came with some very tasty sauerkraut. I had the turkey pot pie, which had a nice buttery crust and a hearty filling. It came with egg noodles on the side, which were fine but didn't really add anything special. Our daughter had fish and chips, which were good, too.

    We had the Martha Washington chocolate mousse cake and the pumpkin cheesecake with candied pecans for dessert and enjoyed both. We also liked the bread sampler, which included Sally Lunn bread, Anadama, and Thomas Jefferson's favorite sweet potato pecan biscuits. The biscuits were my favorite, and tasted a lot like pumpkin bread.

    City Tavern has risen like the phoenix from its ashes. Built by subscription in 1775, City Tavern's… City Tavern has risen like the phoenix from its ashes. Built by subscription in 1775, City Tavern's tables fed the founding fathers. In 1834 the tavern fell to flames. Ten years later the building was demolished. Then, in our century, City Tavern was reconstructed for the 1976 Bicentennial. Today's owner and television cooking show celebrity, Walter Staib, has restored the kitchen's fare with rich, opulent and satisfying colonial delights.
    Our hostess, in colonial dress, directs us to a small dining room on the left with about ten tables. There are other dining rooms, but each is situated such as to go unnoticed. The décor is Quaker plain. Old maps decorate the pale gray walls. The dark unmatched period chairs arch their backs with classic dignity and invite the diner to sit at tables set in quiet elegance with double cloths, a lower white offset with a dark green above, Chinese blue lay plates, hearty silver ware, linen napkins, and pewter goblets.
    This is restaurant week. We opt for the $20 prix fixe. We are three for lunch. Before we order we propose that the three of us will be sharing our selections.  To meet our request we ask for extra plates.  There is no question or objection to our request. In fact, the server seems quite pleased. He notes that we were following the historic colonial custom of communal dining.
    For our first course we requested two orders of the onion soup to be divided three ways and one order of the Russian Eggs which we would also share. For the main course we chose the Sausage Wellington, the Sally Lund Chicken and the Beef Pie.  For libations, as they are called, we took a very pleasant warm cider, a spicy wassail and tea. I would also note that while we are not beer drinkers, the gentleman at the table next to us ordered the Colonial Ale Sampler, a narrow wood tray set with four shades of golden goblets that looked most appealing. While we waited the server brought a very full dish of three very fine breads including a small pumpkin biscuit from a Jefferson recipe.
    The eggs arrived first. While the menu offered only two eggs, our server brought us three for ease of sharing. While the eggs were certainly pleasant, particularly because of the garnish of black caviar, they were picnic style hard boiled eggs. I would have preferred an egg closer to something poached with a soft center. The onion soup was a bit on the cold side and we asked for a reheating. Once back, however, the soup was rich with onion and beef. The toast finger added texture but there was no cheese, no gratinée, the element that gives this soup its name in French.
    Barely had we finished the soup when the server was there to clear our pewter bowls and bring the main course. The service was so quick, that we asked if we might move a bit more slowly. The main courses arrived as though they had been served from an 18th century groaning board laden with the most sumptuous creations.  The portions were the most generous I have seen in any restaurant. The Beef Pie's sweet and flaky crust melted to release the sweet cabbage, mushrooms and tenderloin in the most luscious and tender interweave of taste.  Four very large sausages in a French pastry crust crowned a large mound of delicate linguine garnished with yellow zucchini. The sausage casings snapped crisply between the teeth and gave way to creamy veal stuffing that suggested something like a kielbasa. Chicken breast is often just chicken breast. But chef Staib's colonial kitchen transforms this otherwise common victual to a Rabelaisian gourmandise.  The ample breast rests upon a heaping mound of country style mashed potatoes, complete with tiny lumps, and wears a lush blanket of creamy béchamel, asparagus and zucchini.
    Fully sated by the three main courses and warmed by the cider and wassail we found ourselves in that state of epicurean bliss. Then, our server approached with a long tray in hand. This was dessert. In a sinful display of pastry delights we were to choose our three from no less than six irresistible temptations. Our choices: the Martha Washington chocolate mousse, the raspberry tart with vanilla ice cream and the apple crisp with ginger ice cream. The shared desserts turn in a kaleidoscope of sinful pleasure. The crust of the tart recalls the sweetness of Christmas cookies, the deeply strong raspberry enhances the under the tongue savor of the delicate chocolate mousse. The melting ice cream plays against the crisp full apple slices. By sharing these three decadent delights the taste on the tongue bedrugs the head into a sugar coma.
    City Tavern has often been cast aside as something of a tourist trap with its quaint furnishings and colonial garbed staff. But I would suggest that this is a kitchen with something to say. No, it is not "cuisine." It is "kitchen." There are no chipmunk portions, no deconstructions, no self-important wait staff. Chef Staib has created a glimpse into the hearty foods of another era that please with honest appeal.     Read more

Specialties

City Tavern restaurant is dedicated to recreating an authentic 18th Century dining experience with every little detail, from costumed servers to serving the "Ales of the Revolution," beers brewed down the street at Yards Brewing Company using the very same recipes that America's forefathers did, including Thomas Jefferson and George Washington's recipes for tasty brews.
The menu is comprised of authentic recipes that people ate in the 18th Century in Philadelphia, from cornmeal battered oysters, Colonial turkey pot pie and Martha Washington's chocolate mousse cake.

History

Established in 1773.

City Tavern opened its doors at a time when ale was flowing, revolution was brewing and dining was an experience.
Men Like George Washington, John Adams, Thomas Jefferson and others whose names have become an integral part of history enjoyed its hospitality.  Indeed, John Adams once called it "the most genteel tavern in America."
Today City Tavern still captures the atmosphere and ambiance of its 18th century beginnings.  Colonial Philadelphia comes alive when you enter its doors.  With this refined and elegant atmosphere, our chefs and waitstaff have prepared a unique dining experience for you to savor and enjoy.
Exceptionally prepared cuisine, attentive professional service and authentic Philadelphia history combine the best of the past and the present, in what is sure to be a memorable dining experience.

Meet the Business Owner

Walter S. Business Owner

Proprietor Chef Walter Staib is the host of the Emmy Award winning PBS cooking show A Taste of History, the cooking show that portrays what America's founding fathers ate and how they cooked. He is an expert on 18th Century cuisine and hearth cooking.
Chef Staib brings the City Tavern's history to life and lends his wealth of culinary knowledge in his cookbooks: City Tavern Cookbook (1999), City Tavern Baking & Dessert Cookbook (2003), Black Forest Cuisine (2006), and City Tavern: Recipes from the Birthplace of American Cuisine (2009). His first children's book, A Feast of Freedom: Tasty Tidbits from the City Tavern, introduces young readers to the Founding Fathers and tells the story of City Tavern.
