    Honestly, everything was alright.  The flavor of the broth, the herbs, the veggies, and the marinated beef tendon added to the flavor of the dish.  My biggest concern came from the noodles.  They were chewier than before and had a slightly raw flavor.  They were also slightly thicker than before.  Maybe we just had a bad batch of noodle.

Lan Zhou-- you'll do in a pinch, but you're not best in show.

    Though similar in name, Lan Zhou and Nan Zhou are not the same.  Where to start?  Let's go with service.  We walked into the restaurant and stood at the entrance waiting to be seated.  When it looked like the staff wasn't paying any attention to us, we went ahead and sat ourselves.  When it looked like we were still being ignored, I went up and grabbed menus from the register for ourselves.  Finally, when it looked like we weren't go to get our orders taken, I flagged down one of the waitresses and told her we were ready.  Nan Zhou is like the New Jersey of gas stations - full service, and Lan Zhou is like other states - no service.

    Because I wanted to compare Lan to Nan, I ordered what I normally order at Nan - fish ball soup.  The fish balls and broth here tasted very similar to those at Nan, so no differences there.  The noodles on the other hand appeared to have been sitting somewhere, cooked, for quite a while.  They were stuck together and I had to use both my chopsticks and spoon to pull them apart - that is, unless I wanted to eat them like they were lasagna.

    Since both Lan's food and service were not on par with those at Nan (pricing is similar at both places), I will be returning to Nan and saying goodbye to Lan.

    A rare thing it is to find a well made noodle, and Lan Zhou Hand Drawn Noodle House is indeed a flawless diamond in a sea of coal. Being northern Chinese I have a very strong opinion of hand drawn noodles, and the ultimate test was the thick noodles with Lamb. When done correctly, it is near impossible to screw up the lesser degree of difficulty of thin noodles with duck and easier dishes. To explain that statement one only needs to remember the difficulty in maintaining the texture and structure of a thick noodle, while containing the essence of a lamb broth without overpowering one with a gamy feel.

    To this end, Lan Zhou did not disappoint. The noodle was firm, and provided that xian flavor that all good broth has. The ambiance is what one would expect in a row style restaurant in the middle of Chinatown, and is homely yet immediately transported back in to a restaurant in Beijing. The only reason I have not given this place a 5 star is the pig ear/intestine/tripe combo, as it did not have enough seasoning. Otherwise a great place for any in Chinatown.

    This place boasts their hand drawn noodles, which are done quite nicely.  They aren't the best I've ever had, but definitely better than your run-of-the-mill hand-drawn place.  Although their noodles seem to be maybe a dollar or two more expensive than other places in the area, you're definitely getting more than you pay for by coming here for several reasons.

    Firstly, the size.  After a day of snacking through Chinatown, I had really underestimated the amount of food that I thought they'd give to us.  They give you a heaping portion of noodles, and they pile on the sliced beef and brisket, or whatever else you happen to order.

    Secondly, and maybe most importantly, their broth is VERY well done.  This could very well be a selling point by itself, as the taste of the broth is smooth, not oily at all, and is simply appealing to all five senses.  I've been to many noodle soup, ramen, pho, etc. places, but the broth/soup here is definitely ranking highly of all the ones I've ever had on the East Coast.

    Service is also quick and friendly, a nice bonus that isn't always a guarantee at small Asian-run restaurants.  All this put together definitely makes this a more than remarkable place to try.  I'll definitely be back next time I'm in Philly.

History

Established in 2011.

When the owner cooked the first meal of freshly made noodle soup for her daughter after 15 years of separation, the daughter was so excited to taste the authentic Chinese food in U.S., she asked her father, what's "tasty" in English. He told her: "yummy", so the entire meal, she was keep saying: "Dad, this is 'YUMMY'!". that was the first English word the little girl ever learned, and of course, it became the name of the store when this first opened.
When the girl grown older, she went to many other countries, got to tried and learned many fine asian cuisines. When she took over the store, she rename the Store: "Spice C" (Spices by Cecilia) her passion for Freshly made, authentic Asian cuisines, expecially hand made noodle and dumplings. You can find them here to satisfy your taste bug.
