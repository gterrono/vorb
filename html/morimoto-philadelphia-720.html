    It may not be Always Sunny in Philadelphia, but the food is consistently good!

    My wife and I finally made it to Morimoto in Philadelphia tonight.  One of my really good friends worked with none-other-than the Iron Chef himself as a sommelier when the restaurant opened 12 years ago.

    We were interested in trying the Omakase (their tasting menu), which translates from Japanese as "do as you like".  But we really wanted to try something different than the typical Japanese fare.

    We had our eye out for the Ishi Yaki Bop Buri dish, which was Morimoto's take on the Korean Bimimbop.  It's a rice dish prepared at the table by a waitress in hot river stone bowl cooked with medium-rare king yellowtail.   The sauce was what really set it apart from your average Bimimbop - a tasty combination of sake, mirin, shoyu, and garlic.  It's not an unusual combination in Japanese cooking, but definitely is not the chili pepper bean paste normally found on the traditional Korean dish.

    The best way to my wife's heart is a good cut of steak.  And so we had to try the wagyu steak at medium rare.  Two ounces set us back $40.00, but it was oh-so-worth-it.  We also tried the Wasabi Fried Rice and two nigri pieces of Hamachi yellowtail.  They were nothing to call home about. We also tried one of their specials: Japanese Soba Risotto cooked in a Pumpkin Ragu.  It was very distinctive, but not too delcious.

    My wife also loved the side of sautéed Japanese mushrooms.  Although the menu said these Enoki mushrooms were sautéed in sake and butter, they distinctly tasted more of white wine than sake.  There was also a nice touch of miso and beef stock in the sauce.

    Overall, our experience was, indeed, very expensive but around the ballpark of what you would expect from a joint like this.  Plus, it has inspired for me to try some new things in the kitchen.

    Morimoto makes an incredible first impression. The restaurant's atmosphere features enthralling wavy ceiling patterns and booths lit with soft, glowing lights. The ambiance is simultaneously exotic and comfortably luxurious. Our group ordered several platters of sashimi, entrees, and appetizers to share.

    At restaurants, I typically enjoy appetizers the most because they can be truly unique. The appetizers at Morimoto were mediocre, nothing was memorable at all and the portions were even tinier than I had expected, only large enough to share between a couple members of our party. Disappointing. Now, onto the most important part of the meal: the fish. There's not much to say except that it is incredibly fresh. Each piece I sampled had perfectly mild flavor and impeccable texture.

    I'll award just two stars overall, though, because the whole concept is borderline ridiculous. Over the years, I've learned that the best sushi restaurants stand by nothing more than a simple commitment to quality fish. Morimoto, on the other hand, charges two arms and a leg for its pretentious atmosphere. The service was barely acceptable, not friendly and definitely not worthy of the name tag.

    Overall, OK, but next time I'll go elsewhere.

    It's Iron Chef Masaharu Morimoto!  This man is legendary and being a Japanese food fiend, I was a kid-in-a-candy-store ecstatic to try his food. This definitely was one of the better meals of my life.  More than that, it was a four hour long eclectic experience.

    First off, the ambience is very unique and aestetically pleasing, It's modern contemporary with an ever changing color scheme throughout the evening.  The hostess and server were great; very attentive and knowledgeable.

    I ordered the upper-tier omakase tasting with beverage pairing.  

    I started off with the amuse bouche of Japanese sweet potato soup with lobster and chives.  It was served on a soup spoon and it was one mouthful of a$$-kicking heaven.  The lobster melts in your mouth while the sweet potato soup is an explosion of flavor while the chives add a nice texture to the bite while contributing to the olfactory receptors.

    My favorite dish of the evening was the toro tartare topped with cavier.  It's served in a bowl with a brown sauce lining the bottom. It has a typical tartare texture, crumbling in your mouth, while the added cavier differentiates the dish from a typical tartare. The sauce is the surprise star of the dish, infusing the protein with great acidic flavor.

    A trio of raw oysters (salsa, thai ceviche, and jalepeno) really demonstrates a master of their craft.  Taking three oysters and dressing them yielding such diverse a trichotomy of flavors.

    The red snapper in an aoili and vinegrette emulsifies and melts the red snapper in your mouth.  

    Kanpachi sashimi salad. The salad was a little bland for me but it was a nice contrasts and complement to the sashimi.

    The lobster was just okay for me.  It was good but you could definitely find something comparable for a much lower price tag elsewhere.

    The wasabi encrusted braised kobe beef short rib in a red wine reduction.  The red wine reduction had a strong affnity for the vegetables and made for a nice, yet somewhat soggy explosion of flavor. The kobe beef was unique with the wasabi elements and buttery soft and supple.  

    The assortment of sushi was defnitely tasty and of high quality but nothing to quite write home about.

    The japanese chocolate torte was three layers of decadence. It wasn't too sweet but just right to stimulate the tip of your palate.  

    I don't remember all the different spirits served with each dish but they all melded well with each particular dish.  I was introduced to one of my new favorite beers.  The Rogue Brown Ale is awesome.  

    I also came for lunch and ordered the Ishi Yaki Buri Bop.  It's served in a stone basin and prepped in front of you which is a nice experience.  Reminds me of an asian fricasse. I thought it was just adequate and was honestly disappointed after my prior experiences with the omakase tasting...

    Bottom line, order the omakase!
    FYI: My fellow patron ordered the lower-tier omakase and the difference in ingredient quality was noticeable.

    WOW!

    I watched the documentary in 2001(?) of this place being built and wanted to go ever since! I was living in Mass at the time, just a teen and asked my Mother to take me to look at Drexel and to eat at Morimoto. The answer, of course, was no.

    Flash forward for a few years, Mom moves to Philly, I fly down to visit her for my birthday and when I get picked up from the airport I'm told I have a surprise waiting for me. We pull up to Morimoto and thus the review begins (post inhalation of course)...

    Being an avid fan of the original Iron Chef with Sakei, Kenichi and Morimoto I had VERY HIGH expectations for the restaurant. Mostly because I always cheered for Morimoto and loved his commitment to tradition but with a fresh prospective.

    Upon entering the restaurant it was exactly how I remembered it...in my dreams. I had already studied the floor plan manically and was simply in awe of the visual transition from my memory to reality. My mom and step dad looked on with joy as I reverted to a five year old at Chuckie Cheese. We were early for our reservation so we opted to sit in the bar that overlooks the restaurant. Conversations were amok, the restaurant looked like a massive and loud cafeteria - only with class and flair. I found as I waited this ambiance made what must have been a Michelin Three Star much more inviting.

    Once it was time to eat we were brought down to the fiberglass interlocked tables which had been adjusting its palate from blue to purple to green to orange and so forth seamlessly. It felt as though you were going to be sitting "with" your neighbor when viewing the layout from the bar but once in the calamity of it all, you felt like your table was your own universe; oblivious to the colors of the fiberglass and the noise surrounding you. Sheer bliss!

    Our waitress quickly arrived at the table in what looked like a black, one-piece Star Trek villains outfit; an excellent aesthetic - I must compliment. Over our two and a half hour, ten course dining experience we felt very well attended to and for the price you pay - you should!

    My family is very much the "family style" eaters and I recommend here you try and do the same; bearing in mind portions are very Japanese (i.e. - small). My step dad chose the omakase. Omakase means "in you I trust" or "left to you" in Japanese. It is a big compliment to the chef as you do not choose your dishes, they do! Most Americans I think may be adverse to this but I highly recommend it. Omakase usually will feature the chef's favorite fresh food for that week or night along with some staples and palate appeasing in-betweens. Sometimes you make out and save a lot of money getting this, sometimes you do not - all depends on what is freshest that week. Please refrain from asking what will be featured in the omakase. Although the server is likely American, in Japanese culture it is considered disrespectful and insulting to question the chef when you have "entrusted" your meal to them. Don't like something? Remember that and keep it to yourself - do not tell your server, that is another faux paus. Just be proud of yourself for going outside the box.

    My favorite dishes were the cold green tea noodles and the kobe beef carpacio (sp*). The latter was perfect! Small pebbles of delectable beef just serenading your palate like the sirens' song, drawing your taste buds to the chopsticks as though they were the cliffs. The cold noodles are a Japanese summer favorite and one I crave during the warm weather. The flavor of the tea really opens up when the noodles are served on ice and the ginger-soy blend is the ideal accent to spruce them up a bit but not distract from the simple flavors of the noodles.

    Morimoto is a great restaurant for a date but an even better one for friends. My parents are very much my friends and I found the quality of this particular dining experience to rival all other upper-echelon eateries we've tried in my lifetime. It was surreal for me to take a show I watched adamantly to support my favorite celebrity chef and watch as he built the restaurant of his dreams only for me to one day have the privilege of dining there.

    Save your money and splurge, make it a goal to enjoy this cuisine with four or five of your closest friends and disappear from time and worries while you surrender to good laughs, great company and fantastic food.

    KANPAI! GOCHISOSAMA DESHITA!
