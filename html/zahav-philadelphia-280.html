    Listed in Fine Dining in Philly

    Hearing that Chef Michael had just won a James Beard Award, I had to stop on my trip to Philly. Though I was being offered a table, I asked for a seat at the kitchen bar. This seat allowed for a full view of the kitchen during the meal. AWESOME. As the waitress was about to describe the menu, I mentioned that I was going to have the tasting menu and which dishes I would like to try. (Thanks to fellow yelpers for their suggestions.) I also placed my order for a Tiyul which was delightfully refreshing. Before my drink was delivered, I was provided with complimentary pickles and Israeli olives and Israeli eggplant. The eggplant was a great little bite.

    The salatim then arrived, which is a sampler of the 8 salads of the day.
    1 - Cucumber Salad
    2 - Beets with Tehina
    3 - Taboulleh
    4 - Twice Cooked Eggplant
    5 - Cabbage
    6 - Pickled Turnips
    7 - Moroccan Carrots
    8 - Feta Salad
    During the salatim, I was also provided the hummus tehina with laffa.

    Kibbe naya (Raw lamb)
    Watermelon Salad (PHENOMENAL! I want to recreate at home.)
    House smoked sable (Complimentary dish. I tried a bite of the fish and toast, but shared the rest with the couple next to me as I'm not a fan of egg dishes.)
    Duck Breast

    Here is where the service goes up a notch. The waitress, Emma, was really building up the Halvah mousse dessert. I decided on the Almond-apricot rugelach. When she delivered my dessert, she also brought the Halvah mousse. She said, "I got carried away." Both were great and I think I liked hers better, though the apricots were a wonderful throwback to my childhood when we had an apricot tree.

    At the end of the meal I was provided with a list of the salatim, as I had requested earlier in the evening. Throughout the meal, various managers stopped in to check up on me, though not in a rude suffocating way. I had great conversations with all of the staff which contributed to an unforgettable evening.

    Went here for restaurant week awhile back...  we started off with a tower of "Salatim" consisting of eight Israeli and Middle Eastern salads such as tabouli, cucumber, okra, carrot, and others that I can't think of; all really good, some better than others - the only one we didn't really care for was the wax bean salad which was really vinegary. Also some warm laffa flat bread w/hummus and olive oil that was really really delicious. We washed all this down with a refreshing glass of cava.

    Then each of us got to choose two mezze, or small plates (4 total), which we shared. First was fried haloumi cheese with a delicious date/pine nut spread (it looked like purple peanut butter). Next dish was deep fired cauliflower with a creamy dip of chive, dill, mint and garlic - this was incredible and probably the highlight for me. Next these things called "Kibbee" which are basically deep fried balls of spiced bulgar wheat stuffed with ground lamb. It kinda reminded me of a highly seasoned corn dog but with lamb inside - very good. Last were deep fried crispy sweetbreads which only I ate - also good.

    Then we each got to choose a dish of "Al'Haesh" or "grilled over coals", which we also shared. First was house made merguez sausage with cous cous and this spicy tomato sauce. Really good! Second was two think slices of grilled eggplant with some kind of green tahini sauce. Decent but not outstanding.

    Lastly was desert - cashew baklava which was out of this world and also some sort of layered tahini crème brulee pudding thing (recommended by our waiter) which was one of the most delicious and interesting deserts I've ever tasted.

    They also have a very interesting wine selection of Israeli and Moroccan wines that I knew absolutely nothing about. I selected one of the less expensive bottles of Cabernet-Syrah blend (also our waiters recommendation) which was light and fruity; kinda ho-hum but simple and versatile enough to pair with the many different dishes and flavors we sampled.

    Overall it was a very good and interesting meal. The servers were all really nice and the room is cozy with big windows looking out over dock st. The restaurant week menu was quite substantial (I was pretty much stuffed when we left) BUT the savory dishes all had a sort of generic quality to them. i.e. either cold salad type dishes or highly spiced deep fried items that were prepared ahead of time and just tossed in the deep fryer before serving. That cauliflower dish, however, was really, really good.

    I also have such a prejudice against restaurant wine lists that it kills me to order wine at a restaurant. The Cava, at $9 per glass, was pricey for an inexpensive Spanish sparkling wine that typically sells for around $15 a bottle. I'd say the bottle of red we had probably cost around $10 retail yet it was marked up to $48. Ugggg. This practice certainly isn't exclusive to zahav but annoys me nonetheless.

    I'd love to go back and order from the regular menu. I only wish it was BYO...

    I usually avoid these kinds of "voice in the choir/likely to be skimmed but not really read"-type reviews. The fact that I'm bothering to write one about Zahav speaks to the care with which the restaurant is operated.

    I made a reservation for 10:45 pm last night (the kitchen closes at 11) and was running late thanks to navigating through the congested blight that demands a thousand words, yet can be accurately summarized in five: "Old City on Friday Night". I got to the restaurant right at 11pm, drowning in a sea of frustration and sweat. The servers took it in stride, not only allowing me to dine, but also (quickly!) offering me some water.

    The remainder of my experience consisted of experiencing dishes so well-executed that my previous unfamiliarity with Israeli cuisine became irrelevant by meal's end. I went for the Tay'im, which for me consisted of Salatim (a selection of vegetables), Hummus-Tehina, Kibbe, Fried Cauliflower, and Duck Breast. Describing all the ways in which all of the food exceeded my expectations would only hasten the browser scrolling of the average impatient Yelper, so I'll make it quick and loud: YOU NEED THE HUMMUS AND CAULIFLOWER IN YOUR LIFE. The former was the best hummus I have ever tried - rich, warm, creamy, and thoroughly umami'd. If the idea of paying top dollar for something as simple as hummus makes you recoil, then you need to quickly decoil because this stuff can and will change your perception of what that particular foodstuff is capable of. The cauliflower, on the other hand, combined mint and garlic in a way that perfectly balanced the saltiness of the latter with the sweetness of the former. Fried cauliflower should become A Thing everywhere as far as I'm concerned. The rest of the meal was good, but not amazing - hence the missing star. Don't worry, though - I'll go searching for said star when I return to Zahav.

    EDIT: I actually went to Zahav a couple months ago, not last night. Everything else I said still stands. Also, the owner messaged me personally after this review went up. Professionals through and through.

    For some reason I have held off on reviewing Zahav, which is the restaurant that I frequent the most.  That is great for my mind, body and soul, but not so much for my wallet.

    If I had to compare Zahav to a famous athlete, it would be Chase Utley.  Extremely professional and talented and brings it every night.  Just so loveable.  I'm not afraid to say it.

    On the average weeknight, the Lady and I will split the Salatim plate, which is essentially a wide variety of salads (8 or so included) ranging from an insanely good mashed beet salad to pickled turnips (the salads change from time to time).  The salads are served with hummus (with interesting and fresh spices to mix in to taste as well) and a slightly crispy on the outside and chewy on the inside laffa bread that is baked on site.  I'm a big fan of dipping (who isn't?) so this combo is frighteningly addictive.

    I am generally full after this course and many other mere mortals (aka skinny people) would call it a night, but alas I power on and move to the mezes and main courses.  I know that these next two statements are kind of truisms but I think that they need to be mentioned here...  Cheese is delicious.  Fried foods are extra delicious.  Fried cheese is what gets me up in the morning.  Now Zahav offers halloumi cheese which is fried (not deep fried like mozz sticks) and then mixed with some sort of date puree goodness.  Somehow, Zahav has taken two unbelievable ingredients (cheese and fried - I know, not an ingredient, whatever) and made it BETTER than the sum of its parts.  Also incredible - try the sable with egg on challah (holy moly, and I don't even love sable), the fried cauliflour (spelling?) and the chicken shishtek.  Well, try everything on the menu.  I rarely end up getting full main courses because I get kind of hung up on the mezes but I saw several patrons chomping down on the lamb shoulder.  I love my vegetarian wife - a whole lot.  But I need to find a dining buddy to split that lamb shoulder with me.  Immediately.  It can be a date.

    Also, the dessert is really good and the specialty cocktails are creative and every one has been awesome (I loved the British Mandate but it's no longer on the menu - if anybody wants to lead a petition to have it reinstated I'm down).   The service is  always spectacular as well, particularly the managers who try to make every night a special experience.

    In short - come to Zahav.  It is like Chase Utley.

    Our next course brought us 4 appetizers.  We chose the haloumi, fried cauliflower, Mina, and beef busturma.  The haloumi was crispy and flavorful, although some pieces were a little charred.  
    The cauliflower with yogurt sauce was amazing.  Simply amazing.  I am sure that all of the nutritional value was gone, but who cares.  
    The Mina was also damn good.  It was like a chicken pot pie, but wrapped in matzoh instead of pastry dough.  It was a little greasy, but it was fantastic.  Both DC and I commented that we could have whole plates of the cauliflower and mina - it was comfort food.
    The only dish I could have done without was the beef.  It was more like salami than beef, and the crouton added nothing.  We should have doubled up on the mina.

    For our entrees we chose the Chicken Shiklish and the Trumpet Mushrooms.  The chicken looked like little nuggets of grilled chicken.  I had flashbacks to summer barbecues of my childhood, where my dad served us overdone chicken breasts with a crusty char on the outside.  This was nothing like that.  What it lacked in appearance, the chicken made up for in flavor.  I cant describe it, it was just amazingly seasoned.
    The trumpet mushrooms were a little disappointing.  They had a weird texture.  I wasn't a huge fan.

    For dessert we ordered the rugelach and the kataifi.  Of the 17 different things we were served for this meal, both DC and I rated BOTH desserts in the top 3.  The rugelach was like a sweet cinnamon bun served with an amazing sorbet.  There was some rosewater on the plate to give it a floral touch.  Hot damn this was tasty.

    Overall, Zahav was worth the awkwardness created by the staff.  The food was fantastic, and I will definitely return.

    I went to Zahav not knowing what to expect really.  What the hell is modern Israeli cuisne anyway? I had no clue.  My tastebuds had never experienced this type of cusine.  Well, thanks to Chef Solomonov, now I had the opportunity to find out.  My first trip here, we ordered The Mesibah (Translation: Party Time) and it did not disappoint.  In fact, it was one of the best meals I have ever had.  This chef's tasting menu is full of interesting flavors, from the salatim to the mezze, all the way to the insanely tender, no knife needed, fall-apart whole-roasted lamb shoulder.  Braised for hours and served over crispy persian rice with an incredbile pomegrante sauce. This is one of those WOW dishes.

    The Mesibah tasting is an incredible deal...no...a steal, in my mind.  The amount of food you receive outweighs the amount of dinero you pay, by far (literally and figuratively).  The desserts are always unique and a perfect end to the feast you have just devoured.

    The Lemonnana is a well-crafted cocktail.  Just order the pitcher from the get-go.  Save yourself and your server the time. Because one of these won't be enough.

    I have been back to Zahav several times now. And each time we order The Mesibah.  Each time it is party time.  The food here is a unique experience that will expand your taste buds.  My taste buds certainly know what modern Israeli cuisne is now. And they accept.

    I rate this in my Top 3 Restaurants in Philadelphia.

    5 Stars.
