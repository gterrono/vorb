    This place RULES. Seriously. For some reason I had a weird grudge against this place for no reason at all and I am so so so glad I finally went here to get over it.

    Farmicia is another one of those locally-sourced places that prides itself on quality products from places we all know and love. Yes, it's trendy and hip to do this now but my experience here puts this place high up on the list of my favorites in that category. The menu itself isn't overly big, but they do offer a fair amount of dishes. Their wine list is also excellent, and our server knew a lot about each bottle to help us along. We ultimately went with a reallllllly good bottle of tempranillo, in case you were curious. For food, after mulling over the menu for eons because I couldn't decide, I ended up going with the pan-roasted lamb tenderloins (roasted cauliflower, spinach saute, golden raisin-caper sauce) and him the boneless pork chop (honey-garlic grilled, maple mashed sweet potatoes, bacon braised cabbage, Dijon sauce). We were both literally blown away with the meal. My lamb was cooked perfectly medium rare and super tender (which was a complete 180 from the awful lamb I had at Paradigm the night before). The cauliflower, which was steamed, was not mushy and still had a nice amount of crunch to it. The pork chop was definitely the high point of the meal and we both agreed that it was probably the best pork chop we've had. It was super tasty and the Dijon sauce provided a nice tang against the savoriness of the plate. We passed on dessert, but only because we devoured our entrees.

    The decor is really homey and warm. I felt like I was in the country side instead of Old City. You just feel welcome in this restaurant.

    Service was excellent and, like I previously mentioned, he knew a lot about both the menu and libations. He didn't hover at all, didn't rush us out despite it being busy, and was just a peach over-all.

    Find yourself a cute lady or man and go here for a lovely dinner date. You'll be impressed, I promise.

    I went to Farmicia for dinner on a Wednesday evening to use a gift certificate I'd received.  Philadelphia is full of wonderful restaurants and people who know their food.  When I see a 3.5 star average on yelp, I think two conflicting things: 1) I'd rather eat at one of the many 4-star (or more) establishments; and 2) 3.5 stars from such a foodie crowd could be a positive endorsement, so it's worth a shot.  The gift certificate led me down path 2 in this case.  It was a mistake.

    I'll start with the positives, though.  The decor is charming and the fact that the restaurant's front facade opens makes it a nice place to enjoy a warm spring or summer evening.  The ciapirinhas on the speciality cocktail menu tasted fresh and had high-quality cachaca, but were on the small side.  The chicken enchilada appetizer was actually quite good and the bresaola meat itself was flavorful (the hint of lemon in that dish was also pleasant).  

    And now, the negatives:  First, our service was initially very slow and we waited almost 30 minutes after we ordered before we saw any of the food.   We ordered 5 appetizers to share (hoping to taste more of what the place had to offer), but they came out in two sets, meaning that anything meant to be hot was likely to be cold by the time I got around to tasting it.  When food is this expensive, attention should be paid to when it is served.  

    Second, the Australian Grenache we had was the worst red wine I have tasted in my life.  It was not drinkable.  It should not be on the menu.  (The wine had not gone bad.  If it had had some vinegar taste to it, it would have been better.  Instead, it smelled like port and tasted partly of dishwater.)  

    Third, the descriptions on the menu did not always match what appeared.  The cheese plate's chutney was more like an apple-pie filling.  Most disappointing was the Vietnamesse fried squid, which sounded magnificent and exotic.  It was a pretty typical calimari served with a mildly-spiced ketchup.  It was edible but not enjoyable when it was hot.  But once it was cold, it was rubberband-ish.

    Fourth, the ingredient quality was problematic.  The cheese plate, described as  "selection of cow, sheep and goat milk cheeses," was actually super-market quality feta, goat and some semi-firm white cheese.  None tasted particularly good and none of it paired well with the apple-pie filling.  The artichoke served with the bresaola had the texture of balsa wood.  While the crab in the crab cake had high-enough quality crab, the green goddess sauce didn't seem to be made with fresh herbs, although I make this assumption based on its lack of flavor, so there may have been another problem (it was incredibly bland, so it may just have been underseasoned generally).  

    The bill with two cocktails, a bottle of wine, and five appetizers was more than $125.  I don't mean to disparage 3.5-star rated restaurants generally, but this one is not worth trying.
