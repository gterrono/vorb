    The only emotion I could feel when I first walked into Audrey Claire was my grief.
    I had just taken a disastrous exam, and the only thing that took away my pain was the BYOB rule that allowed me a soothing glass of red wine minus the 300% markup, minus the exorbitant uncorking fee. Btw, Philly's slew of BYOB restaurants has me rearing to relocate.

    The rest of my pain went away with the Mussels which were absolutely perfect. Each and every morsel was juicy and all the shells were cooked wide open. The marinara sauce had an amazing enhancing flavor and clung to each mussel, a feat I had never seen any chef accomplish until that day. This prodigal platter drowned my grief even faster than the wine.

    The Potato Crusted Ahi Tuna entrée had a pretty pink tuna steak crusted with the right number of breadcrumbs. The horseradish topper was tasty but somehow simultaneously unremarkable. Unfortunately, it was the Feta and Garlic Crusted Baby Rack of Lamb that abated my initially enthusiastic assessment of Audrey Claire. Anything with baby in front of it has a more delicate flavor and cries easily. I cried when I realized that I couldn't taste the baby-gamey flavor of the lamb under the inch-thick crust of what tasted like a giant box of Shake n' Bake. The highlight of this plate was a fantastic mint labne for the lamb. I detest mint jelly and this labne was just out of this world. What Audrey Claire lacks in breading, they definitely make up for in sauces.

    Audrey Claire makes some amazing sauces, but it lacks just a pinch of sophistication and innovation. The crust on the tuna tasted similar to the crust on the lamb and the sides were well-made though unimaginative. I still wonder if all that breading was an attempt to soak up the alcohol from the BYOB. I left confused and not just because of the wine, but one conclusion rings true. Despite the flaws, Audrey Claire's mussels have won my allegiance all the way.

    10/25/2012

    A reviewer below mentioned that one of the biggest problems with Audrey Claire is the lack of passion, and I couldn't agree more. I came here last night with a friend,very excited to finally see what all the hype was about. We were seated right by the window overlooking 20th St, arguably the best spot in this tiny, uncomfortable space. The rest of the decor is very minimal, bordering on spartan. At the far end of the restaurant is the open kitchen, which normally makes spaces feel warm and inviting. Audrey Claire's kitchen is loud and smelly. This wouldn't be so bad if perhaps you couldn't really see it, but its obnoxiously bright halogen lights destroy any sense of intimacy and beckon you to look. The kitchen is a full sensory experience in the worst way possible.

    We finally settled into our seats after an impossibly long wait and browsed the menu. I couldn't help but notice a theme: candied, glazed, crispy. I asked the dispassionate server how the "crispy beets" in the ahi tuna were prepared and he said fried. Candied, glazed, crispy. Sweet or fried. I decided not to give this too much thought and we began with the pear and gorgonzola flatbread, followed by the butternut squash risotto (to share). My friend ordered the salmon and I reluctantly ordered the ahi tuna.

    The flatbread came first and it was epic, in the way pizza is epic the morning after a long night. The pears were fine, and it would have been nice to actually taste more of them had it not been for the avalanche of gorgonzola dumped all over the flatbread. It was cheese on top of cheese on top of cheese. There was so much gorgonzola cheese that we had to actually pick it off the bread. I believe the purpose of the dish was to have the saltiness of the cheese offset by the sweetness of the pears, but the poor pear slices stood no chance. I was left with a mouthful of cheese and a fading vision of pears.

    But there can be no time for pears when butternut squash risotto is in your near future. Our server placed the dish casually in the center of the table and we peered into the bowl expectantly. The dish had the color and consistency of Velveeta macaroni and cheese, but in we went anyway for our first bites. It was so thick, so heavy, and so creamy. Basically, everything risotto is not meant to be. The rice was overcooked and the squash was made absolutely irrelevant by the thickness of the broth. It's also unfortunate that the dish tasted exactly how it looked.

    Once the ersatz risotto congealed into one solid blob, our laconic server brought the finale: salmon and ahi tuna. My friend's salmon was thick, juicy, and it challenged any steak for a fight. I had a bite and it was wonderfully seasoned, exploding with taste. My ahi tuna looked like the byproduct of said fight between steak and salmon. The pieces were sad, drizzled with an overly oily vinaigrette that just.sat.there. As one reviewer noted, "potato-crusted" actually means fried in breadcrumbs, which I'm sure the passive pieces of tuna were indifferent toward anyway, much like myself. There were promises of beets, but they were nowhere to be found. Curiously, my friend and I had the same sides of green beans and a small, but much more civilized, portion of risotto with miniscule pieces of vegetables. Enjoy!

    After our server flashed us the last wan smile of the evening, I thought about what had just occurred. I took a look around and the room was emptier than before, the crowd thinning. The space no longer looked as attractive as it did as when we first walked in. It was just there, much the way the food the entire evening was just there. And it dawned on me that only one word could really define our entire experience at Audrey Claire: impassive. Bread can taste great if you smother it in cheese. Risotto might be passable to some if it's thick and creamy enough.Even lightly fried tuna can go unnoticed. But that's not life. You have to care. History can be rewritten, promises in the menu can be made and then broken, but people will notice. It's clear to me that Audrey Claire is phoning it in, doing just enough to get by, but doing nothing to be truly great. Philadelphia has many, many truly great restaurants, and Audrey Claire is not one of them. Good luck!
