    Listed in Sub-par Starr, Top 5 Restaurants in Philly (excluding Vetri), Hot date

    Wow. Have to admit, I was quite impressed with this place...even though it is a product of the culinarily talentless Stephen Starr. The food definitely lived up to the atmosphere...and I'd actually be willing to bestow the supreme honor of placing this in my top 5 restaurants in Philly list. 4.5 stars for sure.

    -Atmosphere: hip, cool, modern, young...great place for a date
    -Service: Attentive and friendly for the most part. The bartender made up for where the waiter was lacking.
    -Drinks: Mojitos were quite potent, and very tasty. The stick of sugarcane was a nice touch.

    Split pea soup- Interesting flavor. Salty, smokey, a little savory...this dish wasn't anything special, but it was (seemed) authentic and I liked it. I enjoyed diving for the pieces of ham at the bottom. I'd imagine that in between watching a baseball game and oppressing his people, Fidel would too.

    Vaca frita: This dish packed a punch...big time flavor explosion. The steak was flavorful and mouth-watering. It was crispy on the outside from being twice cooked, but retained its juiciness while easily being ripped apart. The tomato escabeche was delicious, sweet, and even a bit tangy. The black beans were hearty, and the rice and fresh avocado slices were the perfect bite-by-bite accompaniment. Portion was great too.  Overall, a well-balanced, masterful use of diverse flavors...this dish was really spot on.

    Pork chop: I was only willing to part with a few bites of Vaca frita which I exchanged for some of my friend's pork chop. Pork chops are usually something boring / something I would never order...but this one was cooked to perfection, had a nice crispy sear, was well seasoned, and overall was delicious.  The moist congri rice and tasty orange marinade also helped in elevating the flavor.

    Vanilla rice pudding with passionfruit sauce: my friends weren't crazy about it (maybe because it had the consistency that was a cross between a soup and oatmeal), but I loved it. Original and simple- the flavors were light and refreshing but also satisfied my gigantic sweet tooth. The vanilla wasn't overbearing and the passionfruit added a nice tang to end the meal, instead of the usual rich and over-indulgent dessert.

    My boyfriend and I just moved to Philly together for work and it also happened to be our anniversary within our first month here. I booked us a reservation and we can't imagine a better way to celebrate our time together.

The Thai Mixto variety surpassed all expectations - SO good! The hint of coconut is just perfect.

    All the appetizers sounded so good so we ended up getting the Sampler. It included the Oysters, Marlin tacos, and Royal Palm Dates. We're glad we got a little variety by trying all of them but our favorites were definitely the Marlin Tacos. Those were incredible.     For entree, I had the mahi-mahi with chick pea stew and salsa. It was a pretty hefty piece of fish and definitely a great, filling side dish. Pretty good overall but as always, I almost always prefer the appetizers to the entree.

    Overall, this is a great place for a special occasion. The food in incredible, the prices are absolutely reasonable, and I would even come here just for drinks. I would highly recommend it to anyone who came to visit me!

    Okay, its time to tackle a very tough review I've been putting off for a while. Alma De Cuba. Its my favorite 'high-end' restaurant in Philly even despite the fact that I attend other places more frequently; places which are 'looser', 'slacker'; less rigorous. I only visit Alma once in a long while because its special; its intense; its not to be taken lightly; taken for granted.

    'Striped Bass'..'Le Bec Fin'.. nope--both of those fall slightly short. Even my favorite oyster houses like 'Devon' and 'Sansom St' do not compare to Alma, in the way that I'm stipulating here. Those last two mentioned are bright; loud; sneaker-acceptable places. But you don't come to Alma to be a part of a boisterous crowd; you don't come here wearing a t-shirt.

    Alma is in a separate groove of dining; its more a lounge than a restaurant (all the furnishings suggest this) but the food is as high-caliber as almost anywhere; and anywhere which claims to match Alma in food quality probably doesn't match it in creative flavors and dishes. Its just not simply a 'steak' or 'seafood' place. More on that later.

    Alma is predominantly an experience. Everything is soft and subdued; hazy; fuzzy, diffused. Entering the cozy snatch of Alma is like allowing yourself to be tranquilized or en-tranced; you move --everything moves, actually--as if its all somehow submerged in a viscous, warm, pinkish-orange, throbbing, humming, fluid. Waitresses glide by like nymphs; supernaturally slim and dapper in their tailored white linen jackets with tiny lapels. One steps toward you to receive your order. She is soft-voiced, enthusiastic--pony-tailed--almond-eyed. Its the waitress you've longed for all your life. She promises to return. You sip exotic multi-colored drinks while you wait for her--and the items she will bring--in a kind of detached, amused, suspense.

    All voices are hushed. Everything seems light or floating. Nothing ill or untoward can happen here. Chisel-chinned men lean on granite console tables in blazers and expensive wristwatches and ties; speaking in riddles about yacht schematics; or dogs they loved. Their women sit nearby with their knees close together;  jewelry swaying from their necks and wrists, murmuring about baths or friends they used to know.

    Trays of food drift by; the corners of the room reverberate with--Astrud Gilberto, samba-- your thoughts dwell on some old mansion in some old movie you saw as a kid. Eventually you lapse into a stupefied reverie. You keep up your side of your conversation; but all your senses are buzzing. Its extremely pleasant to recline back and await your dishes; knowing they are on the way; knowing how good they are going to be.

    The food--as I said--is not just 'this plate' or 'that plate'; 'this style' or 'that style'--its an overall Latin or island  experience where all the flavors meld together--with your drinks--immersing you in a set of very harmonious, complementary sensations. Cirtrusy, mint, or spicy elements linger on your tongue between sips or bites.This all sounds very fawning; yes--but 'Alma' is simply the best restaurant for this type of 'enveloping' experience I can name.

    Favorite dish: crab empanadas; a concoction of warm crab mousse and slivers of cucumber. An appetizer. $20. One of six such apps all of which are superb (but this is definitely the standout). Its so good that it makes your eyelids flutter; you cut it and fork it into your mouth with precise care and deliberation; it commands the attention of every cell in your body and you stop talking to just stare wide-eyed at your partner, while you chew. You think about it for months afterwards.

    Alma de Cuba. Stands alone in my mind the way Peter Luger's does in New York. Nothing else I can say.
    .

Stephen S. Business Owner

Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District.
