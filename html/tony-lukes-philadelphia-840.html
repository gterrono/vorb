    My favorite cheesesteak purveyor in Philadelphia. I've tried a bunch of the others, and always seem to come back to this one. The "one" I'm talking about is with Cheez Whiz and onions - pretty much the best way to eat a cheesesteak. Forget the provolone. You want the processed, artery-hardening, transfat-containing, goey mess dripping off the end of the steak with every bite. Now THAT'S an authentic experience. Soft rolls, juicy & hearty shaved steak - salted perfectly too! - and tender onions. I know there are several choices around when it comes to this famed sandwich, but give Tony Luke's a go. They're not famous for nothing.

    In my ongoing mission to hit as many of the joints I see on TeeVee and in the rags, I welcomed my first real trip to Philly. I had hit DiNics at noon (see my review here at Yelp), gone and did the Clinic I was in town to do, and now, It was time for dinner. At the Enterprise Rentacar desk at the Airport, the Gent there advised only the tourists went to Geno's or Pat's for Cheesesteaks. Locals go to Tony Lukes.
    But I couldn't visit Philly and not at least TRY to go to that famous corner, which is smack dab in the middle of a claustrophobic residential neighborhood. Nowhere to park, and lines at both places like those you find at Disneyland. I wanted to grab a picture, but the traffic was so dense, I couldn't even do that. Rats!
    So Tony Luke's it was!
    There are a few Tony Luke's in town, but I'm all about OldSchool, so only the original would do. In an industrial part of town which has prolly seen better days, I kluged a parking spot (i was driving a hot black gorgeous brand-new Chrysler 300c) adjacent to TL's, under the freeway, with a few unsavory-looking Youths nearby. Maybe I shoulda swung with a safer location, but it was getting late. I would tough it out. There was a modest line, and the pic-a-nic tables were jammed. This wadn't a-gonna be easy! I did do a little reconnoiter as how to order at TL's. and for me it was a Cheesesteak whiz wit'. I also called an audible when I saw "Italian Pork with Broccoli Rabe and Provolone" on the Menu. I took my two torpedo-sized Sarnies to a table that had miraculously cleared as I approached. (the Philly Food Gods must have been on my side, as the same thing happened earlier that day at DiNics!) As I sat down, a funky and zaftig young gal moseyed over with her own torpedo. "Mind if I sit with you?" to which I answered "Sure!". She sat down and tore into her Cheesesteak with lusty abandon. This was going to be fun! I pounced upon my cheesesteak, and it was tasty indeed. Kinda sloppy-this is not something I'd want to eat in the Chrysler.
    All-in-all the Cheesesteak wasn't neck-and-neck with the DiNic's sarnie I scarfed earlier in the day. If I hadn't gone to DeNic's I would be jumping up and down over the TonyLuke's Cheesesteak, but it was kinda like an Italian Beef without the Giardinera. The Gal across the table identified herself as the Former Culinary director of Barnum and Bailey's Circus. She certainly knew how to eat. I et half of my Cheesestaek and turned my attention to TonyLuke's Roast Pork.
    Jumping Jesus-This sarnie blew away DiNics! Great garlic note, tangy greens, and an explosion of Flavor. This was the über-Philly experience I had been waiting for.
    The Circus gal said that Tony Luke's was far and away the top dog in town for these sarnies, I had to agree. I dispatched the entire thing, waddled back to the Chrysler, bidding Circus Gal adieu. I was stuffed like a Christmas goose...

    I've been eating cheesesteaks in Philadelphia for 15 years, and I've had a lot of duds.  It's a really simple recipe - bread, meat, cheese, maybe toppings (I like mine 'wiz with') - but it seems like a lot of places manage to mess that up.  I've never had a bad sandwich from Tony Luke's.  It's a little out of the way, and in a weird building right off the highway.  They're sometimes arbitrarily closed, and when they're open, there's usually a line.  But it's worth it.  Oh, so worth it.  

    The bread is freshly baked on the premises, soft and tasty, and you can see them back there managing the meat on the grill.  All of the sandwiches I've had have been generously portioned, with plenty of toppings spread throughout.  They have a lot of options on their fancy LCD menu screens.  I haven't really tried anything except the basics (like their fries, which are good), but people seem satisfied with all of the weird sandwiches offered.  

    But honestly, Tony Luke's has really re-focused my obsession with Philadelphia sandwiches from the cheesesteak... to the roast pork Italian.  Just typing the name this sandwich makes me sad that I'm 100 miles from Tony Luke's and can't have one for dinner.  The concept is simple once more - seasoned slow-cooked pork, broccoli rabe (a.k.a. rapini, a green leafy vegetable), and provolone.  But the end result is so delicious and moist and flavorful, herbaceous and garlicky and just a little spicy.  I always split one of these and a cheesesteak with my partner, and then afterwards we regret that we didn't just each get a roast pork Italian.  Though I noticed on the menu that I could get a cheesesteak Italian-style... my head might explode.

    I wanted to give a shout-out to the fantastic cashier working last weekend who was patient with out-of-towners with questions.  I'm so used to Philly cheesesteak spots screaming at customers (ahem, Pat's) that it was sort of amazing to see someone friendly helping someone place an order.  It doesn't really speed things up to scream at customers while they're ordering, so I'm glad Tony Luke's actually has customer service.
