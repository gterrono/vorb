    Just tried El Rey with a Mexican friend and we both had tried to figure out what this place is all about. My friend being Mexican was baffled on how the staff is so non-Latin and all under 30. The decor retained a lot of the former diner-self other than some reupholstering and a wall of Spanish B-movie posters. She said "Hola, como esta?" to the staff and they looked at her laughing. Music from the 70s and the 80s playing in the air, the place looks like something out of that era or a ranch somewhere. (Is this actually Texi-cali?) Instead of trendy, it looks more like a bunch of kids playing servers out of an old garage.

    The food is ok, I'm not a great deal impressed. We shared an order of Guacamole which is bright but bland, not the worst or the best I've tasted. I ordered Zucchini Blossoms, Corn and Oaxaca cheese Quesadilla which I regretted. There was practically no zucchini blossoms in those little quesadillas which were lying pathetically on the plate with a splosh of guacamole like avocado salad. It looked like it came from the kids menu, with their tininess and the fiesta decorated color-rimmed plate. It's a glorified grilled cheese with less flavor. My friend ordered Napoles Ensalada with cactus, pumpkin seed, tomatoes, pickled red onions and tortilla strips, this dish is a whole lot better in flavor and texture combination. A lot more care is given to the design of the dish and it showed.

    We asked for more tortillas and we were charged for it.  We also ordered some churros that comes with a goat cheese caramel sauce. Nicely fried, even though it's a little tough, the cinnamon sugar is comforting, the goat cheese caramel sauce was good but the churros can do without it.

    All in all, my experience is a bit meh, especially shelving $40 for little portions of average Mexican food is not quite worth it. They could really borrow a page from El Vez or Garces' Distrito. (Even Cosi used to make better quesadillas) The decor can go up a few notches. (Is is because of the economy?) this is probably the most disappointing Stephen Starr restaurant I've ever been.

    For half of the price, I would probably get more authentic, genuine and better Mexican food down on Washington Avenue.

    The first thing I realized when I walked into El Rey -- I had never been in this space before 3 AM.… The first thing I realized when I walked into El Rey -- I had never been in this space before 3 AM.  The former Midtown Diner was a consistent, if not gross, place to grab a drunken tuna melt (served with a bag of wise potato chips), and El Rey really does not look all that different from the Midtown.  In fact that's why I'm not giving it 5 stars.  Sure, it's kitschy, but those booths are just not that comfortable and there's something that skeeves me out about remembering the Midtown.  Now, some of the best food I've had in the world has been from places far less appealing with similar decor (think roadside stand in Latin America), but I have history with this place.

    Now that I'm off my soapbox, the reason that I would normally go 5 stars here is the food. It's really good.  Chef Jimenez is super talented and the food really does stand out.  (Quick note: I had heard from some friends and from yelp about some things NOT to try, so we were a little biased in our selection).

    We ordered 5 dishes and dessert across 3 people -- This was probably the right amount as I was satiated but by no means stuffed.  Our server helped us course the meal and provided really good recommendations.  First, we sampled the lamb tacos -- this is a pretty simple dish (3 corn tortillas each topped with braised lamb).  I like a little bit of crunch in my taco, so I missed having some sort of slaw, and missed having some sort of sauce to complement the meat.  But this was still a great taco.  The lamb is juicy and flavorful, and each bite ignites your mouth with a burst of flavor.  

    Mushroom queso fundido was also simple yet perfect.  The Chihuahua cheese they use has a nice combination of creaminess and saltiness.  In comparison to Distrito's duck barbacoa fundido, I actually preferred El Rey's version.  Yes, I love the duck, but I think I enjoyed the cheese in El Rey's version more.  

    The short rib enchiladas were similar to the lamb.  Perfectly flavorful, perfectly executed.  I personally enjoyed the addition of cheese and red sauce, and prefer flour tortillas to corn, but I enjoyed the lamb meat a little more.

    We also had chilaquiles -- I'm sad to say I've never had this dish before.  Corn chips are mixed in salsa verde and softened, then topped with cheese and sour cream and a choice of steak or fried egg.  While I liked the rare steak a lot, I think I would have preferred the fried egg (with the yolk running) -- or (an idea?) a steak tartare chilaquiles dish :)

    Our last savory dish was the halibut.  The fish was well cooked and the accompanying zucchini, avocado and onion saute (hash?) was really nice, but this dish bored me a little bit.  And I think that's key -- why order a main at a place that succeeds best in small plates?  It sounded nice and healthy but I would have preferred to try the tamales or the quesadillas (or both) in hindsight.

    For dessert, we went with the churros, which come with a dulce de leche.  Now I am not a caramel person, and I really really enjoyed the warm churros.  Nothing fancy or trendy here...just a mean donut.  Perfect food for a diner, I guess.

    One enters El Rey under an illuminated theatre marque which proclaims (in Spanish) that "El Rey will change your life".  These words are a bit of an overstatement, perhaps, but El Rey will certainly change your evening.  Like all of Steven Starr's "venues", restaurant aesthetics is as important to the dining experience as the food.  This place gets 5 stars on both counts.

    The design aesthetic of El Rey, however, is a major departure from the big budget refined productions we are accustomed to with Starr.  The space was formerly occupied by the Midtown Diner - a mid-century nondescript grease-pit of sorts.  The Midtown Diner was subtly retrofitted and redeployed by Starr to create El Rey.  El Rey is reminiscent of dingy border town diner one might expect to see in a Quentin Tarantino flick, with a soundtrack to match (from the speakers blare an inconsistent variety of genres, ranging from old school country and soul, to  Mexican, funk, and surf music).  The mishmash of tunes are delightful.  As are the obscure vintage Mexican wrestling flick posters plastered to the walls, and the framed original prison art.  Very dark and eerie.  Very Tarantino.  The surreal mood is set.

    Servers sport mostly flannel and denim.  They are uber-friendly, hip, and helpful.

    The (intentional) dinginess of the place contrasts superbly with the wonderful food and drink - alive with flavor.  Lubricate the evening with fresh-fruit-filled sangria - rojo or blanco.  Or try one of the several varieties of margaritas.  I enjoy the sour/bitter tamarind version.  Start your feast with one of the fresh fish and lime ceviches (having recently returned from a week on Costa Rica's Pacific Coast, I can attest that the El Rey incarnations are very reminiscent of the roadside offerings ).  The corn on the cob, with its coating of spices and cheese, is a must try.  Then work your way up to flavorful tacos or enchiladas (the short rib variation is my preference).  I also highly recommend the stuffed peppers.  The dishes are Mexican with a twist - but just the right twist (not too much that they loose all of their authenticity).  Clearly this is not authentic Mexican (as other reviewers have pointed out).  But I don't believe it pretends to be.  What it is is a variation on a theme that is interesting and flavorful, and served in a quite unique context.

    If there can be such thing a "subtly over-the-top", El Rey fits the bill.  For the experience, the cost is modest.  One can get reasonably stuffed for plus-or-minus $15.  And at about $7 for a margarita, you can get reasonably buzzed without breaking the bank.
