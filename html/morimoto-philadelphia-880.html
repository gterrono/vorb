    Iron Chef meal, so so good.

    This is my third time in Philadelphia
    This is my second time I'm in front of Morimoto
    I finally made it in this time around.
    Food was outstanding, service was excellent.

    The first time I went to Morimoto, I was walking to go to Geno's for a cheese steak sandwich when by chance I passed it. I stood there for a few minutes debating whether to go in or not. Unfortunately it was raining & I was drenched. Looking at the customers going in (dressed to the nines) I decided to continue my trek for a cheese steak sandwich. This time around, I was determined to go, no matter what happens.
    Luckily, our meeting on the last day was moved backed an hour. Freeing everyone an hour early. That was all I needed.

    I opted for the lunch Omakase (Chefs Choice)

    Here's the rundown:
    Yellowtail Tartare w/  Caviar in Dashi Broth
    A great starter. Tuna was chopped so fine.  It had crispy shallots hiding inside providing a contrasting crunchy texture to the soft tuna. The Dashi broth was so flavorful, I chugged it at the end. Delicious.

    Carpaccio Amadai (Tile fish)
    Hot peanut oil was poured on top of the fish which was laying in a pool of virgin olive oil and some soy sauce. I've seen the iron chef do this on some of his shows, now I know why. It brings out a different taste from the fish. Delicious.

    Sashimi Salad w/ Kampachi & Salmon.
    Micro green salad with a couple of sashimi pieces of kampachi & salmon. Good.

    Pan Seared Scallop w/ Gnocchi.
    Perfectly seared scallops w/ a few gnocchi pieces, beets, peas & pine nut  in a beet sauce. I want more of this, Delicious.

    Black Cod Miso
    Nobu would be proud. The black cod was so buttery. The skin was crispy. The miso sauce was so good. The best fish dish I've ever had. I'm drooling just thinking of this dish. DELICIOUS!!

    Nigiri Sushi
    I got five, Maguro Toro, Kampachi, Red Snapper, Horse Mackerel & Clam
    All delicious. I usually don't order mackerel because I don't prefer the taste. But the Horse Mackerel (1st time having it as a sushi) was really good. Changed my mind on it, will have to start ordering it.

    Cheese Cake, I missed the actual name of this dish. But it is was very good. It's not your typical cheese cake. It was very light and not as cheesy. I'd say Asian style cheese cake. Not too sweet, my kind of dessert.

    I was also able to taste the Wasabi Fried Rice. Oh wow, so good. Don't get scared of by the word wasabi here, it's very mild. Flavors were on point. Delicious.

    Service:
    Our server, Chrissy was excellent. Her knowledge of the menu items were impeccable. Her suggestions were on point. Great personality too.

    Ambiance:
    The place is modern simplicity. Very light music in the background. The furniture was very modern and it changes color, almost like a mood light. Super clean.

    Tip: get the omakase for lunch, it has the same items as the omakase for dinner but at half the price.

    Sorry for using the word "Delicious" too many times. But that is what kept coming to my head describing the food here.

    Iron Chef I bow to you, food was exceptional.
    Five Star Plus in my book.
    I'll definitely be back.

    Listed in Grossly Overrated

    I don't watch Iron Chef.  But apparently that show is supposed to be an indicator that Morimoto is a very good chef.  This is his original restaurant, but it was really the rave reviews, not the show, that led me here.

    When I walked in, I noticed that this place looked more like an underwater dance club than a Japanese restaurant.  Morimoto definitely eschews tradition, and that is something that is evident not just from the decor, but the food itself.  Is there anything wrong with eschewing tradition?  No, not if you at least pay a little respect to tradition.

    I ordered the "Omakase," because that's really the best way to enjoy Japanese cuisine.  I put "Omakase" in quotes because it does not even attempt to honor what Omakase is all about.  Omakase is about making a connection between the sushi chef and the diner.  Morimoto's version is, judging by the fact that it appears everybody gets the same thing, not omakase, but just a tasting menu.  There's nothing wrong with that if you want to try out a variety of the restaurant's specialties, but it's not omakase.  This may sound like pedantry, but calling it omakase not only dishonors tradition, but rapes it for its potential tourist dollars.

    The food was pretty awesome.  There's no denying that.  Overpriced?  Absolutely.  Easily the most unjustifiably overpriced meal I have ever eaten.

    The "omakase" did come with a (small) sampling of sushi, maybe three or four pieces.  The selection was great, and the fish fresh... But the chefs put absolutely no effort into it.  Garnish?  None.  Good sushi chefs top the sushi with small hints of flavor that go well with the particular fish instead of forcing the diners to either spoil it with soy sauce or eat it straight.

    Another thing good sushi chefs do is actually talk to the diners.  I didn't have a single word with the sushi chef.  Even the famously uptight chefs at Sushi Zo in Los Angeles were more friendly.

    At Morimoto, you pay for amazing entrees and a completely cold and pretentious dining experience.  Whether Mr. Morimoto is a top-notch chef is still unknown to me; I do know that he runs a restaurant that is fake and exorbitantly priced.

    An important food lesson was reemphasized with my visit to Morimoto.  Food can be sexy, delicious and amazing in many creative ways but the food that speaks to your soul is unique only to you, your preferences and influenced by how you grew up.

    Without a doubt, the food at Morimoto is art that encompasses all of your senses - visually pleasing, smells amazing, texture, taste and sound (stretching it on the sound).  After all he is an iron chef and to get to where he's at he needed to excel in food in the most creative artful way possible.

    Everything was great except it wasn't the experience for me when I go out for Japanese omakase.  The philosphy of traditional Japanese food is simple and nurturing, taking the time to appreciate the simple beauty and harmony of all the ingredients and dishes.  The servers knew the basic line and descriptions for each dish but they didn't really understand the concept behind the dish.

    I'm torn between giving Morimoto 4 and 5 stars...
    Why?

    Well, as a purist, I have a strong love for pure cuisines, true to the traditions from which the chef was born/trained, bereft of the admixtures that are oft called creative, but just as often failed experimentation.  Yet it is the creative collision of culinary culture that best yields fresh insights and flavours.

    Against my better wishes, I find that Morimoto-san does precisely that in his degustation/omakase menu here.

    The courses were as follows:

    Morimoto's Junmai Daiginjo for a starting libation followed by what ended up being my favourite course: Toro Tartar admixed with fried onions topped with Ossetia Caviar with wasabi and Yamamomo on the side.  3 Kumamoto oysters topped with varied sauces.  This was followed by slices of tai, cured with hot oil and ... absolutely excellent.

    Next was sashimi - Toro and madai followed by an ginger/rosemary infused water as palate cleanser.

    Lobster baked in Chili was next on this list, served with creme fraiche.  Then seared beef in a tataki style with macha salt to the side.  And lastly nigiri sushi... which was served much more thinly than I would have expected.

    Decor was tasteful and the music classic club/lounge electronica.

    The balance to the dishes was excellent, flavourful and well considered.  While I will likely always be  more of a fan of the hardcore traditional, Morimoto's omakase delivered much as the japanese should suggest...

    I relied on his choice, and he did not disappoint in the slightest.

Meet the Business Owner

Stephen S. Business Owner

Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District. STARR Restaurants expanded to Atlantic City, NJ, with Buddakan and The Continental located in the Pier Shops at Caesars in 2006, and to Florida with Steak 954 at the W Hotel Fort Lauderdale in 2009, and Makoto in the Bal Harbour Shops in 2011.
