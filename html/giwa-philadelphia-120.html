    So I have to preface and say I'm a big fan of Korean food. If there was such a thing as a food soulmate, Korean food would be it for me. Usually I'm pretty lenient and generous in giving reviews, and this is my first three-star, but certainly not my last.

    So I read the Yelp reviews for the three Korean restaurants in downtown Philly (wow look at the wealth of options), Miga, Giwa, and Wajoe, and I basically came to the conclusion that Miga is the upscale one and that Giwa and Wajoe are the more cafeteria-style choices. I've been to Miga several times, and though it isn't the best Korean food I've ever eaten, it's certainly not bad and I would go again.

    Reading the comments for Giwa, which were sort of mixed but mostly positive, I decided to let the optimist in me take a gamble and try it. I heard the dolsot bibimbap was quite good, but I didn't come on a weekday (special lunch special prices), so I just got the regular beef bibimbap.

    There was an open kitchen, as several other reviewers noted, but the person who takes your order and the kitchen staff weren't Korean. I hate to be nationalistic or whatever, but usually Korean food not made by Koreans isn't that good. It seems to me that at Giwa that the Korean food was basically grilled down to a science - every ingredient in its proper portions and at the right temperature. Think of it like a modern Korean McDonalds, but without the grease.

    One positive thing is that I received my beef bibimbap really quickly - I think in less than five minutes after sitting down with my order (there's free seating, but during lunchtime it may be hard to find seating, since the place is quite small).

    It looked delicious, it really did, but once I ate it I realized why there were several reviewers on here who warned of the bland taste. The beef was fine, but everything else was just very - bland. There was a lot of lettuce, and I don't think it's the lettuce they usually use for bibimbap, and it just felt like I was eating cow food with a bit of beef thrown in. Now, that comparison might seem really rough but the texture was really similar to like a grazing diet. Sorry Giwa, but that's how I felt. I think the dolsot with the hot pot might have alleviated some of that cow food texture, but unfortunately for me, with the dolsot it just didn't taste right. And it's pretty hard to get bibimbap wrong, in my opinion.

    Now the reason I'm giving a three-star and not lower is that I have yet to try some of the other stuff on the menu, but I'll wrap up my review like this.

    BOTTOM LINE: If you're new to Korean food, Giwa is like a made-to-order store that simplifies the situation and makes it very easy for you to choose what you want based on your tastes and your food requirements. However, if you've had some Korean food and know good Korean food, I warn you stay away. For the hefty price, it really doesn't compare to authentic Korean food.

    I'm ashamed to say that despite my best efforts, the numerous Korean friends and people I've known in my life, and the boundless Korean restaurants around the greater Northeast area. It is an Asian cuisine I've never explored thoroughly, hell, I've even explore Mongolian and Cambodian cuisine more than I have Korean.

    So when walking by Giwa on a cold winter day, I saw what was an odd phenomenon in center city. Koreans eating in a Korean restaurant! Upon entrance it was something of a marvel to behold, the seats were packed and lots of sizzling, boiling, and slurping in every nook and cranny. Whats even more amazing is the fact despite everything, there were plenty of bar seats(just no alcohol). I could have gone for the Giwa special, but instead I went for the Bulgogi. It did not disappoint, the meat was tender, marinated, grilled to near perfection. The best part of the meal as with most Korean meals is the Bancham(IMO), and this place had the standard Kimichi but also this great cold cucumber which reminds me of Hohohot from Northern China.

    One thing I must say about this place is this. The service is excellent, and you can tell they're there to run a business. But also enjoy what they're doing, and no event stands out more than when I received all my food. But the manager noticed something only people who eat alot of spicy food would noticed. That is I went straight for the spicy Kimichi, Marinated Cucumbers, and the peppers/onion on the beef before touching the rice/beef/salad. Its the same as a person first drinking the soup to open up the taste bud and prepare your body when eating good noodles, same for spicy food. Immediately, the manager gave me a side of hot sauce, and told me I should pour it all over.

    I don't think I've ever encountered such a showing of true love of food, something that most people wouldn't notice. But I love small little details such as this which defines a place of business.
