    2/12/2013

    When I first heard that Horizons was closing, I immediately felt a sense of loss. Where would I dine during my travels to Philadelphia?  Soon enough, that question would be answered as the owners, Richard Landau and Kate Jacoby, had closed Horizons purposefully with the plan to open a high-end vegan restaurant in a different part of town.

    Vedge is located downtown near several theaters so it seems they are attracting a bit of a varied clientele -- vegans searching them out as well as people attending the theater who are seeking a different pre-theater experience with upscale, healthy options. As reservations are hard to come by, by the time I called, I could only get reservations for a 5 p.m. slot so we essentially opened the restaurant. From the outside, Vedge looks like a converted townhouse with its red brick facade and a staircase with a black wrought iron railing. Inside, however, it was clear, that it had been completely remodeled.

    We first took a look at the wine menu, which boasted fine wines of all categories -- white, red, rose, and sparkling. Unfortunately, not many are served by the glass so our options were limited. We inquired as to whether the wines were vegan or not and I was surprised to hear the server state that they do not make any claims about the wines. He said it is too difficult to determine which wines are vegan and that that can change over time so they will not say that their wines are vegan. This was surprising to hear at a vegan restaurant, I must say. Regardless, I started with the Bordeaux, Domaine Guitto Fellonneau from Bordeaux, France (2005), which was a nice light red that went well with all dishes. We kind of chuckled when our waiter described the notes as "leather" as I wouldn't expect to hear that in a vegan restaurant. I asked if perhaps he meant "pleather," and he laughed as well.

    We all found the menu layout to be very confusing. At first glance, it appears that the appetizers are $5, and the entrees range from $8 to $15 when in fact, the canapes are $5, and the appetizers and smaller plates are half the menu and range from $8 to $10 while the entrees are the second half of the menu ranging from $13 to $15. The dirt list, which is separate shows the vegetable specials for the month and ranges from $6 to $9 a plate. After explaining all of that, the server recommended that we each order 3 to 5 plates -- at least one from the dirt list, one from the small plates, and one from the large plates. Woah. That just sounded like a lot of food.  We decided to be conservative and start with three plates of food each.

    Once we placed our orders, they brought out some incredibly fresh soft bread served with olive oil that had a mixture of parsley and garlic in the middle.

    My first plate was off the Dirt List and consisted of delicately sliced nebrodini mushrooms cut to resemble thin strips of fazzoletti pasta served in a light wine wine sauce with oven roasted tomatoes and basil. It was like love at first bite.

    For the second course, I had one of the small plates. I was incredibly impressed by the presentation and even more so by the complexity of the flavors. This dish was molded and served as many layers stacked high consisting of salt roasted golden beets, avocado, smoked tofu, and capers. It was topped with toasted rye, which I used as a base for all of the flavors. There was also a frothy light cucumber sauce that paired well with the dish. If I had to use two words to describe this dish they would be complex and beautiful.

    For our main courses, all four of us ordered the exact same thing! So much for trying everything. We all wanted the seitan dish as meat analogues are scarce on this menu and we were curious to see how this one would taste. Plus, seitan dishes at Horizons were always amazing so we were sure this would be a good choice. And it was! The seitan was grilled to a perfect crisp and placed atop black lentils and topped with grilled leeks and smoked romesco.  This was a perfect marrying of flavors as the warm crispy seitan soaked up the chilled black lentil flavor while the leeks and romesco served to complement the dish. We all loved it.

    For dessert, I chose the blueberry fritters because at this point it was clear that the calories no longer mattered. These little fried balls of dough were served with blueberry lemon sorbet atop a mango crudo. It was delightful.

    Having to order so many plates made for slightly pricey dinner. The final bill with tax and gratuity was about $300.  All things considered, that really wasn't that bad. It would cost us that much to have three courses in DC without drinks!  So, all in all, I was extremely impressed with this restaurant -- from the romantic ambiance to the exquisite food and everything in between, I'd rank this restaurant up there with the likes of Millennium and Candle 79. What a great addition to Philly--the city of veganly love.

No reservation, no table - this, in spite of the fact that they had a nearly empty restaurant on a Sunday evening. This was fairly early on, so I'll cut them some slack and assume the manager on duty wasn't particularly experienced.

    Subsequent three trips have been better. I won't recount the menu - many others have done this already. Fantastic food, but small portions at high prices. It really just doesn't seem justified. If they lowered the prices or, better, increased the amount of food on the plate, they'd get that fifth star. The biggest problem I have with the plate size is that I barely get a taste before it's gone; I don't get to savor it. It's like torture, really. Happy hour prices are pretty decent, though. Just order double what you'd expect, which with the happy hour prices is reasonable, and you'll be good.

    As to the menu itself, the descriptions are a bit misleading. It lists ingredients, but be aware that some of those ingredients will be in the sauce, so don't necessarily expect what you might have become accustomed to. When it lists edamame, for example, don't necessarily expect edamame beans; they may be pureed in the sauce on the plate. This, too, contributes to the disappointment in plate size, as I expected more solid food on the plate when reading the description.

    I didn't think I'd like Vedge as much as I did. I'm not a super carnivore, but my SO is, and he was mighty impressed. This is a dude who prefers meat on every plate, and will go for a plate of chicken crispers over dessert.

    Let's talk about what we loved.

    The crispy cauliflower was exactly Korean fried chicken wings in taste, but not texture. Delicious!
    If you're a fan of sun du bu, you'll enjoy the Korean style tofu stew.
    The saffron cauliflower soup had a gritty, creamy texture, though not as thick as you might expect. Creamy cauliflower, yum.
    The fresh hearts of palm with a garbanzo crepe might have been one of my favorites.
    Grilled seitan--tasted like a steak!

    Don't neglect the "dirt menu" when you go, but overall I preferred dishes from their normal menu.

    They have an interesting little beer selection, a lot of Italian stuff that pairs well with the food.

    The dessert was all vegan: We went for the cheesecake and ice cream. I couldn't believe that the cheesecake was vegan! Ugh, it was heavenly. It's made with coconut milk, apparently.

    Don't get me wrong, though. If you're hankering for meat, this place probably won't satisfy. However, if you're feeling especially non-carnivorous and/or adventurous, Vedge won't disappoint. And don't think that you're eating "healthy" because there isn't any meat--there are plenty of fats and carbs on any given plate. However, the concepts are well-executed and the chefs know what they're doing. Ditto on the front of house staff, too.

    Final words: Quality. Delicious. Not a gimmick.

    I wasn't sure when I'd ever get here - so girls night just felt right. We showed up at 7pm on a Monday night... And the place was full and lively. Really! Monday night vegetarian place packed. One might think it must be small... Oh no, four sizable rooms. The place is pretty big. And it's packed. On a Monday!

    Our server was a complete doll, knowledgeable and attentive. So this is what we got (we might have over ordered but we weren't sure if you get full on well you know vegetarian)

    From the Dirt List (March 4th):
    Braised Eggplant Cippolini - very tempting and appealing by calling it Cippolini but the best thing on the plate was the toasted bread that came with it. It wasn't bad, there's just better things to order. (Maybe adding a little spice/kick would help)
    Sunchoke Soup - phenomenal. Creamy rich texture that Ill be craving. Hope this makes it to the standards menu.
    Nebrodini Mushroom "Fazzoletti" - our server brought to our attention that we hadn't ordered anything with mushrooms and she suggested we try this out as it is "similar to pasta". She was pretty close to being correct. It was a tasty substitute to pasta.
    Brussels Sprouts - I have a thing for b.sprouts. Consider me a connoisseur and these passed the test. I suggest checking them out if they're offered.

    Standard Menu:
    Peel n' eat Lupini beans - beautifully roasted garlic taste with a little kick in the end. Very good to get at least once and especially good to order as an app for the table.
    Winter Squash Pierogies - best item we ordered
    Roasted Hawaiian hearts of palm - I wasn't crazy about the hearts of palm but everything else on the dish was delish.

    I was a vegetarian for 16 years before there were many healthier choices available for dinners like there are now. The word vegetarian and excellent were mutually exclusive and aside from an occasional special dish there were no consistently high quality places to chose from because in my opinion the hardest cuisine to master is Vegetarian.

    Let me explain. In most foods it is the animal fats that add that extra zing to a dish. Most dishes rely on the meat portion to enhance the flavors on a plate and why the most important person in a kitchen is the person who makes the stock or sauces. The art of concentrating flavors is the basis for all fine cooking.

    Now when working with just vegetables the lack of concentration of flavors is like trying to fight with both hands tied behind your back and why may vegetarian dishes cheat with adding butter, cheese, and cream. A pure vegetable dish always seems to be lacking something because for most people their taste buds only feel satiated by consuming those animal fat flavors.

    As a side note of all of the foods in the universe the top of the flavor pyramid are bacon bits. They possess the highest concentration of those fats, enhanced with char and smoke)

    As for Vedge, this is by far the best vegetarian restaurant I have ever been to. The depth and breath of foods offered in conjunction with the flavors and variety go beyond the Italian Tomato based, Indian Curry based choices. This is the BEST of the BEST in this cuisine.

    Anyone can master making a great steak, a cream based soup, or a cheese filled omelet but only a true master can conquer a vegetable.
