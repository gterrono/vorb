    What a gem.

    Vegetarian papaya salad, spice level of 2- we're Indian and quite accustomed to hot food, but this was hot. Delicious, though. The papaya was crisp and the flavors well balanced.

    Cha Mussels - nicely sized mussels, nicely sized portion, could not stop drinking the sauce on these. Recurrent theme of the evening- fresh ingredients with a flavor profile that skews towards sour, not in a off putting way.

    Sah-Law Machue Kre' ung- a sour soup that other reviewers have mentioned surprisingly contains celery. It works, though, providing a crunchy texture. I liked the Asian eggplant in here as well. Reminded me of tom yum but better- with less of the heat and more of the sour, I was able to enjoy the taste of everything as opposed to burning my tongue and/or taste buds

    Pork with Pumpkin- savory, with chewy, nicely charred/cooked pieces of pork, and the pumpkin works really well. We had leftovers of this, which held up after reheating.

    Listed in Baby, you're all that I want, Hard to Get

    Absolutely one of my favorite restaurants in Philadelphia.

    The explosive flavors, price to value ratio, great service (Tony is the man), BYOBness, and love that goes into making this fantastic food are tough to beat. The only downsides are that it is cash only, which I can get over, and far from where I live, which I am trying to persuade them to change.

    For those who are unaware, Cambodia borders Thailand and Vietnam, and this is clearly reflected in the food as well. You get the refreshing lightness and acidity of Vietnamese cuisine as well as the curries and spiciness of Thai, all with a unique Cambodian flare.

    There is nothing here I haven't fully enjoyed. Prahok kahteeh (carmelized pork) is my favorite - on multiple occasions I have ordered takeout and trekked down here solely for this dish. The flavor of the pra-hok (fermented fish) marries perfectly with the sweetened pork, and the plate of veggies it's served on is like a work of art. Other highlights are mussels in curry (douse that sauce on your rice to find heaven), beef lok lak (with the citrusy dipping sauce!), and papaya salad (they make it real spicy if you ask). It is truly a family run business (the owner, mom, and aunt are the chefs), and everyone here is super friendly and ready to provide recommendations.

    They do charge you for rice, probably because they know how addictive their sauces are, but maybe this is balanced by the fact that they serve bottled water free of charge (though why do you do this? Tap water in Philly is plenty safe). This place gets PACKED on weekends, and pretty much any day after 6:30ish - so go early and enjoy!

    In our ongoing interest in learning of new cuisines we made our way this afternoon to Khmer Kitchen. We have never before tried Cambodian food and were not familiar with any in the city.  An online search took us to the corner of 6th and Morris in South Philadelphia.  Like several other unusual Asian restaurants this is just a small place, clean, bright and unpretentious. But it did have lovely live roses on the tables.  Our young waiter was a great help to us. My guess is that he is American born and a so a bridge between two worlds.  As such he was a great help to us in selecting our lunch and explaining everything about how to enjoy or afternoon at the table.  One thing to know is that even though the menu at Khmer Kitchen divides dishes into "appetizers," "traditional," "meats," etc. All dishes are served at the same time: a method that is most common in most Asian community restaurants. We have now ceased to try to ask for the soup before the main. It just doesn't happen. Everything is on the table at once and you pick as you go.
    But to our lunch. Our first selection as appetizer (although it arrived with everything else) was the mussels in a ginger sauce.  While we actually did not get to them until a bit later, the mussels appetizer took your tastes to an experience of a sea with salty sweetness and spice: an absolute delight worthy of the finest gourmet kitchen.  From the advice of our serve we also had before us a soup of tiny eggplant, read peppers, celery and chicken in a curried broth and also a dish of ground pork served with a variety of raw vegetables.  The soup surpassed anything you might imagine: an entirely new taste enhanced by wondrously crisp fresh vegetables. The ground pork, our server explained to us, should be eater like a vegetable dip by touching the vegetables into the pork mix. Once again, the crispy raw vegetables mounted with the sweet and salty pork took the tongue to heaven.  By the way, if you prefer spicy, they will give you a little dish of finely sliced red peppers. But beware. One of those mini-slices goes quite a long way.
    Now to Khmer (Cambodian) table manners. The first thing we noticed was that the table setting had no chopsticks but only a fork and spoon. This seemed a bit odd. But then we stealthily observed the Khmer families around us. It seemed that you use the fork to push the rice and accompaniment onto your spoon.  Then the spoon, and only the spoon, was raised to the mouth.  It also seemed that for certain things, you could use your fingers.
    Cost wise, Khmer Kitchen is slightly more expensive than other community Asian restaurants, but only by a minimal margin. The bill comes to a little more than ten dollars per person, per course. If interesting and different cuisine interest you, make an excursion to Khmer Kitchen. Because of parking I would suggest lunch over dinner.

    Imagine making a smelly cheese using minced fish instead of milk, by leaving said fish in a sealed jar in the sun for 2 weeks.

    Call it "prahok" (pronounced pruh-HOK).

    Then imagine that the smell is considered a delicacy by everyone you know over the age of 50. It ranks (pun intended) up there with durian, animal carcasses (not that far off when you think about it), and turpentine mixed with wine that's turned into moldy vinegar.

    You and your cousins used to make jokes about keeping the boogeyman away by putting some prahok under your beds.  You used prahok in every dare you could think of, growing up. You chickened out every time because you remembered the one time your dad had you try it on a slice of cucumber and it tasted like an old sweaty gym sock that had been wrapped around an infection of some sort as a desperate measure, and then burned. THAT smell.

    Then, imagine your surprise when people who know what they're doing take some of this stuff, cook it up with ground pork, and transform it into something that's like a Cambodian chili meat dip that smells less like a chemical weapon and more like a bowl of meaty heaven.

    The above experience also applies to fish sauce, but most of you probably have already had fish sauce. Prahok, I feel, is still in "white people hipster territory."

    Anyway, take a moment to really appreciate that Khmer Kitchen has helped to remove my 28 year old, deeply ingrained aversion tied to multiple bad experiences. I will still never eat raw prahok like my parents do. But, I might ask my mom for a jar that I can use the next time I want to make food taste like home.

    Anyway, you should try Khmer Kitchen's prahok appetizer, and wonder what the hell I was talking about up there.

    Other notable dishes:

    Loc Lac - cubed, marinated steak with lime, salt, and pepper dipping sauce over a bed of vegetables. A classic in both Cambodia and Vietnam, you can't really go wrong with this one, and it's great for less adventurous eaters.

    Srelau kari (they may spell it differently) - Khmer red curry. They make theirs pretty simple with a curry base, dark meat chicken, and potato chunks, served with rice. I prefer mine with green beans, pork tenderloin, sweet potatoes, eggplant, and a french baguette instead, so this dish wasn't my favorite when I had it there.

    Kreung samot - this is an assortment of stir fried sea creatures spiced with kreung, which next to prahok could be considered one of the national flavors. Think lemongrass, kaffir lime leaf (if you've ever had it), chilis, garlic, and some other stuff I can't recall, all ground into a paste. Delicious, authentic, and surprisingly light.

    Pleah sach ko - Cambodian beef carpaccio. Tender beef that's "cooked" in citrus juices. If you've ever had the Vietnamese analogue, this one has a lot more herbs to go with it, not all of which I can name in English. It's also traditionally made as a spicy dish, though the meat itself is room temperature, and also incorporates mint leaves. It's a mix of a lot of different flavors that's both hearty and refreshing. One of my favorites while I was in Cambodia, and Khmer Kitchen does it justice.

    So those are my favorites. They also have standards like "sach ko ang," which is grilled beef kabobs, though they were out of them when I went. I don't know how many varieties of kabobs they have, but one of them is probably spiced in kreung. Try them all.

    The super awesome cherry on top is that this place is a BYO. Now I can bring shame to my family in front of other Cambodians. Woo!

History

Established in 2012.

We wanted and notice that there were no actual true Cambodian restaurant. A Cambodian restaurant that has no Chinese or Vietnamese influence. Not only that, when we think about Asian foods, automatic ally Pho, or Lo-mien come to mind, with shrimp fried rice. So the family agreed lets do something to get true Cambodian cuisine out there and show that our Cambodian culture is very unique, and intriguing. What better way to show case that but through a universal language of food. A language that that can be spoken in with out words but understood by every one

Meet the Business Owner

Sopheak N. Business Owner

First generation American Cambodian. Loves food, loves to cook, and is blessed with wife, a sister, and a mother that can cook unbelievably great. Main intention is to revive a slowly forgotten tradition, culture, and cuisine of Cambodia. Re-introducing the Khmer heritage to the younger (Americanized) Cambodian generation.
Ultimate goal is to distinguish Cambodian Cuisine from the style of plating, to the time of preparation, ingredient, and layering of flavor from your main stream Asian food.
