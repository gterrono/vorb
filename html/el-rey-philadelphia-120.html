    With so many authentic, delicious, dirt cheap Mexican options around, I can't help but feeling fleeced by Steven Starr's El Rey.  It's not so much the actual entrees that are expensive, but the so-so margaritas and appetizers.  That being said, the food here is pretty good and the atmosphere is kitchy and fun, so I didn't mind spending a few extra bucks for a friend's birthday dinner.

    Chips and guac were delectable, but at $10 should definitely be split amongst a group.  I got a chicken quesadilla (snooze, I know, but I was in the mood).  I thought quesadillas were usually flat and cut into four sections (please excuse if this is ignorant and gauche!), but these were rolled up soft taco style.  They were definitely yummy, but didn't have quite the authenticity of somewhere like los gallos or el jarocho.

    One thing that bothered me was that the entrees didn't come with any sides.  Our waiter made of point of saying "the items are a la carte, so the portions are BIG.  Therefore, they don't come with sides - you have to order them separately."  Ummm...the portions are not big.  They're not pretentious restaurant miniscule, but they're not big.  And this is where Steven Starr gets you yet again.  

    For me, El Rey is a "time and a place" restaurant.  If I want cheap, ridiculous whole-in-the-wall Mexican food, I'm not going to El Rey.  But if I want to have Mexican and  a fun night out with friends, I'd come back.

    As an aside, I preferred the guava margaritas but the rest of the table thought they were too sweet and liked the classic better.

    Okay Mr. Steven Starr....I see you steppin your game up.

    I've been wanting to go to El Rey for some time after experiencing over- priced/mediocre/bland Mexican food in Philly.  I'm so glad El Rey did not disappoint!

    So heres the deal:

    ATMOSPHERE: the place seems a little too narrow to be a restaurant, but I'm sure Starr chose it for it's primo location. The bar was also right up front which interfered with the waiting patrons....so this added to the claustrophobic feel. The restaurant attempts to give you that authentic Mexican vibe with its exposed stone walls, flags, cheesy decals, and christmas lights--I can dig it.  I like how this place is not upscale and it's more about the food here than the decor.  I will say the diversity of the crowd sucked, but lets hope with time that will change.  

    SERVICE: I was a little upset when the hostess seated us allll the way in the back booth by the kitchen...that is until I saw our waiter---ca-yute!!  I'm sure he gets some fat tips with that gorgeous smile.  Not only was he easy on the eyes, he was extremely charming and knowledgeable about the food.  Ladies...maybe you should request a seat in the back, and I'm sorry I don't know his name.  

    FOOD:  My table started off with the Chilaquiles with the fried egg.  I enjoyed it, nothing spectacular, but it was good.  I ordered the short rib enchilada, since it came highly recommended by my best foodie friend Yelp. It did not disappoint!  Really good-awesome flavor, good portion size...not tiny, but not massive.  And the price was right $9-"Yay I'm a fan!".  One of my dining companions ordered the Camarones (shrimp, fried garlic, squeeze of lime) and said it was delicious--however, it took her quite some time to separate the shell from the shrimp, so you have been warned that this dish takes some work!

    MARGARITAS:  I first ordered the classic with salt (it's my go-to in Mexican joints).  It was good, worth the $7.50?  Not quite....maybe $5 or $6.   Now that Pineapple Frozen Margarita (also $7.50).....it's on the money!  I usually don't order frozen drinks because I often feel like they cheat me with less alcohol in these.  But this little sucker surprised the hell out of me.  So fresh, so fruity, yet so powerful!  

    I like you El Rey. I'd be more than willing to go on a second date...and if you still have one of those frozen pineapple margaritas waiting for me you may just get lucky and score a third date.

    The first time I went to El Rey, I opened the menu, immediately spotted Guacamole, and frowned. People in the Northeast reduce Mexican food to tequila-inspired drinks and mashed avocados forgetting that it also includes meat and, occasionally, entire meals.  Mention a well-known Mexican restaurant in a room with over five preppy urbanites, and at least one will feel obligated to say "I love their margaritas, and they have the best guacamole!" Fortunately, the menu also featured chips and queso, something that is featured much more prominently in Mexican places in Texas. Lone-star state residents may not know how to use chopsticks, but at least they know that spicy cheese is far superior to lame, vegetable-based dips.

    El Rey's neutral stance on the "queso v. guacamole" debate reflects the delicate balancing act a Mexican restaurant in the Northeast faces. It has to please both people that actually know Tex-Mex and the majority of people that live in Philadelphia. El Rey can satisfy both parties. The tacos are tasty, and exactly the type of food booze-filled Philadelphians will need when watching their sports teams a few doors down at Drinkers gets too depressing. There are also a few options that will please a person who actually cares about food. One special, a burger infused with beef bone marrow, was particularly satisfying.

    Despite the fact that I would like to believe these two populations could not coexist, it somehow works at El Rey. The hipster-inspired staff is attentive, yet relaxed, putting everyone in a cool, calm mood. Prices can also be reasonable. Some choices cost less than ten dollars and are enough for dinner, although you might have to spend more to get more interesting options. A few dishes need a bit of work (the Chile Relleno just misses), but the food is better than most places at this price point, and much better than other Mexican options in Philadelphia.
