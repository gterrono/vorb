    There's something a little bizarre about The Dandelion, one of Stephen Starr's more recent ventures.  Taking on the persona of a British Pub, this dimly lit space tries too hard to look the part.  Between the Hogwarts-esque chandeliers, faded walls, and chalkboard menus on the walls, you either feel like you're at UK Epcot or at a very touristy bar in London's West End.  But nonetheless, Starr proves that he at least kind of knows what he's getting into with The Dandelion.

    There's a lengthy beer list (although mostly bottles) which will satisfy any beer aficionado, but you'd be silly not to get a pint of the Bombardier; a slightly bitter English ale (imported from the UK) that pairs well with pretty much anything on the menu.  Before arriving, I had heard rumors that the triple cooked chips, deviled eggs, and macaroni and cheese were menu standouts, but being at a British Pub, I wanted nothing more than beer and Shepherd's pie.  For $16, the pie comes out in a scorching-hot crock pot and includes a mix of ground lamb and veggies in a savory gravy, covered with mashed potatotes and a thin layer of cheddar on top.  The dish was so hot that I had to wait a little bit to be able to taste the flavors without burning my tongue.  Also, why no spoon to finish up the dish?

    The menu features plenty of other options both traditional (Fish and Chips, Bangers and Mash) and non-traditional (Tandoori Chicken) British pub fare, but everything is Starr-ified (if you don't know what that means, you need to eat out more in Philadelphia).  One other note was the service.  We had at least three different people visit our table throughout the course of the meal: one lady came over to offer us water.  Then she disappeared and a manager stopped by to take our food order.  Then the water lady came back and took our drink order.  And everyone kept pushing bread at us (even after we finished our dinner).  The staff needs to get it together.

    Overall, the food was good and the service was ok.  If i go back, I'll probably just hang at the bar, knocking back an imperial pint of the Bombardier with some deviled eggs on the side.

    What a novel and enjoyable experience!  I almost expected to see Sherlock Holmes eating dinner there, himself, in the dim lighting as I definitely felt like I was no longer in Philadelphia, but instead eating dinner at a corner pub in London of old.

    Menu, while not extensive, is uniquely British.   Burgers, lamb curry, chicken tandoori, fish & chips, bangers and mash, shepard's pie, rabbit pie - just some of choices that await you.   I had the chicken tandoori which was quite good, and the summer tomato salad which was refreshing.   My colleague ordered the lamb curry, which tasted equally good.  Portions are generous, and prices reasonable.  Meals are also presented in an attractive manner. The desserts were scrumptious.   Beer menu is remarkable (but don't expect to order a Budweiser), as is the selection of wines.    

    I found the service to be pleasant, attentive, but not overbearing - just right.  Where this restaurant excels, however, is the atmosphere.  There are so many little details and nuances which add to - as much as they enhance, the dining experience.  It is intimate, relaxing, and conducive to thinking that you truly are in a London pub.   I do recommend reservations, as with any other Steven Starr restaurant, there is the likelihood that it will be crowded.    Also, while there are three floors for seating, the tables on each floor are limited so as to not be cramped.  

    Some other reviews opine that The Dandelion tries "too hard" to look "the part."  That's fair.  Yet while I can perhaps understand this point in certain respects, do ultimately believe that if less effort was put into the decor, you might as well be going to dinner at run-of-the-mill chain pub like Elephant & Castle.   Make no mistake, the extra effort and attention that went into conceptualizing this restaurant ensures that there will be nothing mediocre about the dining experience.   And that, to me at least, is a good thing.

    In the 1915 novel, "The Valley of Fear" Sherlock Holmes states:  "Mediocrity knows nothing higher than itself; but talent instantly recognizes genius."  If I am to apply Mr. Holmes reasoning, then Steven Starr, in my opinion, is exceptionally talented when it comes to reinventing ordinary ideas for restaurants.    The Dandelion merely shows off his genius.

    Bravo.

    Much of what I will write has been offered by other reviewers, but I'm quite in love with the Dandelion Pub at the moment, so...

    First, the perfect:

    Other than that unbelievably fresh piece of battered haddock I had one summer in a seaside village chip shop on the Isle of Arran, Scotland, the fish-n-chips at Dandelion were the best I've ever eaten.  The fish was delicate in flavor, and its texture hit the right balance of meaty vs. flaky.  The chips were crisp on the outside and crusted with sea salt, while the potato inside was Yukon Gold smooth and piping hot.  Rather than the traditional newspaper cone, the dish is artfully served on a charcuterie board.

    The deviled eggs make for a tasty and inexpensive ($4) appetizer.  Their filling is wonderfully flavored with smoked paprika, ground mustard, horseradish, and (did I detect?) a hint of curry.  My friend and I also munched on the sweet and crisp butter lettuce salad and were grateful to enjoy a true ginger beer and a refreshing Belvoir's Elderflower Presse soda (can't find these many other places).

    Much has been made of the decor, and I have to agree:  it is uber-charming.  

    The near perfect (and the near misses):

    On a second visit, I ordered the Cumberland Sausages (trad'l bangers-n-mash).  The sausage was savory and melt-in-your-mouth tender, but as a previous reviewer noted, it was undercooked in the center.  Pink in the middle is great for steak, but I found it unappetizing for pork.  I would order this dish again but would ask that my sausage be cooked throughout (even if it means sacrificing a bit of that tenderness).

    The Honeysuckle Scotch cocktail sounded great on the menu, but when mixed, the laundry list of ingredients canceled each other out.  What I ended up with tasted like a watery Dewars and ginger ale.

    These missteps, however, are so minor that I won't even deduct a star for them.  I can't wait to eat here again.

Specialties

London's culinary revolution comes to Philadelphia with this eclectic English pub. Cozy rooms and intimate bars create a storied, Old World aura, while updated seasonal takes on British food make up the menu. In keeping with tradition, cask and tap craft beers are always rotating, a proper afternoon tea service has a sweet and savory menu, and classic beef family-style roasts are served on Sundays.
