    The Venture Inn has been a tradition in the somewhat seedy world of the Gay-borhood of Philadelphia.  With more and open acceptance of different life styles, however, new, upscale, expensive and even outstanding eateries have popped up all over the place in the Washington Square area that cater to mainly a gay clientele, and therefore some of the old places have been left behind.  The Venture is one of them.  

    Although a "grand dame" of the community, it nonetheless has been left behind and nowadays caters to mainly an adult community, from which the moniker "The Denture Inn" has emerged.  Although unjust, its reputation continues despite new management and what seems to be an honest effort at changing its character.

    The small side street where Venture is Camac Street, one  of the numerous alley-type streets all over the older part of Philadelphia.  These small streets have become more pricey and upscale, however, Camac has maintained a sort of seedy reputation due to its commercial employment.

    We went to the Venture Inn just to see how things had changed.  I'm afraid they haven't sufficiently to go there again.  In four categories, service, food, ambiance, and price, it loses to the neighboring establishments.

    First the service.  Our waiter was obviously used to the older crowd and seemed to want to be our friend from the start.  Frankly, if I wanted to be friends with a waiter, I'd make it outside of a restaurant.  His overtures seemed pedantic, especially compared to the mediocre service we got which included forgetting a round of drinks we ordered with dinner.

    Second, the food.  The mussels I ordered came in a tasty sauce.  However, there were several unopened, bad ones.  That is something any good restaurant would be able to screen.  The food was generally good, although the presentation was "homey", not what you would expect for the price.  No espresso.

    Third, the ambiance.  On a beautiful cool summer evening in Philadelphia, al fresco seating would have been a plus.  The Venture Inn restaurant sits in a building where the smoke stench has permeated the walls over decades and decades. The yellowish walls attest to the nicotine and tar of the pre-smoke prohibition in Philadelphia.  Frankly, I was gasping for breath after an  hour.

    Fourth, the price.  We spent forty per person including a glass of wine each.  Right around the corner, I can get the same at an upscale, new, saucy Mixto restaurant that features Cuban/Colombian infusion with knock-your-socks off service and outstanding ambiance and food.

    It is obvious by the clientele surrounding us that  the Venture is happily within its core clientele of tired gay men in their 60s to whom a disrespectful out-of-shape waiter and mediocre food is a compliment.

    Once upon a time, dining at the Venture Inn Restaurant was a reason to visit Philadelphia; in fact… Once upon a time, dining at the Venture Inn Restaurant was a reason to visit Philadelphia; in fact it was the primary reason.   Not any more.

    Venture Inn was a dark, mysterious hidden treasure where one could enjoy a good meal and camaraderie at a reasonable price.   The walls were dark green, the floor was dark and the lighting very, very subdued.  The atmosphere was like a hunting lodge.  Tables were arranged so single diners could socialize with those at adjacent tables creating a warm, convivial ambience.  The nightly crowds confirmed the restaurant had it just right.

    Then new management decided to brighten up the place with lighter shades, brighter lights and an Applebee's style decor.  Loyal customers and visitors were not pleased.  The food and service remained good but the warm, seductive atmosphere was gone and so were the crowds.

    In its latest incarnation, the walls have been painted a hideous shade of red with lighting you might find in a chain hotel lobby.  And to add insult to injury, the tables were rearranged and separated completely removing the  social aspect of dining at the Venture Inn completely.

    Whether or not you agree with my opinion on the changes, there's no denying that business has declined sharply in recent years.  There was a time when you had to wait for a table and now the tables are waiting for you.  If management really wants to keep the restaurant going, it's time to restore the dark and cozy atmosphere that appealed to so many of us for so many years.   I doubt I'll be back until that happens.
