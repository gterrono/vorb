    We went to this lovely restaurant for a special occasion.
    It is a small sized restaurant, so get those reservations in if you want to go.

    Staff were friendly and helpful. Water glasses were always kept full. And since at this BYOB we forgot to bring the B, we needed the water. Sad that there were no drink options other than water or coffee.
    Ambiance is with warm tones. A porno mirror on the ceiling on one side of the restaurant lets you do people watching on the sly.
    Our waiter had over sized glasses that looked like the old guy from the movie "UP." But, it was trendy in appearance. He also looked a little like a politician from the 80's with a bow tie on, but maybe I should avoid that reference.

    The food is above average. But, there are a minimum amount of choices.
    I ordered a short rib ravioli. It was scrumptous. It had a meaty and cheesey sauce. And the short ribs in the ravioli added a hint of sweetness to it. I wish it had a little more color or flare in appearance.
    My wife ordered the mussells, which I did not taste. It was slightly spicey and came on the waiter's suggestion over the lobster ramen.
    The main course, my wife ordered a special- Opa. A meaty fish that came with a sweet and citrusy sauce. It was plain in presentation. But the flavor really popped out.
    I had a Chile Pork Rib. And my first impression based on smell was wow. And the piece was huge. It must have been an inch thick. A typical barbecue flavor was found, with a nice kick. It was not an overwhelming amount of Chile. Just enough to let you know it was there and needing the kale and lentils to calm it down. It also came with pig tails. I thought they were very tasty. They did look like tails but, were meaty and hearty in flavor.
    On to dessert, we ordered two different desserts. Of course, my wife ordered the one I was debating taking. She had the Chocolate brownie semifreddo. It was clearly very rich and an ample portion. It could have been enjoyed by two people. The brownie was thick and fudge like.
    I ordered a banana turnover, because I wanted the brown sugar ice cream that came with it. I think it could have used a larger portion of ice cream to balance the strong banana flavor. And to my surprise, when it was brought out a candle was on the side of the plate and the words, Happy Birthday written on the plate. No pretentious or loud singing, thankfully. But, a number of staff were certain to wish me a happy birthday and guests on either side of me in the cramped booth seating.
    It was a pleasant surprise. My wife did not ask them to do it. But, she did tell them it was my birthday when she made the reservations.
    This overall was a pleasant experience. I'd give the place a definite recommendation for a well cooked meal.

    Matyson delivers very good, seasonal new-American fare in a byob concept that is sure to please both your palate as well as your wallet.

    Our 5:30pm Wednesday night reservations were booked well in advance, and they still called to confirm.  Wednesday night at 5:30?  Yep, this place is that in-demand and by 7:00 it was full.  Make reservations well ahead of your chosen date.

    I enjoyed the lobster/ramen soup, prepared with ramen noodles, pickled kohlrabi, egg, and chunks of lobster and pork belly in an alluring broth.  The duck was also delicious, prepared medium rare with quince and pak choi.  The other members of my party dined on wild bass served in a foie gras sauce and goat cheese gnocchi.  Our server Tracy was warm and attentive.  She mentioned how the menu changes every few weeks, so there will always be a new experience for the repeat crowd.

    Atmosphere like many small in-line restaurants is tight but not restrictive.  I was a little disappointed by the absence of a cheese selection to start things off, especially given how delicious the food was.  Also, I had the feeling that in trying to be original and seasonal, using local ingredients and changing up the menu, that the dishes lacked that final small step in reaching epic status that a more consistent approach can bring.

    There is street parking up and down 19th as well as several parking garages in the immediate vicinity.

    I really enjoyed my stay at Matyson.  Dinner for four, with four wonderful appetizers and entrees, three desserts, and two latte's came in under $200 and I felt that this was a solid price for the quality of the meal.  Fans of Bibou will find this a welcome new-American option of comparable quality and value for the high end byob scene.

    Distinctive palate, unusual and interesting combinations that aren't just a stunt or a trend. The plates have very smart composition, both appetizers and entrees yield some very layered flavors as you move through eating them. I get tired sometimes of the formulaic ways that some chefs hide the fact that their plates are really a meat-and-two-veg. Nothing we had at Matyson tonight felt that way--everything on the plate had a relationship to everything else. The pig tails, rock octopus and porcini ravioli we had tonight as appetizers were very different from one another and yet it felt like there were some interesting thematic and imaginative connections. The entrees were satisfyingly different in size and scale from the appetizers, and rather appealingly simpler than the appetizers in basic design.

    I also rarely have desserts in restaurants. If I'm willing to keep going, it's because the place really has my attention and my trust. (Desserts just so often seem to be coming from another culinary planet than the main menu). I kept going at Matyson and I was glad I did--the desserts very much come from the same smart, interesting, imaginative place as the rest of the dishes, and have a real aesthetic connection to the overall menu.

    As long as I've got the mike, can we talk for a second about portion size? Our appetizers were really quite substantial--four cuts of pig tail, four extremely rich ravioli, four tentacles on zucchini. The entrees were by my standards--and I'm not an anorexic model, folks--appropriately big: a large pork chop, a whole duck breast, numerous sea scallops. So I am genuinely boggled by folks who write that the food is "tapas-sized" or the portions are unusually small. What's "normal", then? An all-you-can eat buffet? The giant model of Devil's Tower made out of mashed potatoes in "Close Encounters of the Third Kind"? Unless you really aren't happy if you haven't eaten half an entire lamb or something on that scale I think you'll be ok after two courses.

History

Established in 2003.

Matyson opened its doors in the fall of 2003  and became an immediate success under the leadership of Matt & Sonjia Spector. Matt & Sonjia installed the framework for what Matyson has become today. After four great years the couple left to pursue another restaurant in the Napa Valley named Jole, but they left the restaurant in very capable hands. Younger cousin, Ben Puchowitz, whom had been working at Matyson as a line cook since its opening, assumed the role of Executive Chef, and his father, Butch Puchowitz became full owner. Ben has instilled his own personality into the food, without changing the original concepts. The staff of Matyson has continued to win awards and high acclaim for their refined cuisine and cozy atmosphere.
