        I really don't understand the hype about this place if you base it on their food alone.
        I've been here twice, once in the summer when I sat outside and I have to admit it was a magnificent day to enjoy a sandwich while people watching at Rittenhouse Square. But the decor and location are all this place has going for it, if it weren't for the awesome floor tiles in the bathroom and outdoor seating, I'd definitely rate Parc as 2 stars.
        The french onion soup is definitely lacking (3/5 stars) and after hearing everyone rave about it, I was left to doubt people's knowledge of how french onion soup should taste. It was clear that the soup had not been simmering for long enough and water and/or broth had been added to increase the quantity.
        I've also had the Country Chicken Club (2/5 stars) which is supposed to feature a rosemary aioli, bacon, and avocado. The aioli was tasteless and astonishingly greasy and the chicken was dry and chewy.
        The service is nothing special, and the hostess usually doesn't greet you and until you speak to her, which really irks me because... she's the hostess...
        I know it's repetitive but thinking back on the food I want to emphasize that I gave Parc an extra star for the decor and vibe (yeah, the food is -that- bad, and I like the floors -that- much).

        When Parc first opened I wasn't thrilled.

        To be perfectly honest, I never really want to buy into the Rittenhouse scene.

        However, I've been back several times, and the minimal faux paus of a new restaurant are left in the dust as Parc is one of the most pleasant places to spend a lunch/brunch in Philadelphia.

        The last several times I've been there I can say: the croque monsieur really reminds me of the champs de elysee, the quiche is a thicker version of my grandmother's perfect quiche, the beet salad worked itself out (at first it was with frise, and didn't quite hit the mark), mushroom soup: YUM!

        Wandering in around dinner time on a Friday night, I fully expected a long wait.  Wandering into a concept restaurant in a hip part of town on a Friday night, I fully expected an even longer wait, being fresh in from out of town, dressed to travel, tired, hungry and frankly a little schlumpy.  

        The good news: we waited only 20 minutes which was the perfect amount of time to notice the absolutely perfect attention to detail that transformed the space into a french brasserie.  The bad news: they saw us coming a mile away, and we received what has to be the worst table in the house that will seat 3 people.  We were about 2 feet from the 4-person-deep bar and right in the line of traffic to all parts of the restaurant.  I spent a good portion of the evening with someone's rear end at just about eye level.  Not only that, but our waiter took quite a long time to appear at our table, and was clearly disappointed when he did and realized we had already ordered wine at the bar (that is, he doesn't get tipped on that, and we are only ordering water from him)

        Then:  The food came.  Everything else melted away as I sank my teeth into the first bite of country pate from the charcuterie plate.  Slightly rough around the edges, with a slight bite of coarse peppercorns that perfectly punctuated the smooth, earthy flavor of the meat itself.  Also included with the plate were sturdy and satisfying cured sausages, an ethereal chicken liver mousse and some ho-hum prosciutto.  

        I have mixed feelings about the pissaladiere (onion tart)  What was touted as a tart filled with onions, goat cheese, anchovies and olives came to the table with an unremarkable, soggy, salty filling ( i know, i know anchovies are salty but still...) The dough, however was incredible.  Light but substantial, it had the perfect crunch and flavor as you bit into it, but disappears in the next instant leaving you craving 'just one more bite.'

        We also ordered half a dozen each of clams and oysters on the half shell.  (We must have looked a bit out of place, for the waiter impatiently informed us that they would be raw...duh.)  I can't exclaim too much over these.  They were good, fresh, and hard to screw up.  

        Desserts are forgettable: I'm pretty sure we had some standard Creme Brulee and something warm with apples.  Tarte Tartin?

        As our first dinner in Philly (after Pat's and Geno's for lunch) Parc really set the bar for the rest of the trip.  The service improved greatly throughout the course of our meal, so I'm assuming he was just busy in the beginning.  The atmosphere was relaxing, and our location grew more tolerable after the first bottle of wine.  We left full, happy, and excited to see what the rest of Philadelphia's culinary scene had in store for us.

        Daddy Fatsacks was in town so we took him out to get a feel for Philadelphia.  Rittenhouse was a natural choice and Parc had availability on OpenTable, so we went for good, old-timey cocktails, terrific French food and excellent people-watching in one of Philly's most authentic-feeling themed restaurants.

        Pros
        -Food - terrific French cuisine, which is to say everything avec copious amounts of butter. We started with escargots and the charcuterie platter (country pate, a selection of cured meats and the most delicious chicken-liver mousse) and moved onto an assortment of entrees representing land and sea with equanimity. The branzino, hanger steak and skate were highlights.
        -Drinks - Parc features a refreshing list of special cocktails with old time recipes (sazerac and calvados sidecar) as well as an excellent wine list with a good selection in the moderate price range.
        -Service - as a Stephen Starr operation, the service is professional, knowledgeable and customer-focused.
        -Atmosphere - a great take on a French brassierie with very authentic design-details.
        -People Watching - the bar and restaurant have a unique scene, which is a way of saying its a bit of meet market for the late 20s and up crowd. We witnessed first-hand the reality TV-spiced flirtations of gold diggers and $30K millionaires at the bar and thoroughly enjoyed its ridiculousness as a backdrop to our meal. This counts Parc out as a genuinely romantic French restaurant.

        Cons
        -Noise - the bar gets pretty busy and there are a lot of tables in a space featuring hard surfaces so the ambient noise level gets energetic to say the least. We weren't at a large table and at times it was difficult to follow the conversation and/or the waitress.
        -Seating - brasserie style arrangement, so the wicker chair and round, wobbly table seating isn't terribly comfortable, but it does feel authentic.

        I really, really like Parc. The reason I've given only three stars is because I've had two subpar lunches here, which detract from the fantastic dinners.

        Parc has a perfect location right on Rittenhouse Square. The decor, the outside tables...it takes me back to my favorite Left Bank cafe everytime I go. Service is very good, and the staff is knowledgeable.

        Arrive early or be sure to have reservations, even on weekdays. If it's nice outside be prepared for a long wait if you want an outdoor table.

        There is an expansive wine list. Personally, the house wines (all French), are reasonable, good, and come in convenient half carafe and (yeah, like I'd order a half...) full carafe sizes are what I'd opt for.

        The four or five dinners I've had at Parc were all phenomenal. The big classics, like boeuf bourguinonne and coq au vin, were spot on (and yes, I AM comparing to places in Paris and New York). I've ordered the Lyonnaise salad several times. Frisee lettuce, crispy lardons and potatoes, and a PERFECTLY poached egg...you know they've got good staff in the kitchen when a dish that requires so many technical elements comes out perfectly.

        On to the negative :-( I've had two really bad lunches at Parc. Not terrible, given the great atmosphere and service, but definitely a huge difference from the dinner experience. The onion soup (honestly, how can you fuck THAT up?) was grossly oversalted. And I'm not talking an extra pinch or two. Burgers were overcooked. Fries were overdone to near disintegration. I made the comment (purposefully) loudly enough so our waiter could hear that "the B team must be on station today." The waiter looked at me sympathetically, and shrugged his shoulders as if to say, "sorry man, but what the fuck can I do?"

        Bottom line: Go in the evening, get a table outside (weather permitting!), and enjoy the excellent dinners. Hopefully Stephen Starr reads this and gets on his midday staff. Parc currently reigns as the best French bistro in Rittenhouse since Brasserie Perrier folded.

      Specialties

      This stylish addition to Rittenhouse Square pays tribute to French cafe culture, serving breakfast, lunch and dinner seven days a week. Ideal for a quick bite or a leisurely meal, the menu is filled with classics: onion soup, escargots, steak frites and towering plateaus de fruits de mer. The baguettes are made in-house and  the wine list is expansive. Sip a Kir Royale at the zinc bar or enjoy a cafe au lait while basking in the sun.

      Stephen S. Business Owner

      Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District. STARR Restaurants expanded to Atlantic City, NJ, with Buddakan and The Continental located in the Pier Shops at Caesars in 2006, and to Florida with Steak 954 at the W Hotel Fort Lauderdale in 2009, and Makoto in the Bal Harbour Shops in 2011.
