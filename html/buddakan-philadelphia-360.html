    3.5 Stars for sure! We had to sprint from the cab to the front door to avoid being completely drenched from the down pour outside only to be met by a waterfall art piece at the entrance! If it's purpose was to set you into a monk like ease, it's brutally overpowered by the wall of noise and heart-throbbing base emanating from the lofty space. Book-ended by a neon bar area and a giant Buddha, Buddakan embodies the hyper chic young crowd that I knew Philly had hiding somewhere. I came here for "the scene" and hoped for some good food while I was here and that is what I got. While the cocktails seem a bit over priced they pour them heavy.

    As food goes, an assortment of Westernized rice stir fried with crab or lobster meat, fried eggplant more elevated then your favorite corner Chinese restaurant, and some hefty noodles in sauce dishes typify the menu. Nothing blew my mind, and since we had a large group (dishes come "tapas style" and are meant to be shared) we pretty much tasted the whole menu. Nothing knocked my socks off but the waitress was fast and efficient, food came swiftly and the Japanese beer flowed cold to wash down the dishes (a little under salted for my taste, but that is much better then over salted)! All and all a fun and energetic scene, great for groups out to dress up for some solid food. They would benefit from a menu face-lift that brings a little more creativity and boldness to the every day titles and styles of Asian fusion!

    Listed in Two days in Philly: favorite spots for food and drinks

    I have to agree with Emily; the service is great and the space is nice but the food is nothing to write home about.

    Before we sat down I knew that I wanted to order the pad Thai ($20) and the chicken and Ginger dumplings ($11?) but my boyfriend was having a hard time finding something because he's vegetarian. Our waitress (Julie) was a doll and told us that the edamame ravioli ($12?) could be vegetarianized and so could the cashew chicken ($22) with the chicken substituted for tofu. My dumplings were fine, but you can find those at pretty much any Japanese place for half the price. The pad Thai was pretty disappointing and was more akin to one of those bowls you buy from the supermarket rather than a good Thai dish you'd expect from a restaurant. The edamame ravioli arrived in vegetarian form, which means without the sauce. They were creamy and delicious, but imagine having ravioli without any sauce....yeah, not as pleasant. I passed on trying the cashew tofu....now onto the dessert! I had the donuts and he had the 'crying chocolate' which is a chocolate lava cake with Vietnamese coffee ice cream. I believe both dishes were $11 or $12 each. The donuts were excellent; nice and warm and fluffy on the inside and came with 3 dipping sauces: a chocolate ganache, Ginger cream cheese and blackberry jam. The blackberry jam was by far my favorite, but the cream cheese was good too. Again, I passed on the chocolate but tried the ice cream which was good but a little too strong.

    I probably won't be back because I know where to find better food at a cheaper price, but if I were to return I'd get the edamame ravioli and duck buns and donuts for dessert; just skip the entrees entirely and stick to appetizers and dim sum.

    Even though the food was disappointing for the most part, I really have to give the service a hand. The staff makes a huge effort to be nice and welcoming. They even fold your napkin when you get up to go to the bathroom! We definitely felt that our business was appreciated.

    Listed in Philadelphia Center City Restaurant Week, Steven Starr is everywhere in Philly!

    I had my first ever restaurant experience in Philadelphia at Buddakan last week.  I'd been wanting to visit Buddakan since I'd moved into the area, but had never gotten around to it.  I figured restaurant week was a good enough excuse.  

         My boyfriend and I arrived and were seated right on time at our 7:30 reservation even though the place was super crowded.  The lady at the coat check was really friendly.  We were seated on the lower level below the tables that are in front of the big Buddha.  Techno music was playing loudly, so it was hard to hear my boyfriend from across the table.  I felt like I was in a bar or a nightclub instead of an upscale Asian fusion restaurant. I ordered a zen-gria.  Honestly, it tasted a lot like fuzzy navel.  I didn't taste the ginger brandy as much as I was hoping.  Okay, but not worth $9.  
     
         We got to choose three courses from the restaurant week menu.  We both had the shrimp and scallop spring rolls to start.  We only got one each, and they were quite small, but I have to say, they were stuffed full of seafood and were crispy and fresh. No cabbage or other fillers there!  

         My main course was the seabream with mandarin miso, Japanese pickles, and shishitou (little Japanese green peppers).  I was very excited about having shishitou, which I hadn't eaten since August when I was last back in Japan.  The fish itself was okay, but just tasted like a piece of average fried fish and was very small.  The citrus miso was slightly sweet with a hint of citrus, but didn't resemble miso at all in either taste nor texture.  I got two shishitou that of course were tasty as they have a slight heat to them and are a bit smokey.  Shishitou never disappoint.  In contrast, the Japanese pickles were disappointing because I only got one small slice of daikon, a little ginger, and then four or five cucumbers.  I guess I was hoping for more of an assortment with some nasu (eggplant), kinoko (mushrooms), or even different varieties of daikon or more daikon.  Oh well, the dish was good, but tiny, and nothing blew me away.  My boyfriend on the other hand went on about his dish during the whole ride home.  He had the BBQ pork tenderloin.  He said it was incredibly tender.  I didn't try it though, because I don't eat pork.  

          For dessert I had the  "Crying Chocolate" which looked and tasted like your typical lava cake you'd get at any chain restaurant like TGI Friday's or Applebees except it was 1/10 of the size.   However, the Vietnamese coffee ice cream that accompanied it was delightful.  I liked the added crunch of the chocolate pieces at the bottom.  I would rather have had a big bowl of that than the lava cake.  My boyfriend had the "Dip Sum" Doughnuts.  I tried one with the blackberry jam dipping sauce, and it was just an ordinary donut.  I thought the packaging inside the little Chinese takeout box was kind of cheesy and was like something I'd expect to see at a more casual dining spot.  The presentation of our main courses was beautiful though.  

         So overall, the food was good, but nothing was spectacular.  It wasn't a bad deal for $35 during restaurant week, thought I might have felt differently if we'd paid full price for it.  However, the service was phenomenal.  We were seated quickly.  The waiter was friendly and helpful with the menu.  I told him about my dietary restrictions and he was very accommodating.  My water glass was never empty, and the courses were spaced out, but not too much.  The lady at the coat check even helped me put my coat on when we left.  I wouldn't be opposed to going back, but I don't think this is the first place I'd go to if I had a choice and was looking to spend that kind of cash.

    New Year's Eve dinner review, 4.5 stars rounded down.

    Buddakan is an Asian-fusion, Stephen Starr restaurant in downtown Philly, blocks away from Penn's Landing (great for walking to see the fireworks after a late dinner). We came here for New Year's Eve because I had been wanting to try Buddakan, and because they allow you to order off of their standard menu for New Year's Eve instead of forcing you to eat a prix fixe menu for four times the cost like many places in Philly do for NYE.

    Outside of Buddakan being great for NYE, the service is very good. Our waitress was very friendly and took pictures for my girlfriend and I. Pictures turn out well around the big Buddha statue (overall ambiance of the restaurant is nice and not too loud).

    Food is very good, but portions small and on the pricier side. Appetizers run around $15/ea and are very small. We had the Kobe beef satay, which was only three small pieces of beef with some fried veggies. That was $17. Pork dumplings were $12 and were five small dumplings. Both appetizers were good, but I'd pass and try new dishes if I were to return.

    For entrees, I had the lamb chops and she had the Yaki Udon noodles. The lamb chops were actually well portioned and done right.. I enjoyed them. The Yaki Udon noodles were good as well, but $19 for noodles even at this type of restaurant felt too high.

    For dessert, we had the Chocolate Bento Box, which was a great choice. This is a huge dessert, with six different dishes.

    See MY PHOTOS for the food.

    I really enjoyed our NYE at Buddakan. The price was a bit high for what we got, but was still a bargain compared to other NYE options in the area (prix fixe menus), so I still give it a high 4.5. I rounded down because had it not been NYE, I would have given it a 4.

Stephen S. Business Owner

Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District. STARR Restaurants expanded to Atlantic City, NJ, with Buddakan and The Continental located in the Pier Shops at Caesars in 2006, and to Florida with Steak 954 at the W Hotel Fort Lauderdale in 2009, and Makoto in the Bal Harbour Shops in 2011.
