    Listed in Culinary Colonialism, Nobody Likes You, Won't You Be My Neighbor?, Ritten-hungry, You Never Take Me Anywhere!

    I accidentally ended up at Tinto during Restaurant Week, so I thought I'd update my review to tell you about what the place is like during this most wonderful time of the year. In a nutshell, just as good as it is the rest of the year, and if y'all can get a reservation you should head in ASAP.

    So how does one accidentally eat at a Garces restaurant during RW? Well, at 5:30 you decide that you are going to combat the cold with some delicious soup and maybe some mussels and steel yourself to brave the crowds at the bar and wait until you can squeeze yourself in. Except then you walk in and the restaurant is nearly empty and the bar has plenty of open spaces. And you realize that the rest of the city paid attention to the fact that a snow emergency has just been declared and have stayed home. So you sit down and notice the RW menu and send up a prayer of thanks to the gods of overindulgence.

    The RW menu offers a solid list of Tinto's highlights. For the first course, I tried two new (to me) dishes - the Basque Onion Soup and the Arugala. The soup was good, but not quite as good as the Sopa de Castano. It was very light and a little bit sweet, with a good ratio of broth to bread to cheese. The arugala was surprisingly delicious, it comes with almonds, ham, figs and delicious balls of fried goat cheese that would make a wonderful dish on their own. The vinaigrette is sweet and light and very flavorful. I'm not usually a fan of salads in the winter, but I would go back for this. For the second course, I had the Duck Montadito and the Moules Basquaise. The duck montadito was excellent, as always. It's duck wrapped in ham. You can't mess that up. The mussels were great (they were the whole reason I stopped in, after all!), and there were a TON of mussels in my little cast iron pot. The only disappointment was that, for RW, they are being served with toasted bread rather than the frites with lemon aioli. I finished up my meal with the Gateaux Basque, which is what I imagine would happen if shortbread and a pound cake had a baby - dense, lemony, delicious. And oh so filling. By this point I was starting to question the sanity of eating here during Restaurant Week without a friend to share the burden of so many plates.

    If you happen to read this review in the next few hours, it will be worth your while to brave the sleet to snag one of the tables that have opened up on account of the weather. If not, you're just going to have to hope for another fortuitous weather emergency so you can snag your own table.

    As much as I love all of Garces' restaurants, Tinto has a special place in my heart. And in my… As much as I love all of Garces' restaurants, Tinto has a special place in my heart. And in my neighborhood. As much as I appreciate the proximity, though, I'd be willing to travel anywhere to eat at Tinto. For great service, delicious drinks, and absolutely, 100% phenomenal food, Tinto is hands-down the best Garces restaurant. And maybe best restaurant ever.

    The thing that I really wish to impart to you is the wonder that is the Sopa de Castano. The first time I ate this soup, I felt adulterous. I am single. It does not matter. The soup is decadently, deliciously, sinfully rich. It is flavorful and creamy, the tower of mushrooms and duck is savory, the pistachios provide an intriguing and delightful contrast of flavor and texture, and just for good measure they top it off with a quail egg. When I eat this soup, I take a picture to send to a friend who moved away from the area - just to taunt her. Because she can't eat it anymore.

    I try to eat something different each time I visit, which is hard because the food is so good that I end up at the restaurant far more often than they change the menu. I do have some favorites, including:
    * The shortrib bocadillo. Shortribs. And bacon. Do I really need to say anything else?
    * Moules Basquaise. Delicious and hearty tomato sauce with mussels and hearty chunks of chorizo, served with delicious fries - and at the bottom of your fries, a lovely bright lemon aioli. When it is snowing outside, this is what I want to eat.
    * Lamb brochetas. These skewers of perfectly cooked lamb wrapped in bacon come in shot glasses of a sauce that is so good that I have been known to remove the meat and drink the sauce alone. Yes, I will admit that to the internet.
    * Duck montaditos. Canapes with duck confit, la peral cheese, serrano ham - and topped with a black cherry, which might sound weird but is so marvelously delicious and just brings all of the flavors together.
    * Chuleta. This is a relatively new addition to the menu and I'm so glad it's staying. It's a delicious piece of pork tenderloin with apple chutney and apple cider marinade. This is another perfect winter meal.

    I've always had impeccable service at Tinto. Because it's nearby, I sometimes stop in alone - either eating at the bar or seated, I've always had very attentive and friendly waiters, and never been made to feel self-conscious for taking up a whole table for little lonely me (although, in my defense, I could very easily eat enough to make up for that empty chair!). The drink selections are great, I usually go for the temporada sangria, but it's always worth telling the bartender what you're thinking about eating and asking him/her to recommend something. And you should always stay for dessert and coffee, because the coffee is rich and delicious and the Gateaux Basque is incredible.     Read more

    Wow. Yes, WOW. That's how I felt after having the chef's tasting menu at Tinto. Note: this was a special tasting menu that differs from the one listed, and it came with three different wines.

    We started with a complimentary puff pastry. Good, nothing too spectacular (I know, doesn't fit in with the WOW... patience). Then came the "cheese" tray. Let the wow-ing begin. Thinly sliced apples, aged ham, olives, fig spread, warm bread, honey, and chili powder were the accouterments for two soft cheeses and one harder cheese. All delicious, and all up to our imagination. It was fun putting together the perfect combination, using a few, or all of the ingredients.

    Next was the Reveulto. Scrambled egg, lobster, goat cheese, and spinach - served warm and creamy. This was awesome and totally new, at least for me (let me know if this combo is something you're used to). We're now two plates in, and I'm wondering if finishing the cheese plate was a good idea - it was a tasty idea, but the thought of three more plates and dessert was frightening.

    Quack! The Duck canape followed, and it may have been my favorite part of the meal. Slices maybe 1.5 inches thick served with a juicy cherry on top. Very flavorful, very good, try it. The stomach is starting to feel very satisfied.

    The water-loving bird was the precursor to a water-living dish, our fish! The Rodaballo a la Plantxa (turbot) was also excellent, but unfortunately was the last dish I actually enjoyed (dessert being the exception) due to a very full belly. Before finishing the fish, our server laid down what looked to be an incredible New York Strip.

    I'd like to go back to try this again, but I'm sad to say that my report on the beef wouldn't be accurate. My company, however, stated that it might be the best steak she's ever had, so that's a plus (reference the review on Barclay Prime, for comparison - same company). And then there was dessert.

    I said earlier that the Duck canape may have been my favorite - I lied. The flour-less chocolate cake was definitely my favorite part of the meal, and it could possibly be one of the best I've had, especially in Philly (I love chocolate, but you can be skeptical if you want).

    Overall, excellent experience. Do NOT sit next to the vestibule during the winter unless you want to freeze. I was annoyed that we were seated there, especially when there were plenty of open seats on a Sunday night. Won't take a star away for the seating arrangements, but I can't give this five stars because I feel that a perfect tasting menu would've allowed me to finish each and every course, which I wasn't able to do. Check it out!

    After last mintue reservation changes, my friend Jaclyn and I decided to settle on Tinto for restaurant week. When I arrived with her, her fiancé Mark, and their friend Christina, we were surprised to find a full house despite all the snow that had just fallen the day before. The space itself seemed tight but is much bigger than it looks with a second floor dining area and the ambiance is seemingly romantic with the low-lighting effect, but still great for groups.

    The restaurant week menu was simple, yet filled with enticing options. It was separated in 3 courses. Being a tapas menu, it allowed a choice of two plates for the first and second course, and the third course being dessert, one choice totaling 5 plates per person.

    For the first course, we all shared the soft Boucheron cheese and the semi firm Idiazabal cheese. Both of which everyone liked. The second plate of my first course was an arugula salad with Serrano ham, figs, fried goat cheese balls, pine nuts and a light orange vinaigrette. The salad was one of my favorite plates that night. It was so simple and a great plate to start off dinner. Mark and Jac also split the basque onion soup, complimenting the broth and comparing that of Parc's onion soup, Tinto's being better.

    Between the table, the second course consisted of duck montadito, merluza(sea bass) en salsa verde, organic chicken, chorizo saffron rice, and beef brochette. The brochette, cooked at medium rare, was perfect and I would go back just for that. The duck confit was great and not as salty as others I've tried. At first I found the random black cherry that accompanied the confit to be a strange choice, but a bite of the duck and cherry was a nice balance of savory and sweet. Christina loved her organic chicken and I sampled the truffled whipped potatoes that came with it. You can't go wrong with anything truffled and I'm a firm believer that any dish of the sort is also sprinkled with crack. I just couldn't get enough of it, it was that good. But I had to stop myself seeing how it was Christina's plate and all.

    To me, redemption lies in the dessert course. And Tinto redeemed itself. I had the bananas y azafrain which is basically a chocolate ganache type Cake with slices of caramelized banana and decorative squirts of saffron crema. The cake in all it's richness was on the edge of slightly bittersweet, which I love. And the crunchy texture of the caramelized bananas complimented the smoothness of the cake perfectly. I forgot about the sea bass completely. Nice save Garces.

    Tinto has been on my list to try for years. Why I haven't been sooner, I couldn't tell you. I just always assumed it was the same as it's sister restaurant, Amada. But as long as you make smart plate choices, Tinto's menu can hold it's own.

    Already a fan of JG, I had high expectations. Fellow Yelpers Erin F., Alex W., honorary Amy C. and I made a Restaurant Week reservation on a Friday. We rolled in right on time at 9:15 but had to wait -- our coats were whisked out of our arms and we were offered complimentary drinks from the bar as a consolation prize. The delectable Mairritz set the tone for the whole evening.

    The small plates are obvious perfect for sharing and thus we were able to taste a good percentage of the RW menu. The arugula salad (sans ham) was peppery and fresh and we devoured the lightly fried goat cheese. We sampled a cheese plate of four ---the only improvement would've been MORE on the plate. The olives left much to be desired.... having been spoiled by the miraculous variety and flavor of the olives at JG Domestic, I expected more than a mound of one single type-- a tiny morsel that was overwhelmingly briny.

    Moving on. WIthout really paying much attention  I ordered the moules basquaise. My favorite food, after all. Moules, that is. Anyway, I somehow glanced over the warning sign that is chorizo. I tried to eat just the mussels but the sauce was heavily chorizo and highly spiced. I didn't enjoy it, but it's my mistake. The mussels were perfectly cooked but I just prefer them in a (meatless) broth.

    The highlight of the meal was the pulpo. Just the right amount of char, with a stripe or two of intense flavor (what exactly were those stripes, Alex W.?) I would come back for this dish alone.

    The space is lovely -- a tight fit but beautiful. Service was efficient if a bit impersonal. I have every intention of returning to try out their regular menu.

    Went to Tinto while in Philly for a quick weekend trip and it was one of the best meals I or my boyfriend have had in ages. On a busy Saturday night the service was attentive and helpful without being over the top. We were seated downstairs and while the tables are close together, it feels cozy without seeming like you are sitting on top of the other tables.

    The food. They recommend 3-4 per person and this actually seems like a good estimate, though it obviously depends on the size of the dishes you choose. We started with the mixed cheese plate that contained one goat cheese, one blue cheese and one hard cheese. All were very good, and the goat, which I'm usually not a huge fan of, especially pleased me. We had the ham wrapped figs as well, which were also a tasty start to the meal as well as chorizo that had great very slightly spicy flavor. We were debating between two sandwiches and settled on the braised short ribs which was one of the best things I've ever eaten. We also had the duck canape which definitely was neck and neck with the short ribs sandwich. The sea bass and cockles were super tasty and had the perfect texture, though they were on the salty side so be warned. The organic chicken was very good with crispy skin and amazing truffle mashed potatoes on the side. We finished off the meal with the chocolate y cafe for dessert, just because everything else had been so good I couldn't leave without at least trying a dessert. It lived up to the high expectations that had been set by that point.

    All in all, I highly recommend Tinto and think it will be hard for you to go wrong in whatever you order!

Specialties

Tinto features a mouth-watering selection of "pinxtos" (pronounced PEEN-choes), the Basque equivalent of tapas.  Along with pinxtos, Tinto offers a tempting array of charcuterie, cheeses, mariscos (shellfish), brochettes and bocadillos (sandwiches).  

Some of the inspired dishes include:  Duck Confit Montadito, with wild black cherries and bleu de Basque;  Gambas Brochettes, shrimp with grape tomato and sherry espelette chili sauce;  Roasted Rack of Lamb Au Jus, with truffled artichoke puree and candied lemon;  Marmitako de Ventresca, tuna toro, parsley oil, migas and shaved mojama;  Txanguro Montadito, Dungeness crab, avocado and espelette;  and Mousse de Queso, with goat cheese, calabaza, orange, olive oil, caramel and lavender tuille.

History

Established in 2007.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
