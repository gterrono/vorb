    When a casual drive led me toward South Philly this morning, I figured I might as well make the trip to John's, since it's a rarity that I'm around this neighborhood before their 2:30 p.m. closing time. The wonderful ladies at Termini Bros. directed me to this understated spot on Snyder Ave, where I waited patiently in a short line of working class dudes before getting my hands on a cheesesteak and a roast pork sandwich.

The steak and cheese combine so well, the onions gleam in the light and burst with flavor in the mouth. The roast pork looked too good to pass up, and thank goodness I didn't because it tasted like magic. Just pork and probolone packed such a sensational burst of flavor in each bite, the juices dripping into that delicious sesame roll and cortorting my face into an uncontrollable fit of satisfied smiles.
    I don't know a better cheesesteak anywhere in the world.

    Listed in Best Roast Pork

    Its so unassuming as you pass it on the street, thousands of people wiz by it on Columbus Blvd never realizing what they are missing. "That's the place with the best roast pork?" They might say as the point with a discerning face "Why yes, yes it is."

    Easily the best roast pork and maybe the best cheesesteak anywhere. Seriously, If there is one thing i love more then cookies, it is a roast pork sandwich, and John's does it right. The meat is extremely juicy and perfectly seasoned to compliment the wonderful flavor of pig. I haven't yet met a more properly prepared sliced roast pork, its tender while at the same time having a perfect al dente texture (note: not mushy).  The meat has more of an Italian herb flavor then the rest, which is well balanced. The seeded bread is great too and sops up the prefect amount of juice dripping from the meat while still saying crispy on the outside. No need for any other condiments (did someone below say BBQ sauce? Are you friggin' crazy!?!?!), just pork, sharp provolone and greens, that's all you need. The only thing that could use improvement is the greens, they are a little soggy and lackluster, everything else, however, is perfect.

    My mouth is watering thinking about it. When it is time to move out of Philly, its not the cheesesteak that I will miss and yearn for, it is the roast pork, and John's will be my first choice. Why the tourists line up at those flashy cheesesteak places I will never know...

    Speaking of which, I have never ordered a cheesesteak here, but my wife does and it is pretty darn good. I'd put it close to the top of any cheesesteak list. I give john's 4 stars (the food itself is 5 stars) because of location and the fact that it is never open! Too many weekends I have to driven down the road and settled for Tony Luke's.

    TIP: Never ever ever ever order a small. You will be disappointed.

    Too much is made these days over the cheesesteak theater that is Gino's and Pat's, their football-esque 'historic' rivalry, the ritual ordering by those who know and the ritual shaming of those who don't.  That's manufactured drama with more appeal to those who want to "test" themselves against the code, to assert how in the know they are, and to laugh at those who aren't -- that's not about the food.

    John's is about the food.

    I saw John's Roast Pork while passing through South Philly one day; incredulous but hopeful that this unassuming locale was the place I heard had both an incredible roast pork sandwich, and one of the best, if not the best, cheese steak in Philly. I heard right.

    While both the pork and the steak have their moments of fame, the pork was imbued with so much wonderfulness: it was moist, tender, rendolent with garlic, rosemary, pepper, and perhaps, coriander -- bold flavors that moderate, and are moderated by, the rich unctuousness of pork -- all served on a sesame roll with perfect crumb and a great crust, not too firm, but just firm enough.

    My steak was with fried onions and American. Some deride John's for not having Whiz, but then again, why should they? If nostalgia, well, fine, but Pat's sold their first steak in 1930, and Whiz didn't come out for another 20 years, so Whiz gets no points for originality. Besides, the sandwich was so flavorful that it would be a shame to slobber over it with so much synthetic junk. The steak was perfect. It was the archetype of steaks.

    Lastly, but not at all least, the staff at John's were all genuinely courteous -- as if they were going out of their way to show every person who walked in their door that they were, individually, appreciated. That's hard to do; retail food is a thankless job. Guys, know that you, and your excellent food, was greatly appreciated by this guy.

    John's Roast Pork: Great food. No attitude.
