    Everything I've gotten here has been yummy although some dishes could use some modifications to make… Everything I've gotten here has been yummy although some dishes could use some modifications to make them more authentically Shanghainese tasting. I used to live in Shanghai and had the legit soup dumplings from Yu Garden and although the real stuff from Shanghai is 1.5x better (I had originally put 3x but I think I over exaggerated), Dim Sum Garden gets it pretty darn close! I dare say they are best soup dumplings on the east coast. The skin of the soup dumplings is phenomenally thin just like the ones I've gotten in Shanghai. Each dumpling is bursting with soup you'd swear they cheated and injected broth in each one before they steamed them! They also have the authentic tasting dumpling vinegar which is pretty close to what you get in Shanghai.

    I would recommend the soup dumplings without crab meat over the one with crabmeat. I've actually never seen the dried orange crabmeat powder used in the dumpling houses I've been to in Shanghai. I think it's used in lieu of real crabmeat here to cut costs. The dried crab meat ends up overpowering the flavor of the fresh pork in the dumplings and makes the dumplings a tad bid bitter. Don't believe me? Order both and try them side by side. I think the regular will beat out the crabmeat one. The crabmeat dumplings are also bigger than the regular ones for some reason, a little too big so you cannot put a whole one in your mouth at a time.

    I recommend eating the dumplings with a little vinegar. Put it in you soup spoon and just stuff the whole thing in your mouth in one bite, like sushi. Be careful to not break the dumpling skin and spill the broth on the inside! The juice can squirt everywhere.

    I've also tried their shao mai, chicken dumplings, fried chicken sticks, scallion pancakes, and the mini wonton soup. The scallion pancakes are not as thin or crispy as what I'm used to but they were still pretty good. The mini wontons themselves (not the soup) are just what i'm used to get in Shanghai street food vendors. The soup base used just tasted like chicken bouillon with some seaweed and green onions in it so that could be improved. I still liked it though because I just love soup and small wontons. Chicken dumplings were good but I can't ever remember Shanghainese putting chicken in dumplings. The fried chicken sticks were not what I expected, tasted too heavily of meat tenderizer starch so I would not get them again. Shao mai was way too bland and had no salt what so ever. It tasted like plain sticky rice and soy sauce. I would not get that again either. The fried chicken sticks and the shao mai were not bad food in their own right, just not what I was expecting in terms of flavor.

    I highly recommend the soup dumplings and wontons here! The pork filling and the thin flour shell are highly authentic and clearly made with skill. A lot of places mess up the filling or make the dumpling shell too thick. These two dishes truly bring back my nostalgia of street food in Shanghai. I only wish this restaurant carried fried stinky tofu! I really miss the fried stinky tofu in Shanghai. Overall, I'm sure whatever you get from this restaurant will make your mouth and stomach feel very happy. I have yet to get something bad from this restaurant.     Read more

    Do you love to feast on top-notch Shanghai cuisine?  Then Dim Sum Garden in the heart of Philadelphia's bustling Chinatown is your place.  Those who patronized this establishment at its old hole in the wall incarnation on 11th Street are especially going to be wowed by the décor of their new restaurant on Race Street.  It's awesome looking. Thank goodness the food is just as good.

    What to order here?  That's easy - stick with the Shanghai dishes.  Shanghai Bok Choi is simply the best.  Try it.  The duck noodles Shanghai style are also tasty.  But what about the Xiao Long Bao you're asking?  Yes the world famous Shanghai dumplings are made and served here in all their glory.  Today I had the Pork Soup and Crabmeat dumplings.  Outstanding.  But so cool was that a couple of waitresses wanted to teach me their way of eating Xiao Long Bao.  I tried it their way but I wasn't that adept at it! But I did made them laugh.  I was laughing too.  When they left, I went back to eating them the way I always do.  So the moral of this story is: however you choose to eat Shanghai dumplings at Dim Sum Garden, you're gonna love 'em!
