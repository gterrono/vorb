    4.5 stars. :O

    I've been eating nothing but Chinese food for the past week, so yesterday evening, I conjured all my magical powers to coerce my parents into giving someplace new a try. "Branch out! Broaden your life experiences!" I told them. After all, I will likely soon be moving across the country. We might as well start the process now. This popped up as a nearby location on yelp and had only one dollar sign. When we got here, there was a parking spot right in front of us. $2 for 1 hour of parking as opposed to $9 for 20 minutes of parking elsewhere? Really, we just had to come here.

    Maybe it's because I'm grown somewhat accustomed to dining on the streets of Oakland, but I didn't find it all that scary-looking from the outside. (I do find Oakland somewhat scary. :[ ) Sure, it didn't look like a welcome mat had been rolled out for you, but aside from that, it just looked like one of those awesome hole-in-the-wall places that you just have to try. :] So try it we did.

    We got the chicken kabob ($11), the lamb kabob ($12), and the chalaw kadu ($10, pumpkin stew with a yogurt sauce). I think I actually liked the chicken better than the lamb. The pumpkin stew was really good, and the rice portions were perfect, although this may also have had to do with the fact that my mom loves to give all her food away to me (and then asks why I eat so much and oh em gee, I'm gonna get fat if I keep eating like this! Oh, mixed messages, how I adore you!).

    I think the service was perfectly fine. Our server was very polite, we didn't wait long for anything, and everyone was very smiley and friendly. I only took off half a star because to me, one dollar sign on yelp should mean under $10 (I'm a cheapass), which may be the case for lunch, but definitely isn't the case for dinner. However, each of the entrees did come with a side salad as well as warm Afghan bread with a rather spicy dip that I enjoyed, but if you don't like spicy, you can always default to butter.

    I was slightly disappointed with this place, since I was recommended this by two different souces.
    Sansom Kabob house is located between 16th and Broad on Chestnut, but you have to keep your eye open to find it. One has to go below ground level to enter the restaurant, and when I did so, my first instinct was to get out. The place was hot (there was no air conditioning which is unfortunate for these hot summer days) and looked like a take-out joint with some tables laid out for people to eat. However, there are some nicer tables when one crosses the counter aisles which are definitely not visible from the entrance. When I discovered those, I checked my exit and took a seat at one of the tables. With an such a start, I thought the food better be good to justify the reviews I had of the place.

    The menu seemed very similar to that of Helmand, that celebrated Afghan joint in Baltimore, with dishes sharing names like Pallow and Chalow and Kaddu. I ordered a burani, slow cooked eggplant served in a meat sauce with yogurt, as an appetizer and a Kabuli Pulao as the main course. The burani was pretty yummy; the eggplants were very well slow-cooked (something which I have never succeeded in), and it would've been perfect for me if the sourness was a shade less. The appetizer was served along with Afghan bread and a `hot sauce'. The bread was quite dry (maybe not so fresh), but the hot sauce was quite zingy! I wish I knew what it contained. The kabuli pulao, stewed lamb chunks served with basmati rice, topped with carrots and almonds, was a bit below par. The lamb was decently cooked, but probably needed a few more minutes. There were too many raisins making the dish quite sweet. Furthermore, I had completely misunderstood the amount of rice that would be served. In hindsight, I probably would've been better off ordering the kabob platter - oh well! Service was not that great  - I was one of two people in the restaurant (which can seat maybe seven groups at best), and yet I spent most of the main course with an empty glass.

    Summary: The food is good but no where near Helmand (if someone wants really good Afghan food) . I would recommend take-outs from this place, rather than a sit-in. A slightly reserved recommendation.

    Listed in Philly's never-fail good eats.

    4.5 stars. This is totally going to become a regular joint for me.

    What do you do when it's a crisp Friday night out and Monk's tells you there's a 30min wait between you and even getting seated for a burger and ale? Wander around, apparently, and then take a chance on a place that looks like a dive and turns into something fantastic.

    To be fair, from the outside, this place looks like sketch-central. I mean, you have neon signs; it's in the basement; there's rubber treading on the stairs down. There's tile flooring and blue tile all over the front of the counter. There are those horrible formica tables that make you think "4am and this is the only pizza joint that's open; will I fight with the passed-out hobo for a seat?"

    But no, my friends, take heart. As soon as you make your tentative descent you get hit with a whiff of smoke that would make any carnivore salivate: meat. MEAT. Grilling meat.

    You are led back into a dining room that's up the stairs from the shady formica-pizzajoint-Hell and suddenly you're in a kitschy Bohemian fantasy of orange gloss painted walls, rainbow Christmas lights, a STEAMING FOUNTAIN with blue water (oh yes, the 80s are strong with this one) and some actual Afghani-themed decor on the walls. When you encounter these things, take heart: they do not mean you harm, they mean simply to inform you of an alternative aesthetic. A non-native aesthetic. An "oh man, I'm so totally about to get amazing ethnic food" aesthetic.

    The menu is large enough to be exciting while concise enough to not be overwhelming--a rare balance, now that I think about it. We opted for ashak and bulani to start (along with one green and one black Afghani tea), lamb kabob salad and qablee palaw for the mains, and then firni and sheeryakh to finish on a sweet note.

    The place clearly does a lot of take-out business, and the dining room was about half-full; with a staff of three, I would say the wait times were reasonable-to-slow. If we'd had some sort of night out afterwards planned, we might have been irked, but with the kitchen in plain view of our table, it was obvious to see they were doing their best with a small staff. The service we got was courteous, friendly, inviting, and non-intrusive. Lovely.

    As for the food: oh yes. We went through the starters and mains as if we hadn't eaten all day. Everything was spiced just right, and the lamb in my kebab was especially memorable: seared to just a hint of crisp char on the outside, perfectly tender on the inside, and packed with flavor. The palaw was comforting and not overly rich, with a lovely punch of sweetness from the stewed carrots and currants. Our tea was refilled, and all in all we reveled in that rare glow of delicious food at reasonable prices (no, seriously: two apps, two mains, two drinks, two desserts, $44).

    Dessert was the only letdown. We totally saw the waiter duck upstairs (there's a Japanese restaurant above; we have a theory they share freezer space) and come back with scoops of vanilla ice cream. It was then adorned to make it "Afghan" with some rose water and ground pistachios. Totally tasty, mind you, but I guess I was expecting something closer to Indian kulfi. The sheeryakh was ok, but I would have wanted to see a little more almond on top for some texture. None of that takes away from the yumminess of the savory items, though.

    Bottom line for potential diners: go. Go right now. They're yummy, they're cheap, they're kitschy enough to give you something to talk about if you're having an awkward lapse in conversation, and they're a sweet, family-owned joint.

    Bottom line in case the restaurant happens to read this: invest in some curb appeal. Seriously. Your entrance looks like a bad experience about to happen. And maybe try to hire an extra waiter or cook for busy nights.

    Bonus: despite the drab appearance, closer inspection reveals this place to be SPOTLESS. You can even see down the stairs into the basement from a certain table angle, and even that part's tidy and clean. The kitchen area is well-tended and there was no dirt on the floor. Awesome.

    Special considerations: stairs down and then back up, and no ramp or wheelchair; might also be difficult to navigate if you're not in a wheelchair but have knee/hip/etc problems. While their menu has some veg dishes, I doubt it would be a good fit for vegans due to butter and yogurt abounding (and seriously, Middle Eastern food without yogurt is just.. a sad sad thing).

    My hubby and I met in Afghanistan, so we thought we'd check this place out.  It looked like a small takeout-only kabob house at first glance, until our waiter took us to the rear dining room when we told him we were eating in.  We liked the dining room for what it was...a colorful and cozy space decorated with Afghan knickknacks (like the ones we brought home from our time there).  It started to get a little smoky when the grill was fired up to cook our kabobs, but we didn't mind.  Contrary to another review, I did notice air conditioning and there was a fan in the dining room.  I thought the bathrooms were clean and in good condition (at least the women's was...didn't look inside the men's, but my husband didn't complain).

    We ordered the kaddo appetizer (cubed pumpkin sauteed in a spicy/sweet sauce) and a couple of kabob dinners.  The kaddo was tasty, and we mopped up all of the remaining sauce with our naan (bread).  Our dinners came with nicely chopped salads with yogurt dressing, which were also good and quickly devoured.  Fresh off the grill, our kabobs (lamb, chicken, beef, and kofta) were hot, juicy, and tender, and came with nice chunky sliced & grilled onions, bell peppers, and tomatoes...two large skewers worth in total.  Taking up the rest of the platters was sauteed long grain rice drizzled with what tasted like a pureed squash sauce.  We left nothing on our plates at the end of our meal!  Good food, good service, and good overall experience.
