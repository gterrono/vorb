    I had really high hopes for Alla Spina, the Italian Birreria owned by Marc Vetri (yes, of Vetri).  Consistent press and a slew of good reviews made me want to come try this place.  So last night on a whim we decided to hike up here for dinner on the later side of a Saturday night.  Overall, I was thoroughly disappointed with nearly every factor of the experience.

    It started when we arrived and the hostess couldn't find our reservation in the system.  After awkwardly asking us a few times who we were and if we were sure we had a reservation, it turned out they had sat a party ahead of us under our name by mistake (but luckily, we didnt have to wait for a table for that long).  As we sat down, the BF and I struggled with the menu: nothing really jumped out at us, but after some time, we settled on five small dishes to split (and an alternate since they "just ran out of the Tuscan Kale").  

    We started with the pretzels and spicy beer cheese, the cucumber salad, and the tuna crudo (a special).  The pretzels were lukewarm and the spicy beer cheese was cold (also, not spicy).  The cucumber salad was fine, but very very simple: thick julienne slices of cucumber with very thinly sliced peppers in a vinaigrette with a hint of mint.  Even for $8, it seemed a bit overpriced.  The tuna crudo special was ok: tuna was good, but it was heavily dressed, and what I thought were pieces of bacon garnishing the dish were, to my dismay, mini terra chips.  A huge disappointment.

    Luckily, the next two plates fared much better.  The pork belly with beans was great: a very tender and juicy slab of pork belly with terrific flavor and cooked perfectly.  The fat and the meat were practically melting in my mouth.  The beans, however, were a bit tough and very salty.  The other dish, a poutine made with guinea hen bolognese and mozzarella curd, was also terrific.  The first few forkfuls were just like a poutine should be: meaty, cheesy, messy, and that wonderful combination of crispy french fries with a beefy gravy.  However, i was disappointed that the meat and cheese gooeyness didn't really get to make its way into the bottom of the dish, which in my opinion, is often the best part of a poutine.  I could easily use my hands to scoop the clean, gravy-less fries from the bottom of the porcelain skillet: in Quebec, this action would require a fork (or a set of hands not afraid to get messy).

    Because we were seated adjacent to a partition separating the dining area from the bar, our waiter would often talk to us from behind the partition, an experience that both myself and the BF found awkward.  I was also disappointed with the house beer, Victory Via Wiesse.  It tasted very light and watery, like an overpriced version of PBR (but if that's how its supposed to taste, then my apologies).  Finally, dinner for both of us was just north of $60, which seemed a lot for what we got.  Overall, I was expecting much more but I felt that either my expectations were set too high or we just ordered the wrong things.  I would definitely try other items if I was to return, but based on our experience here, a return trip seems highly unlikely.

    I want to love Alla Spina. Some of the reviewers I've read here are absolutely correct. Probably, if you navigate the menu appropriately, you won't be burping pink peppercorns like I am the morning after my meal. In case you are wondering, burping pink peppercorns is a horrible taste and reminder of the meal I did have, but there were some highlights, as well as other faults.

    The venue: Beautiful, I do have to say that Vetri did an excellent job with the design of the restaurant. I would love to come back and see those bay windows open to the street in the summer.

    The ambience: So on a Thursday night, when there are not a lot of people around, at 7pm, you should not be blaring music so that your customers can't talk to the person next to them, especially when people are paying the price they are to be at this restaurant. Instead of treating it like a club, Vetri should be focusing on making Alla Spina a relaxed bistro version of Osteria where the focus is on beer and great beer food. Instead, the plan is to make this place a night club even before the late hours come and even when there aren't many clients sitting down, not to mention the fact that I didn't see any place for people to be dancing.

    The food: High and definite lows. Obviously the kale with pink peppercorns is one of the worst dishes I have ever eaten. I'm not sure who dreamt up the idea of undercooked biter kale mixed with bitter pink peppercorn, but suffice to say, it is an idea that should have come and then gone just as quickly. The house made pickles, while very inspiring, were just vinegary vegetables, although if you are hungry enough after some beers, you'll gobble them. The carpaccio with mushrooms was unbelievably good. Definitely a winner. As well, we had the fried chicken and the pig pot pie. Both are definitely awesome. There's nothing else to say about them. I would go back for both, and I think the pricing is reasonable.

    The beer: It must have been a lack luster night, but for a bar claiming to have a deep italian beer list, there's room for much improvement. Nearly every Italian beer on the bottle menu can be described the same. Medium bodied, medium maltiness, and finishing a bit tart with some hints of fruit. Not bad, if that's the only style of beer you like. And I like almost every style of beer, but they need to get some inspiration. Go talk to the people at Bella Vista. They will inspire up your list a bit. And if you are gonna be serving some rich food like you are, you absolutely need to get some richer bottles of barrel aged, deep malty beers. Not these medium bodied 12% tart characters all over the place. There is so much out there, that I'm just taken aback by how lackluster their list was, albeit, beautifully put together and presented. And, the bar tenders are terrific; But, give them something to work with. I will say that the best thing I drank had to come walked across the street from the Osteria cellar. Not a good sign considering this is supposed to be the beer place. Oh well.

    Overall: I will be back, but have to narrow my menu focus and beer expectations the next visit, and pray the volume has come down.

    Amis is Vetri's trattoria serving a bevy of small plates, Osteria is his rustic Italian restaurant, and Vetri is his masterpiece of fine dining Italian. Alla Spina acts as his venture into gastropubs, something that has been inundating NYC, but is really just starting in Philly (Varga and Khyber Pass come to mind). Alla Spina is on a whole 'nother level though.

    The whole vibe screams cool, relaxed, and unpretentious. Check out the detailed graffiti on the walls. Heck, check out the ceilings!

    I don't know who they got to do the art, but the cool urban-chic vibe made me feel like I was not in the northern part of Broad Street. You know what I'm talking bout - that area between Temple University and Hahnemann Hospital, a barren stretch of Philadelphia that is pretty much the pits in terms of quality of living.

    But enough social topics - let's get on with the food!

    I was hoping to try their pork belly dish, which is typically offered at dinner service. Unfortunately, we came for brunch. I did ask if the kitchen could accommodate us but alas, it could not. Just another reason to return for dinner I suppose. To be honest though, the pig's head poutine more than made up for this ...

    Testina Hash Poutine ($14)
    Pig head, french fries, mozzarella curd and sunny side up eggs

    Vetri's iteration takes it even further - topped with tender meat from the pig's head, fried eggs, and a hot sauce hollandaise, you know it ain't time to remember your New Year's resolutions. Now, I can imagine a few of you might be leery of the term "pig head," but don't be alarmed! We're talking about some of the tenderest parts of the pig. In fact, the luscious cheek is usually the best part of any animal be it fish, cow, and yes, pig.

    So break open the fried egg and let the yolk mix with the hot sauce hollandaise, helping to drench the crispy fries and mozzarella curds. In addition to being tender, the pig head had crispy bits that helped to make the dish over the top. Fair warning though - be prepared to guzzle down a few glasses of water. This dish is over the top - salty, unctuous, and coma-inducing.

    Alla Spina Gnocchi ($16)
    Potato gnocchi, porcini mushrooms and taleggio

    Vetri is without a doubt - a prince of pastas. Since he's been effusively praised for his craft at his other restaurants, you can kind of understand why he might limit the number of pastas at Alla Spina, especially considering it's a gastropub. But when you see a lone gnocchi on the menu, you can't help but gravitate to it.

    These potato gnocchi are pillowy and decadent, draped in a luxurious taleggio cream sauce. The meaty porcini mushrooms help to prevent this dish from being one note. Still, having this on top of a pig head poutine made for one heavy brunch!

    Cheesy Parmigiano Polenta ($4)

    Of course, adding a cheesy polenta didn't help matters! But that's just how I roll. Perfectly salted from the parmigiano reggiano, the polenta was creamy and smooth, while still maintaining the natural grainy texture of the polenta. Luxurious and over the top, especially when coupled with the previous two dishes.

    Overall, Alla Spina hit the mark for me. In typical Vetri fashion, the dishes are elevated, yet comforting. The dishes I ordered were perfect for sopping up a hangover, but not for maintaining your figure. I'm already looking forward to our next visit to have dinner and drinks. And I'd be remiss if I didn't mention that 5% of all profits from Vetri's restaurants goes to his foundation for children. Coming from a pubic health background, his efforts to secure healthy eating and living for children is admirable. So here's to his ever-expanding empire, and even more so to his philanthropy.
