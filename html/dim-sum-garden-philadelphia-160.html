    The move to Race street has made Dim Sum garden a better restaurant.  The generous open kitchen, a rarity in Chinatown, lets you see the busy kitchen staff working hard to turn out freshly made dishes served by a gregarious and efficient wait staff.  Went there  last Sunday at a busy time and even though there was a small line snaking up the ramp where the hostess is waiting to assign tables, we were seating quickly and - amazing given how full the restaurant was - received prompt and smiling service.

    The dishes appeared in quick order, the various dumpling dishes all very good (much has been written about the Xiaolongbao dumplings and they remain a stalwart of this restaurant).  The menu is quite large, so order some of the more unusual dishes, like fresh fava beans with bean curd skins.  My only complaint is that the noodles are a tad too soft for my taste, they lacked the springy, chewy quality I so love in Asian noodles (for that, go next door and eat some Nan Zhou Hand Drawn noodles).

    All in all, the quality of the food, the comfort and service of the restaurant, and the prices make for a very successful experience.  Now if only a real dim sum place that is equally good would open in Philadelphia.

    These are not the xiao long baos I can drift into sleep dreaming of at night. I've only been to the other location once, and remembered it as filling, inexpensive and unremarkable. More recently, we came to this place with a purpose, which, sadly for me, remains unfulfilled. I guess there was talk of soup dumplings and in my head I went somewhere else.

    What we got was not right. The skin was too thick. When I pierced one by accident, it spewed all over my sweater (which I subsequently had to dry clean), my bag and the table & wall, my phone narrowly escaping the projectile hemorrhage. I'm used to a delicate, nearly translucent skin that, when pierced, allowed molten hot broth to gracefully flow out into the soup spoon that are designed just for such a joyous occasion. I should've know when I saw the bao standing up and retaining shape when they came to the table. The skin should not be so thick as to be able to hold itself up like mini igloos, that's for a different kind of bao!

    We also got potato/meat pies. I didn't have any. They looked really unappetizing, each with their own little wading pool of grease sitting on top, like a dollop of cream, but worse.

    I got a five spice beef noodle soup. The beef was tender, and the traditional cut used in the Taiwanese iteration of the dish. For the broth and noodles, I would have rather gone next door  to Nan Zhou.

    That brings me to the seaweed salad. We are at a CHINESE/SHANGHAINESE establishment, are we not?! Why did you give me Japanese/Korean seaweed salad? What is happening! I was expecting long 1/8 inch wide dark green strands of seaweed tossed in chili & sesame oil with some salt! Instead I got the ombre/multi-techno-color sweet and sour tossed with mirin and white sesame seed nonsense that you get at Sammy Chon's or a sushi place or even the deli salad bar when the owner is Korean. Ugh. I mean I like those. I just wasn't in the mood for them. What I was in the mood for, I could have had next door, without this emotionally draining mix-up.

    I might be back for things like their turnip filled pastry, or the pan fried soup dumplings (since they seem to really have the thick wall of dough thing down pat), though I have my doubts about their level of execution. In the meantime I'll cry myself to sleep holding a blown up picture of Din Tai Fung's proper soup dumplings.

    I absolutely loved dining here for lunch with two friends earlier this week. It was my first time trying dim sum, and I'm so glad we chose this place to go to!

    My favorite thing about the place is the number of vegetarian options on the menu. I was worried about finding a place that had a variety, but we had no trouble ordering ample dishes to share.

    We opted for the following: scallion pancakes, chive and egg buns, steamed vegetable dumplings, red bean cakes, pumpkin cakes, and bean curd and vegetables.

    My absolute favorite were the chive and egg buns. The texture of the bun was to die for (I'm still craving more) and the filling was flavorful. This, mixed with the chili garlic sauce at each table, was perfect!

    The scallion pancakes were flaky and crispy simultaneously. It was a bit on the oily and greasy side, but I was expecting that with the order.

    I wasn't a huge fan of the pumpkin cakes or the red bean cakes, just because I didn't like how artificially sweet and inauthentic the center fillings tasted. The outside flaky dough was tasty, and I ended up just scraping out the fillings and eating the outside "shell" instead.

    The bean curd and veggie dish was standard but still delicious. I loved the texture of the fried tofu, and the light white sauce kept the flavors interesting in the dish.

    The veggie dumplings were my second favorite. They were fat and stuffed with tons of veggies, and again the texture and consistency of the dough was perfect and chewy.

    I would come back to this restaurant and JUST order several buns and dumplings; that's how good they were. As a warning, this place is CASH ONLY and I wish I had known that beforehand but nonetheless, definitely will want to come back!

    Finally made it to Dim Sum Garden.

    Located mid-street on Race, it is a deep and narrow space with a step up half way back at the kitchen and mini-bar area.  One wall is slate, and the other smooth with the image of a huge dumpling adorning the wall.  That same design is emblazoned on the black T's worn by the wait staff.  I wouldn't mind wearing one of those.  

    Entering solo around 7:30, the tables were buzzing, but not SRO.  I was pointed to a deuce in the front window, and quickly choose to look inward rather than out toward the street.  If I was in a metaphorical mood that might mean something.  It seems natural to want to enjoy the floorshow.  The menu arrived promptly with a glass of water (cold, not hot), and a few minutes later the server returned to direct me through the offerings.  I knew I wanted the Xiao Long Bao (#1) because that what was most people raved about, but I also wanted a second dish - selecting Eggplant with Garlic Sauce as counterpoint.    

    Part of the schtick here is the servers offer to show you how to eat XLB.  For the uninitiated there is a learning curve and the risk of spraying scalding broth onto your neighbors, so there is some reason for caution.  Mild dexterity in the use of chopsticks and flat bottom spoon in tandem is required, but once mastered the manipulation becomes part of the fun.  Even life long experts can be seen chasing escaped dumplings across the table, so there is no fear of lost face.  

    I was surprised that the XLB were not as good as I expected.  Their dough was thicker than I have had in the famous Shanghai dumpling shops.  True, the pork filling was substantial and decent, and the steaming soup within pretty good, but they were disappointing nonetheless. Even the "Dumpling Sauce" on the tables was not quite right to my palate.  It used a vinegar that too strongly neutralized the flavor of the broth.  There was no ginger to mix with the vinegar. I had to mix the hot chili oil with the sauce on the plate to get to the heat I wanted to supplement the taste.  The second dish was good Eggplant with Garlic Sauce.  I asked for it "hot," and upon arrival it glistened from the oil it cooked in.  I liked it, but added still more chili to bring up the burn.  

    I will certainly return to Dim Sum Garden as there is much more coming out of the kitchen that looks interesting, and because so many of my friends love the place.  It calls itself, "Philly's Premier Authentic Shanghai Soup Dumpling Restaurant."  Who am I to question a marketing slogan, but if you say it, you should be able to back it up.  

    I don't think it did.

They don't have the traditional shui mai or shrimp gai, but that's ok, this is just a different type of dim sum. It's not the dim sum cart type of places; it's more like a sit-down restaurant. When you pass by the kitchen, you'll see the chefs making the dumplings right then and there, so sometimes the food may come a bit slower than typical dim sum places, but it's worth the wait.

    Don't forget to use the dumpling sauce on the table, it's so good they should bottle it! They don't even bother to put soy sauce on the table, which isn't needed anyway.

    If you are new to dim sum, just eat the food with sipping tea. It washes down the grease.

    The decor is nice and there is always a server around to get you what you need.

    It can be packed on weekend mornings, so get there as soon as they are open at 10:30a. Oh, our 7 dishes cost less than $40!

    I can't wait to go back again!
