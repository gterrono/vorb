    I'm surprised it took me so long to make it to Zahav but I FINALLY MADE IT on Friday for Restaurant Week. My she-date (aka, Stephen S.'s Yelpless fiance) who usually eats meat accompanied me on a vegetarian journey through their RW menu.

    We started off with the two must-trys: the salatim and hummus with laffa. The salatim was impressive - although the eight portions of various salads are small, it was a pretty filling appetizer. I sampled everything and enjoyed the wide array of flavors and textures. The dates were the bomb and the tabbouleh was some of the best. Why is it when I try to cook with parsley it tastes like ass but they make a light, seemingly simple tabbouleh and it tastes like a totally different leaf? WTFFF my life. But this is probably because I do not cook at Zahav. Anyways, it was good. The laffa was served steaming hot and the hummous was tasty but did not revolutionize the way I see hummous. Next time, I'll go for the highly-recommended Turkish hummous blend.

    In short, here's what we wound up with for the remainder of our meal: haloumi, fried cauliflower, artichoke halves, smoked salmon, pumpkin, eggplant, almond semifreddo and poached pineapple. All were delicious, the fried cauliflower being the obvious stand out among the smallest plates. The eggplant tasted unlike any eggplant either of us had ever had. It was rich and buttery, which wwas balanced nicely by the barley beneath. The almond semifreddo, topped with a crispy, snappy disc of chocolate was my dessert of choice.

    By the time dessert arrived, I was relieved as I filled up QUICK. I'd love to come back and try their a la carte menu as soon as possible but I still think I got a fairly complete scope of what they have to offer via their RW menu. The service was basic and a little hurried, as it usually is during RW, but the attention paid by those attending to our table other than our hustling server was great. The general ambiance and atmosphere of the restaurant is busy and bustling without being blinding and loud. I love it here, can't wait to return.

    Two girlfriends and I went to Zahav, which is awesome because you feel cool just by being there. It feels like a place that cool people would hang out. We ordered the hummus and laffa, which found the rare balance between tahini, chickpeas, and olive oil; not greasy, not too creamy, not gritty. The theme of the night--everything was "not" too anything, it was all perfectly right.

    The laffa was lightly peppered and hot out of the laffa-oven, and very pleasant to eat. Then Katie and Steph chose the chocolate semifreddo, which is a thick layer of mousse sitting on a crust, with candied kumquats spooned onto it. It resembles Cool Whip pie, and tastes like a Kinder bar with kumquats. The kumquats were the star- the citrus was so crisp it was like having a mouth full of Florida. Citrus and Kinder bars--of course! Of course!

    I chose the Romanian; it seemed appropriate to replace the lamb shoulder with another meat dish. The Romanian is three chunks of hanger steak and three chunks of eggplant, with a big schmear of roasted red pepper chutney. The steak was so juicy that none of us wanted to chew it because we didn't want it to end, and the eggplant was very--eggplanty. I wish I could explain how it all tasted, but I don't know the words--juicy?--flavorful, I guess, and the most eggplant-y an eggplant can be, and the most steak-y a steak can be. How does it work? I don't know. Some of us use Excel and planners to achieve our potential, but apparently all an eggplant has to do is end its short vegetarian life at Zahav.

    Warning: this is a long review, but I like being thorough. Zahav sounded like a good compromise for all of us: we all like Israeli food, since my parents raised us kids on it, and it's vegetarian friendly. The restaurant was emptier than I expected for a Sunday evening, but I'd rather have an empty restaurant with good service than worrying about the noise level and possible hold-ups if the place had been packed. It started to fill up a little more around 8 when we left.

    As with every meal here, we started with the hummus, laffa, and salads. The hummus was good, but not the best I've had, and was a little oilier than I prefer. Having fresh laffa from the oven was nice but watching my mom burn her hand on it was not. The salads were mostly great, though my dad expected a more traditional tabouleh than Zahav's parsley and pomegranate version. The butternut squash dip was excellent, though the spicier salads were too hot to be truly enjoyable. I also would have liked to have had more laffa; we ran out long before we finished the salad platter and we didn't get any more until after the dish had been cleared.

    And then we started drinking. I got the British Mandate, which is a citrus-tea-gin concoction (my three main food groups - law school is tough, kids) that tastes even better than it sounds. My mom got a vodka and grapefruit drink and my dad got some sort of peppered whiskey cocktail. And then came the wine flights. While the wine was good for the most part, I would have liked if it had been served with the food that it would have complimented. The last wine, which was a blended red, was extremely bitter because I drank it with dessert.

    For the mezze course, I got the haloumi, which mixed really well with the apple-onion puree but not the spicy salad that came with it, and the fried cauliflower, which was absolutely perfect. My brother didn't like what he ordered (artichokes and oxtail soup) so that jerk ate a bunch of my precious haloumi. My mom didn't like the artichokes either, but she and I liked the smoked salmon dish she ordered. My dad and my brother had a little hater's ball over it because they had been expecting Jewish smoked salmon rather than this weird but tasty little cake of salmon chunks and salmon paste. My dad got the potato pancake and sweetbreads; he liked them a lot with the exception of the chopped liver that came with the potato pancake, which he found too smoky.

    I preferred the mezze to the grilled courses. I got the butter-poached pumpkin, which was delicious but a little greasy, and the barley pilaf that came with it had too much dill. Everyone else seemed to like their dishes more than I did. (My dad got the branzino, my mom got the beef cheeks, and my brother got the eggplant.) Dessert was also underwhelming: my dad said his semifreddo tasted like walnut foam, my brother was confused by the steamed milk drink that came with his rugelach, and my mom and I expected the poached pineapple to be warm. The pineapple itself was too sweet for my tastes, was pretty difficult to cut, and did not mix well with the half-melted sorbet and the strangely salty pistachio cookie.

    My review might sound harsh, but I really did enjoy myself at Zahav. The food was inconsistent, but the high points (i.e., the first half of the meal) were extremely high. It seems like the key to a great dinner here is knowing exactly what to order - next time, I'll order more mezze and less dessert. I did end up drinking a lot of water since a lot of the dishes were salty, but the waitstaff was impeccable and kept our glasses filled and our table clear. It's a little pricey for me as a perpetually broke law student, but I'd definitely come back for a dressy date, drinks, or another night out with the family.

    3.75 stars  0.25 for the dessert alone.

    I have been to Zahav a few times, the first two times were awesome.  I had been wanting to go back for a while, and was excited for it as I love this kind of food.  Our experience seemed to be lacking something and left us wanting to be more satisfied.

    The hummus and salatim plate is amazing, and by far some of the best hummus I've ever had.   I would have just ordered another one of these and called it a night.

    Service was okay- however our server was up in our personal area just a little too much.  The manager, who was well under dressed, hung around the bar the entire time we were there- not that we needed him I just seemed to note that he seemed uninvolved with the floor.

    We ordered smoked chicken liver and potato latke, which was a let down.  The smoking totally overpowered the already strong flavor of the chicken liver and rendered it nearly inedible- the latke was also disappointing.

    The beef cheeks were awesome, buttery and very well done and floated in a broth like sauce.

    The duck kebab sort of reminded me of a ground duck burger.  It was okay.  I would never order it again and was not really what I was expecting but it wasn't offensive.

    We ordered halloumi, which was cubed and appeared to be deep fried.  Fried cheese can never bad, and this wasn't either; I am used to halloumi sliced and grilled or pan fried.  This version was interesting, I would order it again, however I think something was lost about the milkiness of this cheese when it was cubed up like that.

    The rest of the menu is where you'll have to make some choices. You will get to select two mezze and one entree.
