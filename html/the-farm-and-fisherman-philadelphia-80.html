    The menu changes daily in order to provide the freshest pick of the day. There are four courses and four choices under each course. There were four of us and we decided to get one of each and share everything. This was really the best way to experience our meal since every single dish was exceptionally intricate and delicious. It would have been a shame not to experience it all.

    It started off with a bang. Beau soleil oysters served on a bed of salt rocks topped with hackleback caviar, cinnamon and a tint of blood orange. Small purple herbs were sprinkled throughout the dish making it not only great to taste but also great to look at. The oysters were so fresh I swear they may have been fished out of the ocean that same day.  Next came the cauliflower and mushroom salad.  A mix of greens, hen of the woods and trumpet mushrooms, almonds, cauliflower and goat cheese were elegantly presented.

    The mushrooms tasted earthy. Each bite was a blend of all the above ingredients which when mixed together resulted in one of the most amazing salad creations I have ever tasted.

    At this point we were already oohing and aahing over the food. But what came next left us all speechless. Have you ever had a vegetable steak? And I don't mean that frozen vegan stuff you can by at wholefoods. I mean a steak that tastes so good that you forget there is no meat and in fact wish meat could taste like it. If not then I highly recommend the bloody beet steak that chef Lawler has created. Not only was the presentation of the beet colorful and beautiful but the taste was amazing. Who knew beet could be so good.

    On to the second course. First came the grilled Spanish Mackerel served over granny smith apples with a mix of coriander, mustard oil and radish. The mix of the mackerel with the apple was again new to my taste buds and I absolutely loved it.

    The next dish was one of the top winners of the night (although they were all winners in my mind). Papperdelle with Wyebrook lamb shoulder ragu served with the most delicious tomato sauce, Parmesan and fennel seeds. A dried egg yolk was brought over and shaved on top to give it that extra touch. This is a dish that I always order when ever offered on a menu and so can say I have a basis for comparison. I'll keep it short and simple- it was the best. Another second course on the menu were the Barnegat sea scallops. It was served over smoked barley, creamed kale, sweet potato and maple syrup. I loved ever single ingredient in this dish from the smoked barley to the creamed kale. The importance is in the details and Chef Lawler has really got that down. The next dish was brought out by the famous chef himself. This was one of the most amazing mix of flavors and textures I've yet to taste in my 30 years. What was this mystery dish? The poached Wyebrook farm egg served over cracked rice, green wheat, farro piccolo, quinoa and squash. The texture of crispy, creamy and crunchy flavors mixed with the poached egg yolk was out of this world. I highly recommend you to order this dish if it ever finds its way on to the daily menu.

    The chicken was baked in hay and served on top of farmer's cheese spaetzle, bacon and mustard greens. A simple and hearty dish which hit all the right spots. For dessert we had the layered chocolate ganache and the warm apple pie. Layers of chocolate divided by a thin wafer topped with whipping cream and powdered sugar. Need I say more?

    The warm apple pie was so fresh, so warm and so perfect.

    Perfect seems to sum up my experience at the Farm and Fisherman. A perfect and magical dining experience.

    So this might just be a restaurant that was done in by outside forces.  But, it's hard to separate all the hype around this restaurant.

    A few nights after we decided to visit Farm and Fisherman with some friends, this place was mentioned in the NYT.  Immediately it was impossible to book a table and  everyone was talking about how amazing this place was.  In my mind this is the worst for a restaurant.  It's almost impossible to live up to this kind of hype.

    The atmosphere is your typical small BYO you'll find all over Philly.  A few tables, without a ton of space and subtle decorations.

    The menu is varied enough, but maybe it's just tough to get really fresh interesting stuff in the winter.  I wasn't particularly excited about anything on the menu.  Everything we got ended up being well made, but nothing really blew me away.  I was going to list a highlight, but honestly it was a pretty average level.  Do I just not like this whole "farm to table" movement?  Maybe I should stay away from these places in the Winter and wait for when better fruits and vegetables are available fresh.

    The thing that was both a win and a failure was the service.  It was great that our water was never low.  It was nice to get fresh silverware between plates and that all our dishes were timed perfectly.  But, our waitress was just too "familiar."  I really hate using that phrase, but it's tough to describe it any other way.  She seemed way too pleased about the menu, holding an almost smug tone when she described things.  She liked to make witty remarks or question things we did, but personally my girlfriend and I didn't appreciate them.  Am I getting old?  Or maybe I just expect a more businesslike interaction.  Would you act like that on an interview for a new job?

    Overall, the place is perfectly fine.  For a BYO that's on the higher priced end I would just have expected more.  It's not my fault if my expectations were too high given all the recent hype.

    Besides feeling a bit perturbed by the name of the restaurant, which does not quite make sense in terms of grammar and content, I must say that I like this place.  Is it worth the hype of the NY Times?  I don't know, perhaps not on the night I went.  

    The menu changes daily based on ingredients, so it's probably not worth commenting on specific things I had as they are not even on today's menu.  However, I will say that I appreciate the thought that went into creating rich and balanced flavors.  My only complaint is that my sea bass was rubbery in texture, and it was the first time I've ever had rubbery bass.  Based on some of the other glowing reviews on this site, I surmise that this place can be either hit or miss depending on the day, the ingredients, and the menu.  

    The disadvantage of having a menu that changes daily is that there isn't a signature item that will keep you coming back; it's like playing dinner roulette in terms of what you'll get on the night when you have your reservation ... good for when you're feeling adventurous (but $40-60 per person gamble?), but it's hard to be convinced to come back immediately

    I do appreciate that reservations are taken and honored (didn't have to wait when my dinner companion and I showed up).  Like other Philly BYOBs, there isn't much elbow room, and decor is minimal but tasteful.  

    Would I come here again?  Yes, but only after I have explored the other BYOB options in the area.
