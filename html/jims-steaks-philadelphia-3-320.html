    From steak tartare to bulgogi to your good old-fashioned burger, my culinary experience with our bovine friends has been pretty all-encompassing. With one gap: cheesesteak. How have I never had a cheesesteak?!

    Sought to remedy this problem during my half-day in Philadelphia this summer. Based on yelp reviews, I decided to forgo the whole Pat's vs. Geno's debacle in favor of Jim's.

    Definitely wasn't disappointed, although I really have no reference point. Got one with mushrooms and provolone. Juicy steak, gooey, melted cheese, succulent mushrooms. The hoagie itself is great for soaking up all the juices. Perfect last meal in Philly. Got another one to-go to take to the train station. Still good two hours later.

    Note: if you take the Big Bus hop-on, hop-off tour of Philadelphia, get off at stop 17 (at 2nd & Pine), walk two blocks down to South St, then another two to 4th.

    I've been here a couple of times and I wasn't expecting much, just stumbled upon this place the first time around, and I liked it!  It's a little greasier than Pat's or Geno's but there is a ton of flavor so I like it!  They use typical cheesesteak bread (not very substantive) and I got mine with sauteed onions and wiz.  Yummy!  I usually need to add hot sauce to make up for the lack of flavor in typical cheesesteak sandwiches but this one definitely is fine on its own.  The meat wasn't dry and overall, it was greasy but not in a terrible or overpowering way.  

    The second time I went, I didn't want to wait on the line around the block (why are there so many people wanting cheesesteaks at all times of the day??) so I called ahead and when I arrived, I got to go to the front of the line, pay for my steaks and jump right out.  The sandwiches held nicely and the flavor was all still there.  There isn't anything unusual, unique or special about this place--they just make a solid cheesesteak.  It's not the nicest of places to dine in but it's what you'd expect out of a cheesesteak joint.

    When I got to Jim's the line was just a few people out the door, so it was not too long of a wait. However, the longer I was watching the cook through the glass window display, the more and more I needed to look away to not be grossed out. I like to think the purpose of exposing the cooking process of a place is to show how things are prepared, how the ingredients are fresh, what have you, yadda yadda. Not in this case...it just made it so apparent how dirty and grimy this place was...

    OK...I know it's Philly. I know it's a cheesesteak joint, I don't expect Ruth Chris or anything so calm down! I love me some dirty mom'n'pop joints...to me, the dirtier the better the food will taste. So I was trying to let my appetite develop naturally, but I started to have a not so good feeling about Jim's...

    When I get to order, I got mushrooms, sauteed onions, provolone cheese and peppers. The mushrooms were scooped from the tin they were purchased in from Costco's or whatever and slopped onto my sandwich along with the juices that were at the bottom of the tin. (Canned mushroom juice...mmm). It was not cooked with the meat or anything - it was from a freaking tin can. This surely did add flavor to my soggy sandwich...not.

    So the service was not pleasant. That's fine, whatever, I get it, you're a mom and pop one stop shop, you're famous, so many people love you, you're entitled to be curt and rude. Most places' menu will make up for this, but it wasn't enough for me.

The sweet peppers were good but THAT you buy in a tin can as well with flavor on its own.

    I like to think that Jim's serves Philly Cheezsteaks, since their secret ingredient to me is the cheez-whiz. The line when we went was a pretty decent size and the fans blowing the smell of flat grilled steak magnified the desire to have a cheezsteak right away. The windows as you get closer allow you to see the preparation of the meat and see the splattered goodness of meat past. The grill master definitely has it down to an art. Opening up the roll - slathering the whiz - organizing a portion of beef - flipping the roll over onto the grill to capture said portion of beef - then topping it off with the toppings of your choosing. After my experience I would recommend just going with the grilled onions as the sweet/hot peppers were actually one of the disappointing aspects of Jim's. Overall the sandwich was pretty standard. It was nice to have the meat chopped but I wish they had put a little more whiz on the sandwich.

    Would I go back? Not necessarily. I have certainly enjoyed better cheesesteaks, but if you have the craving Jim's will satisfy it.

    What is there to say about Philly Cheesesteaks that hasn't already been said? Their simplicity and deliciousness has been reported on time and time again. Yet, I find that I've only experienced this true Philly delicacy once before as a young lad. All other incarnations of these delicious treats have been in the form of NY imitators. Naturally on a weekend trip to Philly I had to indulge in a "true" cheesesteak experience.

    After spending a long day walking the streets of Philly (I couldn't get that damn Springsteen song out of my head!!!), I found myself wondering which cheesesteak experience I should have. I have been trained to know that Tony Luke's has always been a staple, but wanted what the natives eat! After questioning numerous Philadelpheans, I was directed to Jim's Steak.

    After a pretty decent walk through the numerous valleys of art encrusted buildings and past all the shady looking baggy sweatpant wielding pot dealers, I found myself wandering South Street and finally at Jim's Steaks!

    The line wasn't too long, but you could tell there was an abundance of tourists littering the joint (much to my dismay). The walls were cluttered with thank you notes from various Philadelphean celebs and sports stars. Stamping the establishment with an obvious level of authenticity.

    After a 20 minute wait, I was finally up to bat to deliver my order and I did it with pinazz and in a way that says.. "yea! I am a native" (they obviously saw through this charade!). I went with a cheesesteak "whiz wit". I took my heavily loaded sangwich and headed upstairs for a feast.

    At first bite, the only thing I could think of was how the meat was an excellent grade and tasted extremely fresh (what a relief, as the preparation didn't seem up to par), and the tangy hint of the cheese whiz heightened the sandwich with a much needed zest. Overall, I found the sandwich lacked a bit of flavor without the cheese and on numerous occasions, found myself swigging soda just to get some of it down.

    Definitely one of the best cheesesteaks I've ever had! The simplicity and tradition leaks out of the sandwich with every bite and makes for a very easy going and delicious experience. Now, I'll just have to head back down to Philly to test out the other joints!

    I realize that food can be a subjective thing, especially when you're dealing with regional cuisine. For one person the best cheese steak is where the meat isn't chopped up much at all or is still a whole slab, for another, the finer it is chopped up the better. One cheese steak purist will insist that it can only have cheese wiz on it, while another aficionado is disgusted by the florescent orange goop and must have American cheese. For the record, I fall into the latter on both categories.

    I've had Jim's steaks and others from within the city and I'm convinced that more often than not it's the mom and pop joints in the strip malls out in the suburbs that tend to make the meanest cheese steak. There are a few places in Philly and just outside the city limits that know how to make a great cheese steak IMO, but Jim's isn't one of them.

    Is a Jim's cheese steak good? Yeah it's good. Is it great? Not by a long shot - though it's arguably the best of the cheese steak tourist traps. I think what pains me most about the high profile establishments like Jim's, Pat's and Geno's is that when people visit from out of town they are invariably going to be drawn to one of the big name places for their "official" cheese steak experience. With the cheese steak being to the Philly region what Po Boys are to Louisiana, that to me is a culinary tragedy.  So it doesn't surprise me that places like Jim's Steaks are peppered with reviews from out of towners making statements like, "what the heck are you people in Pennsylvania eating!?!"

Specialties

Jim's Steaks South Street has been serving the finest quality Cheese Steaks and Hoagies in Philadelphia for 37 years. Offering Cold Beer and indoor seating to accommodate your party large or small, make us unique among the other "named" Cheesesteak destinations.

History

Established in 1976.

This Philadelphia tradition started in 1939 at the original location of 431 North 62nd Street in West Philadelphia, where it still operates.

In 1976, Jim's Steaks expanded to this location at 400 South Street, which is now independently owned and operated from the original location. Our unequaled commitment to quality and customer service has made Jim's Steaks South St. world famous, and a Philadelphia landmark
