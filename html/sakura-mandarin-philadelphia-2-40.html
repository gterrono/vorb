    Sakura Mandarin is easily one of the worst places I've eaten in Chinatown.  The restaurant is clean and friendly looking, and it's ususally full of people, so I expected more.   We ordered garlic pork, fried udon, and the "spicy" shanghai beef. All three dishes were swimming in oil, and mostly spiceless, with a taste that I can only describe as generically super-salty asian brown-sauce differentiated by ginger and garlic.  one dish had a mildly piquant twinge to it that was either vinegar or spoilage. Frankly, I should've known something was wrong when our waitress asked us if we wanted to pay extra to have rice with our meal.  It should have been a foregone inclusion at their price point, and the servings were large, rich and greasy to the point where I suspect it would be medically dangerous to eat them without rice. Trying to eat the food was an ordeal in itself, since they only supply you with teacup saucer sized plates.  We had to ask the server to get us human sized plates and she seemed a bit confused.  With so many better (and cheaper) restaurants in Chinatown, I certainly won't be back.

    I have to admit that I tried this place based solely on the presence of steamed juicy buns (soup buns, xiao long bao).  I became a huge fan of them while living near nyc, and now that I've moved back to Philly I was having serious cravings.

    We pass by this place all the time and it's always crowded. Today, we finally decided to stop by for lunch to see what all the fuss is about, and so glad we did, because now we don't ever have to go back. Even so, I feel funny about giving the restaurant a one star rating, because it wasn't run-away-screaming awful, but nothing about it warrants a second star.

    We rocked up around noon on a Saturday and were seated immediately, albeit right by the door, so we were hit by a gust of cold air every time someone came or left. We also received tea and menus right away, and our server arrived to take our order as soon as we closed our menus. We ordered their specialty "P.A. Summer" sushi roll and soup dumplings (on the menu they're called juicy buns) to start. I was really excited to try the soup dumplings as I'd never had them before. Orange chicken fiend that he is, the boyfriend's choice of entree was no surprise. I was feeling adventurous and opted for the Chef's Special Shanghai Soup without knowing what it was, so perhaps I should accept some of the blame for that one. Our server was gone before we had a chance to order any beverages.

    The soup arrived first, which we could live with, except that it tasted like crap. The only ingredients were chunks of meat (mostly cartilage and fat), pickled bamboo, and knotted... I don't know, skin? I think it was skin. The broth was decent but nothing special. The boyfriend squeezed in an order for Sprite.

    The sushi roll arrived next, and was the best part of our meal, but it was teensy-tiny, especially for the price. The soup dumplings arrived shortly thereafter, but instead of clearing the empty sushi platter, the server started pushing it out of the way with the dumpling basket. My boyfriend quickly picked up the empty platter and put it on the other end of the table before everything came crashing down. Figuring out how to eat the dumplings without them exploding on the table or scalding your mouth (or both) was fun, but both the meat and broth inside were unexpectedly bland. I wish I'd had them elsewhere so I could make a comparison, but I'm pretty sure this wasn't what people refer to when they rave about soup dumplings.

    The orange chicken arrived next, with a disclaimer that the rice was on its way. The boyfriend asked after his Sprite. A few minutes later, the rice still hadn't arrived, so we started picking at the chicken, which was so mediocre we decided we'd better wait on the rice. Finally, the Sprite arrived, followed by the rice, neither of which improved the taste of the orange chicken. We had a few bites each, then decided to cut our losses and go. I relate to an earlier review by Fredwin T., as we also opted out of leftovers, and not because we were full. I don't know what they did to that poor chicken, but the end result was positively underwhelming. Coming from a couple who subsist on hoagies from Wawa, that's saying an awful lot.

    The only thing I approved of was my fortune cookie, which predicted new clothes in my future.

    While I can't speak to the quality of the sushi here (why would you come to a Chinese restaurant for sushi anyways?  Would you go to a sushi place for Chinese food?), their Chinese offerings that I've had have been of good enough quality that I felt compelled to write-up this review--in short, Sakura is definitely deserving of higher praise than the mediocre reviews that have been given thus far.

    You gotta come here with a purpose, and that purpose should be to get your soup dumpling groove on (or, as they call 'em, "juicy dumplings," which sounds so delightfully inappropriate).

    Walking in the door on a Sunday lunch rush, Sakura was entirely filled with middle-aged and elderly Chinese.  And, as we all know with ethnic joints, this is a tell-tale sign that speaks to the quality of a restaurant's food.

    I ordered one basket of pork steamed dumplings, minced beef and bean thread vermicelli hot pot, and stir-fried rice cakes with chicken.  The rice cakes came out first and were very well-balanced in flavor.  Now, with rice cake dishes at restaurants, the texture is key and can often be the downfall--these were perfect:  nice and chewy--not undercooked, not overcooked or slimy.  The bean thread hot pot came out next and likewise, the texture and flavor of the dish was spot on.  But, alas, where were my juicy dumplings??

    As often is the case in asian joints, I couldn't help but notice how the servers were busily delivering their steaming loads of juicy dumplings to every other damn table in the restaurant but mine.  And, as often is the case in asian joints, none of the servers seemed to notice my wandering eyes.  But, hunger took hold and the panic bred from the fear of MIA juicy dumplings superseded any hesitance as I brusquely flagged down the manager and inquired as to the whereabouts of my aforementioned juicy dumplings.

    I know we've all had some nightmare services when it comes to ethnic eateries--particularly if you aren't absolutely fluent in the native tongue.  Much to my pleasant surprise and delight, the manager came over to apologize for the delay on my order and provided my girlfriend and I with a voucher for a complimentary order of juicy dumplings on the house.  Score!

    Moral of the story:  while Chinatown certainly doesn't lack of options for good Chinese eateries, at $22 for 3 entree dishes (+1 complimentary order of juicy dumplings in the near future), Sakura deserves a look and a bite.  And, given the clientele that seems to frequent this establishment, remember:  where would grandma eat?

    After reading a pretty in depth review of this restaurant online. I decided give it a try myself and… After reading a pretty in depth review of this restaurant online. I decided give it a try myself and see where I would rank it.

    As you first step into the store, you get smacked in the face with the colors green and white. Everything just looks so refreshing, new, and clean that you need a couple of seconds to let your senses absorb all the detail..... Done absorbing?? Now onto the food...

    I came into this restaurant intending on ordering exactly two dishes. The shanghai style fried rice cakes and the Xiaolongbao AKA soup dumplings AKA steamed pork dumplings.

    (For those interested in ordering the rice cakes, its not on the menu yet, just say fried rice cakes and they should understand)

    Since I am a huge fan of Chinese styled fried rice cakes, I decided to put Sakura up to the taste test. The two styles I'm used to having are the shanghai style and the foochow style. There are places in Chinatown that sells each style of fried rice cakes and I wanted to see where this new place would rank in comparison. While their fried rice cakes was not bad, it wasn't as good as the shanghai rice cakes I have eaten at Empress Garden on 10th. The flavors tasted plain and less sophisticated in comparison and the portion size was disappointingly small.

    However where this place earns its 4 star ranking is their soup dumplings. I have eaten the Xiaolongbao at Dim Sum Garden and Joe's Shanghai in NYC and I think Sakura garden's dumplings beats both hands down. While the flavor inside the dumplings tastes similar (Owners of Sakura and Dim Sum Garden used to be partners) Where the Sakura dumplings earns its ranking is the skin of the dumplings. Their dumpling skin is thin and at the perfect thickness it doesn't taste as stiff as the other places I've mentioned. The grounded pork inside the dumpling also crumbles as you chew making the flavor spreading process so much more efficient and enjoyable. I guess it better be good to charge 5 bucks for 6 small dumplings.

    Out of the three places in Chinatown and probably the entire city of Philadelphia that sells Xiaolongbao, I've eaten at two, the only place I have yet to try is Zhi Wei Guan - The Magic Kingdom of Dough, but since it's been closed for a LONG while and still closed as of this writing, I will declare that Sakura Mandarin has the best Xiaolongbao in Philadelphia. IMO of course ;)
