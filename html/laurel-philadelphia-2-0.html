    I took my boyfriend here for his birthday because it just opened and I figured it would be fun to try something new to celebrate. Maybe because this was in Fond's old space, forcing some high expectations, but the restaurant was just really good, it was not great.

    A few dishes blew our minds, specifically the Fresh Ricotta Gnocchi and the Caramelized White Chocolate Mousse. Both of these are must haves if you go to Laurel. The gnocchi was hearty, savory and melted in your mouth. I was so surprised we enjoyed the mousse, neither of us like white chocolate, but it was absolutely amazing. It tasted fresh, light and airy-- my perfect dessert. Additionally, the chocolate short bread and fresh figs were the perfect compliments. I would go back and just get both of those dishes because they were that good.

    As for the other dishes we ordered, they were delicious, but I was slightly underwhelmed. My boyfriend said certain cuts of his pork were overcooked (it was cooked four ways - apparently certain ways were better than others). I ordered the daurade (a type of fish that I have never eaten before) and it was really good, I mean I could never cook anything remotely as good as it, but it was not "oh my good that was amazing" good.

    Overall it was a great experience, the dishes looked beautiful and the service was phenomenal. I will definitely go back, but there are other restaurants on Passyunk that I think are worth returning to before I go back to Laurel again.

    Nick Elmi really nails it at his new, tiny BYOB.  

    The chairs, first of all, are the most comfortable seating I've ever experienced in a restaurant.  And there are only about 24 of them, so it's as cozy as can be.  Our server, Alice, was also the manager, and she was an absolute delight the entire night.  And of course I will admit it's pretty neat to watch Top Chef and then have Nick Elmi drop a dish or two off at your table.  

    The food was just exceptionally good, all the way through, and every plate was a little work of art on the table.

    - Fluke with Asian Pear:  A bright combination which I've never seen before, the fish and Asian pear highlighted each other perfectly, and a (mild) horseradish yogurt made it sing.  A light, balanced start that set the tone for the rest of the meal.

    - Ricotta Gnocchi with Pancetta:  Tiny little fluffy pillows in a little bowl, with tiny crumbled sourdough croutons adding some serious crunch.  Creamy and cozy and salty... I could have eaten about 5x the serving size.

    - Foie Gras Terrine: A gorgeous slab of foie gras terrine, marbled with stripes of cocoa.  It was one of the most unique foie experiences I've had, with the bitterness of the cocoa cutting through that rich heaviness, a job usually left to the sweetness of fruit.  Very interesting and quite good.

    - Trout, Burgundy Snails and Black Quinoa:  I like trout a lot, and this one killed it.  Perfectly cooked, melt-in-your-mouth fish with a crispy skin, plus black quinoa and some burgundy snails (which I only got one of thanks to my overzealous fiancee!).  Fishy and delicious.

    - Poulet, Meyer Lemon Reduction:  What a dish.  Chicken is an underrated dish, and this one, like Parc's version, shows just why.  Laurel sends out two juicy, succulent slices of breast with crispy skin in a vivid meyer lemon reduction that made me wish I had the whole bird.  Add to that a single house-made chicken sausage of thigh and leg meat, which was like the most highly-elevated version of a chicken nugget ever made.  Trumpet mushrooms balanced out the plate.

    Laurel has my vote for a return visit, and hopefully very soon!

    As a huge Top Chef fan, nabbing a reservation at Laurel was very important the second I read that it had opened and boy I'm happy I did.

    My guy and I decided to make it just the two of us for our first visit which was almost necessary because the place is super tiny and yet incredibly warm and adorable. From the second we walked in, I knew I was going to love this place. We were very warmly welcomed, I loved the feel and the service was top notch from beginning to end.

    Now onto our meal...
    We started with the Fluke with Asian pear. This was a wonderful start to the meal. The perfect amount of sweetness and then cut with a subtle taste of horseradish.
    Next was the Foie Gras terrine. I had never seen foie like this before and was happy to welcome it to my foie experiences. The foie came out marbled with a coco powder which added a subtle bitterness to the rich smooth foie. It came with a toasted, hot brioche and a sweet pepper like relish. This was great but i wished it was a tad sweeter.
    Then came the ricotta gnocchi with pancetta. Wow. This dish was homey and rich and yet light and delicate. I was in love. The gnocchi was so tender and sprinkled with sourdough croutons to add a perfect crunch.
    Lastly came our main dishes. Generally, I find myself always loving my appetizers more than the main at many restaurants but this time was an exception. We shared the Trout with burgundy snails and black quinoa. This was a wonderful dish. the skin was crispy and the snails were incredibly tender!
    Our other shared dish was the Poulet with Meyer Lemon reduction. I never order chicken while out but this dish just seemed too good to be true and our server highly recommended it so we went for it. This came out with two pieces of breast meat with very crispy skin and what we started to call an elevated chicken nugget! It was described as a chicken sausage, a mixture of chicken leg and thigh meat. Heaven. All this over perfectly cooked and delicious trumpet mushrooms. Order this, trust me.

    Our whole night from food, to service to atmosphere was fantastic.Unfortunately we didn't have any room left for dessert but trust me, we will absolutely be back!

    Before I get to the wonderfully postive things about Laurel, a couple complaints:

    The first, a very minor one, is that our table was wobbly. Not enough to be a real distraction, but at a place like this tables should have zero wobble. To anyone at the restaurant reading this, this was the table nearest to the front door.

    A less minor complaint echoes the concerns of another reviewer: the music. The music was TERRIBLE. Mostly 70s funk which I found quite unpleasant in this setting. I suggested to the waiter that the music didn't match the setting and he replied that the chef/owner wanted to keep a "casual setting," and also mentioned that this is the reason why the waiters didn't wear fancy garb, etc. I didn't pursue an argument but I would say that the idea that the music needs to be somehow *overtly* unsophisticated to maintain a "casual setting" is bullshit. Laurel already feels plenty casual just from the understated decor alone (which reminds me of Le Chateaubriand in Paris, another restaurant serving excellent food that also tries to avoid pretense, but does so without bad music). By extension of the idea the waiter put forth, perhaps the chef/owner would prefer to serve the food on paper plates and have diners bus their own tables. I mean we wouldn't want snobby fancy plating and presentation to give an air of uptightness, would we? I don't have anything against 70s funk per se but seriously, one of the songs playing was "Brick House" by The Commodores and it really wasn't appropriate for the occasion. Take it from me Nick Elmi, people WANT to feel at least somewhat classy when they go to a place like this. I had a "poulet with winter alliums," not "chicken with onions n' leeks" right? I hope someone there reads this and takes the suggestion seriously to change the music, keeping it to jazz, classical, and more muted contemporary selections. Actually what would really be amazing is if a place like this didn't have music at all. This is a separate discussion but as a professional musician I believe that the stream of background music we are constantly fed everywhere we go is bad for our appreciation of music, which is something that should be actively listened to...anyway they probably won't get rid of music entirely but please please get rid of the funk. I booked a table for Valentine's Day already and don't want to listen to that again. /rant

    Despite my hatred of the music I simply must give Laurel five stars because the food was stunningly good, all of it. The foie gras terrine was a beautifully balanced dish and the bites I got with strong cocoa flavor were heavenly (there could have even been more cocoa). The blood orange reduction was very subtle but still came through. Everyone's raves about the gnocchi are on point, they really melt in your mouth. "Poulet" with "alliums" was perfectly cooked and presented, and outstanding in its simplicity. The chcken skin was crispy and the meyer lemon reduction was very lemony which I liked very much in this preparation. My boyfriend's selections were just as good: a tasty salad to start, followed by an amazing charred mackerel with a good dose of jalapeno, then the cod dish which was again cooked perfectly and the snails were a lovely addition.

    I got the white chocolate pudding for dessert, which was just as good as other reviews have made it out to be. No less good was boyfriend's choice of the lemon curd. Service was very relaxed and even though we were there for two hours it didn't feel like the food was taking forever to come out. Laurel is of course pricey if everyone gets all four courses. Our dinner with the tip added in came to $170. However I wouldn't expect to spend much less at this kind of place and I hope they do more extended tasting menus soon.

    There are a lot of places I still haven't been to in Philly but just based on this meal Laurel tops restaurants such as Bibou, Fond, Will, Matyson etc. for going the extra mile in regard to the balance, flavor and seasoning of each dish. I look forward to coming back but please change the music PLEASE. The food is too good for it!

    Big Thumbs Up!

    We first learned about Laurel in September at the 2013 Philly Feastival.  Nicolas served us an outstanding morsel that really got our attention.  We asked for a card and found not only did he not have a card but the restaurant was not even open yet.  We might not remember exactly what he served us that night (there were over 75 restaurants) but we did remember the most important thing, the name Laurel.

    This place is hot.  It took us 3 weeks to get a table for two but the wait was worth it.

    I started with the raw scallop, apple, sea lettuce and celery.  So refreshing and subtle, the scallop had just a hint of the sea and the broth's citrus brightened the flavors.  It's interesting to have a dish where celery is the most powerful ingredient but works perfectly.  It was a home run.   If foie gras is on the menu she has to order it, no questions asked.  So it was the foie gras and cocoa terrine.  I was surprised to read all the other reviews (after our dinner) about how wonderful the coco foie gras was.  Our palates must not be as refined as the other yelp reviewers as the foie gras was not to our liking.  We ate the entire dish trying to make sense out of it with every bite.  We both felt the coco and blood orange reduction totally over powered the foie gras.  In theory the bitterness of the coco could round out the fattiness of the foie and the blood orange could add a touch sweet acid to the mix.  Unfortunately it just did not work for us.

    Her second courses was charred Spanish mackerel, jalapeño and ponzu.  It was not what we expected.  It was charred but perfectly rare (can you describe fish as rare?) in the middle.  Mackerel can be very oily and have a strong flavor but on my first taste I even missed that it was mackerel.  On my second bite I paid more attention and it was like sushi on a fork with a crispy crunch.  We both agreed it was an outstanding dish.   I had the Gnocchi, what can I say that has not been said about these mini-marshmallow sized bites of Heaven.  Take a Top Chef and comfort food made out of pasta, pork, cream and cheese and you get a knock your socks off dish.  I almost ordered a it again for desert.  Our waitress Alice said 4 or 5 people had done that in the past.  If you order one thing a Laurel order the Gnocchi.

    For my third course I ordered the Berkshire pork with mashed chestnuts (I am sure they did not call it mashed chestnuts but I can not remember how they described it on the menu.  Think of mashed potatoes made out of chestnuts and you'll know what I am talking about).  This was pork three ways, pork belly (the mother of all bacon), pulled pork in a casing (like a sausage) and pork tenderloin.  The surprise of the dish was how awesome the pulled pork was, the casing retained all it's lovely juices.  This dish was recommended to me by an acquaintance and I would recommend it to any pork lovers it was great.  She had a question about the Ocean Trout entree and asked Alice what exactly was it was and if it was wild or farmed.  We are not big fans of farmed raised fish.  Nicolas came out and filled us in, its other name is  "spotted seatrout" but it is not really a trout.  Its a white fish with a mild favor, slightly flaky texture, wild caught off the coast of North Carolina.  The filet was much thicker then a rainbow trout.  The slightly flaky part was where the fish was seared with the rest of the flesh almost pillowy.  Excellent dish!  

    We are not big dessert fans so I was debating another round of Gnocchi but ended up going for the lemon curd with meringue brulee and Buddha's hand.  Of course we had to ask about the Buddha's hand. Alice described it as an Asian fruit that grows with many fingers.  It has a citrus like flavor and is candied to remove some bitterness.  The lemon curd was served over the meringue brulee then topped with meringue chips and slivers of the Buddha's hand.  I really enjoyed the mix of textures, airy meringue brulee, firm lemon curd and the crunchy meringue chips.  It was a very satisfying dessert with a great cup of coffee.  She had the cheese plate that came out as a small plate of two cheese, one hard rind cheese and a brie.  

    We had a wonderful night at Laurel and can't wait to go back and try more dishes.  Get your reservations now because you may need to wait for an open table.  This is one place not to miss.

    Troy

    My S.O. got us reservations to Laurel as part of my Christmas present. I was thrilled to go. We've been following Chef Nicholas on Top Chef this season and have been really pulling for him. His food always looked good, but we were anxious to taste it for ourselves.

My only complaint about the decor was that the tables seemed a bit high. For someone that is 6 foot tall, I had trouble comfortably raising my elbows above the table to eat my meal. It's not a big deal, but a minor annoyance. But perhaps this was a purposeful design choice to bring the food closer to the eyes, nose, and mouths of the diners.

    The menu is set up in 4 courses, a cold app, warm app, main, and dessert. On the weekends, Chef prepares a prix fixe tasting menu. I think it was ~$70 for a 7-course meal, with many options from the normal menu and some experimental dishes. Since my date and I had dishes in mind (and she has some dietary restrictions), we went with the standard menu.

    I ordered the tuna crudo, ricotta gnocchi, ocean trout, and the citrus curd. My date had the scallop crudo, mackerel,  Walu, and chocolate pudding. I enjoyed all the food we had and especially loved the variety of fish options. For me, the highlights were the tuna curdo, the mackerel, and the ocean trout. The tuna was well seasoned, but still tasted of the sea and was the perfect thickness and cut. There was playfulness with a cold dish that had even colder components. The mackerel was just a bit oily and fishy (like it should be), but also had that fresh, deep ocean savoriness. Some people are a bit afraid of mackerel, but this was superb. Similarly, the ocean trout was very well cooked with crispy skin, and served over with interesting sauces. The snails were a bit bland, but the turnips really complemented the firm white fish well.

    My dining partner loved the gnocchi, but I found the texture to be a bit too soft, and it didn't seem fit with the rest of the meal, which was an exercise in subtle flavors, interesting ingredients, and plays on temperature and texture. I found them to be too fishy for my tastes, but that's a personal thing as scallops kinda gross me out.

    All in all, the food was great, atmosphere was inviting, the service attentive and I even got a brief word in with the chef. I like that it's BYO, which really help keep the cost down. We can't wait to come back when the menu changes (hopefully in the spring)

Specialties

Laurel Restaurant is an intimate French/American influenced BYO by Chef/Owner Nicholas Elmi. Laurel Restaurant brings world class French/American cuisine with a focus on sustainability and simplicity in an intimate, comfortable setting. Reservations can be made through Open Table or by directly calling the restaurant +1 215 271 8299.

History

Established in 2013.

Laurel BYO Restaurant on East Passyunk in Philadelphia opens November 5, 2013. Chef/Owner Nicholas Elmi.

Meet the Business Owner

Nicholas E. Business Owner

A 2013 Philadelphia Rising Star Chef and current Bravo Top Chef Contestant, Chef Nicholas Elmi has worked at some of the top rated French restaurants on the east coast, including Guy Savoy (Paris), Le Bec Fin, Union Pacific, Oceana and Lutece. Nicholas has built a solid French repertoire that is constantly updated with influences from his travels as well as his upbringing in New England.
