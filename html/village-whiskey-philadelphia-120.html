    1 check-in ROTD 2/8/2011

    I have to admit to being a bit of a Jose Garces groupie--I've been pretty impressed with everything he's done in Philly so far, and Village Whiskey is no exception.

    A small, often over-crowded space with excessively long waits for tables on weekends, and even weeknights, Village Whiskey is an upscale hole in the wall offering upscale bar food, at upscale prices. The eponymous options on the menu are only a fraction of what makes this spot great (I've only ordered a whiskey once).  To me, the cocktails are what stand out the most. They whip up a good cocktail no matter what you order, but the options on the menu are all outstanding. I always end up going for the Commodore, a variation on an Old Fashioned with a bit of lime and a mezcal rinse, taking the otherwise comforting flavors of an Old Fashioned into a brighter, more citrusy and refreshing direction.

    The fries. The fries. They're fried in duck fat. Enough said. The burgers, while a little on the pricy side, are juicy and literally melt in your mouth. I really can't say anything bad about the quality of the food or drinks here. The only dings against this place are the prices, which may be a few bucks too high on certain items, and the crowd in such a small space.

    Village Whiskey is a great place to kick off your night with a killer cocktail, or an unusual whiskey or two, and serves as a great aperitif for a meal at Tinto next door, another of Garces' great spots, and it even holds its own as a full-blown dinner spot. Versatile, tasty, and hip, Village Whiskey is a great place to waste away the day or night.

    Loved this place! My wife and I were in town for the weekend and happened to be in Rittenhouse Square. Village Whiskey did not disappoint.

    It is a small space, and the wait on Saturday night was about 45 minutes when we got there. However, we lucked into a spot in the bar area in pretty short order-- so we took it.

    First, the caveat is, I give Village Whiskey 5 stars, but i fully acknowledge there are things that aren't perfect - but what they do well, they do SO WELL, that i absolutely must give it 5 stars.

    VW is def my favorite burger place, even though the prices, like the decadent burgers, are excessive. It's dark and cozy, with small tables that work best for groups of 2-4 and a long bar that is always packed with people. The bartenders here make some of the best cocktails in the city, and true to the name, VW has an impressive list of whiskey - scores of bourbon and rye, OH MY! Beer list isn't too shabby either. and somehow, the bartenders really seem to know what you'll like.

    *Tater Tots with garlic aoli
    Though, tater tots might nostalgic for the days you bought lunch in your school cafeterias, the tots at VW are probably not what you expect. The tots are shaped like little round balls and the potatoes taste like mashed potatoes that have been coated and deep fried. I didn't really like the garlic aoli that accompanied these tots. It was overall a bit bland and didn't seem to add anything much to the dish. It would have been so much better with extra bite - perhaps spicy dijon.

    *Cheese Puffs
    Very disappointing. The puffs were bland and not even the cheese could help distract from the unappealing texture. Usually, we have to fight over the last remaining item in a dish, but with these, the last one just sat there until the rest of our food arrived at our table and the server cleared the plate away.

    *Whiskey King burger: maple bourbon glazed cipollini onion + rogue bleu cheese + applewood bacon + foie gras
    Many people have many awesome things to say about this, so I won't really belabor the point, other than - it really is that good. that you can taste each ingredient so distinctively is amazing.

    *Village Burger + house-made thousandisland + tomato + boston bibb + jasper hill cheddar + marinated crimini mushrooms + fried egg + smoked bacon
    This is my personal masterpiece. I lovingly call this the VW Burger a la mindy. I think it's PERFECTION (Garces, feel free to add this to the menu). Better than the Whiskey King. Don't get too excited because once you add up all the ingredients, it still costs as much as the Whiskey King signature burger, but tastes BETTER imo.  Fried eggs make almost everything better, wonderful, droolworthy, etc. The bacon adds that extra chewy saltiness, which is balanced out by that gooey egg. The sauteed mushrooms add moisture, while the tomato and lettuce added that always welcome refreshing sweetness and crunch. And the cheddar, of course the sharp cheddar.... yessssss.

    I wasn't sure what to expect from this place; while I'm generally more likely to check out an obscure ethnic hole-in-the-wall joint than a slick celebu-chef jawn, this place was too close to my place not to check out. Our waiter was a touch awkward, but pleasant--let's just say he was polite but didn't go out of his way. After perusing the drink menu (fairly extensive) we opted for a couple cocktails: I got the Sazerac and my friend got the Latin Quarter. Mine was strong, if overly sweet, and lacked the complexity of its subtler ingredients (basically tasted like rye whiskey with sugar, and just a faint, intermittent whiff of lemon zest)--it was ok, but for $13, the execution could have been better. I found my friend's drink just plain undrinkable.

    We both ordered pulled pork sandwiches when we saw someone's gray-looking burger walk past: so much for trying the burgers. We also split an order of the duck fat fries.

    The fries were.. fries. If I really sat there and focused, I could just barely discern the duck fat, which leads me to believe that the fat was cut very liberally with a clean vegetable oil like canola. They were soft rather than crispy, and could have potentially benefited from a thinner (pommes frites) cut rather than a thicker "homestyle fry" cut. Maybe I'm nitpicking, but at $5 for a very small (handful) portion of fries, I'm willing to be critical.

    The pulled pork sandwiches looked very Spartan on their plates when they arrived: white plate, sandwich, and 5-7 slices of fried pickle, just sort of artlessly slapped on there. Again, I'm not expecting parsley and lemon twists, but could we at least try to make it look appealing? There was a tiny portion of cole slaw in each sandwich (about 2Tbsp).

    Underwhelmed by the presentation, I was nonetheless pleasantly surprised by the actual taste. The brioche bun could have stood being toasted, but stood up admirably to the pulled pork. The pork itself was moist, and the sauce wasn't overdone: not too sweet, not too smoky. I wanted a more assertive barbecue flavor--more char, more mesquite, more porky flavor--and I wanted more cole slaw. The pickles were quite yummy, with a peppery kick.

    Final verdict? There was nothing per se glaringly wrong with the meal, but it could have easily been so much better, especially for the money. The drinks fell short, but their whiskey and whisky menu was extensive--I'd say a true aficionado could probably find something of value there. Overall, it seems more like a place to stop by for a drink or two after work with friends you're trying to impress rather than a place to grab a bite on a regular basis.

    Not only is Village Whiskey the best burger in Philly by a complete landslide, it's the best burger I've ever had, period.  The ingredients are incredibly fresh, and, in the right combination, they balance each other out perfectly.  If you're unsure of what combination to order, I've found the wait staff to have excellent suggestions.  My personal favorite is the fried egg, Haystack Mountain chevre, and the caramelized onions.  Some find the chevre too overpowering, in which case I would recommend the Jasper Hill cheddar, which is also fantastic.  If you like your fries thick (i.e., steak fries style), the duck fat fries are excellent, with just a trace of duck in every bite and sizzling hot and crispy when served.  I haven't spent much time exploring the other areas of the menu (while foie gras is my first culinary love, I personally don't think it belongs on a burger), but you absolutely cannot go wrong with the burger and fries.

    The scotch and bourbon list is the best in the city, though a bit on the expensive side.  Their old fashioned is one of the best I've ever had, and while the cocktail menu changes with some regularity, the staff is always happy to make you something off the old list (provided you remember what the drink was called).  The beer list is esoteric but a breath of fresh air compared to many other Philly bars (Monk's excluded).  It's hard to find a good rye beer, but VW has a few great ones on hand (Sixpoint Righteous Rye and Bear Republic Hop Rod Rye, to name two offhand).

    The only downsides to VW are (i) the small space, and (ii) the wait staff is generally overworked (read: you may have to flag down your server as they walk by to get another drink).  However, this is really a non-issue for me.  First, because the place is small, it's not hard to get your server's attention (they'll walk right by you every 60 seconds).  Second, I'm never in a hurry when I go there.  If I have to wait forty minutes for a table, it's no big deal; just grab a place at the end of the bar and have a few drinks while you wait.  Those points aside, I have no idea how people can give VW one star for "poor service."  I've never once received an incorrect drink or improperly prepared food.  Village Whiskey is simply the model for well executed consistency, and one of the best places to have a casual burger and cocktail on a Friday night.

A ground-to-order Angus Beef Burger, a Veggie Burger, a Bison Burger or an Vienna All-Beef Hot Dog with all the trimmings are each served alongside duck fat French fries and Sly Fox cheddar sauce.  Burgers are topped with Village Whiskey's house-made thousand-island dressing, Bibb lettuce, beefsteak tomato and a potato-beer bun, with optional toppings including bacon, caramelized onion, truffles, artichokes and a selection of cheeses.

As one of the most comprehensive collections of its namesake spirit available in Philadelphia, Village Whiskey features a veritable library of 80 to 100 varieties of whiskey, bourbon, rye and scotch from Scotland, Canada, Ireland, United States and even a few Japanese options.

History

Established in 2009.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
