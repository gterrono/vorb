    I wish I had the experience at Tinto that so many other yelpers have!

    I just moved to Philly from NYC and Tinto is very close to my new place.  After yelping for a place to grab dinner and seeing the great reviews, we walked the couple blocks to try it.  It was a Wed night at 8pm and we were told the wait for a table would be an hour and a half.  You have to give the place credit for its popularity, I guess. They were able to seat us immediately at the bar, which we were fine with.

    The sangria was among the very few highlights of our night.  We ordered by the glass and didn't get the watered down flavor that some yelpers have complained about. And then we moved on to the menu...

    My first thought was, wow, I thought I'd save a little money in Philly vs. NYC!  Clearly not by eating here. For the atmosphere, portion size (tiny) and quality of the food, I would never pay these prices again - even in NYC!  We actually considered heading home immediately to order pizza instead....and should have.

    In an effort to help you brave ones that are still going to try Tinto, you should avoid at all costs:
    - Crab with peas and Benton's bacon (what IS this? they make such a big deal of calling it out in the menu and when they bring the food...looks like plain old bacon).  This dish was woefully under-seasoned and tasted like...nothing!  The crab meat looked beautiful, but even that couldn't save the dish.

    - Pork belly with apple. Let me say, I love pork and anything resembling bacon and found this dish shockingly bland and borderline gross. Couldn't finish my one little crustini.

    - Figs wrapped in serano ham.  It's a straight lie to call these "wrapped," the meat was simply placed over raw figs. Meh. Try bacon wrapped dates next time, Tinto.

    Some things were OK:
    - Arugula salad was the lone highlight - sad for a tapas place - but it was a genuinely good salad....if a little to cute (read: very small).

    - Shrimp brushetta - good flavor, but another lie to call this brushetta, at least to Americans.  It was two prawns on a plate, heads still on.  Not a cube of tomato or toast in sight.

    - Organic chicken with mash - again tiny portions of under-seasoned food, but way above most of what we ordered.

    We left hungry and dissatisfied with the bang we received for our $100 bucks (incl tip) and proceeded directly to the market to claim a couple of comforting pints of B&J's.

    I won't be back.  Too many great places in Philly to revisit this flop!

    Listed in Best Bars, Ethnic Food, Meh Spots

    As it turns out, the Basque region of Spain offers my least favorite cuisine. I only found this out last night after a less than inspired meal. It's not to say the food was awful, it's just that I wasn't particularly moved by it.

    Having grown up on a tapas diet (my dad was a Spanish chef), most of my favorite dishes hail from the Mediterranean (camarones al ajillo, patatas bravas, tortilla española, paella - none of which are on Tinto's menu), so it's what I grew to like. His cooking was like haphazard science: without even trying, he knew the perfect balance between salt and garlic and his ailoli (garlic mayo) was absolutely ambrosial. So, what I look for in tapas is food that looks like an angry, albeit talented, chef threw it on a plate only to be rushed out of the kitchen, not something with different colored sauces stylistically arranged to give the dish an air of elegance. I'm not looking for haute-tapas. I feel the same way about Mexican food. Give me something GOOD, not something PRETTY. Pursuing this rant a bit further, I think Center City is the perfect spot for a Tinto and Amada, so it's not to say I don't support the idea. It's just that I wish Garces or someone with a hankering for the Spanish cuisine would open a homey place with authentic, less expensive tapas. I'm talking about food you'd find in a small Mediterranean village in Spain, not a $$$ meal meant for the upper crust.

    There were few things I ate that had me craving seconds though. The short rib sandwich was downright delectable: juicy, tender, flavorsome. I'd highly recommend this. Likewise, the artichokes were a standout vegetable along with the wild mushrooms: finally a dish with enough garlic to kill a vampire. The rest of our meal consisted of sea bass,
    organic chicken, green beans, and Kobe beef.

    If you're a big fan of Basque cuisine, this place is worth a visit. If you're torn between Tinto and Amada, head to Amada for similarly priced Spanish Mediterranean cuisine. Just my preference though.

    Like Jose Garces' other restaurants, Tinto's dishes are full of complexity and flavor.  The combinations, compared to the other restaurant, seem more subtle to me.

    The sautéed wild mushrooms with truffle were dynamite.  There were slices of potato under it all which just matched the fungi beautifully.  I really enjoyed the sausage over lentils as well.  Again, the combination of everything in the dish including the large leaves of parsley made it superb.  The braised short rib sandwich was another standout because of the divine flavor imparted to the beef.  However, I think the delivery in sandwich form makes it difficult to share at the table - you just have pre-notify your fellow diners that its all yours.

    I loved the use of the Basque region's espelette peppers.  There was a bit of this pepper fairy dust on just about everything.  It gave each dish a really nice zing that didn't just match nicely- it was a required ingredient.

    Save room for dessert too!  There was a chocolate cake with caramelized bananas that was amazing.  I had a chocolate coffee layer cake that was akin to eating pastry cream layered with butter, which is very pleasant in my opinion.  However, on top was a Hazelnut ice cream served in an edible Hazelnut nougatine bowl that was to-die-for.  In fact, my girlfriend tried to kill me for it.

    The atmosphere at Tinto is wonderfully small and cozy, not too loud.  And the wines are very approachable.  We stayed with the by-the-glass selection and were very satisfied.

    Overall, I was extremely pleased with each dish, but not knocked over.  For instance, the Octopus was delicious, but I preferred the Amada preparation.  The pork belly canape was delicious as well, but I preferred the Amada preparation.

    This completes my journey through Jose Garces four tapas restaurants in Philly, and I have to say that my preference is: 1 Amada, 2 Distrito, with Chifa & Tinto in a toss-up for 3.

Specialties

Tinto features a mouth-watering selection of "pinxtos" (pronounced PEEN-choes), the Basque equivalent of tapas.  Along with pinxtos, Tinto offers a tempting array of charcuterie, cheeses, mariscos (shellfish), brochettes and bocadillos (sandwiches).  

Some of the inspired dishes include:  Duck Confit Montadito, with wild black cherries and bleu de Basque;  Gambas Brochettes, shrimp with grape tomato and sherry espelette chili sauce;  Roasted Rack of Lamb Au Jus, with truffled artichoke puree and candied lemon;  Marmitako de Ventresca, tuna toro, parsley oil, migas and shaved mojama;  Txanguro Montadito, Dungeness crab, avocado and espelette;  and Mousse de Queso, with goat cheese, calabaza, orange, olive oil, caramel and lavender tuille.

History

Established in 2007.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
