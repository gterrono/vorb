    I'm a foodie, I admit it - so anytime there's something as talked about as say a cheesesteak from philly; I'm eventually going to go try it. I may or may not be disappointed.

    In this case, the first time I went I was immediately a fan. I've NEVER had a bad cheesesteak at Tony Lukes; ever. What backs up the palatte pleasure is the sheer craziness of the atmosphere. You park in the middle of the street (more fun if you show up on say a Saturday in the middle of the night when the 'crazies' are out); order a delicious sandwich without fear of 'screwing up' like you do at Pats, you grab a seat in the very clean and appointed seating area and sometimes you even get to see Ben Franklin. No joke. You meet all kinds of people, it's fun.

    Recently we went a little crazy and visited Pats, Genos and Tony Lukes all in one day (hey we were there for a hockey game, why not...you gotta eat right?). Location wise Tony Lukes wins, food quality yup, Tony Lukes again, and definitely Tony Lukes for customer service and overall experience.

    I enjoyed my provolone/wit at Pats but the bread was a little dry and thick, nothing wrong with that....but then again I can bake bread at home so I'd prefer a little less when I'm after a cheesesteak. The location is totally sketchy but because you have so many tourists going to Pats and Genos it's a little less intimidating...well; in a philly the city of not-brotherly love kind of way.

    Genos - fugheddaboudit; yuck. All I have to say. The food, the customer service (or lack thereof) and the experience. Never again...but now I can speak from experience instead of theory and hearsay.

    Tony Lukes wins my love hands down - cheesesteak with spinach rabe and provolone anyday baby. I'm in.

    This is a long overdue review of when I first visited back in October 2011.  I was referred to Tony Luke's by a native New Yorker who I so happened to have sat next to at my friend's wedding.  She had suggested to come here instead of Gino's or Pat's because they were such tourist traps and over-hyped.  

    My husband and I took her suggestion and went here instead after my friend's wedding.  There was a line (not too long) that moved just enough to order within 5 minutes.  It looks deceiving when you first walk in because you think the line starts all the way at the back, but really its just people waiting for their number to be called out.  

    So my husband and I waited for about 15-20 minutes to get our order and jammed out to share a meal in our rental car parked outside of a grocery store outlet.  We both ordered the Cheesesteak hoagie (rib-eye steak, lettuce, tomato, mayo, cheese and pickles), and my husband gobbled his down quickly.

    I on the other hand took a bite and was a little disappointed with the meat's flavor.  The bread gave perfect texture to the ooey gooey-ness of the melted cheese, but the steak's flavor was kind of disappointing.  I could only eat half of my sandwich, which my husband later ate for a late night snack.  If I were to visit Philly again, I would probably come here and order something else to redeem my first visit.

    Tony Luke's should certainly be lauded for its cheesesteak.  Hands down, it is one of the best I have ever tasted.  The combination of a seemingly never ending amount of molten cheese and beef juice take it over the top.

    However, as I learned this past Saturday, if you are coming here for the cheesesteak, you are coming for the WRONG sandwich!  The true culinary delicacy at Tony Luke's is the roast Italian pork sandwich.  The pork seasoned with just the right amount of Italian herbs and a hint of red pepper flakes is perfectly juicy and sliced ever so thin.  Complimenting the richness of the pork, the broccoli rabe provides excellent contrasts with its earthy and slightly garlic taste along with its chewy texture.  Bringing all of the flavors in to focus is the sharp provolone cheese.  The sharpness of the cheese cuts through the broccoli rabe perfectly and adds a gooey richness to the whole sandwich.  The bread is certainly up to the challenge.  Not too doughy, it is able to stand up to the moisture of the sandwich while the crust remains crunchy.  The Italian roast pork is truly a great sandwich and example of the whole being grater than the sum of its parts.
