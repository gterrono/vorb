    9/26/2010

    Last night, I had the opportunity to go to Zahav for restaurant week with my fiancée and some friends. Upon immediate entry, I was impressed with the décor... it was very modern without feeling too cold. The main dining area was warm and inviting... very pleasant feeling.

    My fiancée and I were somewhat early so we decided to order a drink from the bar. I was impressed that we were able to order a drink immediately. While there were definitely people eating and drinking at the bar, we were still able to get our drinks without waiting.

    Now for the meal... when we sat down, our server, Ben, seemed pleasant. He told us about the Restaurant week menu and how the
    first course would be served immediately.

    Here's what we ordered off the menu(I'll review each meal next to each listing... this is where it gets really really mixed):

    FYI... We asked our server, Ben, if he could go over what each of the entrees were since none of us were overly familiar with the cuisine.  He rubbed me the wrong way because I saw him rolling his eyes. I noticed that his smile was fake and insincere. I found him condescending when he was describing the food as if we were wasting him. I kept my mouth shut because no one seemed to have noticed.  The rest of the serving staff was very nice though.

    First Course
    Salatim - Selection of four salads: very nice and refreshing... we were able to sample all of the different salads. Some we liked more than others, but everyone agreed, the dish was well made and was a good start

    Hummus-tehina - Served with house-baked laffa: some of the best hummus I've ever had. It was light, but full of flavor and went well with the accompanying bread.

    Second Course
    Crispy Haloumi - Cypriotic sheep's milk cheese, dates, pine nuts: total homerun... my fiancée and I could not get over how good this tasted. I have to say that I was impressed with the boldness of the flavors and how well everything went together.

    Watermelon Salad - Bulgarian feta, black olives, parsley: I enjoyed this, but my fiancée didn't. I found it invigorating actually... the contracts in texture and flavor really seemed to play with each other.
    House-smoked Sable - Challah, fried egg, green garlic: I had previously been told by a co-worker that this would be one of the best dishes I've ever had in my life... period. I thought it was great, but nothing overly special. The flavors were subtle and the fish was nicely cooked though.

    Third Course  
    Fried Kibbe with Tahini - Lamb, Bulgur wheat, pine nuts: This was ok, nothing great... but enjoyable nonetheless  

    The Galil - Eggplant, tomato couscous, asparagus, tehina: And here it is.... The worst dish of the night. We couldn't believe how salty it was... simply inedible. It had to be one of the WORST dishes I've ever had from a restaurant. Seriously... I'd rather have eaten a Sizzli from Wawa. I found it so inedible that I wanted to send it back, but we didn't see Ben until much later in the meal.

    As I understand it, restaurants soak food in brine to give the food a little more saltiness since the salt burns off when cooking. It seems like they didn't cook the food long enough to get the extra salt out. It's a shame because we were really looking forward to this dish.

    Side note, near the end of the meal, I asked Ben if the Galil was supposed to be as salty as it was, he said, "well, it had been soaked in brine... so maybe that was it." He asked if we wanted a replacement portion. I thanked him, but passed.. Without looking me in the eye, he said, "I apologize..." but before he was even done saying those two words, he had already turned away. You can tell that he was really sincere with his apology... nope.

    Fourth Course  
    Cashew Baklava - White chocolate, date syrup: I didn't enjoy this much... I like Greek style more. It tasted like a dog treat.
    Almond Semifreddo - Chocolate crisp: pretty good... nothing great, but it was enjoyable.

    Overall, most of the food was very pleasing. There were dishes we had that were almost inedible and our waiter was a plain old "d-bag"(yes, I said it, but I can't think of any other words to describe him... I didn't even list all of the things he did to make me come to this conclusion. And, even one of the other people at the table mentioned it.). Having a rude waiter can definitely ruin a meal(as it nearly did for me....), but the night was successful.  Even with some of the off notes in some of the food, a great waiter can negate that... changing the night from successful to memorable.

    I'd give the food, an overall 7 out of 10... and I'd give Ben a negative -5 out of 10. It's waiters like Ben that make people not go back to restaurants.

    END SCENE

    9/29/2010 Updated review

    I went back for the famed Mesibah meal which features the somewhat famed lamb shoulder and tried a few other dishes.

    Unlike the previous restaurant week luncheon, this experience at night is different in many ways. Other than our waiter Michael and one of the bus person, the other people doesn't seem as friendly anymore.

    I was very pleased to try all 8 salatim, I do like the dazzling variety and mostly they are very well done. The beets with tahini does get some getting used to because somehow it resembles blue cheese.

    Since 3 people dine this time, we're selecting 5 mezzes which in itself is an amazing amount of food and honestly after talking to my friends, we were already full after the hummus/laffa/salatim. For the mezzes, we choose the fried cauliflower since it was highly recommended for first timers and I don't want to rob my friends of that experience, We also went to a more adventurous route with the grilled lamb tongue, the crispy sweet breads, the watermelon salad which came recommended by fellow yelpers and a moussaka just because I've always loved the Greek equivalent.

    My second time at the cauliflower yield nothing too special. It's well done, fried on the chive yogurt sauce. It's not bad, but it's like eating a plate of fries, it's good but it's not something too, too special. The watermelon salad is not really that special either. It taste like watermelon with blue cheese and fried parsley around. I do love the fried parsley and there is a nice contrast between the watermelon and the blue cheese but there seems to be something missing, maybe it's bulk, a hint of spice or acid. The moussaka comes with a potato foam, the dish is pleasant but small and hard to share between 3 people. Although lamb was in the description, it's hardly felt in the dish. It was decent and I would be happier just to eat it by myself.

    The crispy shortbreads came out gamey but I do enjoy the fried chickpeas. They should probably redesign the dish and feature the chickpeas more and maybe turn the sweetbread into a sauce. The lamb tongue however is a star. They are perfect. Though the buckwheat pilaf can use a lot less cinnamon or better yet, turn it into something more savory with mushrooms or something. (Are mushrooms Israeli?)

    The meal could really stop right there, but I made a special trip here just for the lamb shoulder and god forbid if I don't get a taste. The main dish arrive very soon after and it was quite impressive in size. There's a crispy, salted outside layer and the lamb inside was soft and moist. It's indeed quite amazing. If only I had a bigger appetite. The Egyptian rice is crisp and a bit hardened. Too bad the whole thing is not on the regular menu.

    There were tons of leftovers and I can't help but think that it's a bit of a shame that the majority of the main course didn't get to be eaten fresh. If only there were less appetizers. Three dessert were brought over not long after, a rice pudding, the almond semi-fredo and a brownie. The semi-fredo is everybody favorite, the rice pudding and brownie just seemed to be too filling after our gut busting meal.

    Oh and the bottle of Tempranillo was smooth beyond belief and I'd recommend it to any diners in full confidence.

    All in all, it's was a good and special meal, I might just have my expectations too high after my first too-good experience.

    Just went to Zahav for lunch during Restaurant Week and it was one of the best restaurant experience… Just went to Zahav for lunch during Restaurant Week and it was one of the best restaurant experience I've had. For $20, you get salatim, hummus with laffa, a mezze, an al ha'esh and a dessert, it's simply brilliant.

    For the salatim, we got a small plate of baby okras in harissa, a cucumber tomato salad, a fennel salad and a diced carrot salad. They all have their own unique flavors and they are all delicious. The hummus and house-baked laffa is also brilliant. Fresh baked hot laffa flavored with sesame, cumin and sage with a generous puddle of hummus and olive oil. I can eat just that for days. We asked for extra laffa since there were hummus left and the server ordered us another one free of charge.

    For mezzes, I ordered the fried cauliflower while my dining companion ordered the borekas. The borekas are spinach, cheese and perhaps potatoes wrapped in puff pastry dough. It's flaky, buttery and interesting in taste. My fried cauliflower comes with a dill yogurt sauce and it is very tasty. Just from the appetizers, I was already filled up but we were still expecting entrees and dessert.

    The entrees are small in size, I ordered the Farsi since everyone on here recommended it. The Farsi is three cubes of beef on top of a Persian rice with raisins and fried corn. The beef are tasty and a tad bit gamy which add to the exoticism. My friend, being a vegetarian, ordered the Galil which is eggplant, tomato couscous and tehina. The eggplant comes up charring hot and the couscous are larger than usual which provide a great texture.

    For dessert, I had an almond semifredo with specks of ground almond in it which is excellent in texture, my friend ordered the halvah mousse with fried chickpeas and blueberries. It's refreshing and pleasing.

    Overall, I really liked the place, it's very well decorated. I love windows with big windows that let out great sunlight for the place which is a shame if the restaurant only opens for dinner in regular times.The servers are nice and all very handsome which doesn't hurt their business at all. I will definitely recommend it and come back with my friends.     Read more

    Zahav is an interesting experience, but maybe not quite as amazing as most of these reviews would suggest. I'm no stranger to Middle Eastern cuisine, so that's not why I didn't love this place. It's just that most of the food I tried here was merely okay.

    The hummus and laffa were good. The salatim were okay; the presentation was nice and the flavors were unique and interesting, but very strong/spiced to the point of overpowering: either extremely pickled, salty, sour, or sumac'ed. The appetizers did not impress me much either; the borekas were kind of dry and average and the lamb's tongue was inedibly gamey with a definite barnyard flavor. The haloumi was good but a little over-fried, and our fried cauliflower was burnt. The sweetbreads I didn't particularly care for, but maybe I'm just not a fan of thymus. Also, they weren't offering the raw kibbeh during restaurant week (we went during restaurant week by accident), which was very disappointing. I tried a similar dish in Turkey and it was fantastic. I had hoped Zahav's would compare, but that remains to be seen.

    The steak entree was delicious (the Farsi)  but the portion was minuscule. For dessert I had the halva mousse, which was so overly sweet I could barely force down half of it. I believe my dining companions were not thrilled with their desserts either (baklava burnt, semifreddo melted). The service was great and the atmosphere was classy. But because the portions are so small, I don't think the a la carte menu would have been worth it. Going during restaurant week was barely worth the price.

    I really wanted to like this place and had been wanting to try it for quite some time, but it just didn't impress me. Going once was okay, but I don't think I'll be going back (except maybe to try the raw kibbeh).
