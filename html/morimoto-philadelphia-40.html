    I've been to Morimoto a handful of times and sometimes it has been amazing and sometimes merely good.  The restaurant itself is visually stunning - curving bamboo walls, softly glowing booths and so on.  The phallic lights on the table I find a bit mystifying, what were they thinking?

    Service is inevitably good - the wait staff is willing to explain things and make suggestions or be unobtrusive and efficient if that's what you prefer.

    The raw tuna pizza appetizer is surprising and damn good.  The Kobe beef appetizer is luscious and texturally wonderful.

    Entrees are good although a black cod miso was overcooked  and poorly flavored (I've since had it again and it was quite good - maybe the first time was just a bad night).  Morimoto's version of Bibimbap is quite good but left me wishing for the real thing.  The sushi at Morimoto is probably the best in Philadelphia.  Good fresh fish is a wonderful thing and when it is prepared by people who know what they're doing, it is sublime.

    I came here with a friend of mine when I was 18.

    We were young, naive, and perpetually broke. So when I had come into some money (ok it was like $200 from doing some work for my dad) the first thing I did was call up my friend and say "Pack your bags... We're goin to Morimoto's!"

    So we put on our nicest faux chic clothes, and head over to pseudo olde city. Upon arrival, we were seated quickly. Walking to our table I might have tripped over my jaw, which had dropped to the floor. Being only 18 at the time (and never having been to a Starr restaurant) I was overwhelmed by the atmosphere. Here I was expecting something more... well... traditional... and we end up in what looked like the lobby of a tokyo nightclub.

    The service there was supurb. Everyone there was incredibly knowledgeable about the food they were serving. When our Yosadofu (spell check), tofu made at your table, was finished, a server came over to spoon it out for us, and explained what each of the two sauces were, and how the first one was meant to cleanse your palate and so on.

    Up to this point, it wasn't immediately apparent that this was the restaurant of an Iron Chef. Upon receipt of the entrees however, this changed... instantly.

    I'm not a calimari person, not by a long shot. But when I tried a piece from my friend's salad, I almost passed out from sheer taste bud shock. Also, I was incredibly confused as to what happened to the calimari when I ate it. I was used to chewing my food before swallowing it, but this LITERALLY melted in my mouth.

    The sushi was better than average, but nothing spectacular, with the exception of the plating. You can tell that a lot of effort goes into not only the preparation, but the presentation of the food.

    All in all, Morimoto's was incredible. In fact, to date it is still my favorite restaurant, and I'm looking forward to visiting the newer one in NYC.

    Morimoto Philadelphia - prepare to be challenged (& delighted)!

    Philly in the late  80's - a regular comfort food town (Scrapple, stickybuns, red sauce joints and the ubiquitous cheesesteaks). But time doesn't stand still, so imagine the shock & delight (after a 20 years absence) of passing from a -20 windchill street through the entrance (airlock?) into Morimoto's "lava lamp" interior. After a brief moment of fixation (like other Yelpers) on the lighted booths & their suggestive lamps, we turned our attention to the menu and wine list, and the procession of dishes and drinks that resulted: sakizuke (consisting of poached monkfish livers - think marine foie gras, seasoned tofu "lollipops", pickled vegetables); sashimi sampler with an array of sauces so good we had to repeat; the famed spicy king crab; tempura with blue cheese sauce. The few entrees we shared were a little more mainstream (the searing stone bowl tuna and the crispy barrimundi) but still a planet away from the meat & potatoes of last-century Philadelphia. In general, the wine lists in Japanese-oriented establishments are poor cousins to the cocktail & sake offerings - not so at Morimoto, and we found (and relished) both a Rias Biaxas and a Gavi whose complexity greatly complemented the dishes. For those up for desert (myself unusually included), the chestnut tasting and a glass of Hardy's late-harvest Semillon completed a memorable evening

    WHY MORIMOTO ISN'T WORTH THE $$$!

    First, let me say that the food is solid and the positive reviews of the food are largely accurate.   We are frequent high end diners (largely for work) and Morimoto certainly aspires to top end status.   The strengths are many:

    - A hip dining room that provides an energetic dining environment.
    - Fresh fish and creative dishes that distinguish Morimoto from the average Japanese restaurant
    - Solid wait staff that is knowledgeable and attentive without being "snooty".

    In the end, however, Morimoto is a restaurant that just misses, tempts but ultimately disappoints.   We sampled the omakase menu with two variants--one for frequent returnees and one for newcomers.  Both have a mix of sushi dishes with cooked dishes and in general, the sushi dishes were excellent.  The cooked dishes, ranging from lamb to various fishes, are a step below Nobu's and other top end sushi restaurants from around the country.   Flavors tended to be more plain and somehow lacked the distinctivness to make these entrees memorable.   Dessert was the weakest link in the chain-- a chocolate torte that could have been ordered at any neighborhood cafe.   A disappointing finish to what should have been an opportunity to shine.

    The final disappointment was the service at the front desk.  The staff lost a $150 scarf of one person in our party and the parking valet was AWOL for nearly 15 minutes.  Although the valet is not affiliated with the restaurant, the customer experience is inextricably linked.  Unfortunately, the front desk staff completely disowned responsibility for this which is not appropriate for a restaurant in this price range.   The general manager offered to pay for the scarf which was a nice gesture and appreciated.  But, in combination with the poor valet parking service, left a bad taste in our mouth.  

    Morimoto represents a solid effort in fusion Japanese cuisine.  But, it still ranks a level below Nobu, Sushi at Gari (both in NYC) and is less satisfying than many other restaurants in Philadelphia.   At the $150 per person price range, I'd try elsewhere.

    Well, I figured it was finally time I write a "real" review for my beloved Morimoto & update my original entry of:

    "Ah...Morimoto, how much do I love thee, let me count the ways...
    1. rock shrimp tempura (I've dreamt about it at night, no joke);  2. ishi yaki buri bop;  3. tasty tasty cocktails;  4.  loungey atmosphere and tunes;  5. incredible decor;  6. JUST GET YOUR ASS A RESERVATION ALREADY!!!!  
    Every time I go, its just as amazing as the time before.  Never have I been anything but completely delighted by my experience!"

    So, here goes.

    In my humble opinion, Morimoto has got it pretty much all spot-on right.  The ambiance, the design, the service, the drinks & of course, the food.
    In all my experiences at Morimoto I've never had any complaints or disappointments, in fact each visit seems to be even more enjoyable than the time before.  I always try to order different foods off of the menu from visit to visit so that I can experience everything there is to offer.  The one exception being the rock shrimp tempura appetizer - I order it every time.  Its the best rock shrimp tempura I've ever tried & always prepared to perfection.
    Of all the entree's I've tried each has been fantastic in its own right.  The food is always fresh, beautifully presented & full of interesting flavors & textures.  I can't even chose a favorite, however I can say that my boyfriend's fave is the ishi yaki buri-bop & indeed is is pretty damn wonderful.  Its prepared in front of you at your table in an exceedingly hot stone bowl to cook the egg & help sear the fish (or kobe beef if you like - I've not tried the kobe version as my last visit was the first time I saw it on the menu, but I opted for the omakase).

    Morimoto's omakase is an experience not to be missed.  There are various price ranges fir the omakase - I was told that the varying prices mean the more you pay, the more exotic are the items which you are served.  If there's anything you absolutely do not want to be served, just let your server know & you won't have to worry about finding it on your plate.
    My omakase included seven amazing courses over the matter of about two and a half to three hours.  The portions are by no means huge, but I don't think you'll leave hungry.  I was presented everything from monkfish liver to kobe beef, Thai lobster bisque to chocolate noodles.  Of course we also had a nice serving of sushi which were all delectable pieces of fish.  Amazing, amazing, amazing.

    I have always found the staff to be very friendly, accommodating & helpful.  Generous, too, at least in the case of my New Years Eve trip there when the complimentary champagne just kept on pouring after midnight!  ;)

    I would definitely recommend arriving before your reservation time so that you can enjoy some cocktails & conversation in their funky bar overlooking the dining area.  I love the sake cocktails!  I'm honestly not a huge fan of sake, but you'll find some very yummy mixtures here.  My current Morimoto cocktail fave however is the MC Squared (champagne, Midori & lime juice)!  

    I've not yet tried lunch at Morimoto but have plans to in the very near future (which I'm sure I'll make an updated Yelp about, natch).  If its half as tasty & enjoyable as my dinner experiences I'll be very pleased indeed.
