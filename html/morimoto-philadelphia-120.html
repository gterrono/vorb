    I ate here twice within one week period of time.  The reason being: I was on company's expense account.  Score!  Gotta milk that baby... ;)

    Honestly I expected a lot from Morimoto.  But they didn't quite deliver and for that they don't deserve 5 stars.  I tried: ichi-yaki-bap, maguro sushi, soft shell crab roll, and morimoto ramen.  The ichi-yaki-bap was not bad, but I was really missing the hot pepper paste common on bibimbaps.  Boo!

    The nigiri sushi and maki are pretty good.  I do have to give props for the quality of sushi here.  The fish is fresh and even the rice is polished in house, how crazy is that?!  The soft shell crab maki was delicious but I think the chef didn't pack it tight enough because it kept crumbling on me.  Like a doomed relationship, it was hard to keep it together...

    The morimoto ramen was not too bad.  I think it would make a perfect meal for anyone having a bout of cold or flu.  It's pretty plain: just noodles, chunks of chicken, and a few shreds of scallions.  The broth was flavorful and there's a thin layer of fat floating on top.  I think this is the traditional ramen trademark.  But really, it didn't wow me.  I've had better ramen in the Bay Area.  And when I think of ramen I really think of big steaming bowls of noodles.  This was too chi-chi-frou-frou.

    Bottom line is: this is a great place to see and be seen.  Not bad either if you want to entertain business clients.  But overall, I think it's too overpriced for the food they serve.

    2 check-ins

    Saturday was the second time we've been to Morimoto. The first time it was brand new and we were just getting into eating at fancy restaurants so we stuck with a pretty uninspiring menu. But, that was then, and this is now...

    Morimoto is a cool looking place. From the all the white exterior to the pod like under the water interior, you get a sense you're in a different kind of a restaurant. We arrived early and went upstairs to the bar.  It's a very tiny bar with low tables and a frameless window that you can look down at the people below.  You can order sushi and regular food up there so if you don't have a reservation and still want to try it, make your way upstairs.  Morimoto has his own beer line. Who knew? I ordered the Hazelnut Ale. It was a dark beer with hints of hazelnut and a malty aftertaste. Very enjoyable!

    We didn't wait that long before getting seated. There were four of us and we decided to just order lots of stuff instead of sticking to one entrée per person.
    Here's the breakdown of our dinner (in the order it arrived):

    Chef's selection of sushi and sashimi platter - we went with the middle of the line chef's sushi package for $50. It was a lot sashimi and 6 tuna rolls. We liked the rolls so much that we wanted to try some salmon rolls. They were good, but not as good as the tuna ones.  Also, the most interesting thing on the plate was pound cake. Pound cake made with shrimp paste. Very interesting, but good too.

    Soba Carbonara - Soba noodles with Edamame, bacon, and cream sauce.  EXCELLENT. Probably one of the best things we ate all night long.

    The surf and turf -This was the best sounding thing on the entire menu. Kobe beef, black pepper and sesame crusted main lobster with brown butter for $49. It was also the most disappointing thing we got. The meat was tough to cut with the spork like fork. The lobster was just a big miss.  It 's a sin not to finish kobe beef so it was all eaten, but still...

    Duck Duck Duck - this is one of the new items on the menu. I am not a big fan of duck so this dish really had to be amazing to win me over. It wasn't. I know that duck is served a little under done, but it seemed really on the rare side. (or maybe i had hit my raw limit)  It was served with risotto topped with a sunny side up duck egg. The egg was supposed to be broken over the risotto, the egg yolk then flavoring the risotto.  I don't even think we ate half of this dish.

    Wasabi Fried Rice - I was the only person that wanted to order this as a side, but everyone loved it. It tied with the carbonara for being the best dish.

    I should end with saying that our waitress, Alexi, was very nice. I think she was one of the best servers we've had in a very long time. To sum up, Morimoto is worth going, but it's easy to get lost in all the hype.

    Listed in The Streets of Philadelphia

    I absolutely loved our meal at Morimoto. For a special occasion, this place can't be beat. Check out the photos and you'll get an idea of what it's like.

    Definitely get the omakase, or chef's choice tasting menu. Some of the courses may seem small, but after seven of them, you'll be very satisfied. The three levels of omakase, $80, $100, and $120, all have the same quantity of food, but the ingredients are different. The very adventurous will enjoy the $120 menu. We decided to go middle-of-the-road with the $100 menu.

    Since I have some dietary restrictions (no dairy or red meat), four of my course were the same as BF's and three were different. We each had three different fish dishes to start with, then for the entree dishes, he had halibut with a creamy sauce, while I had black cod that was absolutely delicious. When he had lamb chops, I had lobster! It was so exciting to be presented with a surprise dish and hear the description.

    We both were served a sushi sampler, and then for dessert I had the best sorbet I've ever had in my life. It was a trio of chocolate, pear, and coconut. I wish I could buy sorbet like this in the market.

    The quality of the ingredients, the complex flavors, and the beautiful presentation definitely made this meal worth $100. It's a splurge, but if you're only going to have one fancy meal while in Philadelphia, Morimoto is an excellent choice.

    My husband got a GC to any Starr Restaurant so we decided to try this one. We arrived a little early for our reservations so went upstairs for a drink while we waited. I'm not entirely sure why we had to wait, as there were a dozen or so tables with no one at them (and when we left, those same tables were empty), but it did give us an opportunity to check out the little bar. The vibe was like a space station that wasn't trying to look like a space station (unlike Pod). I got a delicious lychee martini and my husband a mandarin ginger (non-alcoholic) that tasted more like an orange-ade.

    Shortly after, we were seated at a table close to the front door. Given the dozen empty tables further in, it would have been better not to be so close to the exit, but we could have spoken up, so I'll take some responsibility for that. We ordered edamame as an app for me, and rock shrimp tempura for my husband--both delicious (though it is hard to make edamame poorly).

    As a vegetarian, I noticed that options at Starr restaurants in general are limited. I asked the waitress for suggestions, and she pointed me to ishi yaki bop, but with tofu. While it is not on the menu, her quick response led me to believe it a common substitute (and it was a named dish on the receipt). The sauce was good--not just a soy/vinegar mix I often get at steakhouses and not oily; tofu adequate though nothing special. Overall, I would order this again, particularly given the price ($16, cheap when compared to other menu items--though a dish of mostly rice and tofu really wouldn't justify a high price anyway, no matter how creative the sauce). My husband ordered the NY strip steak medium rare and was pleased with the seasoning.

    The dress code listed here--dressy--is a bit misleading. While certainly people were dressed nicely, there were plenty in jeans with a nice top as well. But that seems a limit of Yelp more than the voters--options are "casual" or "dressy" and really, it's in between.

    The waitress was pleasant, never once off-putting (as one might be worried about if not a regular visitor of upscale restaurants). The only thing I would change in the future is to request a better table.

    Listed in Five Star Favorites, International Treasures, Mass Outings, Sushi Mania

    Morimoto is worthy of the hype, even 8 years after its much-celebrated opening. That's practically forever in restaurant world, especially when you're talking about celeb-driven entities where style is more prized than substance. Nope, the sushi is still to die for here, as is the attentive service (big ups to John, our friendly waiter) and the minimalistic decor that reminds me of being on a Nordic submarine surrounded by penises. (Yes, you read that right. Take a look at the centerpieces on each table and you'll understand what I mean).

    The boy and I went on a recent Saturday night, having secured a last-minute reservation on Open Table for 7:45 (score!). We had actually been a few times in the past few years but moved on to other places in the city for our dining enjoyment - how do you think I write so many reviews? But since my birthday weekend was in full swing, I wanted to go to a lovely place where I could eat well without busting out of my dress. I chose Morimoto.

    It was buzzing but not packed, lively but not over the top. We were seated promptly and informed (by the affable John) that fugu was on the menu. Since we were celebrating the accomplishment of me making it through another year, it only seemed fitting that we risk our lives by eating a potentially deadly fish. It was delicious, and reminded me of a whisper or a ghost. Try it if you can.

    We went straight up sashimi / sushi in our choices and weren't disappointed. For dessert I had the chocolate pot de creme, and rejoiced in the marshmellowy texture of the creme, chocolate and caramelized bananas.

    I know it's my business to buzz around town, trying out different places. But Morimoto has proven itself to be reliable, and that goes a long way in a town built on fickle tastebuds. They've secured a place on my favorites list for sure.

    I missed this restaurant as I walked right by it and walked down a block further.  The front of the restaurant is a plain white, with a small hole in the wall which bears the name "morimoto".  We walked through the neon green doors and were immediately fascinated.  The restaurant had that neat, futuristic, and cozy feel.  Not many were dining yet since the restaurant just opened about a minute or two prior to our arrival.

    While waiting for our table to be prepared the hostess suggested we go up to the lounge for a drink.  The lounge was located on the 2nd floor right next to the front entrance of the restaurant.  There was an open window in the lounge which allowed you to look over the entire restaurant.  It was a pretty awesome view.  The drink menu had wine, sake, Japanese/Asian beers, and specialty house cocktails.  I started off with a 24oz. Morimoto/Rogue Soba Ale and my girlfriend ordered a Lychee Cosmopolitan.  During dinner I ended up ordering a Morimoto/Rogue Hazelnut Ale.

    After 5 - 10 minutes, we were seated at one of the 2-person tables against the wall.  At first glimpse it didn't look like these chairs were going to be comfortable, but they definitely were.  The table was kind of an opaque colored glass with a futuristic lightpiece fixed into the middle of the table.  The waitress was a very friendly, helpful woman.  She knew a lot about the restaurant and had worked there for about 3 years already.  There was a bunch to explain and they even had a special menu for the weekend we went since it was the weekend of the Philly Flower Show.  They had a 4-5 course menu consisting of dishes which had "flower" presentations and such.  We were tempted to get that, but we couldn't due our longing to try the Omakase we've heard so much about.

    So the Omakase is basically a chef's tasting menu.  The price breakdown on the menu says priced at $80, $100, $120 and up.  I then asked the waitress what does it mean....and up?  How expensive can this possibly get?  She said that they sometimes have $200 Omakases based on the seasonal ingredients they have.  So you'll still be getting a good amount of food regardless of which level you get, but the quality/exoticness of the ingredients are higher as you pay higher.  The Omakase starts off with cold dishes, then has a couple hot dishes before finishing with a dessert.  We finally decided to go with the $120 Omakase.  Normally if you've never had the Omakase before, they give you a starter menu.  This is so that if you're a frequent "Omakase'er" you won't get the same thing.  Unfortunately some people like me couldn't even afford to go to this place that often, so I asked if there was any way we could both get a different dish each time, which she asked the chef and we got the ok.

    The Omakase started off with a Shrimp/Prawn Tartare which was roe topped and had some sauce in the plate.  The second course was a 3-oyster dish and each oyster had a different topping.  The third course was a "toro-tilla" which was slices of toro and I think a basil leaf inside a tortilla and spicy oil drizzled over.  My fourth course was a yellowtail sashimi with a salad.  Between the fourth and fifth course they give you this "sake-soda" which is meant to clear your palate before you take on the "hot" dishes.  The fifth course was a pan seared scallop topped with foie gras and a blood orange slice.  The sixth course was slices of Kobe beef with mushrooms and scallion pancakes.  The seventh course was a sushi roll of mushrooms and (can't remember ingredient), both were topped with different things (one roe, not sure the other).  Our final dessert course was a flourless chocolate cake topped with this preserved strawberry & other fruit custard which was topped with a pink champagne gelatin.  (I have posted pictures of all the dishes)

    All the dishes and drinks were fantastic and I felt as if I were in a food heaven as I ate.  The dinner took about 3 hours, so if you do decide to do this I would come early and make sure you make a reservation as this place seemed to crowd up as the night went on.

    For a steep price tag, it was totally worth every penny and I would definitely come here again.  Definitely check this place out if you want a neat and fun experience while dining on some great food.

Meet the Business Owner

Stephen S. Business Owner

Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District. STARR Restaurants expanded to Atlantic City, NJ, with Buddakan and The Continental located in the Pier Shops at Caesars in 2006, and to Florida with Steak 954 at the W Hotel Fort Lauderdale in 2009, and Makoto in the Bal Harbour Shops in 2011. For more information, please visit www.starr-restaurant.com.
