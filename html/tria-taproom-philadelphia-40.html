    Although I'm happy that the Taproom has joined my block, I just thought the food was simply OK when I tried it a few nights ago.

    Beer and wine selection - can't go wrong. I haven't had a complaint about it, except I can't decide if I'm a fan of the cheesy iPad thing yet or not.

    Food wise, me and my friend got the fried oyster starter and then the burrata flatbread and I just felt as if everything was OK. The oysters were a little bit over fried then served on brioche with a tiny amount of the apple slaw - just too much bread and breading in my opinion. The burrata was good, minus the fact that there was barely any cheese and a massive amount of spinach.     I've been here a couple times now and I live within walking distance so I'll be back a bunch more, and I'm definitely still open minded reading previous reviews.

    I'm probably one of the three Philadelphians who has never been to Tria (yeah, I know) but I DID get a chance to check out Tria Taproom a few Saturdays ago.  My overall impression was that it is a really cool spot to hang out, relax, have some snacks, and drink some good beer.

    We had a reservation but two of us (of a party of five) arrived 15 minutes early.  They were able to seat us within a few minutes and didn't give us the whole "we can't seat you until your entire party is here" nonsense... it was a +1 for service at that point.  On that note, throughout the night, service was excellent.

    Like many others have mentioned, all the beers are listed on an iPad, which gives a description and the percentage of availability.  I thought this would be cooler than it actually was.  I was feeling unadventurous and went with just a Allagash Witbier-- heavy winter beers aren't really my thing--- but the others in my group enjoyed their selections.

    As for the food, there are a number of small plates, flatbreads, and cheeses.  This is definitely more of a "snack" place rather than a "meal" place.  We tried a sausage flatbread and some cheeses (with accompanying honeys/jams/etc), all of which were very tasty.

    The prices are on the higher side, but this is Rittenhouse.  This wouldn't be an every night of the week destination for me, but I think it is a perfect location for a small gathering of friends that want to enjoy some quality beverages with some quality snacks

    i am a way bigger fan of tria taproom than i am of the original tria.

    for starters, thanks to this place, i now know the concept of draft wines that are stored in kegs and poured on taps.

    Listed in My Own Private 2014 Challenge, Beertopia, Brotherly Love

    For those who have read my many beer-centric reviews, you know it's hard for me to be stumped on a type of beer I haven't heard of, or be thoroughly impressed by a beer list, even at a taproom. Tria Taproom did both, and had me bowing down in a Wayne's World like "I'm not worthy" respect for their selection of unique foreign and domestic brews. Unlike other places that use the "Taproom" moniker, Tria takes the "taproom" part of their name seriously. (Perhaps a bit too seriously for some. As their website states "there's not a bottle in the house".) Wine, cider, craft beer, craft soda all pour from their roughly 30 taps. They have a small menu of snacks and flatbreads as well, but what pours from the taps are the real stars of Tria Taproom.

    The wife and I landed here after we finished at the Mutter Museum and decided to put the kibosh on the typical tourist stuff and just relax and have a few drinks to wind up our last afternoon in Philly. Once again, the handy dandy Yelp app (and a search for craft beer, of course!) pointed me to Tria. They had a good crowd for mid-afternoon on a Saturday, but we found a spot at the bar where we camped. The staff is the usual hipster crowd you'd expect for a wine/craft beer/cheese bar, but with an added friendliness and helpfulness in beer selection that exceeded my expectations.

    My favorite beer styles are the sours, so I naturally started with the Bayerischer Bahnhof Berliner Weisse with Rye Malt. I had never seen a Berliner made with rye before, the first of a few "firsts" for me at Tria. Our awesome bartender (Tessa) and I began to discuss various sour beer styles, and she sold me on some other must-try selections.

    The next brew I tried was my introduction to the Lichtenhainer Weiss -  a style of beer that, like Gose, was nearly extinct just a decade ago but has seen a revival as sour beers grow in popularity. Similar to a Berliner Weisse, and often miscategorized as one, it has the same wheat base and tartness of a Berliner, but the high level of smoke malt used adds a smokiness to the brew. So it's basically a combination of my two favorite beer styles - sour beers and smoke beers. The one that Tria offered - Freigeist Abraxxas - was like drinking liquid heaven.

    Next was the Brett Peat Daydream from the Italian brewery Ducato. This is a blend of three different beers - a peated Barley Wine, a smoked Marzen with a part aged in Scotch barrels, and an open-fermented (Brett) ale. The combination would fall more under the "Wild Ale" classification, and was a great mix of yeasty and smoky notes, with the sourness a bit more subdued than the Berliner or Lichtenhainer.

    Then I tried the KelSo Fuku - a combination of a kolsch and smoke beer with a pinch of salt and sour malt added (like in a gose). It's by the Brooklyn brewery KelSo, and was brewed for David Chang's Momofuku Restaurant Groups. It's an American attempt at a Lichtenhainer, but the smoke taste is more forward, with the sour taking a backseat.

    The beer progression went nicely from sour to smoky, and there are very few places where you'd even have a chance to find these beers. Meanwhile, my wife enjoyed a draft Sprecher root beer and the very strong and tasty Crabby Granny cider from Frecon Farms. I was planning to wrap up, but then overheard that they had just added Stiegl Grapefruit Radler on tap. A 50/50 mixture of German lager and sparkling grapefruit soda.

    With a selection like this, it's easy to see how food can get lost in the shuffle. But with all of this alcohol, we did need to balance it with some num nums. It seemed like ordering a cheese plate was a trend of our vacation, so we decided to keep the trend. Like with their alcohol, Tria's menu describes the different cheese options well, and offered good portions of three cheeses for $12.

    I left Tria Taproom a bit wobbly and thoroughly impressed with their beer selection. They are definitely in my Top 5 for best overall beer selection of any place I have been to.
