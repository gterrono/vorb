    Listed in philly phaves, yellow fever, cream of the crop (asian edition)

    3.5 stars.

    as a self-proclaimed ramen aficionado, it's been a source of great joy to see that philly has finally caught onto the ramen craze, with ramen joints popping up left and right in the past year or two.

    as a disclaimer, i haven't tried all the different ramen places in the city, but i've tried enough to know that ramen here used to be seriously lacking, in both quality and quantity. that being said, i came here with hopes of being impressed, based on all the good things i had heard.

    i ordered the terakawa ramen, because when evaluating a ramen shop on the quality of its ramen, always go with their signature dish because it's the one that's supposed to best represent their ramen philosophy. normally, i usually choose something like the tan tan ramen or the spicy ramen (spicy miso base and spicy pork broth base, respectively) because i'm a sucker for anything spicy -- but i also know that's a hard dish to make right, and i wanted to give terakawa a proper initial review.

    the GOOD:
    1. a push towards customization of your ramen, including firmness and type of noodles, as well as how salty you want your broth. this is great because everyone's taste buds differ, and you can't make a standard ramen that will please everybody.
    2. the default noodles are perfectly al dente, just the way i like them.
    3. portion sizes are huge with a good variety of ingredients. soft-boiled egg and bamboo shoots ftw! now add some seaweed please :)
    4. prices are very affordable -- ie. $9-10. this is the price i was used to paying for ramen back in california before i got introduced to the ridiculousness that is ippudo in nyc. if you've ever been, you know what i'm talking about.
    5. great service!
    6. the ambiance -- love the decor and the fact that they try to keep to the japanese traditions even though the restaurant is clearly chinese-owned.

    the BAD:
    1. the broth is overly salty, but lacks any real depth of flavor. a classic pork bone broth should be very rich in flavor and almost have a texture to it. this broth tastes like it's basically seasoned with salt.
    2. the chashu was too lean and dry. sure, it's "healthier." but real ramen chashu should be cut thicker, with more fat to add flavor and tenderness.

    while it seems like this review is overly positive, the reason i'm giving terakawa 3.5 stars is because the broth makes or breaks a ramen shop. it doesn't matter how good your noodles and ingredients and portion sizes and prices and service are. if the broth is crap, the ramen can only be so good.

    so, in the philly ramen scene, terakawa deserves 4 stars, because it's easily the best ramen i've had thus far in this city. this is compared to nom nom ramen, as well as a smattering of other restaurants that claim to serve ramen (yakitori boy, tampopo, doma, sampan, etc).

    on an "objective" ramen standard, terakawa is an average 3 stars. great for a filling and cheap meal if you're not too picky. i'll most definitely return to try their other ramen dishes in the future.

    RAMEN = LUCKY CHARMS FOR ADULTS

    When I was a kid, and braver and dumber than I am now, I conducted a series of experiments with lucky charms:

    1) I ate all of the bread pieces separately, then I ate all the marshmallows at once.  Result: This was strangely underwhelming.  Like alcohol, the joy of a little curdles as volume is added.

    2) I attempted to find the ideal cereal marshmallow ratio, and ideal milk, experimenting with different spoon sizes, whole, 2%, and skim.  Result: I used skim milk with a table spoon, and a 5:3 cereal : marshmallow ratio. This was ideal for taste, but I realized that part of the fun is in the not knowing.

    3) I decided I would eat the entire bowl without looking down until I neared the end.  I would let the vagaries of fate decide the cereal/marshmallow ratio.  Result: Excellent.  Thought I eventually tired of skim milk, super sweet cereal, and Saved by the Bell (but never Thundercats, never).

    Sitting down to the first bowl of non-instant ramen in my life (the soy chicken one, I can't eat pork, the Achilles heel of this reviewer, I feel the flaw humanizes me), I couldn't help but think that the pleasurable existential concerns that arise: When do I eat the egg?  What's that thing with a spiral*?  What are the noodles made of?

    And most importantly:

    When do I eat the meat?

    These questions are at the heart of ramen's cross cultural appeal.  A quick google search revealed that I was right, and yielded the following tips:

    Start eating the ramen as soon as it arrives.  Ramen waits for no man.  Men wait for ramen.  

    Taste the broth first!  You want to taste it before it melds with the other ingredients like a true believer.  

    Take the meat, and push it down into the soup, so it becomes tender and keeps adding to the flavor of the broth.  

    The spoon is for b#tches.  Fold your noodles in half, and then slurp them, so you get plenty of air in between.  Bizarre for Americans, this is the height of Japanese dining etiquette.

    Now chomp down the rest.  Done "correctly," this shouldn't take more than 10 minutes.

    I loved every bite, and I realized that it's also healthy, delicious, and has paleo options (they can replace the noodles with vegetables if you ask).  I didn't do this, I wanted my first time to be on the house's terms.  And it's the other side of sushi, it's rich and refined, but it's simple working class fare.  If this was 1995 I'd call it "Zen in a bowl."

    But even in 2013, this isn't the passive "be here now" Zen that you can read when you're drowsy and can't get to bed.  There's fire here, real passion, spice, dedication, and-yes-there is curry.  Karaagi chicken, with it's lightly breaded heat, and accompanying spicy mayo, the curry, and the open faced sandwiches are good enough rising sun soul food offerings to temporarily steal the spotlight from the ramen.

    But in the end, there's just something about the hearty "Irasshai!" when you enter, the wafting scent of meat, noodles, broth, veggies, and eggs, that you will crave again and again.  New York quality ramen for Philly C-Town prices.

    Go on in and tell Max I said hi, and ask him if he can still break dance ;)

    PS A block away from Tea Do and Hop Sing...

    Finally there is a reason to go to Chinatown for ramen soup! Like New York, some of the best ramen soup restaurants are scattered throughout Philly. Unlike New York, there is no real "Japantown" for all things Japanese. We have our areas of Korean and Vietnamese concentration, but Japanese people appear to be underrepresented in downtown Philly. Why that is is a mystery to me considering all of the sushi restaurants that exist. I know, I know, many sushi joints are owned by Chinese and Koreans who are cashing in on the sushi craze. The uninitiated will eat anything drizzled with spicy mayo on it. How authentic are ramen soup joints? Aren't they also owned by Chinese and Koreans? Maybe. Even so, some of them are making amazing food. Look at Terakawa. They claim "our chef's recipes originate from the Kumamoto region of Japan. The broth is simmered for two days. Due to the extensive cooking period, our broth has a deep, well developed flavor that can only be described by experiencing it." Their noodles are handmade and cooked al dente. I ordered an appetizer of Takoyaki, also known as octopus balls. These takoyaki are lightly fried Japanese mountain-potato balls filled with octopus. They're topped with Japanese barbeque sauce, Japanese mayo, and shaved fish. The dish was a delight to my senses. The signature Terakawa Ramen is filled with Heritage Berkshire Pork bone soup topped with roast pork, bamboo shoots, kikurage mushrooms, red ginger, chopped scallions, and a seasoned boiled egg. The broth is a bit saltier than Nom Nom and Ramen Bar, but the quality in the ingredients and preparation is all there. You also get a lot more food for your money at Terakawa. Budget conscious people will love this fact.

    Mayu ramen - When I ordered this, both the chef & server acted surprised and asked me if I've ever had ramen before, and told me what the broth was made of and that its the same broth that's in the Terakawa signature ramen but a step above it. Come on now, what Asian do you know who hasn't eaten ramen? I know they meant well. I just said yes and that I was sure that's what I wanted. Ok, the broth is made from a pork bone base so the color is a milky brown but they also add in mayu which they tout as a dark roasted leek & crushed garlic oil mixture, which adds a layer of black oil seasoning that floats at the top. The broth is mild in flavor and is very rich and filling. Some reviewers mistake it as bland but I don't think it's meant to be an overpowering flavor. The toppings of char siu, red ginger, mushrooms, scallions, and egg all compliment the broth nicely. I've never had anything like this so I'm glad I tried it. It's definitely not for the average ramen eater - you've got to be a fan.

    Tan tan ramen - My partner added a hard boiled egg to his dish. I tasted the broth only. It's a bit sweet for a tan tan base and not spicy. I've had a better version at Goma Tei in Honolulu which is spicier.

    SERVICE:
    Walked in at 3pm on a Monday afternoon and got seated immediately as the place was empty. Our female server was very friendly but disappeared after our order was taken. Someone else brought over our food. She didn't check on us or refill our waters. The chef was very engaging and cared about what his customers were eating as he consulted with us while ordering.

    OVERALL:
    Nice & clean ramen shop in Philly's Chinatown with great food and average prices. Definitely a come back spot!
