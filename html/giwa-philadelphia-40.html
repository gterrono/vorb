    Listed in Sansom St. Superstars

    You know those days when it's basically a blizzard, and you're walking around trying to find a place that's open for lunch because everything's closed due to "weather" ?

    Well, Giwa was open.

    I opened the door, smelled frying rice, spiciness and kimchi and knew I was in the right place. A few other brave souls wandered in with the same idea although the guys behind the counter seemed surprised anybody came in at all. I figured it was a good sign that people were willing to slog through the slush to get there.

    The menu is pretty simple, but when you're good at something, you might as well stick with it!

    The dol sot bibim bob is not to be missed. Imagine a huge, sizzling, steaming stone bowl filled with veggies, mushrooms, rice and whatever meat you want. Hot sauce, kimchi and beans on the side. Stir up the goodies with the handy extra long handled spoon and dig in. It'll take a while to get to the bottom!

    I was in Korean food heaven. The stone bowl reminded me of Pusan but without the smell of steamed silkworms from the street vendors. It's a little more than I'm used to spending for lunch, but for the amount of food you get, it's a pretty good bang for your buck.

    You're definitely not going to find cheap Korean food in this part of town, but Giwa offers a much better value than its more expensive nearby competitor Miga. (I haven't been to Miran yet.)

    The bulgogi I had here was more succulent than the bulgogi at Miga, and the dol sot (hot stone) bibimbap was actually in a bowl that was, well, REALLY HOT. And made a lot of crackly fried rice on the sides. Normally I don't/can't finish dol sot bibimbap in a single sitting, but this was so tasty that I gobbled it down entirely. I wish the sauce had been spicier -- MIGA wins on this point -- but the beef and vegetables were good enough with the sauce that I forgive them.

    There's no waiter service - you order at a counter, pay, and then grab your tray of food after it's done cooking. But despite the lack of waiter service, it's nicely decorated and pleasant to eat in -- it's not just a place for take out.

    Note that there's a $15 credit card minimum. Most dishes are between $10-$13.

When I got back to my office with my order I found many of the elements of my bibimbap to be individually packaged - rice in one container, spicy red sauce, kimchi, seaweed strips (in a cute tiny foil package), and cucumber in their own little packages, and the main bowl full of shredded veggies, tofu, greens, sesame seeds, carrots, red pepper strips, and maybe one or two assorted goodies. You may argue that all of these little containers are doing nothing to reduce the size of my carbon foot print, but I appreciated that they ensured freshness after my 1 block walk back to my office.

    The veggies were crisp and fresh and the red sauce was spicy but not distracting. They use an interesting purpley-brown brown rice that was especially tasty and I inhaled the bowl in less than 20 minutes. In addition to bibimbap they have a lot of other good stuff, including the sizzling pancake of crispy rice and kimchi that I saw one of the many men wearing blue button-up shirts eating lunch there order. It was crowded, but the guys working are moving fast and it was quite pleasant sitting at the counter on a stool. I'll add this to my list of lunchtime go-tos and maybe next time I'll eat in and get a dolsot bibimbap so I can singe my fingertips off on the stone bowl which someone of my level of brilliance is bound to do.

    This is a great place to come for fresh, delicious, healthy Korean take-out. If you choose to eat in, the atmosphere is pleasant and relaxed, and there are about eight nice, wooden two-person tables. You will still have to order from the counter and do your own bussing, though.

    So why only three stars?  
    1. Price. The vegetarian bibimbap (no tofu added, just the vegetable base) with brown rice and a can of soda came to $15.     If you can afford a long wait and a high-ish price point, Giwa is an excellent choice.

    Hmmm, I hate to be a party pooper but this place is pretty "Americanized" so for those looking for… Hmmm, I hate to be a party pooper but this place is pretty "Americanized" so for those looking for authentic Korean food, this is not the party to crash.  The place is nice and cute, clean and friendly.  From the outside, I always thought the place was going to be pretty authentic (they've got a limited menu of mostly bibimbaps or rice dishes so they should be good at those, no?) but some Korean classmates had told me that this place was just ok.  Dolsot bibimbap for $12 is a reasonable price for a place in the city and they give you 2 types of kimchi.  The radish kimchi with carrots pickled in sugar and vinegar is fine, but the regular baechoo (cabbage) kimchi is TERRIBLE.  This thing has been brushed with some red peppers and that's it.  If you research kimchi or seen your mother or grandmother make it from scratch, there are a ton of ingredients and a very specialized process involved in creating what is authentic kimchi.  But bad/fake kimchi is the least of my worries.  

    Dolsot or regular bibimbap should always come with a raw egg or fried egg, respectively, on top.  This is essential because in the dolsot, the egg slowly cooks in the hot stone bowl, adding a creaminess and an extra egg flavor to the dish.  Also, in all my dolsot adventures, I've never seen raw vegetables in my dolsot (especially not alfalfa sprouts or red cabbage, both which are super bitter raw!!!).  One of the pain-in-the-butt things about making bibimbap at home is that each ingredient is marinated and prepared separately before neatly placed in a circle atop the bed of freshly prepared rice--so that's the sauteed zucchinis, carrots, mung bean sprouts, go so lee, beef, etc.  If you are eating a seasonal bibimbap, then the summer varieties will usually have some raw ingredients but as you probably know, cooked veggies and raw veggies taste VERY different.  So the Giwa dolsot definitely leaves you with a "clean" feeling, but this is not what dolsot tastes like.  Not that the authentic dolsot leaves you with a dirty or greasy feeling, it's just a lot more flavorful and doesn't leave you feeling like you ate a bowl of raw veggies covered in gochujang with some noo lung ji (the crispy rice that fries at the bottom of the dolsot).  Needless to say, my bf enjoyed it since he was looking for something light for lunch!

    We had the soondoobu chigae as well which was just ok.  It was spicy and piping hot but again, no raw egg, and there were a few pieces of fatty bulgogi (that was flavored well but authentic soondoobu does not have marinated bulgogi in it--go to BCD House to get you some real soondoobu chigaes), and a decent serving of soft tofu.  I looked over at some of the other customers and dolsot is definitely a favorite and some of the rice dishes seemed popular but kinda bland looking.  I have to say though, this place was filled with 50% Koreans and 50% Caucasians.  The owner and cook is Korean and he is extremely friendly and attentive (e.g., asking us how our meal was).  

    If I want a light lunch, I would come back here.  I wouldn't come back to have "KoreanKorean" food though.     Read more
