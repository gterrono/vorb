    First to Review

    After getting four feet of snow in four days, I really wanted to head out of the house for lunch. I was going to pop over to King of Tandoor, but saw this place was open instead, and I have been craving sushi for awhile and decided to pop in.

    The inside is nice, albeit small. There are only about three dozen seats in this BYOB. Although it does not seem as cozy as Umai Umai, I still enjoyed the atmosphere.

    I had two basic rolls, tuna and marinated daikon. Both rolls were very good with high quality tuna in the one. They also deviated from the norm by having tuna rolls with more than the little dot of pink surrounded by a sea of rice - the amount of tuna in the roll was a bit larger than most places, which I liked. The daikon roll was crispy and delicious as well. Thankfully, they had some potent wasabi, as some places have some that are about as hot and flavorful as a wad of play-doh.

    The chef also brought over a petite portion of their bean sprout salad tossed in a little sesame oil that was very refreshing. That dish tasted like it will be a summertime staple for me come July, although the flavor of the sesame made it work in the cold winter season as well.

    All in all, a nice place. It was the first day and there were some hiccups, but all of which were pretty minor and will likely be ironed out shortly.

    In a sea full of (albeit very good) American bar food in this neighborhood (see, e.g., Kite & Key, St. Stephen's Green, Rembrandt, Jack's, London Grill), Domo Sushi offers a refreshing change of pace.
    Their menu features a bit more traditional fare than Umai Umai.  I thought the pork belly buns were the best I've eaten in the city, the bun nicely fluffy and the pork not too overtly fatty.  The rock shrimp tempura was lightly battered and flavorful, and the dashi in the udon soup was excellent.  As the previous reviewer noted, their sushi rolls are generous with tuna, and tasted very fresh and clean.  The combo of hamachi and kiwi in one roll was surprisingly good.
    The service was also excellent.  The table next to us brought in a six pack of beer, and were treated to complementary shots of sake.  They offer 15 percent off the check if you pay in cash.
    All in all, a delightful surprise and a total score for the neighborhood.  I will definitely be back.

    I went here last night after eagerly anticipating the opening of a sushi place on what is becoming a fantastic restaurant block.  (Sabrina's, Kite and Key, and King of Tandoor are in good company.)  

    I started off with a complimentary seaweed salad (which was solid) and a complimentary chardonnay (!!!).  I'm a sucker for anything complimentary, so high marks all around.

    As an appetizer we split the dragon roll, which had very tasty eel and avocado.  The only problem was that the rice wasn't sticky enough and the pieces were big, which meant that it all fell apart when picked up with chopsticks.  It would have been nice to eat the roll as it's meant to be eaten and without spilling half of the rice on the table.  This seems like something that can be easily fixed though.

    I had the chicken kushiyacki for my entree because it was freezing outside and I wanted something warm to eat.  The chicken was very tender and delicious.  The noodles were good as well but I wish they had been cooked in a little less oil.  Although I didn't try it, the bibimbap looked really good and was tasty although lacking some of the extra toppings you can get at other Korean restaurants.

    Overall, I really liked this place.  I actually like it more than Shiroi Hana because it's got a quirkier, more laid back atmosphere and it's in my neighborhood.  Also, I felt the prices were more reasonable.  This seems like a great lunch place.  I will definitely be back!

    Doma claims to serve Japanese and Korean cuisine, but its menu is certainly Japanese-heavy.  Nevertheless, it was an oddly cold spring evening and I was craving dolsot bibimbap (Korean dish of rice served on a hot stone pot to create the requisite crunchy cooked rice feel).  I also figured that since it was my first time there, I should try something that would be hard to screw up.... and thankfully they didnt screw it up.  (If you do order it, beware: your clothes will smell smokey.  The strong aroma produced by the steam of the hot stone pot combined with the Korean hot pepper paste has permeated my clothes and Febreze will not neutralize that smell.)

    Upon perusing the menu, I was pleased that the Zoma had a focused menu and that it didnt emphasize the "experimental" Asian (con)fusion concoctions that other places will prepare to appeal to a "broader" audience.   Of course, if I wanted better Korean food, I would have explored the enclave of reputable Korean establishments in North Philly, but suffice to say that this was a decent place for my neighborhood.   No panchan (variety of small Korean hors d'oeuvres that precede main meals) is served here.  

    I found the modern interior of the restaurant appealing.  There is a bar/counter area that accommodates 3-4 guests.  There also appears to be a private area for larger parties that can accommodate up to 8 guests.  

    Service, on a whole could be improved.... my water glass was not filled immediately and upon indicating the lack of water, I had to repeat my requests.  Specials were not announced automatically by the person who took my order.  The requested check/bill was not brought over promptly.

    Will I come back here?  Sure, why not?  Next time, to try the Japanese side of the menu.
