    Locals call this simply, "The Famous" and for generations this restaurant/deli has served up some of the best Jewish nosh in Philadelphia. This location should be a  historic landmark as the original 1920's interior still shines.

    I've been here for brunch with my mother who was practically weened on Jewish delis in NYC and now that she lives down south often asks me to bring her some authentic pickles when I come home. I've brought over containers of their matzo ball soup or "Jewish Penicillian" to boyfriends when they were sick. I've ordered out a complete Passover meal including brisket and knish, and even a bagel, lox and schmear platter that was described to serve 10 but could easily serve to stuffing proportions 20.

    I don't know what the Yiddish word for the tradition of getting more than you expect in service and proportions of food, but The Famous holds this tradition dear still today.

    Tip: Come early on the weekends as this gets packed early, but even so, the line moves quickly. Definitely a family friendly place.

    Had brunch on Sunday with friends.  The service was efficient and fast, and the food was quite good.  I ordered the lox, onion, and eggs.  It's a HUGE portion, so be forewarned.  I was hungry, but couldn't finish it.  It comes with a bagel, potato pancakes, sour cream, etc.  The lox was a bit too salty for my taste, but my dining partners said that is to be expected.  One of my friends ordered the Nova lox, onion, and eggs.  I tasted her dish, and it was much more palatable.  I would recommend that you get the Nova lox if you prefer your food less salty.  The pickles are excellent (got a container to go), as is the cole slaw.  Again, just be forewarned that the portions are very big.  One order is probably okay for two people to share.  I've been here a number of times over the past 10 years, and the food is consistent and good always.  You may have to stand in line to get a table, depending on the day, but well worth it.

    The name Famous Deli is kinda stating the obvious.  This is one of the famous Jewish delis on the East Coast and specifically in Philadelphia.  It has a charming old-fashioned feel. Just be prepared to wait in line to eat here.  But while waiting you can soak in the atmosphere from the deli counter filled with meats and salads or look at the old photos on the wall.  And of course you are going to notice how big the servings leaving the kitchen prep area are.  Actually "big" is not the right descriptive word here - "gigantic" is.

    When you're finally seated and you place your order, you will have a wide choice off a large menu.  One of the best intros to Jewish deli food is the appetizer combo platter.  (This is big enough for your meal here and the next one at home as well.)  This platter has knishes, kreplachs, kishkas, kasha, kugel .... and finally a food that doesn't begin with the letter "K" - latkes.  It's all tasty.

    And on the way out notice the old cash registers on display.  Famous Deli has been ringing up customers for generations!

    The Famous 4th St. Deli is a Philadelphia institution since 1923.  They have two locations, one at 4th and Bainbridge, and the other on 19th St. between Chestnut and Market.  

    The menu is huge, and has pretty much everything you'd expect a Jewish deli, with a very wide range of smoked and cured meats and fish, brisket, corned beef, pastrami, salads, platters, breakfast items, and desserts.  Portions are very large, so even the regular sized sandwiches might be a bit much for one person.  The matzoh ball soup comes with a matzoh ball the size of a softball...no kidding.  Desserts are the same, which the slice of checkerboard cake we ordered easily enough for two and even three people, depending upon how hungry you are.  The cheese blintz was a crepe, wrapped around sweet ricotta (and I believe cream) cheese, and was also huge.  The cabbage roll was about six inches long, and has a very flavorful sweet tomato sauce with a bit of vinegar and what I think was also some orange peel.  The filling I found a bit dry, however.  

    My GF got a hot pastrami with cheese, and I got the mixed fish platter with whitefish salad and Nova Lox.  The pastrami was great (they make it onsite) and piled high atop soft rye bread.  It's really exactly what you'd expect in a good deli pastrami sandwich.   The fish platter was a bit of a disappointment.  The Nova was great, and the accompaniments were fine, but the whitefish salad I didn't enjoy.  The way they make the salad results in large chunks of smoked whitefish throughout, and they appear to use less mayo that I'd like.  It's certainly not bad, but not something I'd get again.  I was trying to avoid the corned beef and brisket, when in fact I should have just gone for it.    

    This is a great place that's open 7 days a week, and have options for breakfast, lunch, and dinner.  A really great part of Philly history, and one of the few Jewish delis in the city.
