    Came here for an early dinner before a play at the Arden.

    The decor is really cool, with lamps that look like martini olives, and a kind of upscale  diner feel.  They have a range of appetizers and small plates, we enjoyed the chicken lettuce wraps and the crispy fried calamari/shrimp. But the dish that I keep dreaming about is the lobster mac and cheese. Its made with orzo rather than macaroni, with chunks of lobster in a creamy white cheese sauce, and topped with browned bread crumbs. So good!  

    I also really enjoyed the "strawberry fields" martini  which came in a little shaker and was poured tableside into a martini glass rimmed with balsamic/sugar.  The shaker held more than a glassful so it was left on the table for seconds. It was a really nice drink and I felt it was a good value despite the $12 price tag.

    Overall, I loved this place and would come back again. I recommend it whether for drinks and a snack or a complete meal.

    The Continental gets high marks for service and efficiency.  I saw food coming out in a steady stream.  The bartender was friendly enough, and the waitstaff seemed to be also.

    Unfortunately, the decor is kind of dingy, perhaps it was better a few years back.  When you sit at the bar, you can see behind it, where they store all the stuff you probably shouldn't see.  The menu did not look very interesting, except for those shoestring fries that kept coming out.  A HUGE plate of them.

    Unfortunately, the drink prices are higher than Fork for example, a few doors down the street, and a few levels of sophistication higher.  It would have been ok to have a well-made martini for $13.50, which is what mine cost here.  You know, you walk into a restaurant with huge decorations of martinis on the front that bills itself as a martini bar and you expect to have at least a halfway decent martini.  I didn't.

    First, the glass was depressingly tiny.  The tiniest I have ever seen.  Is that supposed to be cool?  It's like pony beers.  You just ask yourself, why?  Second, they give you the tiny pitcher that they mixed it in so that you can top off your drink with what's left in it when you've had some from the glass.  I have a problem with that.  While you're sipping your martini from the glass, the martini that's left in the pitcher is getting diluted.  Fast.  So, if you pour more from the pitcher, your entire martini is now diluted.  If you wait until your glass is empty, you get a second glass that is incredibly diluted.  If the drink is mixed properly, it tastes right.  Why would you ruin its integrity by having a second bit that is watery?  I guess because they want you to drink it fast to avoid that?  Hmmm.

Specialties

This stainless steel 60's-style diner offers an exciting menu of global cuisine, including crab pad thai, cheesesteak eggrolls and lobster mashed potatoes. Famed for its sensational cocktails, the extensive bar features a wide variety of libations and over 15 types of martinis.

Meet the Business Owner

Stephen S. Business Owner

Founded in 1995 by Stephen Starr, Philadelphia-based STARR Restaurants is one of the fastest growing multi-concept restaurant companies in the country. STARR Restaurants include Buddakan, Morimoto, The Continental, Barclay Prime, Continental Mid-Town, El Vez, Jones, Alma de Cuba, Pod, Parc, Butcher and Singer, Pizzeria Stella, El Rey, Ranstead Room, The Dandelion, Talula's Garden, Frankford Hall, Il Pittore, Route 6 and seasonal food-stand SquareBurger in Philadelphia, as well as, Morimoto and Buddakan in New York City's thriving Meatpacking District. STARR Restaurants expanded to Atlantic City, NJ, with Buddakan and The Continental located in the Pier Shops at Caesars in 2006, and to Florida with Steak 954 at the W Hotel Fort Lauderdale in 2009, and Makoto in the Bal Harbour Shops in 2011. For more information, please visit www.starr-restaurant.com.
