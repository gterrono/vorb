    I often come down to this place on Washington Ave for affordable, flavorful Vietnamese food. There are several decent Vietnamese places in Center City, but I find that the Vietnamese food in this neighborhood is slightly more authentic and really satisfies my Vietnamese cravings.

    On cold days I enjoy a nice bowl of pho. Pho is all about the broth, and the broth here is rich and flavorful. I find adding a touch of hoisin sauce and sriracha also bumps up the flavor a little more.

    I often get the barbecue chicken rice platter (topped with a fried egg) because the chicken is well seasoned, piping hot and perfectly tender. If my memory serves me right it's #81 on the menu.

    Other stand outs include banh mi sandwiches (take some to go), the steak cubes on rice, and lamb stew with french baguette. The lemon soda is also a great way to cool down when it's hot (unfortunately it's freezing now). Don't forget spring or summer rolls too!

    They do have free wi-fi, but the one time I tried to study there ended up being a disaster as I ended up watching Robo-Cop or whatever other television show was playing on the several screens mounted on the walls. Go for delicious Vietnamese food. Don't go to study.

    Oh by the way, they have a Vietnamese snack and candy store attached to the front. I usually leave with Nerds Rope or sour belts.

    We are four today: four for lunch. Our ongoing search for a rich broth Vietnamese Pho took me today to Viet Huong Pho and Café. If you are familiar with Wing Phat Plaza on Washington Avenue, you know that it can be a parking merry-go-round. Eventually, after several tours, I found a place. Inside, Christmas lit artificial plants and changing floor levels divide round tables, square tables and booths.
    Quick and friendly service greeted our table. While we were certain that we wanted the main pho, we asked our server to guide us with two subsequent dishes. Our first selection was an appetizer, four shrimp in bean paper. The complete pho would follow. Afterward we would try the vermicelli with grilled shrimp and the clay pot fish.
    Within just a few moments the appetizer was before us: four little diamond shaped cakes that resemble baklava. The crisp and flakey bean paper crunches airily between our teeth to release  the sweet and savory shredded cabbage and shrimp inner layers.  
    Barely had we touched the appetizer when several servers came to our table with not only the pho but also the other two main courses.  Serving all courses simultaneously is something quite common in Vietnamese restaurants. From previous experience we should have remembered to ask the server to bring only one course at a time. Tasting one course at a time may not be traditional, but when you want to experience each recipe, each dish needs time to itself.  I hope I was polite about my request to hold the other courses until the preceding dish was finished. But there did seem to be a slight sign of consternation on their faces.
    The anticipated pho, the reason for our lunch at Viet Huong did not disappoint. The broth, rich and complex, suggested the infusion of other gentle flavors suggesting cinnamon and ginger and anise. A good variety of meats seated themselves in the center of the bowl, although I do not recall tripe among them. The garnishes, lime, beans sprouts and Thai basil might have benefited by the inclusion of mint. The quality of the rice noodles completed the pho. The noodles slurped against our lips and tongues with distinct texture and taste. The shortcoming here was the size of the small individual bowls. In other Asian restaurants, when we have asked to share, the restaurant offers individual bowls of a functional size. Viet Huong's single serving bowls, intended for rice, are problematically small for soup. Overall, Viet Huong's pho surpasses those of several other Vietnamese restaurants with the exception of its neighbor Nam Phuong with whom it may tie.
    When our servers saw that we had finished the soup, they asked if we were ready for the next course. Within a moment, they returned with the Bun Tom Nuong, grilled shrimps on vermicelli, topped with bean sprouts, lettuce, herb, cucumber & crushed peanuts. We poured the sauce over the bowl and gave it a good turn. The serving was easily enough for four. The hot sweet shrimp nested deliciously against the cool crisp greens and tender noodles. Half way through we added a bit of the table's hot sauce to add a dimension of spice.
    The servers now understood our eating peculiarities. Having cleared our empty vermicelli bowls, they brought out the clay pot fish with a side of rice. The rice side was not in the neat mold shape of other tables. Clearly, they had reheated our original serving. Nonetheless, the clay pot fish, cat fish, swelled with the classic Asian blend of sweet and salty, creamy and light. While the portion size may not suffice as a single dinner serving for one, the fish accompanied by rice, was sufficient to share as a conclusion to a perfect Vietnamese lunch. The bill made our lunch even more enjoyable. For the fairly comprehensive selection for four people, the actual check came to just over thirty dollars. With tip, we finished at forty. I must also say  that the service was really quite good, despite our somewhat non-conforming requests.
    I have too long overlooked Viet Huong in favor of its neighbors in Wing Phat Plaza. Part of the reason was the word "Café" in its title. I never thought of it as a full restaurant. Don't make the same mistake. Viet Huong offers a Vietnamese cuisine that is rich and satisfying yet one whose every dish enlivens the palate with a fresh and clean taste.
