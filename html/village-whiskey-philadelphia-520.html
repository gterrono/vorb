    Ended up making a day trip to Philadelphia, and one of the spots that I had to stop by for lunch was Village Whiskey. I've heard great things about this place, so I HAD to try it out and see for myself what all the buzz was about.

    This place is small but we didn't have any problem getting a table for 4 without having to wait. You can either sit inside, with a bar-styled seating, or go outside and enjoy the weather. Obviously we decided to sit outside because it was a perfect day. With Village Whiskey, you're either coming here to drink, or eat their burgers. For $11, you can get a solid Village Burger, that tastes amazing. I went the extra mile and added the truffled mushrooms for $5, which I so do not regret.

    The burger is huge. You literally might even get lock jaw trying to eat this bad boy. The bun comes nicely toasted, with a kind of thousand island spread to it. Your patty is like no regular patty. It's literally over an inch thick, and has a nice light char to it. Inside, you get a nice juicy patty which brings joy to your taste buds. On top of you burger, you have to have something else to snack on.

    The duck fat french fries ran $5 an order. Sure, it was pretty tasty, but I'm actually not sure what the buzz is with duck fat. It's not like it tasted any different from regular oil. Sure I ate every single one, but for $5, I had at least hoped for a larger portion. Either that, or my taste buds are seriously flawed.

    Village Whiskey is in an awesome location. Right in the heart of downtown Philadelphia, this place is a treat. I was lucky to have this for lunch on my first trip to this city, and if I come back, I'll make it a point to stop by and grab another burger.

    Went here recently for lunch with a friend. Definitely not on the cheap side for lunch two burgers, one shared appetizer, one order of duck fries, and two iced teas, total $48 without tip. So even though it is fairly pricey, the consolation is that it is absolutely excellent food.

    I don't know why I ordered fried shrimp for an appetizer -- this is a dish which I think has just become impossible to do differently.  Village Whiskey gives you the same dipping sauce for the fried shrimp that they put on their burger rolls. It tastes good, don't get me wrong, but it should have something unique to the dish. The shrimp were tasty and hot and perfectly cooked, but I guess I was expecting something a little more outside the norm, and it was definitely norm.

    The burgers, they are incredible. Perfectly formed, juiciest I have ever had. No cook is pressing down on them to get them to cook faster, which drives me nuts because it forces out the flavor of the juice. No flaws in this burger at all, just meat, cheese, roll.

    Perfection with sesame seeds on top.

    The duck fat fries are tasty. Mine were served a little cold, which was unfortunate. I think these could be better, especially if they were not thick cut fries but, instead, were cut thin like frites. When they are thick, you get a ton of potato flavor. They don't do anything to crisp the outside of the fry (like toss it in a little corn starch first before frying), so the fries have a slight sogginess to them. If that's your thing, then you'll love the fries. I happen to mike mine with a bit of crisp.

    Upon finishing a half-marathon this morning (no big deal...), I overheard a fellow racer express how much he was jonesing for a cheeseburger - my words, not his. I could not for the life of me get that thought out of my mind and, as such, I became a woman on a mission for a burger, sort of like Harold and Kumar.

    I'd actually never been to Village Whiskey before, because their wait for dinner has always been too long, so after a few tries I kind of threw in the towel (Aaliyah would not be proud). Wanting to finally treat myself to what is considered the King of Burgers (not Burger King) in Philly, I took a shower, put on a simple black dress and flats, and headed to the Village Whiskey around 11:30 am on a Saturday. I took a seat at the very handsome bar and ordered the Village burger, cooked medium.

    I also ordered a mint julep, which was not part of the original plan, but after all the place is called Village Whiskey. And in light of the Triple Crown, I wanted to be festive and pay homage to the Kentucky Derby. My festivity was not well rewarded because one sip and I remembered why I don't drink whiskey. Oh well; I guess I won't have another :) This is not to reflect on the actual quality of the cocktail, which seemed to be made to perfection by the bespectacled bartender. He, by the way, was cute and kind in a Gentile Seth Rogen-y way, which probably added to my first solo bar experience.

    On to the burger... The patty was pretty amazing; it was thick and juicy and charred to perfection, as decadent as a steak. It is definitely the focal point of this very simple burger, which was minimally dressed with a slice of bibb lettuce, tomato, and a layer of russian dressing (a rip on the In-n-Out burger, perhaps?). It doesn't come with fries, so the complimentary pickle is really a saving grace in terms of breaking up the heaviness.

    I'm glad I chose this occasion to finally experience the Village Whiskey. The price of the Village burger is actually quite reasonable at $11, but it's not something I'm going to have again anytime soon because it's sort of intense. Maybe the next time I run a half-marathon though, it can help replenish my proteins again :)

Specialties

A ground-to-order Angus Beef Burger, a Veggie Burger, a Bison Burger or an Vienna All-Beef Hot Dog with all the trimmings are each served alongside duck fat French fries and Sly Fox cheddar sauce.  Burgers are topped with Village Whiskey's house-made thousand-island dressing, Bibb lettuce, beefsteak tomato and a potato-beer bun, with optional toppings including bacon, caramelized onion, truffles, artichokes and a selection of cheeses.

As one of the most comprehensive collections of its namesake spirit available in Philadelphia, Village Whiskey features a veritable library of 80 to 100 varieties of whiskey, bourbon, rye and scotch from Scotland, Canada, Ireland, United States and even a few Japanese options.

History

Established in 2009.

Founded by Chef-Owner Jose Garces in 2005, Garces Group has grown from one award-winning restaurant in Philadelphia,PA's historic Old City to more than a dozen nationwide. Beginning with the runaway success of Amada, an authentic Andalusian tapas bar and restaurant, Chef Garces went on to open: Tinto, a Basque tapas bar and restaurant; Distrito, a modern Mexican restaurant and tequila bar; Chifa, a Latin-Asian restaurant; Garces Trading Co., a European-style market-cafe and on-site wine boutique; Village Whiskey, a Prohibition-era brown liquor bar and restaurant; and JG Domestic, an American farm-to-table restaurant and bar, all in his adopted hometown of Philadelphia. Garces Group also expanded into other cities: Chicago,IL, in cooperation with Sage Restaurant Group; Scottsdale,AZ and Palm Springs,CA, in cooperation with Joie de Vivre hotels and the Sydell Group; and Atlantic City, NJ, at the Revel Resort. Garces Group also operates a thriving Catering and Private Events division.

Meet the Business Owner

Iron Chef Jose G. Business Owner

Since opening his first restaurant, Amada, in Philadelphia less than a decade ago, Chef Jose Garces has emerged as an enormous talent and one of the nation's most gifted chefs and restaurateurs.  Today, he is the owner and operator of more than a dozen wildly successful restaurants, plus a thriving catering division, in five cities:  Chicago, IL;  Scottsdale, AZ;  Palm Springs, CA;  Atlantic City, NJ;  and his flagship locations in Philadelphia, PA.  He is a 2009 winner of the James Beard Foundation's prestigious "Best Chef, Mid-Atlantic" award, and one of only a few chefs in the country to hold the coveted title of Iron Chef, appearing regularly on Food Network's hit series Iron Chef America.  Chef Garces is also the author of two cookbooks, The Latin Road Home (Lake Isle Press, October 2012), a culinary and cultural tour of Ecuador, Spain, Mexico, Cuba and Peru, and his stunning first cookbook, Latin Evolution (Lake Isle Press, Fall 2008).
