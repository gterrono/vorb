    Listed in My Top Restaurants in Philadelphia

    This rating is based on my restaurant week experience. It was amazing. I think I read that a good percentage of the offerings were from their main menu. The one thing that threw me off was their choice of music. It was a dim, candle lit spot with classy decor and Biggie Smalls complimenting the ambience. I think I heard some spatterings of Champagne Supernova as well.

    I almost filled myself up on the complimentary starters. This included 6 vegetarian 'salads' aka the Salatim such as Tabulah, Babaganosh, some type of delicious curried carrots, a vinegar cucumber salad, and a red pepper mush that had some delicious undertones of chocolate. Also complimentary was the Hummus-Tahin,  that came with a big plate of house baked Laffa AND a small bowl of pickled vegetables. It was so good I really couldn't stop eating. I was half way to full before the appetizers even rolled out.

    For the appetizers, there is a huge list and each person is able to choose two. We ordered  Kibbe Naya (Raw lamb, walnuts, sumac shallots), Beef Cheek Pastilla
    (Braised greens, coffee), House-Smoked Sable (Challah, fried egg, poppy), and some sort of Fried Cod that was delicious. The Kibbe Naya was passable, but had the usual raw meat no flavor taste. All the flavor was in the dipping sauce. The Sable was cold and delicious on the challah and a fried egg in the middle. The Code was all sorts of delicious. And the Beef CHeek Pastilla was some sort of israeli beef egg roll and amazeballs.

    The main course allowed for each dinner to choose one, again from a huge selection. The Duck Kebab (Ground duck, pistachios, saffron) was soft tender and tiny. It came on a bed of some type of pillaf, but wasn't really much a kebab. It was two pieces of medium rare duck burgers that were delicious and tender, but tiny. I guess this is why they fill you up with appetizers and free stuff, no real complaints because I was near deathly full by now. The other tasting was Salmon Shishlik (Moroccan couscous, spicy tomato stew). The stew wasn't anything special and Salmon Shishlik was just three small pieces of salmon on couscous. The flavor was all in the stew and I wasn't too big a fan of it.

    For dessert we had Konafi (Ricotta, apples, labaneh ice cream) and a Pumpkin Cake that had some type of sour cherry sauce and custards something or other (it's not on the CC Restaurant Week menu). The konafi stole the show. It was rich, creamy, crispy, and cheesey which went perfectly well with the ice cream. |labaneh must mean delicious in israeli! Puahah. The pumpkin cake was good, not too sweet, but went well with the rest of the Spatterings of peanut sauce and pistacos and sour cherry sauce.

    I was surprised at the size of the restaurant, it's on the smaller side but it was big enough to not feel cramped.  We were seated right smack next to the food pickup window which I was not happy about at first, however I wasn't bumped into or bothered by anyone going to and fro throughout the dinner--impressive!

    Our waiter was beyond fantastic. He answered our 1000s of questions regarding the menu and he even found out from the kitchen what ingredients were in some of our favorite dishes. Very personably without being too intrusive during our meal.

    We ordered and shortly after a tower of vegetable salads was placed on our table as well as some hot bread and a huge bowl of hummus. I was in heaven as this is some of my favorite foods! The veggie salads included roasted beet, spicy chickpeas, carrots, roasted red pepper, tabouli, eggplant and 2 others I cannot remember, but they were all very fresh and delicious. My sister who is anti-vegetable ate a lot of it-- We definitely all over indulged in the appetizers.

    Here's a little rundown of dinner:
    Haloumi - Which was a fried cheese with date jam. I think this was my favorite overall item I had here. I can't even explain the taste.
    Borekas with mushrooms & feta - These were not that great unfortunately. Just tasted like puff pastry. The only disappointing thing I ate.
    Fried Cauliflower - AWESOME. It wasn't crispy but tasted like deep fried goodness!
    Watermelon and Feta - Another awesome dish. The feta was so different then anything you find anywhere else. Very tangy and refreshing.
    Stuffed Baby Peppers - I didn't try these but my friend said they were tasty.
    Fried Kibbe with Tahini - Fried balls of lamb and spices. Spices were really nice in these and the tahini went well with it.
    Chicken Freekah - was good but too plain for my taste although my non-adventurous eater friends enjoyed it.

    I had the lamb sausage (Merguez) for my main dish. It was really spicy and cooked perfectly served atop couscous with a tomato like sauce. I really enjoyed this as well. And finally for dessert I had the chocolate semifreddo with honey and sesame seeds, which was divine. I was so full at this point though I couldn't finish it all.

    When the check came our waiter gave us each coupons for $10 off our next visit (I WILL be back) and a little packet of spices to take home. How cool is that????

    Zahav's décor invites diners to two distinct rooms. The larger room, high and spacious, opens on one side to vista windows and on the other side to the long polished bar. The smaller room, darker and partially veiled in draping, intimates the blue tiled courtyards of the Middle East. The hostess guides us to a hammered copper top table that glows in candlelight. It is restaurant week. Our server presents the menu and guides us in our selection. We are four and we will share as we always do.  All the selections entice and the variety dizzies. Each diner may choose two mezze and since we are four, we can select nearly all the offerings.  There are also four dessert possibilities: an easy share.
    Zahav begins its evening of Eastern delights with a large bowl of humus tahini surrounded by a salatim of some six different delicacies. A still hot and steaming dish of airy laffa bread provided the utensil. We slowly savored each taste and found ourselves content enough to have already satisfied the evening.  Our eight mezze followed.  Our server brought them out only two at a time and so allowed us to pace ourselves slowly. Each mezze takes your taste to a different experience: too many to list each here. Although I must make special note of the smoked sable with poached egg on challah floated like velvet on silk and the sweetbreads were nothing short of celestial transcendence.
    The wait staff attended to our every need. Clean plates appeared several times as we changed dishes. There was always an extra sharp knife on the table to ease our shared cuts. Part way through the dinner I dropped my napkin. As I bent to retrieve it a server came to me immediately with a clean one.
    The main course now waited. The term main course does not quite seem the right for Middle Eastern food as served at Zahav.  Each plate, as with the two preceding courses,  might easily stand as a principal offering in its own right. Nonetheless our "Shipudim," the main courses, offered four excursions in sight and taste and textures. The meat preparation of some these dishes may present a small challenge. Middle Eastern meats are often somewhat processed by grinding and mixing with crushed grains. The final step is to form the ground meat into balls before grilling. While the ground meat preparations are certainly a new experience, I find them the least interesting of Zahav's dishes.
    The four dessert offerings take you from flights through airy threads of sweet pastry to drowning plunges into rich creams.  As if the service and servings at Zahav were not already beyond expectation, our dessert tray included a make-you-smile dish of mini-kreplach.
    Our evening turned like a kaleidoscope of taste experience: flavor transforming to flavor; delight merging to delight. We benefitted from restaurant week's $35 dinner menu in our enjoyment of Zahav, but for just a few dollars more you can enjoy the same "Tayim" menu at any time.

    This review is very much delayed, as I went to Zahav in July 2012, but catching up on all of my restaurant experiences in the past year.

    My boyfriend and I met in Israel, and upon our return were very excited to try Zahav. It's very rare that you find Israeli cuisine in such an upscale restaurant, so we had to try it. The restaurant is beautiful, and the stone floors mimic the old world feel of Israel, specifically Jaffa and Jerusalem.

    Upon checking out the menu, I knew my boyfriend would opt for the more expensive tasting menu because of the inclusion of a whole roasted lamb shoulder. He's a sucker for anything roasted. Because the whole table had to participate, I went along with it to make him happy...even though I was thinking it was going to be WAY too much food.

    To start we got the salatim and hummus with laffa. The vegetables were all very reminiscent of Israel, and the hummus was great. Not to mention, we both got Gold Star, a cheap Israeli beer that is miraculously good. The waitress then brought out a dish from the chef, some sort of egg with bacon and a great sauce.     I've eaten in justtttt about 80% of the best restaurants in Philly. And I'm talking about restaurants renowned for their AMAZING food, whether they were budget corner spots or $50+ a plate. I adore their simple, open decor that reminds me of a market in Tel Aviv. It was clean and sophisticated but comfortable and unique, with that private feel that you get from an elevated restaurant in a dark corner of a quiet side street, surrounded by bushes and landscaping.  It's dressy casual so it's perfect for date night or post-happy hour dinner with your best friend.

    Now that we have all the extras out of the way, let's talk about what really matters: SERVICE and FOOD!!!

    I've been a server for many years. It's not rocket science, but not everyone can deliver rockstar service. It's a bit of an art, and the best service requires teamwork. This place had possibly THE BEST SERVICE I've received in the city. Our server was knowledgable and volunteered advice and explanations about the fairly exotic Israeli food. She never steered us wrong...all of her suggestions were amazing....but we'll get to that in a minute. The drinks took a little while but the bar was full and that's totally forgivable. Otherwise, our water glasses were always full, and an empty plate never sat for more than a minute before it was removed. It was perfectly synchronized service between the server, food runner, busser, and any other staff that walked by. Everyone chipped in to make the experience flawless!!

    Now, for the good part: their unbelievable, delectable food!!
    Zahav offers both ala carte and prix fixe options. The two prix fixe option are $39 and $48, with the main difference being that the higher priced option offered a chef's selection of mezze and the entree was their signature lamb shoulder. My boyfriend wasn't feeling the lamb (although after seeing it come out of the kitchen a few times, he definitely wanted to come back and try it...I second the idea), so we decided on the $39 menu which included salatim and hummus (a mix of salads and spreads), a choice of 2 mezze per person, one entree, and a desert. My boyfriend wanted to try an extra entree so he ordered an extra kebab (that we really didn't need).  Given that each item is at least $8, it's a no-brainer to go for the prix fixe!

    Let me tell you, it was one of the best meals I've had since I can remember. The hummus and laffa were delicious, as well all of the salatim! They reminded me a lot of food I'd eat as a child (Russian cuisine has a lot of similarities with Isreali cuisine).

    For our mezze, we ordered the brussel sprouts, sea bass crudo, grilled duck hearts, and the house smoked sable. All were delicious, but the duck hearts were an instant favorite....don't ewwww. They were amazing, and if you can get past the mental image of Aaliyah in Queen of the Damned sucking the blood out of a beating heart, they might be your new favorite too!

    I'm not sure what our entrees were...but they were equally fantastic! I definitely want to come back for the lamb shoulder!! That just looked like a giant hunk of incredible!!  

    Our deserts were the perfect end to a perfect meal and weren't too sweet (I'm sweet enough as it is, I don't need the extra sugar!). And their coffee was STRONG as coffee should me. Speaking of strong, we put back a couple cocktails while we were there. My boyfriend loved his!! I liked mine but because I had a cold, I went for the whiskey and bourbon drinks (Irish cough syrup!!), which were delicious but the strong flavor didn't match the light flavorful food.

    ANNYYYWAYYYYY......To sum up this epicly long visual of my phenom experience, I would definitely recommend this as an authentic, incredible alternative to your typical date night or family dinner or whatever. The prix fixe menu is THE way to go. You will not be disappointed! The amount of food will leave you full but not in a food coma (a sacred art). The service rivals that of any other 4 star restaurant in the city. Don't be afraid to go out of your comfort zone, duck hearts might become your new fave!!
