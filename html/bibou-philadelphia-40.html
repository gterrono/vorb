    When you walk into Bibou, you feel like you're entering a nice little bistro in middle of Paris. Inside Bibou, it's not hard to imagine opening the door to the view of Arc de Triomphe or Eiffel Tower (instead of the *comparatively* harsh-looking neighborhood on Washington Ave).

    Charlotte and the staff are professional yet friendly. Pierre, the head chef, always comes over with a friendly "bon soir" and asks how the meal is to each table; surprisingly, he never seems to get annoyed by rather stupid questions we diners often ask...to which he had given the same answer more than he could count, I'm sure.

    At Bibou, I feel comfortable. I don't feel like I have to dress up, show off, or deal with pretentiousness from others. No, at Bibou, I feel like I am part of the big group with other 20 diners, enjoying our night out in Paris (ahem, I meant in Philly) with good meals with fine wine (you have to bring your wines though).

    The menu on Sunday is a $45 prix-fixe menu with optional cheese sampling (cheese sampling adds additional $10). The menu changes seasonally. Among 4 of us, we tried most of the things on the Sunday menu. Every bite was delightful, perfectly seasoned. I took some pics this time and i'll let the pictures do the talking, although the pictures themselves cannot do justice to how comfortable and satisfying the meal was.

    My only hope is that Bibou will always remain our small, wonderful retreat to France in Philadelphia. I shall return time and time again. Until next time, au revoir, Bibou...au revoir.

    Magnifique!

    My boyfriend and I went to Philly for a romantic weekend away -- our dinner at Bibou was an absolute highlight, and I'm sure we'll both be talking about it for months if not years to come!

    A lot of reviewers have commented about the hospitality of the staff, and I couldn't agree more.  Charlotte was a wonderful hostess - warm and courteous from start to finish.  Pierre, the Chef, is not only talented but charismatic and attentive -- I felt like I knew them by the time we left their restaurant.

    I started with a generous portion of foie gras served with poached pear -- it was perfectly seared and seasoned, and truly melts in your mouth.  I have never had foie gras that well prepared - simply amazing.  My boyfriend had the escargot, which was perfectly tender and paired with a fascinating bean, mushroom, and tarragon ragout.  (Both of us wished we hadn't devoured the bread basket before getting the appetizers, as we would have made sure no sauce was left behind!)

    I had the boeuf, which was crisp and flavorful on the outside, perfectly cooked on the inside.  The cut may change depending on what is fresh, available, and to the Chef's liking, but if my experience was any indication, Bibou prepares a flavorful and perfectly executed beef entree.  Even the garnish was fantastic -- well-seasoned and perfectly prepared asparagus and potatoes.  My boyfriend had the pigs feet, but before taking our orders to the kitchen, our server told him a little bit about the preparation and taste to make sure he wanted to commit to it, a gesture we appreciated, as it showed that Bibou wants its patrons to enjoy their experience.  True to all the reviews he read here, the dish was unbelievable.  The unique texture and taste of that one dish were well worth the trip!

    For dessert, we shared the chocolate sampler and a peach tatin.  Both were absolutely outstanding.  The tatin was served hot, and the peaches were perfectly done -- perhaps accentuated by almond extract(?) and finished with a spoonful of vanilla ice cream.  The chocolate mousse was absolutely divine as well.  Thick, creamy, and flavorful -- the perfect end to a perfect meal!

    We will absolutely be back -- with family, friends, or just ourselves for another romantic evening away.  I can't wait to see what new dishes Bibou offers as the seasons change, and we both look forward to seeing Charlotte and Pierre again.

    I too came here having read many positive reviews of this charming-looking restaurant, and the inconspicuous facade appealed to me. It was a Sunday night, for the tasting menu. While the staff was very kind, the food went from curiously good but not-quite-right to really quite unfortunate.
    As is common in my Philadelphian dining experiences, things started off alright: in this case, with imported butter that has been mentioned elsewhere. The bread was unremarkable. Next came a shrimp bisque that tasted good, but was oddly and inappropriately spicy, and reminiscent of premature acid reflux (the burn, not the flavor, which was good). Next, the small course for two of us was seared fois gras. I enjoyed this and it was executed well, but accompanied by a rather unripe and unflavorful pear, and spiced (perhaps pumpkin) bread that didn't help things much. The fois gras itself was also prone to connective tissue in it which made the texture imperfect. But it lacked the bloody undertones that fois gras sometimes has.
    The main course I chose was effectively bone marrow stuffing, as it was bread and vegetables chopped up and stuffed into a large bone. It was rich, and had an unpleasant background flavor which I guess I must attribute to the melted marrow... it was rather organ-like in flavor. To one side of this were small potatoes that really needed salt and a small salad that had far too much, so together that worked out well.
    Finally, dessert consisted of goat milk creme caramel and a chocolate trio, which was a sort of brownie with jam, ice cream and a mousse. The goat milk caramel was very plain, though the caramel was quite dark, successfully masking the rank flavor that goat milk usually possesses. However, the creme caramel itself was quite underwhelming: not particularly smooth or overcooked, it was very average.
    The chocolate trio was far below average, however, with the chocolate ice cream firmly at the top, followed by a very thick and grainy mousse and last an uncertain brownie (no one could tell what it was supposed to have tasted like, but surely not what it actually did..) with strawberry jam on top. It was our mistake not to have tried the sorbet, which was made elsewhere. All this was topped off by really, very hard and chewy coconut macaroons. Maybe to teethe.
    Anyhow, for $45 this might be worth it, but certain parts were just not enjoyable.

    After another excellent meal last night, I wanted to write an update.  Since my first visit to Bibou, I've been back for the 7-course tasting at the bar near the kitchen and twice with friends (who were really just excuses for me to go back).  On both of those trips, we ordered a la carte.

    First, the bad.  I don't know whether the novelty has worn off (we do tend to order some of the same things each time because we love them) or the execution has been slightly off since the initial visits, but I've grown slightly less impressed by the food.  That's not to say that there aren't still surprises, or that the food isn't some of the best I've ever had.  I struggle to recall any missteps and, while I haven't liked some dishes as much as others, they're always extremely well put together.  It's only that the food has gone from being revelatory to just extremely enjoyable.

    The good:  The more we come, the more familiar we get with the staff.  We talk about other restaurants (and some of the people who work there) with the servers.  Or where they're from.  On my previous three visits, we've been the last party to leave, and Charlotte has pulled up a chair with a glass of wine after everyone's left.  She's absolutely charming and she'll talk about her family, Bibou's success, and occasionally say something delightfully mischievous.  I told her how I was struggling with puff pastry that was essential for my plans to show off at a potluck, and she gave me advice and told me to call her later if I still had trouble.  The intimacy adds so much to the experience and if there have been any slight changes in how much I enjoy the food, I still love the restaurant more each time I go.  My only complaint now is that it's hard to respond to their friendliness when I'm already struggling to maintain some small degree of dignity and not  lick the plate or have food spill out of my mouth.

    After months of trying to get a reservation for the Sunday tasting menu that wasn't ridiculously… After months of trying to get a reservation for the Sunday tasting menu that wasn't ridiculously early or late, I finally got one for last night a few weeks ago and had to endure several weeks of waiting in the interim.

    The journey to Bibou began a bit inauspiciously.  First, I debated whether to dress up.  It seemed like a special occasion, and dressing up makes me feel important .  As it turned out, other people at the restaurant were wearing jeans, so in the future I won't bother.  Then, the bus was late, so I sent my dining companion ahead to get there on time while I sought a non-existent ATM that the Citizens Bank website directed me to.  Eventually I found a different one and managed to show up a few minutes late.

    The restaurant was charming and intimate.  I envied the other customers with whom Charlotte, the hostess and wife of the chef, talked, wondering when it would be my turn.  Towards the end of the meal she did say hello and conspiratorially whisper that my dessert choice, the baba au rum, was her favorite as well.  The pacing of the courses was perfect, and service was excellent--they were quick to refill bread, water, and wine, without being intrusive in the slightest.  The only snag came at the end of the meal, when it took a bit longer to get the check than I wanted.

    We had the gazpacho, escargot, foie gras, veal sweetbreads, steak, a cheese plate, the baba au rum, and a peach pie.

    The gazpacho was refreshing and balanced, with a few drops of olive oil and some flecks of chive on top.  The escargot was earthy and wonderful.  I'm not sure if the snails themselves had much flavor, but they had great texture, and the sauce and mushrooms did, with a nice hit of tarragon to bring some freshness to it.  The foie gras was perfectly seared and buttery with a sweet fig sauce and some really good brioche that absorbed the sauce.  The hanger steak, which was ordered medium, was a bit chewy, although quite flavorful.  My dining companion thought it was good, but not the best he's had.  I lost my sweetbread virginity and kind of wish I hadn't--I don't know where else I'll be able to get them.  They were also nicely browned on the outside, mild and creamy and wonderful on the inside.

    I've already been too long-winded, so I'll try to sum up the rest quickly.  All three cheeses on the cheese plate were great, although I wasn't quite sure if the preserves (plum with cardamom, I think, and very good) were supposed to pair with all of the cheeses or just one or two of them.  Both desserts were fine, although they weren't up to the same standard as the rest of the meal--I would've liked my baba a bit more rummy, with the cake a little less etherial.  Pierre, the chef, came over at the end of the meal, which was nice.

    Overall, the meal came to about $140 for 2, not including the bottle of wine.  It was money well spent, and I look forward to trying the larger tasting menu in the future.     Read more
