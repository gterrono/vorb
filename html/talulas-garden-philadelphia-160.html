    Everything about Talula's Garden shines, from the beautiful outdoor setting to the warm friendly service to the friendly patrons at the bar to the food itself.  I'm a tad late getting to this review and the menu changes a lot but hopefully you will get the idea.  Talula's Garden has an eclectic crowd but is it certainly a place to see and be seen.  We saw people of all ages and states of dress.

    I had the Forager at the bar to start, a gin-based drink with sage and orange, it was fantastic.  Ordered the sweetbreads with wonderful pickled peppers.  The flavors were amazing together as were the textures.  Combinations that you wouldn't expect with sweetbreads.

    We each had an appetizer and a main course when we sat down to dinner.  I had the sweet pea ravioli, which was out of this world.  They had peas done at least 4 ways, if I remember correctly, including shoots.

    Next was the snapper with grapefruit, among other delights.  We shared a bottle of excellent chablis with the dinner.  What impressed us most about the food is the beautiful combinations and attention to so many components of an excellent dish -- flavor, texture, color, originality.  

    It's a bit expensive, but most great restaurants in downtown Philly are.

    We started with the pea ravioli and the crab salad. Both were good. Nothing special.

    We got Lancaster chicken and salmon as our entrees. Again, not that special. And why was my fish served in a bowl? Weird. On the upside, the salmon skin was cooked to a crispy perfection that just can't be done at home without a torch.

    For dessert we got the donuts (which I saw someone raving about on here) and the blueberry cobbler. Truth be told, the sweet corn ice cream that came with the cobbler was the highlight of the night. The donuts were just OK.

    I'm appreciative to the person who got us a Starr gift card, but really wasn't impressed with Talula's Garden. Could someone explain the lure to me, please? I certainly didn't see it in the food.

    Lastly, the outdoor seating is cramped. I could hear the people next to us very clearly and was reluctant to talk to my husband over our anniversary dinner as I felt the people next to us could hear everything as well.

    Bottom line: there are so many better (cheaper) restaurants in the city and there is no one thing drawing me back to this place.

    Been here a couple times now and both meals were excellent, and in some way different from other Philly restaurants I've been to.

    While most reviews rightfully focus on the food, I think Talula's deserves attention elsewhere.  The space, as many have noted, is very comfortable, even peaceful.  Despite how busy it gets you never feel cramped or like you need to yell in here.  That's big.  The service has always been great - you can tell the staff is well versed in the menu if not a bit jumpy in telling you so.  Heard from an employee they even send staff out to the farms where they procure much of their food to learn where things come from.  That's pretty cool.

    This is not to say the food isn't amazing.  Get a cheese plate.  I recommend the Master Collection for its variety, but the others I've seen or tried have been great as well.  Plenty of accompanying fruits and crackers that only enhance the experience, and the staff does a great job explaining each cheese when plating.

    I would recommend getting two of the smaller plates rather than one main course since they're about half the price, more than half the size and this allows you to try as many things as possible.  I've had the salmon rillette, a goat ravioli that is no longer on the menu, and the NJ clam pasta.  All were light and refreshing yet filling.  Perfect.  Have heard nothing but good things from groups I've been with about what they tried as well (lamb belly, fluke crudo, hallibut).

    Go.

    As someone who's spoiled for choice when it comes to dining in the Bay Area, I'm absolutely impressed by Philadelphia's burgeoning dining scene. On a recent visit to Talula's Garden, I was heartened to see a slice of home greeting me, in the form of Bay Area slow-food pioneer Alice Waters's words adorning the central dining room: "a garden brings life and beauty to the table." It's clear that owner Aimee Oxley and executive chef Sean McPaul have taken this philosophy to heart with a menu highlighting regional, seasonal ingredients.

    Oxley's cheese expertise is one of her selling points, and there are half a dozen plates to choose from. We chose the master plate for its variety, and indeed, it was an enjoyable array of cheeses made from goat, cow, sheep, and buffalo milk from the world over.

    The crab salad and a side of marinated beets were a refreshing way to ease into the main courses, which in our case were hanger steak and salmon. Overall, everything felt fresh and satisfying going down, without leaving any heaviness or excessive flavors. Ricotta doughnuts and excellent Haitian French press coffee (sourced from La Colombe, a favorite local cafe and coffee roaster) were an excellent way to round out a delightful dinner.

    Inside, the ambience is both spacious and airy, with high ceilings, and also rustic and intimate, with wooden tables and barrels. In particular, the bar manages to combine bucolic charm with just a hint of hipster trendiness. Outside, there's a beautiful, cozy garden dining area that's especially beautiful when lit up at night.

    Last but not least, Jenna, our server, was exceptionally sweet, engaging, and attentive. Overall, a highly enjoyable experience and a restaurant we'd emphatically recommend to locals and visitors alike.

    On the west edge of Washington Square rests the modest entrance to Talula's Garden.  Once you step into the gate you enter a space that attempts to remove you from the grittiness of the city. A fresh, LED lighted garden space full of seating leads to the main restaurant.  Upon entering, you will notice the dramatic layout, high ceilings, and finer touches that make this a thoughtfully planned eatery.  

    My girlfriend and I were fortunate to have reservations on a busy Friday night, and we were even more fortunate to have Katie (Kate?) as our server.  She was indeed meritorious in her service to us that evening.  We started the evening with a couple cocktails (of which I've hear many positive things) and a cheese plate.  My girlfriend was smart to get "The Rainmaker," a mint julep muttled with blackberry, and I initially chose "The Forager," a Hendrick's martini with orange, sage, and egg white.  It was not to my liking as it had little flavor at first and then smelled like a wet dog. Katie was so nice to bring me a different cocktail at no charge, so I opted for another Hendrick's gin & tonic cocktail that was far more satisfying and appropriate for the restaurant.  I have to say, I was suspicious of The Forager at first, and rightfully so.  

    As far as the cheese, we opted for the "Bring on the Funk" selection of six very ripe cheeses, with the Epoisses de Bourgogne being the highlight. It was good, but not great. A few of the cheeses had a decent funk, but there are at least three or four palatable cheeses that could have made the list and brought all the funk (a real Muenster for example).  Good but not great.

    Onto the first plates.  I ordered a lamb belly (think pork belly but with a middle eastern spin), and my girlfriend had the sweetbreads.  The lamb belly was magnificent, and the pickled vegetables and hummus with lamb jus were the perfect combination of sweet, sour, and savory for this dish. While the sweetbreads were spot on in flavor, they started to lose their crispy texture after a minute, and the spicy jalapeno and corn salad with it felt a little bulky and heavy-handed for such a delicate protein.  

    The main courses were outstanding mostly. I had the brined pork with peaches, accompanied by green runner beans, onion puree, and pork jus.  The flavors worked perfectly well together.  Initially, I though the absence of a carbohydrate would leave the dish unsatisfying, but the peaches do well in that position.  It's a very satisfying dish with the onion puree being the best part. The only drawback was that the pork is intentionally cooked medium instead of well.  This is typically fine, but mine seemed slightly less than medium at times, and it was chewy instead of tender.  My girlfriend ordered the scallop with fig, veggies, fig glaze, and pomme puree.  It was perfect. It is everything you want a scallop to be and be surrounded by.  I would eat that every day if possible.

    We were too full to have dessert.  Maybe we'll go back for that.

    So what's the bottom line on Talula's Garden? It's good not great.  There is at least one other restaurant in the city I would choose over Talula's when it comes to high end, thoughtfully prepared dishes. I wanted this food to be great, and I was slightly underwhelmed. Does it mean it was bad?  No.  Not at all.  You should definitely try it at least once. But, does it live up to the hype? I suppose it could, though it's not the best in the city. Also, something that is difficult to note in the main portion of the review is that the server was good, but the food came out so slowly. It took two hours to get from cocktails to the main course. Not even to finish the main course. That's slow.  Lastly, this is a hard review to write given that this was the most expensive meal I've ever had. But, the lesson to learn hear is that highest price does not always beget highest quality.
