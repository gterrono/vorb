    I've been craving noodles for quite a while now, so I decided to check out Nan Zhou after seeing it pop up on the yelp chat boards everyone once in a while.  I had no idea what to expect, but knew it was going to be a ton a food.  I was prepared.

    I read a few reviews that said the beef brisket noodle soup was the way to go, so that's what I got along with two lamb skewers.  The bowl of noodles was HUGE for such a small price - I like this place already.  Then I dove in with the spoon and chopsticks.  The noodles were a tangled mess of delicious and the soup was so flavorful.  The beef brisket had some REALLY fatty pieces, so I just put them off to the side.  No big deal!  I know the fat added to the rich flavor of the soup.  The lamb was also very good.  I wasn't expecting much at $1.75/each, but they were fantastic.  The only complaint is that they were a little heavy on the seasoning, which was "zingy."  I knew eating the whole meal was a bad idea (especially before going to concert at the Electric Factory), but I just couldn't help myself.   I almost had to be rolled away.

    I will definitely be back!  So much good food for so cheap!  The only thing they're missing is making this BYOB.

    As it's name descriptively states, Nan Zhou is a noodle house.  But, I say it's a little more than that.  Unlike what most people probably expect from a Chinatown restaurant, Nan Zhou is clean, with its decor obviously furnished by Ikea.  It seems to be common practice here to seat guests at tables together with other strangers (i.e., like the communal tables found at Le Pain Quotidien).  This is fine, except when it's just two people, and you're both sitting next to each other at a round table.  Conversation gets a bit more difficult and awkward.

    Anyway, onto the food!  We ordered the steamed pork and chive dumplings, fish ball noodle soup, and brisket noodle soup.  The steamed pork and chive dumplings sound delicious, but we were both less than impressed.  I had the fish noodle soup, which came in a very generous portion - the noodles (which you can get hand drawn or shaved) filled up pretty much 70% of the bowl, with the rest of the space taken up by the fish balls, veggies, and broth.  I'm always a little hesitant to order "fish balls," because, really, what is in "fish balls"?  But, I decided I'd rather leave that a mystery because they were delicious and there was no need to ruin the experience.  

    Out of five stars, I would give Nan Zhou a 4 - taking one away because it's cash only (I'm a credit card kind of guy) and the dumplings tasted like they came from Trader Joe's (no offense to all you TJ fans out there)!

    One of my Philly favorites. Hands down.

    I fell in love with this place freshman year, when I happened across in their original location on the other side of 10th St. I asked around town about a place to get some noodles and one of the bakeries pointed me to the doors of Nan Zhou.

    I don't even know where to start with this place. The quality of their noodles. The richness of their broth. The value and sized of their bowls. All of it deserves an A+. Back at their old place, you could see the kitchen from most tables and it was cool to watch the cooks pull the hand-drawn noodles, seeing it for from one lump of dough to a fist full of fine noodles.

    I honestly come by myself a lot of times if I'm craving noodles, but it's a great place to bring friends. At less than $7 a bowl, it's perfect for almost any budget. There are also lots of different cuts of beef/other meats, so there's something for everyone. My personal favorites are the original beef noodle soup, beef tendon, and beef tripe. All are very different, but oh so tasty.

    Can't complain about service either. Whether you order in English or Chinese, they're speedy, polite, and very knowledgable about the menu.
