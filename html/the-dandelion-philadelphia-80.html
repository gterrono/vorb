    4/7/2011

    So, after many walks-by of this new and very attractive (from the outside, at least) cornershop of Rittenhouse gastronomy, I finally scored an invite from the in-laws (my sister's, not mine) for a casual dinner around 7.
    The first thing that you notice is the downstairs bar - in the quick glance I got before being pushed upstairs towards the lame non-drinkers, eating-focused second floor, it looked spacious, fun, and home to some interesting decor (both on the walls and sitting on the barstools ... mental note to investigate sans in-laws at a later date).
    Joke: how many Dandelion hostesses does it take to greet you, find your reservation, and make you unnecessarily wait for your table? Last night, in my experience, it was 5. Yes, 4/5 were pretty good looking, but it was stiflingly hot and while the place was crowded, there was no need to keep up waiting for 10 minutes or so. Minus one star. Our table was in what I considered to be the Victorian-bedroom space - not my style at all, but nicely done all the same, I suppose? Tables are quite cramped and there were awkward elbow-bumping and neighbor-excusing moments. Minus one star.
    Waitress was slow, only moderately knowledgeable, and dressed like a medieval harlot or beer wench ... no star subtracted because she was cute.

    Ok, food. I was pleasantly surprised on this front. I had the buttercup salad to start, and then the burger with a side of mixed greens in place of the fries Yelpers seem to be hating on.  Nice salty, crusty bread to start, and the salad was amazing - lettuce was crisp but tender, great dressing (a bit too much of that, though) and the candied walnuts were great.
    If you love a sweet-savory salad with much more than just vegetables, get this - you'll love it. Maybe go with the dressing on the side though. My tablemate seemed to enjoy his Caesar, too, which had some intriguing anchovies on toast to accompany it. Might want to get one of those.
    Ok, but the parfait - duck I believe it was - was incredible. Another dining companion was nice enough to share this pate-like treat and it was incredible. With the cranberry sauce on that Texas Toast-like platform? To call it "sweet meat butter" sounds gross ... until you taste it. Not along the lines of more savory pate with whole grain mustard, this was straight-up candy for the palate ... delicious though guilt-inducing.
    Burger was a pleasant surprise - actually came out medium-rare, with a tasty (soft!) bun and a variety of toppings already on it (pickles? sprouts? special sauce? something like that). A bit of ketchup, and BAM, I was a happy camper. Mixed greens were fresh and not over-dressed ... skip what i'm sure are fairly weak fries and ask for something different like I did.
    Had a great 20oz pint of Stodt's (sp?) Pils that lasted for most of the meal and was tasty.

    Would probably go back but only for drinks and/or appetizers at the bar.
    Also, perhaps all British pubs are fackin' loud, but the Dandelion is definitely authentic on that account - i don't mind loud joints but want to be able to talk to somebody within a 1-2 foot radius. Could've been the crowded tables, on second thought.

    Came for dinner on a weekend night.  The ambiance is not exactly authentic, but hey, it's Starr.  It was cozy, though, especially on a rainy night.

    If you like British beer, you'll be intrigued by their selection - though it's certainly not cheap.  What's fun is their offering of two draft sizes: 10 oz and 20 oz. (if you're looking for "a pint", get the 20oz.).  Just like the "short beers" of the old working days, the 10 oz is a fun option if you want to try a few...or have to stay awake during a show.

    The fish & chips are exquisitely cooked.  While the presentation is a bit too precious for my taste (a single triangle of fish, set in the middle of a sheet of brown paper on a vast wooden board), it was fried as perfectly as Mr. Laban says.  The chips come in a metal cup on the side, piping hot and cut thick like steak fries.  I had to throw some extra salt on them, but they were properly fried too.

    We tried the deviled eggs (exactly the same recipe you'll find at any picnic) and the banoffee trifle.  This was a bummer, if you've ever had real banoffee pie - more like a bowl of whipped cream with some bananas thrown in.  Try something else on the dessert menu.

    The concept is great and the place has personality, but it's really not all that I had hoped for.  With sticky toffee pudding in mind, I convinced my friends after a failed attempt at Tria to go across the street to the new D Pub.  The interior looked almost provincial - like a cross between a traditional steakhouse and a beer hall from Lord of the Rings.  Masculine but styled.

    My companions ordered the burger and fries and I had the rabbit pie.  They enjoyed their burgers and I enjoyed some of their fries.  I liked the cubicle cut of the fries, which gave them a nice uniform crunch.  

    My rabbit pie was small.  It was rich and flavorful, but not what I expected from such a masculine place.  It reminded me more of a dainty souffle than a big meaty rabbit stew Samwise Gamgee would make for Mr. Frodo.  I'll stop now with the LOTR references.  The filing was creamy and the rabbit was tender, but I wouldn't recommend it for someone really hungry.

    At last came the sticky toffee pudding.  It was warm, chewy on the sides, with a side of delicious rum raisin ice cream melting into the syrup.  I can see why this is so popular in England, it's the warm sweater you want to wrap yourself in before braving the chilling fog.  

    All in all, this place is interesting and worth a try, but I would reserve Dandelion for times when you want to spend a bit more on something quirky rather than when you're really looking for a hunger-satiating grub hub.

    If you don't like it here, check your pulse.  Before you even get in the door, you can't help but be mesmerized on the beautiful job that was done restoring the building.  Dandelion has an aesthetic value that you can enjoy just by walking down 18th street, which is far more than can be said for most restaurants.  

    Inside things are no different.  It's like dining in an antique store and I'm personally into that sort of thing.  The taxidermy, weird furniture and kitschy bric-a-brac you'd find in your wacko Aunt Cindy's house adds to the charm of the place.  The waitstaff is very easy on the eyes to boot.

    The beer selection offers brews that can't be found anywhere else in town, such as my personal favorite -- Bombardier by Wells.  

    Dandy might not be priced to allow you to drink there every night, but treat yourself (and the one you're with) to a drink and a snack here every once in a while just to remind yourself that it's possible for a restaurant to totally transport you to another place and time.

    It was with considerable excitement and some trepidation that I made last second reservations to go… It was with considerable excitement and some trepidation that I made last second reservations to go to the Dandelion, the latest member of the Starr empire that recently opened just off Rittenhouse Square. Mounting the stairs to the second floor hostess station, you immediately feel like you are transported to a brownstone somewhere in Nodding Hill with hustle and bustle all about, and the promise of a well kept pint and some Yorkshire pudding to boot.

    I should explain that my family always had the roast and Yorkshire pudding for Christmas dinner. For me I  was not only trying out a new restaurant, but was walking down memory lane, back to my childhood, replete with the family gathered with friends for Christmas dinner, with the roast, the pudding, the creamed onions and the spinach souffle, with the anticipation of the flaming Christmas pudding and more Christmas cookies to finish off the meal.

    The decor feels like a ramshackle old house, a restaurant version of the Cloisters, an architectural hodgepodge sewing different pieces of restaurants and pubs together. I was seated in a side room on the second floor on a corner seat attached to a large fireplace, (though the fire in this fireplace was an electric glow rather than real wood.) This little seat gave me a wonderful view of the room and a glimpse out of the large window onto 18th Street.

    Service was attentive, and not pushy at all. I had a wonderful cask British Bitter, served not in the standard imperial pint glass but rather a cut glass mug with handles that is so rarely seen in the US that I positively beamed when they put it down on the table.

    Bread service was good with a honey stout bread, and a country loaf served. The butter accompanying it had a bit of a horseradish bite to it. I opted for the roast as that was what made me come down on the the spur of the moment. The plate consisted of a large piece of sirloin roast, well marbled and cut quite thick. It was sitting in its own pool of juices or gravy. Also there were watercress, in a mustard dressing, two large roasted potatoes. Served on the side were roasted carrots, and the Yorkshire pudding. Meat temperature was right (I requested medium rare) and the pudding was good, though a bit dry. In my opinion it was closer to a popover than a true Yorkshire pudding. At home we make ours in a large casserole pan rather than in individual muffin like tins, which gives a much different ratio of crust to pudding part.  Still quite good, especially after smothering it in some of the left over juices from the beef. Half way through the plate, my beer ran out and I had a half pint of a very nice cask conditioned porter. Again points to the Dandelion for serving half pints, something that should be more common in the US than it actually is.

    I pushed on and decided to order dessert as well, opting for the Guinness cake, purported to have ginger in the cake and Guinness ice cream on top. It was served in an iron skillet like dish, and in many ways felt more like a pudding than a cake.  

    I will say that it is a shame they do not emphasize more organic and local ingredients. They should look to great restaurants like the Two Fat Ladies at the Buttery in Glasgow for ways that restaurants are producing innovative cooking with fresh local ingredients. Perhaps overtime it will get there.

    Overall, it was a nice meal at the restaurant. I think that after the masses have stopped mobbing the place, and the restaurant adds lunch and tea, it will take on more character. It doesn't feel like a pub to me, either one where you go to shoot darts, or one where you sit at the bar for a quick Ploughman's and a pint, but it has promise for a nice atmosphere, comfort foods and the service that Starr restaurants are known for. Specialties

London's culinary revolution comes to Philadelphia with this eclectic English pub. Cozy rooms and intimate bars create a storied, Old World aura, while updated seasonal takes on British food make up the menu. In keeping with tradition, cask and tap craft beers are always rotating, a proper afternoon tea service has a sweet and savory menu, and classic beef family-style roasts are served on Sundays.
